2. Acknowledged Gaps — Standard Framing
These are topics where no peer-reviewed source exists for the specific claim needed. The honest position is named, the gap is declared, and practitioner consensus is presented with that framing. Copy the template wording into each article — do not invent a more confident framing.
Topic	Honest position	Standard framing template
Hygral fatigue	The 'swelling-damage cycle' claim is not supported by current evidence. Wet hair is mechanically weaker; the risk is rough handling while wet, not the wetting itself.	"The popular term 'hygral fatigue' implies a swelling-damage cycle that is not well supported by current evidence — the concern is not the wetting itself but what you do to the hair while it is wet."
Chelating shampoo frequency for 4C specifically	No 4C-specific frequency study exists. All recommendations extrapolate from general textured-hair guidance. Salon consensus is monthly chelating max.	"What isn't well-documented is the right frequency for 4C hair specifically — every source extrapolates from general guidance. The salon consensus, for what it's worth, is around once a month in a hard-water area, always followed by deep conditioning."
Protein treatment frequency for high-porosity 4C	No peer-reviewed trial on safe frequency for specific curl types. Adjust by hair response.	"No peer-reviewed trial exists on protein treatment frequency for 4C hair specifically. The practical guide: if hair feels mushy or stretches without springing back when wet, it needs protein. If it snaps without stretching, it needs moisture. Adjust by what your hair does."
Protective style duration	The widely cited '6–8 weeks max' figure has no controlled study behind it. Signs of tension and scalp health matter more than a calendar number.	"There's no controlled study on safe maximum duration for protective styles. The practical signals matter more than a number: new growth tangling into the style, tension on the scalp, or dryness you can't address without removing the style."
Satin vs silk for hair	Both reduce friction vs cotton. No peer-reviewed hair study compares them directly. Polyester satin generates static; silk is the cleaner option but significantly more expensive.	"Both satin and silk reduce friction against hair compared to cotton — no peer-reviewed study has compared them directly for hair outcomes. Polyester satin is the affordable option but generates static on some hair types; silk eliminates that but costs more."
Wash frequency by curl type	No peer-reviewed wash frequency guidance exists for specific curl subtypes. Tighter coils generally need less frequent washing but more frequent moisturising. Adjust by scalp health.	"No study has established the right wash frequency for 3B or 4C hair specifically. The practical guide: wash when the scalp needs it — itching, build-up, or odour — not on a fixed schedule."
Air drying vs diffusing in UK humidity	No RH/diffusing study exists for specific curl subtypes. UK ambient RH is generally higher than US contexts Oct–Apr. Practitioner consensus: diffuse to 80% in high-humidity conditions to set the gel cast before frizz sets in.	"No study has compared air drying vs diffusing outcomes for 3B or 4C hair in high-humidity conditions. The practitioner consensus for UK winters and wet months: diffuse to around 80% dry to set the gel cast, then let it finish air drying. Air drying alone in high humidity can mean frizz sets before the cast forms."
Toddler hair development (tangle-proneness)	No trichology study specifically on toddler curl tangling. Finer diameter and higher cuticle exposure per bend are the structural reasons; cooperation window is the practical constraint.	"Toddler hair tangles more readily than adult hair for reasons that are partly structural — finer strands, more bends per centimetre on tighter curl patterns — and partly practical: toddlers move constantly, sleep hard, and haven't yet learned to protect their hair."
3A–3C subtype diameter boundaries	Andre Walker typing system has no peer-reviewed basis. Diameter descriptors are practitioner consensus. The follicle-shape mechanism is established cosmetic chemistry.	"The 3A–3C labels are a practical shorthand, not a biological classification — there's no peer-reviewed study that draws the line between them. What the science does confirm is that curl tightness is driven by follicle shape: the more elliptical the follicle, the tighter the curl."

3. Banned Claims
These claims look sourceable but are not. Never make them, regardless of how the draft frames them.
Claim (do not make)	Why it fails	What to say instead
4C hair does not vary in growth rate vs other types / grows at the same rate	Directly contradicted by Loussouarn 2001 (BJD). African hair grows measurably slower per day. The retention reframe holds regardless.	"Even at the slower published rate (~0.9 cm/month), that is 11 cm of new growth a year. If length isn't changing, the problem is retention, not growth."
Vitamin D deficiency causes hair loss	Association with TE is established; causation is contested. 2024 review suggests VDD may be compensatory response, not cause.	"Strong association with shedding; causation still argued in the literature. Worth ruling out, not treating as the answer."
Hygral fatigue damages hair through repeated swelling	Not supported by current evidence (Lab Muffin Beauty Science, Jan 2026 debunks swelling-damage cycle).	Use the wet-hair-is-mechanically-weaker frame only.
Hard water damages the follicle / affects growth at the root	Hard water causes mineral build-up on the shaft, not follicle damage. The effect is mechanical (shaft coated, moisture blocked), not biological.	"Hard water leaves mineral deposits on the hair shaft that block moisture and add weight, causing breakage. It does not affect the follicle or growth rate."
BTMS is a sulfate / harsh like SLS	BTMS is a quaternary ammonium salt, not a sulfate detergent. Common misconception in the natural hair community.	"Despite 'methosulfate' in the name, BTMS is not a harsh sulfate detergent — it is a conditioning agent that works by electrostatic attraction, not by stripping."
Specific curl-type diameter in mm from a study	No peer-reviewed study has measured 3A/3B/3C diameter boundaries. All figures are practitioner consensus.	"Around the diameter of a marker" — no clinical figure, no study citation.
UV / sun exposure meaningfully damages 4C hair in the UK	Removed from Article 3 — no defensible UK-specific data; UV is a minor factor in UK conditions relative to hard water, mechanical handling, and moisture loss.	Do not include UV as a hair damage factor in UK-context articles.
