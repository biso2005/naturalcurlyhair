# 1. The spine
 
| **The principle everything hangs off** *The site does not tell users what their hair needs. It helps them figure it out, with better information than they would have otherwise — and the honesty to admit when it does not know.* |
| --- |
 
This is the operating principle. Every editorial, design, and product decision should be traceable to it. Three things follow:
 
- The site is a thinking-partner, not an authority. It surfaces the most likely cause, the standard first thing to try, and the honest next branch — and lets the reader make the call.
 
- The site takes credit for good outcomes and is not responsible for bad ones. The reader owns the decision and the outcome. The site earns trust by being right often enough that credit accrues — not by claiming a power it does not have.
 
- The site is silent at the limit of its competence. Where the data is thin, where the case is medical, where the answer genuinely is "it depends" — that is what gets said. Hedge the claim, not the action.
 
# 2. Positioning statement
 
| **The statement** *For people with natural curly and Afro hair in the UK who are tired of advice that is really a sales pitch, NaturalCurly is the independent hair resource that helps you figure out what is actually going on with your hair — including telling you when there is no product fix, when it is technique, when it is your water, when it is medical — because we have nothing of our own to sell you.* |

Test it passes: a reader in distress hears the offer ("help me figure this out") rather than the claim ("I have the answer"). Competitors who claim to have the answer cannot reposition into helping the reader think — their economics depend on selling.

# 4. Brand attributes
 
Five words customers should reach for to describe us:
 
- **Useful** — the defining trait. The reader leaves with something they can do, not just something they read.
 
- **Honest** — including about the limits of what we know. We say "we are not sure" when we are not sure.
 
- **Independent** — no product to sell, no brand to please.
 
- **Warm** — of-the-community, on the reader's side, never cold or clinical.
 
- **Clear** — plain-spoken; respects the reader's time and intelligence.
 
*Anti-traits* — words we never want to earn: salesy, preachy, definitive, expert-by-self-label, pastel/girly-by-default, generic, performative.
 
Note on what dropped: "knowledgeable" came out. It read as a self-claim of authority. We demonstrate knowledge through accurate, specific, lived content — not by labelling ourselves with it.

# 6. Brand voice
 
Five traits, each defined by what it is and is not. This is the standalone voice reference — use it for every piece of copy.
 
| **We are** | **We are NOT** |
| --- | --- |
| Honest | Blunt for its own sake |
| Confidence-graded | Falsely certain or hand-wavingly vague |
| Warm | Saccharine or performative |
| Plain-spoken | Dumbed-down or jargon-heavy |
| Dryly witty | Jokey, punny or cute |
 
## Honest (not blunt for its own sake)
 
- Sounds like: "Honestly? Skip this. The £6 one does the same job."
 
- Sounds like: "We haven't tested this in UK water yet, so we are not going to pretend to have a verdict."
 
- Sounds like: "This worked for us — but if your hair is high-porosity, it probably will not."
 
- NOT: "This product is garbage and anyone who buys it is wasting their money." (honesty without the care)
 
## Confidence-graded (the new discipline)
 
Every claim signals how sure we are. High-confidence things are said strongly. Low-confidence things are said softly. Uncertain things are acknowledged as uncertain. This is what separates us from sites that sound confident about everything regardless of the evidence.
 
- Sounds like: "This is well-established — hard water deposits minerals that block moisture absorption." (high confidence — settled)
 
- Sounds like: "The standard first move most curl specialists recommend is a chelating shampoo." (medium — described, not claimed)
 
- Sounds like: "This is contested. Some chemists say the float test is meaningful; some say it measures surface damage, not internal porosity. Here is the honest case for both." (low — disputed)
 
- Sounds like: "We do not have a confident answer on this. The most likely causes are X or Y. Here is what to track to figure out which." (no answer — said plainly)
 
- NOT: "Studies show..." without a study. NOT: "Experts agree..." when they do not.
 
## Warm (not saccharine)
 
- Sounds like: "If this isn't your hair type and you are doing your child's — start here, no judgement."
 
- Sounds like: "You have probably got a graveyard of half-used bottles. So did we."
 
- NOT: "We are SO excited to be on this beautiful curl journey with you, gorgeous! 💕"
 
## Plain-spoken (not dumbed-down)
 
- Sounds like: "Hard water leaves mineral build-up that blocks moisture. A monthly chelating wash removes it."
 
- Explains the term once, then uses it. Assumes intelligence, not prior knowledge.
 
- NOT: walls of trichology jargon. NOT: "the wet stuff that comes out your tap" over-simplification.
 
## Dryly witty (not jokey)
 
- Sounds like: "The product you blamed was probably innocent. The water was the problem all along."
 
- Wit comes from honesty and observation, never from puns or hair-related dad jokes.
 
- NOT: "Curl power! Let's get coily with it! 😜"
 
**Always: British English. UK spellings, UK products, UK pricing.**
 
# 7. Brand story
 
Most natural hair advice in the UK is one of two things: written for someone else's hair (US sites, US products, US water), or written by a brand that needs you to buy its bottle. So people with curly and Afro hair spend years and a fortune working it out by trial and error — a cupboard of half-used products, advice that contradicts itself, and reviews they cannot trust because the reviewer is selling something.
 
NaturalCurly exists to do something none of them do: not to tell you what your hair needs, but to help you figure it out. We surface the most likely cause for your symptoms, in your area, with your routine. We tell you the standard first thing to try. We tell you what to watch for. And if it does not work, we tell you the next thing to look at. We have nothing of our own to sell, so the verdict is never bent toward a payout. Written by people who actually have this hair, for the UK we actually live in.

# 13. Operating principles
 
The operational rules that protect the spine. These exist before they are tested, so the answer is decided when it is cheap rather than when it is expensive.
 
## The article pattern
 
Every diagnostic article expresses the spine at reading-speed. The shape is consistent:
 
- Open with the surface complaint (the reader's own words), not a definition.
 
- Name the most likely cause for someone in their situation — confidence-graded.
 
- Give the standard first thing to try, specifically, with a how-to-know-if-it-worked marker.
 
- Name the next branch if the first thing did not work.
 
- Exit to a professional where the symptoms warrant it — in parallel to the hair advice, not replacing it.
 
## The tool
 
A decision-support tool, not a prescription engine. Built rules-based, not LLM-based. Specific about action, hedged about claim. Confidence-graded outputs. Exits are silent and structural — the user can leave at any screen with something useful in hand. Registration is offered as a profile (resume, more on your topic, sharper over time), not as a newsletter signup.
 
## The verdict-never-bent rule
 
| **Process beats willpower** *The verdict on any product is decided editorially, before affiliate links are added, by a person who has not seen the commission rates. The day we tilt a verdict for a payout is the day we start spending the trust we are monetising. This is the asset. It is fixed.* |
| --- |
 
## The LLM rule
 
No LLM-generated content in articles, tool outputs, or user-facing copy. LLM use in the production process (brainstorming, drafting structure, sense-checking) is fine — the user never sees it. The site competes by being something a chatbot cannot replicate, not by being a smaller version of one.
 
## The email / profile rule
 
The email list is a service obligation, not a marketing asset. Emails are triggered by specific reasons connected to a specific profile — a follow-up to a recommendation, a new article that matches the reader's situation, a reply to feedback they gave. The site does not send a weekly newsletter. The profile delivers value to the user (resume, relevance, sharper recommendations over time); the user gives an email in honest exchange for that value.
 
## The faceless rule, refined
 
"Faceless" means no founder personality and no parasocial creator-style. It does NOT mean no humans. Named contributors — UK trichologists, curl specialists, salon owners — are encouraged and probably necessary to earn the authority the spine needs. The site has a brand voice; specific contributors get bylines on the pieces where their expertise is load-bearing.
 
# 14. The operational nos
 
Things the site definitely will not do. Decided now while the answers are easy. The honesty positioning is only as strong as the rules that protect it from drift — and the drift is most likely the moment one of these becomes a live option.
 
- No sponsored content. Ever. "Sponsored" includes paid placements, paid reviews, paid features, paid "partnerships" of any kind. The affiliate model is declared, transparent, and is the only commercial model.
 
- No own product. The site never sells its own range. The credibility of the recommendations depends on having nothing to sell.
 
- No gifted reviews. Products are bought, not sent by brands. PR samples are declined or returned. If a product is reviewed, it was paid for.
 
- No newsletter (in the traditional push sense). Communication with readers is through profile-driven, relevance-matched emails. No weekly digest, no scheduled blast.
 
- No LLM-generated content. As above. Production tools are fine; output is not.
 
- No medical claims. The site does not diagnose, prescribe, or replace a trichologist, GP, or dermatologist. Where symptoms warrant it, the site routes the reader to a real person.
 
- No paywalled content. The site is free. The commercial model is affiliate and display. A paywall would contradict the help-you-figure-it-out posture.
 
- No tracking the reader outside their session for ad targeting. Profile data is service-facing, not commercial-facing. No selling, sharing, or repurposing of reader data.
 
# 15. Do's and don'ts
 
## Do
 
- Lead with the most likely cause and the first thing to try, including "skip the spend" answers when relevant.
 
- Confidence-grade every claim. Strong where strong. Soft where soft. Honest where unsure.
 
- Declare affiliate links plainly, every time, and frame the disclosure as proof of independence.
 
- Use real curl-to-coil photography and the reader's own words.
 
- Be UK-specific — products, water, schools, pricing.
 
- Build in an exit ramp where symptoms warrant a professional.
 
## Don't
 
- Bend a verdict toward a higher commission. Ever. This is the line that protects everything.
 
- Claim authority over the reader's hair. We have not seen it. We do not know it. We surface; they decide.
 
- Drift into pastel/girly beauty-blog or luxury-beauty cues.
 
- Slide into generic wavy / curly-girl-method content.
 
- Use puns, hype words ("game-changer," "dive in"), or performative enthusiasm.
 
- Pretend certainty when there isn't any. "We do not know" is acceptable copy.
 
# 16. Quality check
 
The tests a piece of work has to pass:
 
- Could a stranger write on-brand copy from this document? If not, it is underspecified.
 
- Does this piece help the reader figure something out, or does it tell them what to do? (The former is the spine; the latter is the prescription posture we have moved away from.)
 
- Is every claim confidence-graded? Could a chemist or trichologist read this and find an unjustified assertion?
 
- Is it clearly different from brand blogs, Treasure Tress, and US sites? (Independence + UK + help-you-figure-it-out.)
 
- Would a real reader in the audience nod at the positioning statement?
 
- Is every element intentional, not arbitrary?
