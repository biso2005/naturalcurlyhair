## 3. Products to Consider (Generic)

- **Lightweight liquid leave-ins** — suited to low porosity; heavy creams sit on top of the cuticle
- **Humectant-rich conditioners** (glycerin, aloe vera base) — draw moisture in for low porosity in humid climates
- **Protein treatments (light, e.g. hydrolysed wheat or silk protein)** — relevant to high porosity to temporarily patch gaps
- **Butter-based sealants** — high porosity sealing step; writer to test layering order
- **Clarifying shampoos** — low porosity builds product residue quickly; frequency is testable
- **Gel with flexible hold** — 3B needs definition without crunch; cast formation should be observed and noted

## 4. Gaps in Current Top Results to Exploit

### Gap 1 — No original testing, anywhere
Every top result states porosity advice as fact with zero documented evidence. No site has filmed, measured, or recorded a test on an actual 3B head. Publishing even a basic spray-bottle water absorption test with photos and timing data immediately separates this piece from all ten competitors.

### Gap 2 — Medium porosity is invisible
Results split exclusively into low vs high. Medium porosity 3B hair exists and is arguably the most common. A section treating it as a real category — with its own routine logic — captures searches and readers that every current result ignores.

### Gap 3 — Porosity and 3B curl pattern treated as separate topics bolted together
Competitors either cover 3B generally or porosity generally, then combine them loosely. No result explains *why* the tight spiral of 3B (vs 3A or 3C) creates specific challenges — e.g. product pooling at bends, slower water absorption due to overlapping curl layers. This mechanistic explanation is absent and ownable.
