Wash day for a mixed race child is one of those things that sounds manageable in theory and becomes a negotiation in practice. The hair needs moisture, the child doesn't want to sit still, and the window between "cooperative" and "we're done" is shorter than you think.

The routine here is practical first: what to do in what order, why each step matters, and how to adjust by age. UK-specific where it counts — hard water across most of England matters for children's hair too, and central heating in winter is a real factor for looser curl types.

---

## How Often to Wash

Every seven to ten days for coily or curly hair (type 3 and 4). Less frequent than adult hair of the same type because children are often less exposed to product buildup and their scalps don't produce oil at the same rate as adults.

Every five to seven days if the child is very active or the scalp gets visibly oily.

Washing more than weekly is rarely necessary and can dry the hair out. Between wash days, a small amount of leave-in or a water spritz maintains moisture without a full wash.

---

## Wash Day Steps

### 1. Detangle before wetting (if hair is significantly tangled)

For type 4 hair especially, dry or slightly dampened pre-detangling before water reduces shrinkage-related tangles during washing. Apply a small amount of conditioner or detangling spray to sections, work through with fingers or a wide-tooth comb from the ends upward, then section the hair into four or six parts with clips before washing.

For looser curl types (type 3 or mixed 3/4), this step is often unnecessary. Wet detangling with conditioner is sufficient.

### 2. Wet the hair thoroughly

Fully saturate. Use warm water, not hot — hot water dries the scalp and can irritate a child's more sensitive skin.

In a hard water area: consider a final rinse with filtered or distilled water if hard water seems to be causing buildup or dullness. See below.

### 3. Shampoo (scalp only)

A gentle sulphate-free shampoo or a co-wash (cleansing conditioner), applied to the scalp and massaged in gently. Do not scrub the length of the hair — the shampoo rinses through it adequately and scrubbing causes tangles.

**Monthly in hard water areas:** a children's-appropriate or very mild chelating shampoo removes mineral deposits. Look for EDTA or phytic acid. Hard water affects children's hair the same way it affects adult hair — mineral buildup makes the curl or coil look dull and makes products work less well. In London and the South East this is worth adding monthly.

### 4. Condition

Generous amount of rinse-out conditioner from mid-length to ends. Leave for two to five minutes.

**Detangle here** — while the conditioner is in and providing slip. Fingers first, then wide-tooth comb if needed. Work from the tips upward in small sections. This is the step where patience matters most. If the child finds detangling painful, more conditioner and smaller sections are the first fixes.

Rinse partially or fully. Leaving a small amount of conditioner in is fine and adds moisture without a separate leave-in step.

### 5. Apply leave-in while hair is soaking wet

A leave-in conditioner or curl cream applied immediately after rinsing, while the hair is still dripping, locks in moisture as the curl forms. Section the hair into four parts, apply product to each section, and work it through with fingers.

For type 4 hair: a richer leave-in or curl cream seals moisture better. For type 3: a lighter leave-in.

**UK winter adjustment:** add a sealing step — a small amount of lightweight oil (coconut, jojoba) applied over the leave-in helps the hair retain moisture in dry centrally-heated indoor air.

## Drying

### Plopping

Gather the hair into a microfibre towel or old t-shirt for 10–20 minutes. Absorbs excess water without disrupting the curl pattern. Toddlers often cooperate with this because it's brief.

### Air dry or diffuse

**Air dry** in warmer months when the indoor air has enough moisture. Let the hair dry fully before bed.

**Diffuse** in autumn and winter — centrally-heated rooms in UK winter dry out curls and coils before they can set. Low heat, diffuser cup, gentle. Many children find diffusing enjoyable when it's framed as "making the curls fluffy."

Do not tie wet hair up or put a child to bed with wet hair — this causes matting, which makes the next wash day significantly harder.

---

## Night Routine

**Satin bonnet or satin pillowcase.** Cotton pillowcases dry out curls and coils overnight and cause friction frizz. A children's satin bonnet worn to bed is the single highest-impact routine change for maintaining definition between wash days. Many children adapt to wearing one quickly if introduced early.

**Pineapple for older children:** gather hair very loosely at the very top of the head with a satin scrunchie. Protects the curl shape without compression.

**Between wash days:** a small spritz of water and a small amount of leave-in or curl cream refreshes the hair without a full wash. Distilled or filtered water in hard water areas.

## By Age

### Babies (0–18 months)

Wash once a week or less. A gentle co-wash or plain water rinse is often enough. No need for styling products unless the hair is very long. The main care at this stage is keeping the scalp clean and the hair moisturised.

### Toddlers (18 months–3 years)

Every seven to ten days. This is when the routine starts mattering more — the hair may be growing longer, the texture may be changing, and tangles become more of an issue. Detangling during conditioning, lots of conditioner, and small sections.

Keeping wash day positive: let the child hold a toy or watch something. Keep it to 30–40 minutes maximum. Stop if they become distressed — a calm, cooperative child next time is more useful than a perfect routine this time.

### Children (4 years and up)

Every seven to ten days. Begin involving the child in the routine — explaining what each product does, letting them choose a scent, putting some leave-in on themselves. Children who understand their own hair and feel ownership of the routine care for it better as they get older.

## Troubleshooting

**Detangling is causing tears and resistance:** more conditioner, smaller sections, fingers before comb, and work from the very tips upward. Never pull through a tangle from root to tip. If the child is very resistant, stop and try again another day — forced wash days create lasting resistance.

**Hair looks dull despite conditioning:** hard water mineral buildup. Add a mild monthly chelating wash.

**Hair dry within a day of wash day:** products too light, or the leaving-in step is missing. Apply leave-in while hair is soaking wet; add a light oil seal in winter.

**One side always tanglier than the other:** the side the child sleeps on. Satin bonnet and pineapple reduce overnight friction.

## FAQs

**How much product should I use on a child's hair?**

Enough to coat the section without the hair feeling heavy. A pea-to-grape-sized amount of leave-in per section for fine type 3 hair; up to a 50p-coin amount per section for dense type 4 hair. Start small — you can always add more.

**My child has very tight 4C hair. Is the routine different?**

The steps are the same but the moisture requirement is higher and detangling takes more time and care. Pre-detangling before wetting, generous conditioner during wash, rich leave-in and sealing oil after washing. A deep conditioning treatment under a warm cap every two to four weeks helps significantly.

**Do children's hair products work better than adult products?**

Not necessarily. "Children's" labelling often means fragrance-free and lower-irritant, which is useful for sensitive scalps. The ingredient quality varies as much as adult products. An adult leave-in with gentle ingredients is perfectly suitable for a child.

**The school puts my child's hair up in a style I don't like and it's causing damage. What can I do?**

Speak to the school directly. UK schools are increasingly aware of natural hair care requirements, and many have adopted policies recognising protective styles. A GP's note is not needed — a direct conversation about what styles are suitable and what causes damage is a reasonable first step.
