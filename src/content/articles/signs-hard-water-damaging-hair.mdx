If your hair has felt rough, dull, or resistant to conditioner for a while — and nothing you've tried has fixed it — hard water is worth ruling out before you buy anything else. Here's how to tell if it's the culprit, and what to do if it is.

---

## First: Is Your Area Hard Water?

Hard water is tap water that carries dissolved minerals — mainly calcium and magnesium — picked up as it passes through chalk and limestone rock. The more minerals, the harder the water.

Most of London, the South East, East Anglia, and the Midlands has hard to very hard water. Scotland, Wales, and much of the North West are generally softer. But it varies even within a single area — the only way to know for certain is to check.

**Check in two minutes:** go to your water supplier's website and enter your postcode. Thames Water, Anglian Water, Severn Trent — they all publish this. Anything above 200 parts per million (ppm) is hard. Above 300 ppm is very hard.

Or: try lathering plain soap between your palms under your tap. Hard water barely lathers and leaves a filmy feeling. Soft water foams freely.

## The Five Signs

### 1. Your hair feels rough straight after washing

You've just washed and conditioned, but the strands feel coarse or wiry — not soft the way they should. This is the most consistent hard water sign. Minerals settle on the hair and roughen the surface, which is why no amount of conditioner seems to fix it. You're conditioning over a mineral layer, not through it.

### 2. Conditioner and products stop absorbing

You apply your usual leave-in or styler and it just sits there. Your hair feels coated rather than moisturised. That mineral film is blocking absorption — products can't do their job when they can't get in.

### 3. Curl definition is fading

Curls that used to clump and spring now separate or go limp within a few hours of drying. You haven't changed your routine. This is one of the more frustrating hard water symptoms because it's easy to blame the product or your technique when neither is the problem.

### 4. You see more breakage than usual

Not shed hairs — short snapped pieces. When minerals rough up the hair surface, strands snag on each other more during detangling and styling. That friction adds up, and breakage follows. Shed hairs with a white root attached are normal. Short pieces mid-strand are a sign of damage.

### 5. Your scalp is persistently itchy or flaky despite shampooing

Hard water minerals settle on the scalp too. Anti-dandruff shampoos might give temporary relief, but if the itch keeps coming back after every wash, it's worth questioning whether your shampoo is actually clearing everything — because a standard shampoo can't remove mineral build-up.

## Hard Water vs. Product Buildup vs. Protein Overload

These three feel similar and the solutions are different, so it's worth knowing which one you're dealing with.

| | Hard water | Product buildup | Too much protein |
|---|---|---|---|
| Hair feels | Rough, wiry, dry | Heavy, greasy, tacky | Brittle, snapping |
| Curl definition | Gradually loosening | Clumped but weighed down | Tight but fragile |
| Responds to clarifying shampoo | Partly, briefly | Yes, noticeably | Yes — may feel worse after |
| Responds to chelating shampoo | Yes, clearly | Partly | No difference |
| Responds to deep conditioner | Temporarily | Not much | Yes, dramatically |

If your hair bounces back fully after one good clarifying wash and the improvement holds for a week or two, product buildup was the problem. If it improves briefly then reverts — or a chelating wash (one with EDTA or phytic acid on the label) works better than a clarifying wash — hard water is likely the cause.

---

## The Test That Tells You for Sure

Do one wash with a chelating shampoo. Not a clarifying shampoo — a chelating one, which is designed to pull minerals off the hair. Check the label for the words EDTA or phytic acid.

Follow it with your usual conditioner. Compare the result to your last few washes.

If your hair feels noticeably softer, your curls look more defined, and detangling is easier — hard water was the problem. The difference is often immediate and obvious.

If nothing changes, look elsewhere: product choice, technique, or porosity mismatch.

## What to Do If It Is Hard Water

**Monthly chelating wash.** In hard water areas (200 ppm+), add a chelating wash once a month as a reset. Every two weeks if your water is very hard (300 ppm+). Always follow with a deep conditioner — chelating cleans thoroughly and the hair needs moisture back in.

**DIY rinse between washes.** A citric acid rinse — half a teaspoon of food-grade citric acid powder dissolved in a litre of water, used as a final rinse — helps keep minerals from building up between chelating washes. ACV works too but is milder and the smell lingers.

**Shower filter — worth knowing.** Most shower filters sold for "hard water" reduce chlorine well but don't remove calcium and magnesium. They can help with scalp sensitivity linked to chlorine, but they won't solve mineral build-up on curly hair. Don't rely on one as your only fix.

## FAQs

**Can hard water permanently damage curly hair?**

The mineral build-up itself is removable — a chelating wash clears it. But if you've had months of extra breakage as a result, that physical damage has to grow out. The sooner you address it, the less there is to recover from.

**Does hard water affect all curl types the same way?**

Tighter, more porous curl types — 3C through 4C — tend to show hard water symptoms faster because the structure gives minerals more places to settle and hold. But any curl type can be affected in a hard water area.

**My hair is fine in the summer but worse in winter — is that hard water?**

Possibly, but seasonal humidity is also a factor. If your products stop working in winter across the board, read the humidity article first — the two problems look similar and the fixes are different.

→ [Why your wash and go reverts in UK humidity](/hair-care/wash-and-go-reverting-uk-humidity/)

**Do I need to invest in a whole-house water softener?**

Not for most people. A chelating shampoo on a regular schedule does the job for hair. A whole-house softener changes all your water — including drinking water — and is a much larger investment. Start with chelating first.
