No. Trimming does not make your hair grow faster. Hair grows from the follicle at the scalp — scissors touching the ends have no effect on that process. Growth rate is determined by genetics, health, and scalp condition, not by what happens to the tips.

What trimming *does* do is prevent breakage from travelling up the hair shaft, which means the length you grow actually stays on your head.

## Why it feels like trimming helps growth

If your ends are split or single-strand knotting, those sections will snap off during detangling and washing. You're growing hair at the scalp, but losing it from the ends at roughly the same rate, so length stays the same month after month.

Trimming removes the weak sections before they break higher up the shaft. You lose a centimetre now to keep three centimetres over the next few months. That's why people say "trim to retain length" rather than "trim to grow faster."

## How often to trim natural hair

There's no universal schedule. Signs you need a trim:

- Single-strand knots that won't brush out
- Ends that feel rough or look thin compared to the rest of the strand
- Breakage during detangling that seems to come from the mid-shaft or lower
- Styles that don't form at the tips

Some people with well-maintained natural hair trim twice a year. Some need to trim every three months. Some go a full year without needing one. Protective styling, moisture balance, and gentle detangling habits affect how quickly ends deteriorate.

## Searching for length

If you're not seeing growth despite trimming regularly, the question is retention rather than growth rate. Check:

- Are you losing length to breakage during detangling? (Detangle gently, section by section, with a lot of slip.)
- Are you losing length to single-strand knots in a dry climate or with low-porosity hair? (Seal your ends after every wash.)
- Are you in a hard water area? Mineral deposits cause mechanical breakage that mimics slow growth. A chelating shampoo monthly can change this significantly.

Hair grows roughly 1–1.5cm per month for most people. If you're seeing less than that over time, the length is leaving — not failing to arrive.
