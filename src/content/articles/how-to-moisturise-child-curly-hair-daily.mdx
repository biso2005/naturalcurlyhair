Wash day is once a week or so. The days in between are where the hair is actually living — getting dry, getting compressed overnight, getting handled at school. Daily or every-other-day moisture maintenance is what keeps curly and coily hair manageable between wash days and makes the next wash day easier.

This doesn't need to be a production. The routine here takes five minutes.

---

## Why Daily Moisture Matters for Curly and Coily Hair

The spiral structure of curly and coily hair means the scalp's natural oils travel down the shaft slowly and unevenly — much more slowly than on straight hair. This is why the hair gets dry between washes in a way that straight hair doesn't. External moisture, applied regularly, compensates for what the scalp can't distribute on its own.

Dry curly and coily hair:
- Tangles more easily
- Breaks more easily during detangling
- Loses curl definition
- Is harder to manage and style

Well-moisturised hair between washes means the hair stays in better condition, detangles more easily on wash day, and is more comfortable for the child.

---

## The Five-Minute Between-Wash Routine

### Step 1: Spritz with water

A light spritz of water reactivates the moisture and any product already in the hair. Don't over-saturate — enough to make the hair damp, not dripping.

**UK hard water:** use distilled or filtered water in the spritz bottle. Tap water in hard water areas (most of England) deposits minerals with every spritz; over a week this adds up and makes the hair feel rough and the next wash day harder. Cheap at supermarkets.

### Step 2: Apply a small amount of leave-in or curl cream

A pea-to-grape-sized amount worked through each section. For type 4 hair, a heavier leave-in or curl cream; for type 3, something lighter.

Apply to each section, work through with fingers, and smooth gently. This doesn't need to be a full product application — a small amount refreshes what's already in the hair.

### Step 3: Seal (optional but worth doing in winter)

A few drops of lightweight oil — coconut, jojoba, or argan — applied over the leave-in helps seal moisture in. This step matters most in autumn and winter when centrally-heated indoor air dries the hair faster.

### Step 4: Style or leave loose

Smooth into whatever style the child is wearing that day, or leave loose. A simple puff or two-strand twist takes another two minutes and protects the hair from manipulation during the school day.

That's it.

## What Not to Do Between Wash Days

**Don't dry-comb or brush coily hair.** If the hair has become tangled, spritz with water first, add conditioner or leave-in, and work through the tangles with fingers or a wide-tooth comb from the ends upward. Brushing dry type 4 hair causes breakage.

**Don't skip the water step and apply product to dry hair.** Products applied to dry hair without water don't absorb and just sit on the surface, adding buildup without moisturising.

**Don't over-apply product.** A small amount refreshes; too much accumulates and makes the next wash day harder.

## Night Routine: The Highest-Impact Step

What happens overnight often undoes the morning's moisture maintenance more than anything else.

**Satin bonnet:** the single most effective thing for maintaining moisture and definition between wash days. A children's satin bonnet worn to bed prevents cotton pillowcase friction, reduces overnight dryness from central heating, and means the hair is in significantly better condition in the morning. Children adapt quickly when introduced early.

**Pineapple (older children):** hair gathered very loosely at the very top of the head with a satin scrunchie. Protects the curl shape from compression without a crease.

Without a satin bonnet: the moisture you added in the morning is largely gone by morning, and the curl definition has been compressed by eight hours on a cotton pillowcase. The routine starts from scratch every day rather than building on yesterday's work.

## By Hair Type

### Type 3 (curly, ringlets, S-curls)

Lighter leave-in — a liquid spray rather than a cream. Less oil or skip it. The curl definition tends to hold better between washes than type 4; daily spritzing is often enough without a full product application every day.

### Type 4 (coily, Z-pattern, tight spirals)

Richer leave-in or curl cream. More oil in winter. Daily moisture maintenance matters more here — type 4 hair loses moisture faster than looser curl types and needs more consistent attention between washes.

---

## UK Winter Adjustment

Central heating in UK winter dries out children's curly and coily hair faster than in warmer months. The between-wash routine in winter:

- Add the oil seal step even if you skip it in summer
- Consider a light mist of water in the morning even if you did the full routine the night before
- Satin bonnet every night without exception — dry heated bedroom air overnight is significant

## FAQs

**How often do I actually need to moisturise between washes?**

For type 4 hair: every one to two days, or whenever the hair looks or feels dry. For type 3: every two to three days, or as needed. There's no strict schedule — if the hair is feeling dry or looks dull, that's the signal.

**My child hates having their hair touched in the morning. How do I make this faster?**

Keep the routine to spritz + leave-in + bonnet removal and shape-correction. Skip the oil unless it's needed. Two to three minutes is achievable. Make it consistent and fast enough that resistance reduces over time.

**Do I need a different product for between-wash days versus wash day?**

No — the same leave-in works for both. You use more on wash day (full application to soaking-wet hair) and less between washes (refresh only).

**My child has school PE and their hair gets sweaty. Does that count as moisturising?**

Sweat contains salt and doesn't replace water. After sweaty activity, a water spritz and small amount of leave-in is still the right step — don't assume the sweat has moisturised the hair.
