A school in the UK cannot require a pupil to cut their locs, cover their locs during school hours without a specific and proportionate reason, or exclude a pupil from school because of their locs. A policy that does any of these things is almost certainly unlawful under the Equality Act 2010.

This article is factual and designed to be used. If you need to write to a headteacher, share this article or cite the legislation directly.

---

## The Legal Framework

The Equality Act 2010 prohibits discrimination by educational institutions on grounds of race (including ethnic or national origins). Locs are a hairstyle strongly associated with Black and African heritage. A school policy that prohibits or restricts locs disproportionately disadvantages Black pupils — this is indirect racial discrimination, and it is unlawful unless the school can demonstrate the policy is a proportionate means of achieving a legitimate aim.

In 2023, the UK government updated statutory guidance for schools on uniform and appearance policies to make clear that:

> "Schools should be careful that any rules around hair do not directly or indirectly discriminate against pupils because of their race or any other protected characteristic."

> "Schools should make sure their policies do not, for example, prevent pupils from wearing natural Afro-textured hair or from wearing hairstyles that are culturally significant to them."

This guidance applies to state-funded schools. Independent schools have more flexibility, but are still bound by the Equality Act 2010.

---

## What Schools Cannot Do

Schools cannot:
- Require a pupil to cut their locs
- Require a pupil to cover their locs during school hours as a condition of attendance
- Exclude a pupil from school because of their locs
- Apply a uniform policy that singles out Afro hairstyles or locs while permitting equivalent styles on non-Black pupils
- Use subjective descriptions like "extreme" or "unconventional" to justify restrictions that only apply in practice to Black pupils' hairstyles

---

## What Schools Can Do

Schools can:
- Require pupils with any hair type to secure hair for safety reasons (science labs, D&T, food preparation)
- Apply uniform policies that are genuinely neutral and applied equally across all racial groups
- Set standards for hair colour or styling that do not disproportionately affect any particular ethnic group

The key test is always whether the policy applies equally in practice, not just in wording.

## What to Do If a School Raises Your Child's Locs

**Step 1: Request the written policy.** Ask for the school's hair/uniform policy in writing. It should specify exactly what is restricted and why.

**Step 2: Ask what legitimate aim is being pursued.** Under the Equality Act, indirect discrimination must be a proportionate means of achieving a legitimate aim. Ask the headteacher to state what that aim is in writing.

**Step 3: Write to the headteacher.** A calm, factual letter citing the Equality Act 2010 and the 2023 statutory guidance is the appropriate first step. Sample language:

> "I am writing regarding the school's policy on [my child's] locs. I understand that the Equality Act 2010 and the 2023 statutory guidance on school uniform policies make clear that schools cannot apply policies that indirectly discriminate against pupils on racial grounds. I request the school confirm in writing that [my child] will not face any adverse action regarding their hairstyle, and that the policy be reviewed for compliance with the above legislation."

**Step 4: Escalate if unresolved.** If the school does not respond appropriately:
- Contact the local authority (for maintained schools)
- Contact the Regional Schools Commissioner (for academies and free schools)
- Complain to the Education and Skills Funding Agency (ESFA)
- Contact the Equality and Human Rights Commission for advice

**Step 5: Keep records.** Document all communications — dates, names, what was said. This documentation supports any escalation.

## For the Pupil

Older pupils who are managing this themselves need to know:
- Their locs are protected by law
- They do not need to cut or change them to comply with a lawful school policy
- They can ask for the policy in writing
- They have the right to raise a formal complaint

Supporting a teenager through this while not making it entirely your fight is the balance worth striking — the confidence that comes from navigating it with backing, rather than having a parent take over entirely, is part of the outcome.

## Common Scenarios

**"The school says locs are 'extreme' or 'unconventional.'"**

Ask whether the school applies the same description to styles with equivalent volume or length worn by non-Black pupils. If not, the policy is discriminatory in application. Request the written policy and cite the 2023 government guidance.

**"The school excluded my child for refusing to cut their locs."**

Exclusion for a protected hairstyle is very likely to be unlawful. Contact the local authority and seek advice from the Equality and Human Rights Commission immediately. Document everything.

**"The school says the policy applies to everyone — no 'non-conventional' hairstyles."**

The application, not just the wording, determines whether discrimination is occurring. If in practice the policy is only applied to Black pupils' styles, it is indirect discrimination regardless of wording.

**"The school is an independent school. Does the law apply?"**

Independent schools are still bound by the Equality Act 2010. They have more flexibility on some appearance policies, but cannot apply policies that are racially discriminatory. The 2023 statutory guidance applies primarily to state-funded schools, but the Equality Act applies universally.

## FAQs

**My child's school is otherwise excellent. Should I push back?**

That's your decision. What's useful to know: you have more standing than you might expect, and most schools respond to a politely worded written letter citing the legislation. Many discriminatory hair policies are applied out of habit or ignorance rather than intent — the letter itself often resolves it.

**The school says it's about uniform consistency, not race.**

This is the most common response. The test under the Equality Act is not intent but effect — whether the policy disproportionately affects pupils of a particular racial group. A policy that is applied consistently in practice, and that disadvantages Black pupils more than others, is indirect discrimination regardless of intent.

**We're in a predominantly white area and my child is one of very few Black pupils. Does the same law apply?**

Yes. The Equality Act 2010 applies regardless of the demographic composition of the school. One affected pupil is sufficient for the law to apply.
