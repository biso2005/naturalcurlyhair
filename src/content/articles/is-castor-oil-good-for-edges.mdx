Castor oil is commonly recommended for edges, and it's useful — but not for the reason most people think. The claim that castor oil regrows thinning edges doesn't have strong evidence behind it. What it does is provide a practical medium for scalp massage, and it works as a sealant on the delicate hair in the hairline area. Both of those things are worth doing.

This article is consistent with the position in our [castor oil evidence review](/products/castor-oil-hair-growth-evidence-uk/): castor oil is a good sealant and a reasonable massage oil. It is not a proven hair growth treatment.

## What castor oil does for the edge area

**Provides slip for scalp massage.** Regular scalp massage has slightly better evidence for hair health than castor oil does — there are a small number of studies suggesting it increases hair thickness over time, possibly through improved blood flow to the follicle. Castor oil's viscosity makes it stay where you apply it during a massage rather than running off. The oil is the vehicle; the massage is the thing with some evidence.

**Seals moisture into the hairline hair.** Edge hair is often finer and more fragile than the rest of the hair. Castor oil as a sealant after a leave-in or light moisturiser helps prevent the edge area from drying out faster than the rest of the hair.

**Provides some slip for styling edges.** Mixed with a lighter oil or a water-based product, castor oil can help edges lay flat without the build-up risk of a pure gel or wax product.

## What castor oil won't do

It won't regrow edges that have been lost to traction alopecia. Traction alopecia — hair loss at the hairline from repeated tension (tight braids, extensions, ponytails, cornrows pulled too tight) — damages the follicle over time. If the follicle is still viable, removing the source of traction gives it the best chance of recovering on its own. Castor oil applied to the area doesn't accelerate that recovery in any way that's been demonstrated clinically.

If your edges have been thinning for more than a year despite reducing tension, see a trichologist or GP. At a certain point, follicle recovery becomes unlikely without medical intervention, and continuing to massage castor oil into the area is delaying a more useful conversation.

## How to use it

Apply a small amount to your fingertips and massage into the hairline and scalp around the edges for two to three minutes. Do this on wash days or between washes — not a daily habit that requires heavy daily removal. A little goes a long way; castor oil is thick and excessive amounts are hard to wash out.

Jamaican black castor oil (the most marketed variety) has no demonstrated advantage over regular castor oil for this purpose. Both are worth using only as much as you actually need.

## The honest position

Castor oil edges: real benefits as a massage medium and sealant. Not a proven regrowth treatment. If your edges are thinning, the priority is removing traction and giving the area rest — and seeing a professional if rest alone doesn't help within six months.
