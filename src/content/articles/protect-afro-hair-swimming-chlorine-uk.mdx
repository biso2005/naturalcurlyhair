Chlorine strips moisture from all hair types, but its effect on Afro-textured hair is faster and more severe because the coil structure has more surface area, more exposed cuticle points, and less natural oil distribution than straight hair. After a school swimming session with no preparation, type 4 hair can come out brittle, tangled, and difficult to manage.

The fix is straightforward — three steps, all done before and immediately after getting in the pool.

---

## The Protocol

### Before swimming

**Step 1: Wet the hair thoroughly with clean water and apply conditioner.**

Wet, conditioned hair absorbs significantly less chlorinated pool water than dry hair. The hair is already saturated — the pool water has less room to penetrate, and the conditioner creates a surface coating that slows chlorine absorption.

Use a generous amount of conditioner — more than you would for a normal wash. A thick, creamy conditioner works better than a lightweight one for this purpose.

**Step 2: Put the hair in cornrows, flat twists, or two-strand twists.**

Loose Afro-textured hair in a pool tangles significantly from the water movement, then mats further as it dries. Contained in a style, the individual strands can't move freely against each other.

Cornrows or flat twists are the most durable option — they hold through vigorous swimming and stay tidy under a swim cap. Two-strand twists also work but may partially unravel in the pool.

**Step 3: Swim cap over the top.**

A swim cap reduces (it does not eliminate) chlorine exposure. It also keeps the style neater under water.

UK swimming pools typically allow swim caps. Some types of swim cap work better for high-volume natural hair:

- **Silicone caps with extra depth** — standard swim caps are designed for flat, straight hair and don't accommodate a full puff or high braids. Deep silicone caps or wrapping caps designed for natural hair fit better and provide better coverage.
- **Lycra/fabric swim caps** — provide less water resistance than silicone but fit more comfortably over cornrows and braids.

---

### Immediately after swimming

**Rinse the hair with clean water within five minutes of getting out of the pool.**

The longer chlorinated water stays in the hair, the more damage it does. A thorough rinse at the pool shower immediately after swimming is significantly more effective than rinsing at home an hour later.

**Apply a leave-in conditioner before leaving if possible.**

If there's time before the child needs to be dressed and out of the door, a leave-in conditioner applied to the rinsed, damp hair and distributed through the style replaces some of the moisture lost during the swim.

**Deep condition at home on wash day.**

A swim session counts as a stressor. The next wash day should include a deep condition — fifteen to twenty minutes with a moisturising deep conditioner — to restore moisture lost through chlorine exposure.

## School Swimming Specifically

School swimming is usually a set day each week for a half-term. For most UK school swimming programmes, this means:

- A morning install before school (Tuesday cornrows, say)
- Swimming in the afternoon
- The style coming out over the next few days as the week progresses

**The most practical approach for weekly school swimming:**

Install cornrows or flat twists at the start of the school week. Pre-soak with conditioner before the swimming lesson. Rinse immediately after the lesson. The style stays in for the week. At the weekend, wash and reinstall.

This approach keeps intervention during the school week minimal — the style contains the hair, the pre-soak and post-rinse are the protection, and the damage accumulation over a half-term is manageable.

## If the School Doesn't Accommodate Natural Hair Needs

Some schools push back on swim cap styles, complain about conditioner in the pool, or have changing facilities that don't allow time for post-swim rinsing.

**Conditioner in the pool:** rinsed conditioner has negligible impact on pool water chemistry. This is not a legitimate concern. If raised, it can be addressed factually.

**Swim cap styles that don't fit the uniform:** the school cannot require a swim cap style that is only compatible with straight hair. This falls under the Equality Act 2010 — a requirement that disproportionately disadvantages Black pupils because of their hair type is indirect race discrimination.

**No time to rinse:** a five-minute rinse station access after the session is a reasonable adjustment under the Equality Act. Raise it with the teacher in charge of swimming if it's not available.

## FAQs

**My child's hair comes out from swimming as a tangled mass every time. Why?**

Most likely: hair was dry when they got in (no pre-soak), or was loose rather than in a contained style, or the post-swim rinse didn't happen. All three changes together — wet + conditioned + cornrowed before, rinsed immediately after — produce a significantly better result.

**Can my child wear a bonnet or satin cap instead of a swim cap?**

No — a bonnet or satin cap provides no protection in water and won't stay on. Silicone or deep silicone swim cap is needed. Some families put a satin cap or satin scrunchie under the swim cap to reduce friction on the style, but the outer layer must be a proper swim cap.

**How often does the chlorine damage become noticeable?**

With no protection: immediately, and cumulative damage over a half-term becomes significant. With the pre-soak and post-rinse protocol: most families find it manageable across a half-term, with a normal wash and deep condition routine at weekends.

**My child refuses to wear a swim cap because it looks different from their classmates' caps. What do I do?**

Deep silicone caps or wide swim caps specifically designed for natural hair are available in the same colour ranges as standard caps and look less conspicuous than some of the novelty natural hair caps. Normalise it at home — the cap is functional, not unusual. If other Black children in the class are in the same position, raising it collectively with the school carries more weight.
