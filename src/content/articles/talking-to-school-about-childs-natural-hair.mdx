Most conversations with schools about natural hair happen reactively — your child comes home having been told their style is wrong, or you get a letter, or you hear via another parent. This guide is for the night before that conversation: what to say, how to frame it, and what to do if the first conversation doesn't go well.

---

## Before the Conversation

**Know what you're dealing with.** Is the school's concern:
- A named style (cornrows, braids, locs)?
- Hair "size" or "volume" (afro too big)?
- A general "tidiness" standard being applied unevenly?
- Accessories (beads, satin scrunchie)?
- Something else entirely?

The answer shapes the conversation. A school that's worried about "tidiness" needs different language than one that has named braids specifically.

**Know what you want from the conversation:**
- Confirmation that the style is permitted with no further issues
- A change to the written policy
- An apology and assurance it won't happen again
- Clarity on what the school's actual policy says

Being clear on your goal before the meeting means you can tell when you've reached it — or when you haven't.

---

## What to Say

**Opening the conversation:**

> "I wanted to talk to you about something that happened with [child's name] this week. They were told [describe the incident]. I want to understand what the school's position is and to make sure this doesn't happen again."

This is a factual opening. It describes what happened, expresses that you want clarity, and doesn't start with an accusation, which makes it easier for the teacher or head to hear you.

**If the school says the style is against policy:**

> "I'd like to see the written policy, please. My understanding is that under the Equality Act 2010, schools can't restrict natural Afro-textured hair or associated protective styles — cornrows, braids, twists, and locs — because those are directly linked to race. Can you point me to the specific part of your policy and how you believe it applies here?"

You don't need to be combative to use the law. Asking them to show you their written justification is reasonable.

**If the school says they're just trying to be consistent:**

> "I understand the intention is consistency, but consistency can still produce discriminatory outcomes if the standard doesn't work the same way for all hair types. My child's braids are what 'tidy' looks like on their hair. A 'tidy' standard that their hair can't meet is the issue."

**If the school is immediately apologetic and says it was a misunderstanding:**

Take that outcome. Write a brief follow-up email that same day summarising what was agreed — "I'm glad we could sort this out. I just wanted to confirm in writing that [child's name] can continue wearing their [style] without any issues." Having it in writing matters if the situation recurs.

## If the Conversation Doesn't Go Well

Some schools get defensive. Some teachers genuinely believe their policy is lawful. Some headteachers double down. If the conversation doesn't produce a resolution:

**Don't escalate in the room.** End the meeting politely and follow up in writing.

> "Thank you for talking with me today. I'm not satisfied that we've resolved this, and I'd like to put my concerns in writing to the headteacher."

## Conversations You Might Not Expect

**With the class teacher, not the head:** sometimes the issue starts at classroom level — a teacher comment, a note home, a child being spoken to. In this case, the class teacher is the right first conversation, but you're also checking whether this is that teacher's personal view or school policy. Ask explicitly: "Is this a school policy or the teacher's personal decision?"

**After your child has already been told to change their style:** this is the most common and most difficult situation. Your child may be upset or embarrassed. Acknowledge that first before logistics. "That shouldn't have happened and I'm going to sort it out" is the right opening with the child before the school conversation.

**When another child has commented on your child's hair:** this is different from a school policy issue — it's a pastoral matter. Talk to the teacher or class teacher about it as a pastoral concern. Schools have obligations around race-based bullying or comments under the same equality framework.

**When the PE teacher is the one raising it:** PE teachers sometimes create their own rules around hair styles for sports. "Hair must be flat for gymnastics" is sometimes a safety requirement (reasonable), sometimes aesthetic preference (not reasonable). Ask specifically what the safety justification is and whether a tight cornrow or a low bun would address it.

---

## A Note on Choosing Your Battles

Not every conversation needs to end in a formal letter. Some situations are a teacher who didn't know and won't repeat it, a misunderstanding about the policy, or a new school staff member who hadn't seen the written policy. A calm conversation that educates without antagonising can resolve many of these.

The formal route — the letter, the citations, the escalation — is for situations where the school is actively defending a discriminatory position. Save that for when you need it. Start with the conversation.

## FAQs

**The school says a parent complaint was made about my child's hair accessories being distracting. Do I have to change the accessories?**

No. Accessories associated with natural Afro hair styles (beads, satin scrunchies) are protected under the same equality framework as the styles themselves. A parent complaint is not a policy basis. Ask the school to show you the policy and explain how accommodating parent complaints about racial characteristics is consistent with the Equality Act.

**My child doesn't want to talk about their hair at school and doesn't want me to raise it with the teacher. What do I do?**

Respect their position, but keep a record of what happened in case they change their mind or the situation recurs. Some children — particularly secondary school age — find having parents intervene more difficult than managing the situation themselves. Make sure they know their rights, give them the words if they want them, and let them lead.

**I've been talking to the school about this for months and nothing has changed. What now?**

Escalate in writing. → [Afro hair discrimination at school UK: rights and how to respond](/parents/afro-hair-discrimination-school-uk-rights/)

A formal letter citing the Equality Act 2010 and requesting a response within ten school days changes the nature of the conversation. Months of verbal conversation without resolution is exactly the situation that warrants it.

**The school has been fine for years and something changed this year. What usually causes this?**

A new member of staff applying their own interpretation of policy, a new headteacher reviewing uniform policies, or a policy update. Ask specifically whether the policy has changed. If it has, ask to see the previous and current policy and when the change was made.
