## Do I have hard water?

**How do I know if I'm in a hard water area?**
[Use the water hardness checker](/tools/water-hardness/) — enter your postcode and get district-level hardness data. The hardest areas in the UK: London and the South East (Thames Water supply), the East Midlands (Severn Trent), Cambridge and surrounding areas, Luton, and Brighton. Softest: Scotland (Scottish Water, primarily Loch-sourced), Wales (Welsh Water), Manchester and Liverpool (United Utilities, Pennine-sourced), most of the North West.

**My hair was fine until I moved to London. Is hard water really responsible?**
Almost certainly yes, for some of the change. London water is among the hardest in the UK at 290–320mg/L in most areas. Moving from a soft or moderately soft area — the North West, Scotland, Wales — to London is one of the most common triggers for natural hair routines stopping working.

**I've always lived in London and my hair was fine before. Why is it suddenly a problem?**
Hard water effects are cumulative. Mineral build-up accumulates over time, and a routine that coped with it initially stops coping as the layer thickens. A product change, hormonal change, or shift in wash frequency can push the balance over the edge.

**Can I test my water hardness at home?**
Test strips are available online (search "water hardness test strips UK") and give a rough reading. The checker on this site uses supplier-published data which is more accurate for most postcodes.

## What hard water does to natural hair

**What exactly does hard water do to the hair strand?**
Calcium and magnesium ions in hard water bond to the hair shaft during washing and rinsing. They form a mineral layer on the outside of the cuticle that prevents moisture from getting in and makes the hair surface rough. This roughness increases friction between strands, worsens tangling, and makes the hair feel coated regardless of how much conditioner you use.

**Can hard water cause hair loss?**
Hard water doesn't damage follicles or cause true hair loss. It causes breakage — which can look like hair loss when it happens close to the scalp. If you're finding short, snapped pieces of hair without a root bulb, that's breakage from mineral-induced brittleness. → [Full article: can hard water cause hair loss?](/hair-care/can-hard-water-cause-hair-loss-uk/)

**Why do my products seem to have stopped working?**
The mineral layer on the hair shaft blocks absorption. Conditioner applied on top of a mineral coating doesn't penetrate properly. The products haven't changed — the surface they're applied to has. Removing the mineral layer with a chelating shampoo usually restores product performance within one or two washes.

**Does hard water affect my scalp too?**
Yes. Mineral deposits accumulate on the scalp as well, potentially contributing to itching and flaking. This is separate from dandruff (which has a fungal cause) but can look similar. Chelating shampoo applied to the scalp, or a shower filter, helps.

## Fixing it

**What's a chelating shampoo and how is it different from a clarifying shampoo?**
A chelating shampoo contains ingredients (EDTA, phytic acid, citric acid) that chemically bond to mineral deposits and allow them to be rinsed away. A clarifying shampoo removes product build-up (waxes, silicones, heavy products) but doesn't remove mineral deposits effectively. In hard water areas, you need both in rotation — chelating monthly, clarifying as needed for product build-up.

**Which chelating shampoos are available in the UK?**
Malibu C Hard Water Wellness Shampoo (available online, Boots.com). Joico Chelating Shampoo. Kenra Platinum Silkening Mist (also chelating). For a budget option, Neutrogena Anti-Residue Shampoo is widely available at Boots and has chelating properties, though it's mild — fine for moderate hardness, less effective in very hard areas.

**How often should I use a chelating shampoo?**
Once a month in most hard water areas. In very hard water areas (London, Luton, Hull) some people chelate every three weeks. Don't go more frequent than every two to three weeks — chelating is stripping and should always be followed by deep conditioning.

**Should I deep condition after chelating?**
Always. Chelating removes the mineral layer but also strips some natural oils and moisture. Deep conditioning immediately after is non-negotiable. This is one wash day where skipping the deep conditioner will leave your hair feeling worse, not better.

**Do shower filters work?**
Yes, for reducing mineral content at source. A filter fitted to your shower head reduces the calcium and magnesium in the water before it touches your hair, meaning less accumulates between chelating sessions. They don't eliminate the problem entirely — you'll still benefit from monthly chelating — but they reduce how quickly the build-up returns. Filters need to be replaced roughly every three to six months depending on your usage and water hardness.

**Can I use filtered or bottled water for a final rinse?**
Yes, and it makes a real difference. A final rinse with soft or filtered water after conditioning closes the cuticle without redepositing minerals. Practical for some routines, not for others. If you have a filter jug, filling it before wash day and using the filtered water for a final rinse is a low-cost option.

## Long-term management

**Will hard water damage my hair permanently?**
No. The mineral build-up is a surface coating, not a structural change to the hair strand. It's reversible with chelating. Hair that's been breaking due to mineral-induced brittleness will stop breaking once the mineral layer is regularly cleared.

**I started chelating monthly. How long until I see results?**
For most people, the first chelating wash shows a noticeable change — products absorb better, hair feels less coated. Full improvement (curl definition returning, product performance stabilising) usually takes two to three washes. If you're not seeing improvement after three monthly chelating sessions, the issue may not be primarily hard water, or there's a technique issue worth reviewing.
