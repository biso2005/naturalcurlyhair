Glycerin is a humectant — it attracts and holds water molecules. In humid conditions, that means it draws moisture from the air into your hair. In dry conditions, it draws moisture from your hair into the air. In UK winter, with central heating running and outdoor humidity low, glycerin-heavy products can leave natural hair drier than before you applied them.

This is the glycerin split: the same product that works brilliantly in July can cause frizz, dryness, and definition collapse in January.

---

## How Glycerin Works

Humectants work by attracting water to where they are. When glycerin is in your hair product, it tries to equalise the moisture level between your hair and the surrounding environment.

**When outdoor relative humidity is above 70%** (UK summer, coastal areas, rainy days): glycerin pulls moisture from the air into the hair. This is the good scenario — the hair gets more moisture, the curl definition holds, frizz is reduced.

**When outdoor relative humidity is below 40%** (UK winter with central heating, cold dry days): glycerin can't find moisture in the air. It draws from the next available source — the water inside your hair shaft. The result: the product that was supposed to moisturise your hair is actively removing moisture from it.

UK central heating drops indoor humidity to 30–40% from October through March. The same product used in the same way in summer and winter can produce opposite results.

---

## The UK Seasonal Pattern

**Summer (May–September):** UK outdoor humidity averages 65–80%. Glycerin-heavy products work well. High-glycerin leave-ins, creams, and gels perform as intended. Wash-and-gos hold definition through the day.

**Autumn transition (October–November):** central heating comes on. Indoor humidity drops fast. Products that worked in September may start producing frizz or dryness by October.

**Winter (December–March):** central heating running daily, indoor humidity 30–40%. Low outdoor humidity on cold days. Glycerin-heavy products are most likely to underperform or actively worsen the hair in this period.

**Spring (April–May):** heating goes off, humidity rises. Glycerin-heavy products work again.

## What to Do

**Check your product ingredient lists.** Glycerin (glycerol) appears near the top of the ingredient list in many popular natural hair products — SheaMoisture, Camille Rose, and many others have glycerin in positions 2–5 in their formulations. High-glycerin products are the ones most likely to cause winter problems.

**Keep high-glycerin products for summer.** Don't discard them — they earn their place for six months of the year.

**Use low-glycerin or glycerin-free alternatives in winter.** Cantu Shea Butter products are generally lower glycerin than SheaMoisture equivalents. Mielle Organics Pomegranate & Honey Curl Smoothie is moderate glycerin. Ecoslay Orange Marmalade is glycerin-free.

**Seal with oil in winter.** If you're using a glycerin-containing leave-in, sealing immediately over the top with an oil (jojoba, argan, grapeseed) before the hair is exposed to dry air creates a barrier that slows the glycerin from pulling moisture out.

**Indoor humidity management.** A humidifier in the bedroom running overnight raises indoor humidity toward 50–60%. At this level, glycerin behaves normally — drawing from the air rather than from the hair. This is the most effective fix if you don't want to rotate your product line seasonally.

## Which Products Are High Glycerin

Products where glycerin appears as ingredient 2, 3, or 4 — right after water in the list. This is common in:

- SheaMoisture styling products (the Manuka Honey range, the Coconut & Hibiscus range)
- Camille Rose styling products
- Many curl milks and creams from US natural hair brands sold in the UK

Products that are relatively lower glycerin (glycerin further down the list or absent):
- Cantu Shea Butter range
- Mielle Organics (varies by product)
- Ecoslay (formulated glycerin-free)
- Umberto Giannini (UK brand, check individual products)

## FAQs

**My hair was fine in summer and started frizzing and going dry in October. I didn't change anything. What happened?**

Your product routine crossed the seasonal threshold — when the central heating came on, the indoor humidity dropped below the point where glycerin performs well. You may not need to change products permanently, just adjust for the season or add a humidifier.

**Is glycerin bad for natural hair?**

No. It's one of the most effective moisturising ingredients in hair products. The problem is environment-specific, not a problem with glycerin itself.

**How do I know if my current products are causing winter dryness from glycerin?**

Check the ingredient list of everything in your routine. If glycerin appears in positions 2–5 on two or more products, and the dryness started in October or November, glycerin is a likely cause. Try replacing one product with a lower-glycerin option and compare results over two weeks.

**Are there glycerin-free natural hair products available in the UK?**

Yes. Ecoslay products are formulated glycerin-free and are available direct from their UK site. Some Mielle Organics products and most oil-based sealants (pure jojoba, argan, grapeseed) are glycerin-free by nature. The mainstream supermarket brands (Cantu, Garnier) are generally lower glycerin than the SheaMoisture and Camille Rose ranges.
