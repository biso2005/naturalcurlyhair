Castor oil will not make your hair grow faster. There is no robust clinical evidence that it does. It is a good, thick sealant oil that works well for sealing moisture in coily hair and for scalp massage — but those benefits are not the same as stimulating hair growth, and conflating them is what most castor oil content does.

That's the honest answer, and it's worth saying clearly before the rest of this article, because castor oil is one of the most heavily marketed products in the natural hair space and the growth claims are not supported.

---

## What the Evidence Actually Shows

**Ricinoleic acid:** castor oil is high in ricinoleic acid, a fatty acid. Some early laboratory studies have shown ricinoleic acid may have anti-inflammatory properties at the scalp level. Scalp inflammation can contribute to hair loss — so the theoretical mechanism exists. But theoretical mechanism is not clinical proof of effect in humans.

**Clinical trials:** there are no large-scale, well-designed clinical trials demonstrating that topical castor oil application increases hair growth rate, hair density, or hair thickness in humans. The studies that exist are small, poorly controlled, and industry-adjacent.

**HBOT-castor oil claims:** one small study on hair loss from chemotherapy is sometimes cited. It measured hair regrowth rate and found castor oil did not improve outcomes. This is frequently not mentioned in the content that cites it.

**The honest summary:** castor oil has not been proven to grow hair. The evidence base is too weak to make the claim.

---

## What Castor Oil Actually Does Well

**Sealant.** Castor oil is one of the thickest natural oils available and creates a strong surface coat on the hair shaft. As a final sealant in the LOC or LCO routine — applied over leave-in and cream — it slows moisture evaporation effectively. It earns its place in a natural hair routine as a sealant, not as a growth treatment.

**Scalp massage.** Castor oil is thick enough to stay where you put it during a scalp massage, which makes it a practical vehicle for the massage itself. Scalp massage has better (though still limited) evidence for hair growth than the oil does — there are two small studies suggesting regular scalp massage increases hair thickness over time, likely through increased blood flow to the follicle. The castor oil is the massage medium; the scalp massage is the thing with evidence.

**Shine and surface feel.** As a finishing oil on the hair surface, castor oil adds significant shine and smooths the cuticle visually. This is an aesthetic benefit, not a health benefit, but it's real.

## Jamaican Black Castor Oil vs Regular Castor Oil

Jamaican black castor oil (JBCO) is castor oil made from roasted castor beans. The roasting process creates a darker colour and a different smell. The ricinoleic acid content is similar. There is no evidence that JBCO outperforms regular castor oil for any purpose — including the growth claims applied to both. The higher price is brand positioning, not formulation advantage.

---

## If You Want to Use Castor Oil

Use it as a sealant at the end of your routine, or as a scalp massage oil. If you're doing regular scalp massage — which has slightly better evidence — castor oil is a reasonable choice for its viscosity. Expect sealing and shine benefits. Don't expect measurable hair growth from the oil itself.

UK sources: Jamaican Pride JBCO is widely available at Boots, Superdrug, and BSS. Regular food-grade castor oil is available at Holland & Barrett and most health food shops. For sealant purposes, they perform the same.

## FAQs

**But I used castor oil and my hair grew. Isn't that proof?**

Hair grows constantly regardless of what you put on it — the average is consistent whether you use castor oil or not. Without a controlled comparison (same person, same conditions, one side castor oil, one side nothing) it's not possible to attribute the growth to the oil. Most people who start using castor oil also start paying more attention to their hair overall — better moisture, less manipulation — and the growth improvement comes from those changes.

**What about the edges? Everyone says castor oil grows edges back.**

The "castor oil grows edges" claim has the same evidence problem as the general growth claim. What does work for thinning edges is removing the source of traction and resting the area — the castor oil scalp massage may be helping with blood flow, but it's not doing the structural work. → [Thinning edges on natural hair UK](/hair-care/thinning-edges-natural-hair-uk/)

**Is there anything with actual evidence for hair growth?**

Minoxidil (topical, available over the counter in the UK) has the strongest evidence base for stimulating hair growth and is effective for androgenetic hair loss. Finasteride (oral, prescription) has strong evidence for male-pattern hair loss. Neither is a natural hair product. For general hair health in natural hair, the bigger evidence base is for reducing breakage through moisture and reduced manipulation — which produces visible length retention without growing hair faster.

**The oil I bought says clinically proven on the label. Is that true?**

"Clinically proven" on a cosmetic product label is a marketing claim, not a regulated designation in the UK. The product may have been tested in a company-funded study with a small sample and a specific, narrow outcome measure. It does not mean the same thing as an independent peer-reviewed clinical trial.
