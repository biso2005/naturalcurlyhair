The way parents talk about a child's hair — casually, during routines, in response to comments from other people — shapes how the child feels about their own hair for years. This matters more for children with natural curly, coily, or Afro-textured hair because the world around them will offer plenty of signals that straight hair is the default and their hair is the exception.

You don't need scripted responses or a framework. You need a few consistent habits.

---

## What Not to Say (Even With Good Intentions)

**"Your hair is so hard to manage."** Even when it's true in the moment, this frames the child's hair as a problem to endure rather than something worth caring for. Children hear this and internalise it.

**"We need to tame your hair."** Same problem — the word "tame" implies the hair is wild or wrong in its natural state.

**"I wish your hair was easier."** Again: their hair, framed as a burden. Even if genuinely felt, this is worth keeping inside.

**Comparisons to siblings or relatives with different textures:** "Your cousin's hair is so much easier." This one is obvious when written out, but it happens.

**"You look so pretty with your hair straight."** Fine as a standalone observation. Problematic if it's the only time appearance compliments are offered, because the child learns that straight = pretty.

---

## What to Say Instead

**Describe the hair accurately and positively:** "Your curls are really defined today." "Look how big your hair is getting." "Your coils are so bouncy."

**Make care conversations neutral:** "Time to moisturise your hair" rather than "time to deal with your hair." The phrasing carries information.

**When wash day is hard, acknowledge it without editorialising:** "I know this isn't your favourite. We're nearly at the conditioner part." Not: "I know, it's such a pain."

**When your child asks why their hair is different from their friends':** "Because you have curly [or coily] hair. Not everyone does. It needs different care, and that's what we've learned to do." Matter-of-fact, not defensive.

---

## Responding to Comments From Other People

Children notice how their parents respond to comments about their hair. If a stranger at the supermarket reaches out to touch it and you say "Oh, go ahead!" — your child learns their hair is available for strangers to handle. If you say "We don't touch people's hair without asking" and move on — your child learns their body has boundaries.

Some common situations:

**"Can I touch your hair?" (from a stranger or acquaintance):** Your child can answer this themselves when they're old enough. Before that, you can say "We'd rather not, thanks" without making it a scene.

**"What kind of hair does she have?":** You can answer simply and without apology. "Afro-textured" or "coily" or "naturally curly." There's no explanation required.

**"I love your hair — it's so exotic.":** This one is harder. A brief "Thank you" and moving on is usually enough for a stranger. For a relative or friend you'll see again, "It's just her hair" or "She'd prefer not to be described as exotic, thanks" covers it.

**Another child making a comment at school:** this is worth discussing with your child directly and separately — what they can say in response, that it's okay to tell a teacher, that the comment reflects the other child's limited experience rather than something wrong with theirs.

## Books and Media That Help

Representation matters for how children see their own hair. UK-specific or UK-relevant recommendations:

- **"Sulwe" by Lupita Nyong'o** — for younger children, about skin tone and self-image; the themes extend to hair
- **"Hair Love" by Matthew A. Cherry** — about a father learning to care for his daughter's natural hair; warmly illustrated, widely loved
- **"I Love My Hair!" by Natasha Anastasia Tarpley** — a young Black girl's relationship with her natural hair; simple, affirming
- **"My Hair" by Cozbi A. Cabrera** — celebrates natural hair textures and styles

Media featuring Black and mixed race characters with natural hair in everyday, non-exceptional contexts is also useful — the message that natural hair is just hair, not special or problematic, comes from seeing it represented normally.

## When Your Child Says They Don't Like Their Hair

This happens, sometimes from a fairly young age, especially if they're comparing themselves to peers or media figures with straight hair.

Don't rush to reassure. Ask first: "What don't you like about it?" Sometimes it's something specific and practical — it takes too long, it hurts to comb, they can't wear certain styles. These are solvable. Address the practical thing.

If it's more about comparison — "I wish I had [friend]'s straight hair" — the conversation is different. "I understand you notice your hair is different. Your hair is part of who you are, and there are things it does that other hair types can't. What's something you like about yours?" You're not dismissing the feeling; you're redirecting toward their own experience rather than comparison.

If feelings about the hair are connected to broader identity questions, that's a longer, ongoing conversation — and it usually is. The consistency matters more than any single response.

## FAQs

**My child is mixed race and is asking questions about why they look different from me. How do I handle the hair conversation alongside identity questions?**

Honestly and without anxiety. "You have a mix of hair types from your family" is a true and sufficient starting point for young children. Identity questions expand over time; you don't need to answer everything at once. Being comfortable with the question yourself makes a larger difference than the specific words you use.

**My child wants to straighten their hair to look like their friends. What do I say?**

Acknowledge the feeling: they want to look like their friends, which is normal. Then explain what heat does — it's a temporary change that affects the hair's condition over time. Older children can make an informed choice about occasional heat styling; that's different from being told it's wrong. Focus on what's actually at stake (hair health, not identity politics) and let the child's age and maturity guide how much agency they have.

**A teacher made a negative comment about my child's hair at school. What do I do?**

Raise it with the school. A teacher making negative comments about a child's natural hair texture or style is not acceptable and, depending on the comment, may constitute discriminatory behaviour under the Equality Act 2010. Document the comment and what your child reported about the context before raising it.
