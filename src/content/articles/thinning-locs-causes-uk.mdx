Thinning locs — where a loc becomes noticeably thinner at one point along its length, or where locs at the hairline are becoming sparse — have specific causes. Most of them are addressable if caught early. Left unaddressed, thinning leads to loc breakage.

---

## Four Causes of Thinning Locs

### 1. Traction from over-retwisting

The most common cause. Retwisting too tightly or too frequently puts sustained tension on the hair at the root. Over months, this tension pulls the hair follicle, causing the hair to thin at the root.

**What it looks like:** thinning specifically at the root of the loc where it meets the scalp. Often most visible at the hairline and temple area where the hair is finer and more vulnerable. May accompany small bumps or folliculitis at the follicle.

**What to do:** stop all retwisting. Give the affected locs a full rest — no manipulation, no product at the root — for six to eight weeks. If there are visible bumps or inflammation, see a GP or trichologist. The thinning often stabilises and some recovery is possible if the traction is removed early.

**What not to do:** try to fix it by retwisting more carefully or with a different product. The follicle needs rest, not a different kind of tension.

### 2. Extension weight

Locs with added extensions or loc extensions installed are significantly heavier than natural locs. Over time, the weight pulls on the root. The thinner the natural loc relative to the extension weight, the faster this causes root thinning.

**What it looks like:** thinning at the root, similar to traction. More diffuse across the whole head rather than concentrated at the hairline. The natural loc below the root may look fine; the root itself is thin.

**What to do:** remove the extensions. Allow the root to recover without weight or tension. Assess whether extensions are viable for your natural loc thickness and weight tolerance.

### 3. Weak loc sections (budding stage)

During the budding stage (months two to six), locs develop a thinner section somewhere along the length — sometimes called a "budding point" — where the interior isn't fully knotted yet. If stress is applied at this point (brushing, pulling through accessories, sleeping without protection), the loc can thin or break at the weak point.

**What it looks like:** a thin section mid-length, usually in early to mid-stage locs. The base and ends may look normal; a narrow section in between is visibly thinner.

**What to do:** handle that loc gently. Don't put accessories through it at the weak point. Let it pass through the budding stage — the interior will knit and the weak point strengthens over time. If it's very thin, a small amount of loc wool or wrapping can provide temporary structural support until it locs through.

### 4. Scalp condition or systemic cause

Alopecia areata, androgenetic alopecia, seborrhoeic dermatitis causing repeated inflammation, nutrient deficiency, thyroid conditions, and stress (including post-partum hair loss) can all cause or accelerate hair thinning. In these cases, the thinning affects the follicle before it reaches the loc.

**What it looks like:** thinning across multiple locs, not concentrated at a specific mechanical stress point. Possibly circular bald patches (alopecia areata). Or gradual overall density reduction across the scalp. Scalp scaling or inflammation may be present.

**What to do:** see a GP or trichologist. This is not addressable through hair care changes alone. Rule out or address the underlying cause first.

## The Hairline as the First Warning Sign

Hairline locs — at the temples, edges, and nape — are always finer and more vulnerable. Thinning that starts here, particularly with traction or extension weight, is often the first visible sign that something needs to change. The hairline has less reserve than the crown; what the crown tolerates for a year may damage the hairline in months.

If hairline thinning is visible, act on it now. Traction alopecia at the hairline responds to early treatment (resting the area) and may not respond if treatment is delayed until significant hair loss has occurred.

---

## Loc Breakage vs Thinning

**Thinning:** the loc is still attached to the scalp but is narrower at one or more points. The root may still be healthy.

**Breakage:** a loc that has snapped at a thin point and is no longer attached. Usually the result of thinning that was not addressed in time.

A broken loc cannot be reattached. The options are wearing it shorter, loc wrapping to create the appearance of continuity, or allowing a new loc to form from the remaining root over time.

## FAQs

**My locs are thin in one spot in the middle but thick at the root and end. Is this thinning or normal budding?**

In locs under twelve months old, a thin mid-section is often a budding point — the loc knotting from root and end inward, with the middle not yet locked. It generally resolves as the loc matures. Handle it gently. If the loc is mature (past twelve months), a mid-shaft thin spot is unusual and worth monitoring — it may be a weak point from a period of stress or damage.

**Two of my locs have thinned to almost nothing at the root but look fine below. Can I save them?**

Possibly. Stop all retwisting on those locs immediately. If there's still hair at the root, the follicle may recover with rest. A trichologist can assess the follicle and advise whether the hair can regrow. If the follicle has permanently scarred from sustained traction, the hair won't return — but that's only determinable by assessment.

**Is there a product I can use for thinning locs?**

Not for the root thinning causes — products don't address traction damage, extension weight, or systemic health causes. For budding-stage mid-shaft weakness, a very light protein treatment on the exterior of the loc can temporarily add some resilience while the interior locs. Nothing inside the loc.

**My trichologist said I have traction alopecia. Can I keep my locs?**

Discuss with the trichologist. In some cases, modifying the routine — no extensions, longer retwist intervals, no tight styles — is enough to allow the follicles to recover while keeping the locs. In severe cases, removing the locs entirely to allow the scalp to rest may be recommended. This is a medical question and the trichologist's assessment is the right guide.
