## Getting started

**My child has curly hair and I don't know where to start. What's the most important thing?**
Moisture. Mixed race and Afro-textured children's hair needs significantly more moisture than straight hair. Start with a gentle sulphate-free shampoo, a rinse-out conditioner with slip for detangling, and a leave-in after washing. That's the complete minimum.

**How often should I wash my child's curly hair?**
Once a week is appropriate for most children with Afro-textured or mixed curly hair. More often than that with a stripping shampoo will dry the scalp. If the scalp looks or smells dirty between washes, a quick co-wash (conditioner only, no shampoo) is fine.

**My child cries during wash day. What helps?**
Work in small sections. Use a conditioner with a lot of slip. Detangle in sections while the conditioner is in, starting from the ends. A wide-tooth comb rather than a fine-tooth comb or brush. Some children find a spray bottle of water and conditioner to keep sections damp helpful. Playing a favourite show during wash day also helps significantly.

**Do I need to buy products specifically for mixed or curly children's hair?**
No. Products labelled for "mixed race" or "curly kids" are usually good but not unique. The requirements are: gentle cleansing (sulphate-free or mild sulphate), rinse-out conditioner with slip, and a leave-in or light moisturiser. Brands that work well in the UK: Mixed Chicks (Boots), Cantu Care for Kids (Boots and Superdrug), Shea Moisture Kids (various stockists).

**My child's hair is different textures on different parts of their head. Is that normal?**
Yes, very common in children with mixed or Afro-textured hair. The crown, nape, and temples often have different patterns. Style and care for each area with its own texture's needs in mind rather than applying one approach across all of it.

## Detangling and daily care

**How do I detangle curly children's hair without it being painful?**
Wet or damp, with conditioner in, starting from the tips and working upward in small sections. Never dry. Never from the roots downward. The right tool matters: a wide-tooth comb for initial detangling, fingers for separation. A leave-in conditioner or detangling spray before starting helps significantly.

**Should I comb my child's hair dry?**
For Afro-textured or very curly hair, combing dry will cause breakage and pain. Detangle only when wet and conditioned. For looser curl types (2C, 3A), some can comb through damp hair with a wide-tooth comb, but wet + conditioner is always easier.

**How do I keep my child's hair moisturised during the week?**
A light leave-in or moisturiser spray (water as the first ingredient) applied every two to three days to the ends and length, sealed with a light oil. Protective styles — braids, twists, cornrows — reduce daily manipulation and help moisture stay in longer.

**My child's edges are thin. What should I avoid?**
Tight hairstyles that pull at the hairline: tight braids that start at the hairline, high ponytails with elastic right at the hairline, braids with extensions that are too heavy for the amount of hair at the root. Children's hairlines are particularly vulnerable to traction damage. Keep styles loose at the hairline.

## Styles

**What are the easiest protective styles for children?**
Two-strand twists, cornrows, flat twists, and puffs (for shorter hair). For younger children who won't sit still long, two simple puffs or chunky braids are the most practical. Protective styles that last several days are useful for reducing daily manipulation.

**How long can a child keep braids in?**
Two to four weeks is typical. Longer than four weeks in very young children's hair risks build-up at the roots, matting as new growth comes in, and increased breakage when the style is removed. Moisturise the scalp underneath with a light oil every few days.

**Can I use heat on my child's hair?**
Occasional low heat is less damaging than frequent heat. A diffuser on low for drying, very occasionally. High-heat tools (flat irons, hot combs) used regularly will relax the curl pattern over time. For children who have never had heat on their hair, there's no particular reason to start unless there's a specific need.

## School and social

**My child's school has a policy against certain hairstyles. What can I do?**
Schools in the UK have a legal obligation not to discriminate against pupils on the basis of race. A blanket ban on Afro hair, natural styles, locs, twists, or protective styles indirectly discriminates against Black and mixed race children. Raise it formally with the headteacher in writing. Reference the Equality Act 2010. The Halo Code (a voluntary school hair dignity pledge) is useful evidence that the issue is taken seriously nationally. → [Full article on hair discrimination at school.](/hair-care/afro-hair-school-discrimination-uk/)

**Other children are commenting on my child's hair. How do I handle it?**
Prepare your child with language they can use: "My hair is natural — it curls like this because that's how it grows." Normalise their hair at home so they approach these interactions from a position of confidence rather than shame. Schools have a duty of care around racial harassment; repeated comments directed at a child are worth reporting.

**My child is asking to have their hair straightened. What should I consider?**
Children ask for what they see around them. Before using heat, consider whether there are styles that give some elongation without heat (twists, banding). If you do use low heat occasionally, use a heat protectant. A discussion at an age-appropriate level about why their hair is curly and what it means is worth having — not to make them feel different, but so they understand their own hair.

## Hard water (UK-specific)

**Can hard water affect my child's hair?**
Yes. If you're in London or the East Midlands and your child's hair feels perpetually coated despite washing, hard water mineral build-up may be a factor. A chelating shampoo once a month (look for versions safe for children, or adult chelating used sparingly) can help. → [Check your area.](/tools/water-hardness/)

**My child's scalp looks flaky. Is it dandruff?**
In children, scalp flaking is often dry scalp rather than dandruff in the adult sense. Insufficient scalp moisture (from over-washing or a dry environment) is the most common cause. Reduce washing frequency, apply a light oil to the scalp between washes, and see if it resolves in two to three weeks. If it persists or is accompanied by redness, see a GP or dermatologist.

**Are there UK community resources for parents learning to care for curly or Afro-textured children's hair?**
Yes. Facebook groups such as "Afro Hair Advice UK" and "Mixed Race Hair UK" have active communities of parents sharing experience. Local Meetup groups for natural hair in larger UK cities are also worth searching. Books: "Millie's Magic Hair" by Abena Jones (specifically for children); "My Beautiful Hair Book" by Katie Lyne for parents.
