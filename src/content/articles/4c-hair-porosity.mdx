Porosity is the single most useful thing to know about your hair. More than your curl type, more than your density — knowing whether your hair absorbs moisture easily or resists it will tell you which products to reach for and which to put back on the shelf.

For 4C hair specifically, there's a widespread assumption that tight coils always mean high porosity. They don't. Here's how to actually find out what yours is.

## What Porosity Actually Means

Porosity describes how easily your hair lets moisture in and how quickly it lets moisture back out. Low porosity hair resists both. High porosity hair is easy in and easy out — absorbs quickly, dries out quickly.

**Low porosity 4C hair:**
- Takes a long time to fully wet in the shower
- Products sit on top rather than absorbing
- Builds up easily — creams and butters pile on the surface
- Responds well to heat when conditioning (warmth helps moisture get in)
- Can feel weighed down even though the hair looks dry

**High porosity 4C hair:**
- Absorbs water and products quickly
- Dries out just as fast — moisture doesn't stay
- Frizzes in humid conditions
- Feels rough or coarse to the touch when dry
- Breaks more easily, especially at the ends

**Medium porosity 4C hair:**
- Absorbs and retains moisture reasonably well
- Products work consistently
- Less common in 4C hair, but it exists

## The Float Test Is Unreliable on 4C Hair

You've probably seen it: drop a clean strand in water, watch whether it sinks or floats, call it done.

Skip it for 4C hair. Tight coils trap air pockets inside the curl, which creates false floats even in genuinely high-porosity hair. Product residue changes the result further. The test was designed for straighter textures and doesn't translate well.

## Two Tests That Actually Work

### The spray bottle test

Spritz a small section of clean, dry hair with plain water and watch for 30 seconds.

- Water **beads on the surface** and takes a while to absorb — low porosity
- Water **absorbs quickly** and the hair darkens fast — high porosity
- Water **sinks in but slowly** — medium porosity

This is the most useful test for 4C hair because it shows how a whole curl cluster behaves rather than isolating a single strand. Do it after a clarifying wash for the most accurate read — product residue can make low-porosity hair behave like high-porosity hair.

### The feel test

Run a single clean, product-free strand between your fingers from tip to root.

- Feels **smooth, almost slippery** — cuticles are flat, likely low porosity
- Feels **rough or bumpy, snags slightly** — cuticles are raised, likely high porosity
- Feels **fragile or breaks mid-stroke** — high porosity with some damage alongside it

## Your Roots and Ends Are Probably Different

This is almost completely absent from natural hair content, but it's important: most 4C hair with any real length has mixed porosity.

Your roots are newer hair. They haven't been through years of washing, detangling, and sun exposure. Your ends are older — and older hair is almost always more porous.

This means your roots might resist moisture (low porosity behaviour) while your ends are thirsty (high porosity behaviour). If you use the same product and the same amount on both, you're under-moisturising your ends and potentially overloading your roots at the same time.

Do the spray bottle test on your roots separately from your ends. If the absorption speed is noticeably different, you have mixed porosity hair — and your routine needs to account for it.

## What to Do Based on Your Result

### If you have low porosity 4C hair

The challenge is getting moisture in. Once it's in, your hair holds it well.

- **Use heat when deep conditioning** — a heat cap, warm towel, or steamer helps your cuticle open up so the conditioner actually reaches the hair
- **Apply products to soaking-wet hair** — the wetter, the better; don't wait until it's just damp
- **Choose lighter products** — heavy butters and thick creams sit on top of a closed cuticle rather than absorbing; lighter oils and water-based leave-ins work better
- **Clarify regularly** — build-up is a bigger issue for low-porosity hair because products just pile up

### If you have high porosity 4C hair

The challenge is keeping moisture in once it's there.

- **Use heavier sealants** — castor oil, shea butter, and thick creams create a barrier that slows moisture leaving the hair
- **Apply while still damp** — sealing works best when there's moisture already in the hair to lock in
- **Consider occasional protein treatments** — protein can temporarily reduce how fast moisture escapes; useful for chemically processed or heat-damaged hair, but don't overdo it (too much protein makes 4C hair brittle)
- **Protect overnight** — high-porosity hair loses moisture to cotton pillowcases quickly; a satin bonnet or satin-lined pillowcase helps

### If you have mixed porosity hair

- Treat the roots and ends differently if you can — lighter products at the root, heavier at the ends
- Focus your deep conditioning time on the ends
- Use a heat cap to help moisture reach the roots while the ends get more product

## Common Mistakes

**Assuming high porosity without testing.** Many 4C naturals read that tight coils are inherently high porosity and build a whole routine on that assumption. If your hair is actually low porosity, you'll end up with build-up and products that never seem to absorb.

**Adding more product when moisture won't soak in.** If you have low-porosity hair, more product doesn't help — the cuticle is the barrier, not the quantity. More product just creates more build-up.

**Using one approach for your whole head.** If your hair is any real length, your porosity varies. One-size approach means part of your hair is always under-served.

**Not re-testing as your hair changes.** Chemical treatments, heat damage, and new growth all shift your porosity. If a routine that worked six months ago has stopped working, your porosity may have changed — test again rather than assuming the products are the problem.

## FAQs

**Is 4C hair always high porosity?**

No. 4C refers to curl pattern, not porosity. 4C hair can be low, medium, or high porosity. They're separate characteristics.

**Can porosity change?**

Yes. Bleach, heat, and significant mechanical damage shift hair toward higher porosity. New growth at the roots is almost always lower porosity than older lengths.

**My product worked last year and now it doesn't — is it porosity?**

Possibly. If you've done any chemical treatments, used a lot of heat, or your hair has grown significantly, your porosity profile may have shifted. Re-test and compare to last time.
