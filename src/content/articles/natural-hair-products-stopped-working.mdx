The product didn't change. Your hair did.

That's the reframe that matters here — because most people who reach this article have already spent money on replacements that didn't fix anything. The curl cream, the deep conditioner, the leave-in that used to work: they haven't changed. What has changed is your hair's porosity, your water supply, the season, or all three. Once you know which, the fix is usually straightforward.

There are four primary causes and one exception to rule out first. Here's how to diagnose which applies to you.

## The Four Primary Causes

### Build-up on the hair shaft

Product build-up is the most common culprit and the most straightforward to rule out. Every time you apply a styler, leave-in, or conditioner, a small amount of residue remains on the hair shaft — particularly if your cleanser isn't strong enough to remove it fully. Over time, this creates a coating that blocks subsequent products from penetrating or sitting correctly.

Signs this is your issue:

- Your hair feels waxy or heavy before you apply anything
- Products seem to sit on top of the hair rather than absorbing
- Wash day takes longer and longer to produce a result

The fix is a clarifying shampoo — but only once you've confirmed build-up is the cause, because clarifying too frequently strips moisture the hair needs.

### Your hair's porosity has changed

Porosity describes how readily your hair absorbs and retains moisture. It isn't fixed for life. Chemical treatments, heat damage, UV exposure, and the natural ageing of the hair fibre can all shift your hair from low porosity (resistant to moisture) to high porosity (absorbs quickly but loses moisture just as fast), or somewhere in between.

A product formulated for low-porosity hair — typically lighter, designed to penetrate a resistant cuticle — will behave very differently on high-porosity hair, which needs heavier, sealing ingredients to prevent moisture loss. Coils cause cuticle lifting at twist points, which increases the tendency toward high porosity in tightly coiled hair (NYSCC, 'An Overview on Hair Porosity'). If porosity has shifted since you first found products that worked, those products may now be entirely mismatched to what your hair actually needs.

### Hard water mineral build-up

This is the cause that most articles skip over, and it is particularly significant in the UK.

Hard water contains dissolved calcium and magnesium ions. When you wash your hair with hard water, those minerals deposit on the hair shaft and create a film that interferes with how conditioners and stylers adhere to the hair. London and the South East have water hardness at 250–350 mg/L; the Midlands and East Anglia are similar. Manchester is typically 60–80 mg/L; most of Wales and Scotland is under 80 mg/L (TapWater.uk; Drinking Water Inspectorate).

A standard clarifying shampoo will not fully address mineral build-up. Only a chelating shampoo — containing EDTA or phytic acid, which bind and lift mineral ions — can do this. This is a meaningful distinction that often gets lost. If you've recently moved, or if your local water authority has changed its supply blend, your water hardness may have changed without your awareness.

### Seasonal humidity and temperature shifts

This one catches people out because nothing technically changes — the same product, the same routine — yet results vary dramatically between January and July.

Glycerin absorbs approximately 25% of its weight at 50% relative humidity (Chemists Corner, Perry Romanowski). In high humidity, humectant-heavy products pull moisture from the air into the hair. In low humidity — UK indoor winter air in centrally heated homes runs at 30–45% relative humidity (Guinness Homes; Sussex Damp Experts) — the same humectants can pull moisture out of the hair shaft instead, causing dryness and frizz where the product previously gave definition.

If your products work in summer but fail in winter (or vice versa), seasonality is almost certainly the cause — not product failure. The fix is adjusting your product selection by season, not replacing products permanently.

## How to Diagnose Which Cause Applies to You

### Check your water hardness first

This is the fastest diagnosis and the most overlooked. Use your water supplier's postcode checker (Thames Water, Anglian Water, Severn Trent, and others all publish this data), or buy water hardness test strips — a few pounds, available widely online.

Anything above 200 mg/L means hard water is a plausible factor in your product performance issues. Run one chelating wash before making any other changes. If your results improve noticeably, water hardness was the problem — not your products.

### Assess your porosity

The float test is a rough accessible indicator. Take a clean, product-free shed hair strand and drop it into a glass of room-temperature water. After two to four minutes:

- **Floats near the top** — may indicate low porosity
- **Sinks slowly to the middle** — may indicate medium porosity
- **Sinks quickly to the bottom** — may indicate high porosity

This test has real limitations — hair that isn't fully clean will give a misleading result. Treat it as a starting point only.

A more reliable approach: compare how your hair behaves on products with different formulations. High-porosity hair typically benefits from heavier creams, oils, and protein treatments. Low-porosity hair responds better to lighter, heat-activated formulas. If your hair has moved toward high porosity, lighter products will seem to do nothing — which is usually when people assume the product has failed.

### Check the build-up first, porosity second

Before attributing a performance change to porosity shift, rule out product build-up with a single clarifying wash. If results improve immediately and then decline again after two to three washes, build-up is the cause. If results stay improved, the clarifying wash has given you a cleaner baseline — assess porosity from there.

### Rule out reformulation before replacing

The one Intent A exception: brands reformulate products without announcement. If a product you've used reliably for years suddenly performs differently — same packaging, same name, different texture or scent — compare the current INCI ingredient list against an archived version via the Wayback Machine (web.archive.org) or INCI Decoder. If the top five ingredients have changed, the formulation has changed.

This takes five minutes and can save you ruling in a hair or water cause that isn't actually there. Once you've confirmed the formula is the same, you can proceed with the diagnosis above.

## How to Fix It

### Hard water: chelating shampoo, not clarifying

If your water hardness postcode check returns above 200 mg/L, introduce a chelating shampoo once or twice a month as the foundation of your routine. Check the ingredient list for disodium EDTA, tetrasodium EDTA, or phytic acid — these are the active agents. A product labelled "clarifying" without one of these ingredients will not address mineral build-up.

Deep condition immediately after every chelating wash. Chelation removes mineral deposits but also strips some protective lipids; a moisture-focused deep conditioner used under heat for 20–30 minutes is the standard recovery step.

### Porosity shift: match the product to your current hair

If porosity assessment suggests your hair has moved toward high porosity, the fix is heavier products and protein support. Low-to-mid molecular weight proteins penetrate the cortex and help restore structure; high molecular weight proteins coat the surface only (Malinauskyte et al., 2021, *International Journal of Cosmetic Science*, 43:26–37). A leave-in with hydrolysed protein followed by a heavier cream or butter is a reasonable starting point.

If porosity has moved toward low, strip back to lighter formulas and introduce heat on wash day to help products penetrate.

### Seasonal shift: adjust by humidity band

In winter, reduce or remove high-glycerin products from your routine, or layer a light sealant (oil or butter) on top of humectant-containing products to prevent moisture being drawn out. In summer, that sealant layer can be reduced or dropped.

You don't need different products entirely — you need the same products applied with an awareness of what the air is doing.

### Build-up: clarify, then maintain

A clarifying wash resolves build-up. The maintenance question is how often to clarify without over-stripping. General guidance:

- Heavy styler users (butters, thick creams): clarify once or twice a month
- Lighter routine users (gels, light leave-ins): once every four to six weeks

Always follow with a deep conditioning treatment.

### When the hair itself has changed

Several things alter hair texture over time: accumulated length (older ends are more porous than roots), hormonal shifts (pregnancy, postpartum, perimenopause, thyroid changes, contraception changes), accumulated heat or chemical damage, and some medications. If none of the above causes explain your performance shift and the change has been gradual and progressive, a consultation with a registered trichologist is worth considering before replacing your entire routine. The [Institute of Trichologists](https://www.trichologists.org.uk/qualified-trichologists-united-kingdom/) (PSA-accredited) maintains a UK directory.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can hair genuinely become immune to a product?

Hair doesn't develop immunity in the biological sense. What happens is that your hair's condition, porosity, or environment changes — and a product that was once a match no longer is. The sensation of "immunity" is usually build-up, porosity shift, hard water accumulation, or a seasonal humidity effect.

### How do I know if I need a chelating shampoo rather than a regular clarifying one?

If you live in a hard water area and standard clarifying hasn't resolved your product performance issues, a chelating shampoo is the more targeted tool. Check the label for EDTA or phytic acid. If neither appears, the product will not lift mineral deposits regardless of how it's marketed.

### My products work in summer but not in winter — is that normal?

Yes. This is a well-documented effect of humidity on humectant-containing products. In cold, dry air, humectants draw moisture out of the hair shaft rather than into it. Switching to less humectant-heavy formulas in winter, or sealing with a light oil after styling, is the standard adjustment.

### Do I need to do a full routine reset?

Not necessarily. A chelating or clarifying wash, followed by a reassessment of your current porosity and water hardness, resolves the problem for most people without replacing any products. A full routine reset is warranted only after those steps have been taken and the issue persists.

### How often should I check ingredient lists on my regular products?

Once or twice a year is reasonable — particularly if a product's texture, scent, or performance feels different without any change in your routine. Photograph your INCI list each time you buy a new bottle; it takes seconds and gives you a reference point if performance shifts later.
