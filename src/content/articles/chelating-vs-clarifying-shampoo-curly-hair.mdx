If your curls feel dull, coated, or weirdly stiff even after washing — and a clarifying shampoo hasn't fixed it — you're probably using the wrong reset. Clarifying and chelating shampoos do different jobs. Here's which one your hair is actually asking for.

---

## The Short Version

**Clarifying shampoo** removes product build-up: gels, creams, silicones, dry shampoo residue. It's strong enough to strip the stuff that builds up from styling.

**Chelating shampoo** removes mineral deposits: the calcium and magnesium left behind by hard water. Clarifying can't touch those. Only chelating can.

If you're in London, the South East, or the Midlands, your water is likely hard enough that mineral deposits are building up every time you wash — and a clarifying shampoo is leaving that layer completely intact.

## How to Tell Which One You Need

### You need a clarifying wash when:

- Products have stopped performing the way they used to
- Your scalp feels itchy or congested despite regular washing
- You've been using a lot of heavy stylers, dry shampoo, or silicone-heavy products
- You're switching from a silicone routine to a silicone-free one
- Your hair feels sticky or waxy, not rough

### You need a chelating wash when:

- Your hair feels rough or straw-like even after deep conditioning
- Conditioner seems to sit on top rather than soaking in
- Clarifying hasn't resolved the stiffness or dullness
- You've recently moved to a harder water area
- Your colour is going brassy faster than expected

### Not sure? Do the chelating test

Do one wash with a chelating shampoo. Follow with your usual conditioner. If your hair feels noticeably softer and your curls look more defined than after a normal wash, mineral build-up was the problem. If nothing changes much, look at product build-up or porosity instead.

## How to Read the Label

You can't tell from the front of the bottle whether a shampoo chelates. You have to check the ingredient list on the back.

**For chelating:** look for EDTA, phytic acid, or sodium gluconate somewhere in the list. If none of those are there, it won't remove mineral deposits — whatever the marketing says.

**For clarifying:** stronger surfactants (sodium lauryl sulphate, sodium laureth sulphate) mean more stripping power. Sulphate-free clarifying shampoos exist and work for most people, but if your build-up is heavy, you may need sulphates occasionally.

A product can be labelled "clarifying" without any chelating ingredient. It will do its job on product build-up. It will do nothing for minerals.

---

## How Often to Use Each

Start with once a month for chelating if you're in a hard water area, and adjust based on how your hair responds. Hair that feels rough again within a week or two needs more frequent chelating. Hair that stays soft for four to six weeks can space it out.

Clarifying is more personal — it depends on how many products you use and how heavy they are. Most people find every two to four weeks works. After any extended protective style, always clarify before starting fresh.

We don't know the exact right frequency for your hair — no one does without knowing your water hardness and your routine. Start with monthly, notice what your hair tells you, and adjust.

---

## Can You Use Both on the Same Day?

Yes, and sometimes it makes sense. If you're doing a full reset:

1. **Chelating shampoo first** — works closest to the hair shaft, on the mineral layer
2. **Clarifying shampoo second** — removes what's sitting on top
3. **Deep conditioner after, always** — both strip significantly; don't skip this step

You don't need to do both every time. For most people in moderately hard water areas, monthly chelating with gentle cleansing in between is enough.

---

## What About Apple Cider Vinegar?

ACV rinses are popular and they do something useful — they smooth the cuticle and give a bit of shine. But they're not a chelating treatment. If mineral build-up is the problem, ACV won't clear it. It's a maintenance rinse, not a replacement for chelating.

Citric acid rinses are a step up — cheap, effective for light maintenance between chelating washes, and more consistent than ACV. Half a teaspoon of food-grade citric acid powder in a litre of water, used as a final rinse.

## FAQs

**Can I chelate every week?**

For most people, that's too often — chelating is thorough and can dry the hair out if overdone. Fortnightly at the most, and only if you're in very hard water with noticeably fast build-up. Always deep condition after.

**Will chelating damage my curls?**

Used correctly and followed by a deep conditioner, no. Used too often without conditioning after, it can lead to dryness and breakage — same as any stripping wash.

**My curls feel great after clarifying — do I still need to chelate?**

If you're in a soft water area and your routine is working, possibly never. The sign you need chelating is when clarifying doesn't fully resolve the roughness or dullness — that's when minerals are the remaining layer.

**Does chelating affect colour-treated hair?**

It can accelerate some fade, so space chelating washes out more if you're colour-treated, and follow with a colour-protecting conditioner. Untreated mineral build-up fades colour faster than chelating does — so it's worth doing, just with a bit more care.
