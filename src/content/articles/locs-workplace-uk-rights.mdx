Locs are a protected hairstyle in UK law. An employer who requires a Black or mixed heritage employee to cut, cover, or change their locs as a condition of employment or continued employment is almost certainly acting unlawfully under the Equality Act 2010.

This article sets out the law, how it applies to locs specifically, and what to do if you encounter a workplace policy that discriminates against them.

---

## The Legal Framework

The Equality Act 2010 prohibits discrimination on grounds of race, which includes ethnic or national origins and colour. Discrimination includes direct discrimination (treating someone worse because of a protected characteristic) and indirect discrimination (applying a policy that disproportionately disadvantages people with a protected characteristic, without justification).

Locs are strongly associated with Black and African heritage. A policy that prohibits locs — or treats employees with locs less favourably — disproportionately disadvantages Black employees. This makes it indirect racial discrimination unless the employer can show it is a "proportionate means of achieving a legitimate aim." This test is difficult to satisfy for most standard workplace policies.

In 2023, Acas updated its workplace guidance to make explicit that employers should not make employees change their natural hairstyle as a condition of employment. The Equality and Human Rights Commission has confirmed that natural Afro hairstyles, including locs, are protected.

---

## What Employers Cannot Do

Employers cannot:
- Require employees to cut their locs
- Require employees to cover their locs during working hours without a legitimate safety or hygiene justification
- Decline to hire someone because of their locs
- Discipline, dismiss, or refuse promotion to someone because of their locs
- Apply a blanket "neat and tidy" policy in a way that only targets Afro hairstyles or locs

---

## What Employers Can Do

The law does not prevent all workplace standards for appearance. Employers can:
- Require hair to be secured during food handling, medical procedures, or other roles where loose hair creates genuine hygiene or safety risks — but this applies equally to all hair types, not specifically to locs
- Apply uniform or dress code policies that are genuinely neutral and applied equally across all ethnic groups — the key test is whether the policy disadvantages any particular racial group

In practice, a policy is much more defensible if it is genuinely neutral (e.g., "hair must be secured above the collar in food preparation areas") than if it targets specific styles (e.g., "no dreadlocks").

## If Your Employer Raises Your Locs

**Step 1: Ask for the policy in writing.** Any workplace policy that affects appearance should be documented. Ask what specific policy is being applied and where it is written.

**Step 2: Ask for the legitimate aim.** Under the Equality Act, an employer applying an indirect discrimination policy must demonstrate it is a proportionate means of achieving a legitimate aim. Ask them to state what that aim is.

**Step 3: Document everything.** Note the date and content of any conversation, request, or written communication about your locs. This documentation is valuable if the matter escalates.

**Step 4: Raise a formal grievance.** If the employer continues to require a change to your locs without a clear, legitimate, proportionate justification, raise a formal grievance through your employer's grievance procedure. Put it in writing.

**Step 5: Seek advice.** ACAS (Advisory, Conciliation and Arbitration Service) provides free, confidential advice: 0300 123 1100. The Equality and Human Rights Commission also provides guidance. If the matter proceeds to employment tribunal, you will need legal advice — many employment solicitors offer free initial consultations.

## Exceptions and Genuine Safety Requirements

There are genuine safety contexts where hair management is required. Operating machinery with entanglement hazards, food preparation, some medical roles. In these contexts, employers can require hair to be tied back, contained, or covered — but this must apply equally to all employees with hair that poses the relevant risk, not specifically to loc wearers.

A kitchen that requires all staff with long hair to wear a hair net is applying a neutral policy. A kitchen that requires loc-wearing staff to wear a hair net but permits other long-haired staff to leave their hair loose is not neutral.

## Common Scenarios

**"Our dress code requires a professional appearance and we don't consider locs professional."**

This is almost certainly indirect racial discrimination. "Professional appearance" is a subjective standard that reflects cultural norms, and applying it to exclude locs disproportionately disadvantages Black employees. Unless the employer can demonstrate a specific and proportionate business justification — beyond aesthetics or client preference — this is unlawful.

**"We have a client-facing policy and our clients may not accept locs."**

Client preference is not a defence for discrimination. An employer cannot justify discriminatory treatment by pointing to the discriminatory preferences of clients or customers. This was established in Showboat Entertainment Centre Ltd v Owens [1984] and has been consistently upheld.

**"The policy applies to everyone — no extreme hairstyles."**

If "extreme hairstyles" is being applied in practice only to Afro hairstyles and locs, the policy is discriminatory in effect regardless of how it's worded. The application, not just the wording, determines whether indirect discrimination is occurring.

## FAQs

**Can I be dismissed for refusing to cut my locs?**

Dismissal for refusing to cut or change a protected hairstyle would likely constitute unfair dismissal and/or racial discrimination. If this happens or is threatened, seek legal advice immediately via ACAS or an employment solicitor.

**My manager said clients have complained about my locs. What do I do?**

Document the conversation. Ask your manager to put the concern in writing. As above, client preference is not a legal justification for discriminatory treatment. Raise a formal grievance if the pressure continues.

**I'm in a probationary period. Am I protected?**

Discrimination protection under the Equality Act applies from day one of employment — it does not have a qualifying period. Unfair dismissal protection has a qualifying period (two years), but discrimination claims do not.

**I work in a conservative sector (law, finance). Does that change my rights?**

No. The sector does not affect the application of the Equality Act. A law firm or financial institution applying a loc-specific policy is subject to the same legal framework as any other employer.
