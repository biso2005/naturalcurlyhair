The natural hair community's position on silicones is that they're bad and should be avoided. The accurate position is more specific: non-water-soluble silicones cause buildup on natural hair that reduces moisture uptake over time. Water-soluble silicones rinse out cleanly and don't build up. The blanket avoidance is overcorrection.

Here's how to tell which is which and what to actually do.

---

## What Silicones Do

Silicones coat the hair shaft. The coating does two things: it makes the hair surface smoother and shinier, and it creates a barrier on the shaft.

The barrier is the tradeoff. On straight hair with regular shampooing, this is mostly fine — the silicone washes out with shampoo and the cycle continues. On natural coily hair that may use sulphate-free shampoos or co-wash, non-water-soluble silicones don't fully wash out. They accumulate over weeks and months, gradually blocking moisture from entering the hair shaft. The hair feels coated, loses definition, and becomes increasingly dry and dull despite moisturising routines — because the moisture can't penetrate.

---

## The Distinction That Actually Matters

**Non-water-soluble silicones:** these are the problematic ones for natural hair routines that avoid sulphates. They require sulphate shampoo to fully remove. Common examples:

- Dimethicone
- Cyclomethicone
- Amodimethicone
- Cyclopentasiloxane
- Dimethiconol

**Water-soluble silicones:** these rinse out cleanly with water or with sulphate-free shampoo. They don't build up. Common examples:

- Dimethicone Copolyol
- Lauryl Methicone Copolyol
- PEG (anything PEG-modified silicone — look for "PEG" before the silicone name)
- Bis-PEG/PPG silicones

**Quick identification rule:** if the silicone name ends in -cone, -conol, or -xane without a PEG modifier, it's likely non-water-soluble. If it has PEG in the name, it's water-soluble.

---

## What This Means in Practice

**You don't need to avoid all silicones.** Water-soluble silicones in a conditioner or styling product are fine on natural hair — they rinse out without requiring a sulphate shampoo and don't build up.

**You should be careful with non-water-soluble silicones** if your routine relies on sulphate-free shampoo. The silicone accumulates with every application and only a sulphate shampoo fully removes it.

**If you use a sulphate shampoo regularly (monthly clarifying wash):** non-water-soluble silicones are more manageable — the clarifying wash removes the buildup. Some natural hair routines work this way intentionally.

**If your routine is sulphate-free throughout:** non-water-soluble silicones will eventually cause problems. A monthly chelating or clarifying wash removes them; without that, buildup is cumulative.

## When Silicone Buildup Is Already an Issue

Signs of silicone buildup:
- Hair feels coated or waxy, particularly at the ends
- Leave-in and moisture products feel like they're sitting on top of the hair rather than absorbing
- Definition and curl pattern has gradually declined
- Deep conditioning isn't improving the hair's moisture level

Fix: one sulphate clarifying wash. The buildup clears in a single wash. Follow immediately with a deep conditioner — sulphate shampoo strips more than silicones and the hair needs moisture restoration.

## FAQs

**Should I throw out all my products with silicones?**

No. Check whether the silicones are water-soluble or not. Water-soluble silicones in your conditioner or leave-in are not a problem. Non-water-soluble silicones are only a problem if you're not clarifying regularly enough to remove them.

**I went fully silicone-free and my hair felt worse, not better. Why?**

Several possibilities: the products you switched to don't work as well for your hair type, or the silicone-free products you chose are heavier or have different formulations that aren't suited to your porosity. Silicone-free isn't automatically better — it just removes one specific buildup mechanism. If the routine overall is worse, the problem isn't silicones.

**Is the Curly Girl Method's silicone prohibition correct?**

The Curly Girl Method's rule of avoiding silicones is a simplification that's useful as a starting rule for beginners. The nuanced position — avoid non-water-soluble silicones unless clarifying regularly — is more accurate. The CGM rule is conservative: it produces fewer problems for beginners than the nuanced approach, because beginners are less likely to clarify consistently.

**Do silicones damage the hair?**

No. Silicones coat the hair and can cause buildup, but they don't damage the hair shaft. The problem they cause is blocking moisture — which causes dryness and breakage indirectly. The silicone itself is inert.
