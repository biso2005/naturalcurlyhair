Porosity matters less dramatically for 3B hair than for type 4 — the ringlet is more accessible to products than a tight coil, and most mainstream curl products are designed with something close to 3B medium porosity in mind. But porosity still determines whether your conditioner absorbs or sits on top, whether your gel holds or feels crunchy, and whether moisture lasts through the day or evaporates within hours.

If your 3B routine is inconsistent — works sometimes, doesn't others, or products that worked last year no longer do — porosity is worth checking.

## What Porosity Means for 3B Ringlets

**Low porosity 3B:**
- Takes longer than expected to fully wet under running water
- Products sit on the ringlet surface and bead rather than absorbing
- Buildup accumulates quickly — creams and oils pile up after a few days
- Ringlets look moisturised but feel dry to the touch
- Heat during conditioning makes a significant difference

**High porosity 3B:**
- Hair gets wet immediately and absorbs product readily
- Moisture also escapes quickly — dryness and frizz return faster than expected
- Frizz in outdoor humidity is more pronounced
- Ends feel dry or rough even with conditioning
- More common in colour-treated, bleached, or heat-styled hair

**Medium porosity 3B:** The most common category for 3B hair. Products absorb and hold reasonably well. Most mainstream curly hair products are designed with this in mind, which is part of why 3B hair is relatively well-served by off-the-shelf options.

## The Float Test on 3B Hair

The float test — drop a clean strand in water and observe whether it sinks or floats — is even less reliable on 3B than on tighter types. 3B ringlets trap air within the loop of each coil. A high-porosity strand with air trapped in the spiral floats convincingly alongside a low-porosity strand, making the test nearly useless for distinguishing the two.

---

## Two Tests That Work

### The spray bottle test

Spritz a small section of clean, dry 3B hair with plain water. Watch for 30 seconds.

- Water **beads on the surface** and takes a while to absorb — low porosity
- Water **absorbs quickly** and the section darkens — high porosity  
- Water **sinks in but slowly** — medium porosity

Do this after a clarifying or chelating wash for accuracy. Product residue on the ringlet changes the absorption rate and gives misleading results.

### The feel test

Run a single clean, product-free strand between your fingers from tip to root.

- **Smooth, almost slippery** — flat cuticles, likely low porosity
- **Rough or bumpy, slight snag** — raised cuticles, likely high porosity
- **Very rough or breaks mid-stroke** — high porosity with damage

## Mixed Porosity: Ends vs Roots on 3B Hair

If your 3B hair has length, your roots are almost always lower porosity than your ends. Newer growth hasn't been through years of washing, UV, manipulation, and product exposure. Your ends have.

The practical result: your roots may resist moisture slightly while your ends absorb quickly but lose it fast. If you notice that your roots look fine but your ends are consistently dry and frizzy, mixed porosity is a likely explanation.

The spray bottle test on roots separately from ends tells you whether this applies to your hair.

## What to Do Based on Your Result

### Low porosity 3B

The goal is getting moisture past the cuticle surface.

- **Heat during deep conditioning.** Without it, conditioner on low-porosity 3B sits on the ringlet surface and rinses off. A heat cap or warm damp towel over a shower cap for 20–30 minutes makes a measurable difference.
- **Lightweight, water-based products.** Thick creams and heavy oils build up on low-porosity ringlets quickly. A lighter leave-in — water as the first ingredient, some slip, not too heavy — absorbs more readily.
- **Apply to soaking-wet hair.** The wetter the hair when products go on, the better. Don't let it partially dry first.
- **Clarify regularly.** Buildup happens faster on low-porosity hair because products pile up rather than absorbing. Monthly clarifying resets the surface.

### High porosity 3B

The goal is slowing moisture loss from the ringlet.

- **Seal after leave-in.** A light oil or cream applied immediately after leave-in, while the hair is still wet, slows moisture evaporation. This is the step most often missing from high-porosity routines.
- **Don't skip the sealant step.** High-porosity 3B ringlets that aren't sealed lose moisture to the air faster — which in UK low indoor humidity means dryness by midday.
- **Occasional protein.** Protein helps high-porosity hair hold moisture more consistently. Every six to eight weeks or when the hair feels mushy, stretchy, or loses its spring when wet. Don't overdo it — excess protein makes ringlets stiff and prone to breakage.
- **Satin at night.** High-porosity hair loses moisture to cotton pillowcases more readily. A satin bonnet or pillowcase is more critical for high-porosity hair than any other type.

### Medium porosity 3B

- Most mainstream products work — you have the most flexibility
- Focus on the seasonal adjustment: lighter products in summer when outdoor humidity keeps ringlets moisturised, slightly heavier sealant in winter when indoor air is dry
- Adjust based on how your hair actually responds rather than following a strict protocol

## Hard Water and Porosity: Getting a Reliable Result

If you're in a hard water area — most of England — mineral buildup on the ringlet can make low-porosity hair test as high-porosity. The mineral film disrupts the cuticle surface and changes how quickly water appears to absorb.

Do a chelating wash before testing. The result after chelating gives you your actual porosity, not a result influenced by mineral coating.

## FAQs

**My 3B products work on wash day but the curl is dry by the next morning. Is that porosity?**

High porosity or missing sealant — or both. Check whether you're applying a sealing layer (oil or cream) after your leave-in. If you are and moisture still doesn't last, you may need a heavier sealant or a protein treatment to slow moisture loss.

**I've been using the same products for years and they've stopped working. Is my porosity changing?**

Porosity does change — chemical treatments, heat, and simple aging shift hair toward higher porosity over time. It could also be hard water gradually accumulating if you haven't chelated recently. Test both: chelate first, then test porosity.

**Low porosity sounds like a problem. Is it?**

Not really. Once moisture gets in, low-porosity hair holds it well — which means hair that tends to stay moisturised longer than high-porosity hair once the routine is right. The challenge is getting past the cuticle, which heat solves reliably. Low-porosity ringlets can look very healthy and have good definition once the routine matches.

**Do I need to deep condition if my 3B hair is medium porosity?**

A deep conditioner with heat a few times a month gives consistently better results than regular rinse-out conditioner alone, even for medium-porosity 3B. It's not essential every single week, but skipping it entirely means the ringlets are rarely at their best.
