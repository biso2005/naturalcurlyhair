Unlocking locs is possible in some cases, for some stages of loc maturity, with significant effort and some degree of damage. It is not possible in all cases, and the process described online often understates how much hair is lost and how long it takes. This article is the honest version.

---

## When Unlocking Is and Isn't Possible

### Starter and early locs (under 6 months)

Most starter locs at the two-strand twist or coil stage can be carefully undone. The hair isn't fully knotted yet — the interior is still a loose structure that conditioner and patient detangling can work through.

Expected outcome: most of the hair is recoverable with some breakage. The hair may look different from before the locs because the twist or coil shape is set in — it washes out over time.

### Budding locs (months 3–9)

Increasingly difficult. The interior of the loc has begun to knit. Some sections may come undone; others may have permanently matted. The result is often uneven — some hair recovers, some breaks off at the knotted point. This stage is the most unpredictable.

### Teen and mature locs (month 9+)

Fully interlocked, knotted hair. The interior cannot be detangled without breaking the knot — which means breaking the hair. Attempting to unlock mature locs almost always results in significant breakage and loss of length. The hair that comes out is usually shorter than expected and in poor condition.

**For mature locs:** the realistic option in most cases is cutting the locked portion and starting fresh with the new growth. The new growth at the root that hasn't locked is recoverable; the locked portion is not.

---

## The Unlocking Process (For Early Locs)

This is the process that has a reasonable chance of success on starter and early budding locs.

**What you'll need:** a spray bottle, a very slippery conditioner (one with good slip), a wide-tooth comb, a rat-tail comb, sectioning clips, and significant time. Expect each loc to take 20–45 minutes depending on its stage.

**Method:**
1. Saturate the loc completely with water and conditioner
2. Leave the conditioner in for 15–30 minutes
3. Start at the very tip of the loc — the end — and work upward toward the root
4. With fingers first, then a rat-tail comb, very gently work the knots apart from the bottom up
5. Add more conditioner as the section dries
6. Do not pull — pull breaks the hair. Separate.
7. Clip out the detangled section and move to the next loc

**Realistic time commitment:** a full head of starter locs can take six to twelve hours of continuous work or multiple sessions over several days. Many people choose to have this done by a professional who specialises in loc removal — salons that offer this service are not common but exist in larger UK cities.

## After Unlocking

The hair after unlocking will be:
- **Damaged.** The locking process involves the hair scales locking together. Undoing this stresses the cuticle. More so with older locs.
- **Shorter than expected.** Shrinkage is significant for natural hair, and the detangled hair will reveal the true length which is often shorter than the loc appeared.
- **In need of protein treatment.** A protein treatment followed by deep conditioning helps restore some resilience to the cuticle-stressed hair.
- **A different texture than before.** The hair texture changes during the locking period. The new growth that hasn't locked will be in its natural state; the recovered hair may have a different feel.

Trim the ends after unlocking — they'll be in the worst condition of any section.

---

## Alternatives to Full Unlocking

**Loc removal with a cut:** cut the loc at the transition between new growth and locked hair. The new growth is recoverable; the locked portion is removed. This is the most practical option for any locs past the budding stage.

**Waiting for new growth:** some people grow out the new growth significantly before cutting, to preserve as much length as possible from the post-loc hair. This takes patience but gives more to work with after the cut.

**Colour or texture change first:** if the post-loc hair will be coloured or treated, many stylists recommend waiting until the recovered hair has been trimmed of its most damaged sections before any chemical treatment.

## FAQs

**I've seen videos of people unlocking mature locs with conditioner. Does that work?**

Some videos show this successfully. What they often don't show: the amount of hair lost during the process, the condition of the recovered hair, and that the locs in question were typically early-stage (under six months) or had an unusual internal structure that made them easier to unlock. Mature, fully-knotted locs do not typically respond well to conditioner unlocking regardless of how it's presented online.

**I have mature locs and want to go natural again. What's my best option?**

The most practical path is a loc removal cut — identifying where the new growth ends and the locked portion begins and cutting at that point. Combined with a big chop if length loss is acceptable, or a gradual cut over several appointments. Consult with a natural hair stylist who has experience with post-loc hair.

**I want to try and unlock my locs first before cutting. How do I know if it's worth trying?**

Test one loc from a less visible section. Follow the unlocking method above. If the loc at that section is starter or early budding stage, you'll have a sense of how the rest will go. If significant knots resist even gentle separation with generous conditioner, that section won't unlock without breaking — and the rest probably won't either.

**My locs are two years old. Is there any chance?**

Very unlikely without significant breakage. The hair is fully knotted through the interior. The most realistic expectation for two-year mature locs is that unlocking retrieves the last one to two centimetres of new growth from each loc, and the rest will need to be cut.
