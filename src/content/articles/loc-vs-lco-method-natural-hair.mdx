**LOC if you have type 4 hair or high porosity. LCO if you have low porosity or type 3 and looser.** That's the short answer. The rest of this explains why and how to confirm which works for you.

---

## What LOC and LCO Mean

Both are layering sequences for applying moisture after washing:

**LOC:** Liquid → Oil → Cream
**LCO:** Liquid → Cream → Oil

The liquid is water or a water-based leave-in that delivers moisture. The oil and cream seal it in. The argument is about which order of oil and cream does a better sealing job.

---

## LOC: Oil Before Cream

In LOC, a light oil goes on over the leave-in before the cream. The oil creates a barrier that slows moisture evaporation from the leave-in; the cream then sits on top and adds weight, hold, and additional sealing.

**Works best for:**
- Type 4 hair (4A, 4B, 4C) — dense coils benefit from the extra sealing the oil provides underneath the cream
- High porosity hair — the cuticle is open, loses moisture fast; the oil under the cream extends how long moisture stays in
- Hair that dries out quickly between wash days despite regular moisturising

**How to apply:**
1. Leave-in on soaking-wet hair, section by section
2. A few drops of oil — jojoba, olive, argan — worked through each section while still wet
3. A curl cream or butter over the top, scrunched in

---

## LCO: Cream Before Oil

In LCO, the cream goes directly over the leave-in, and the oil goes last as the final seal. On low-porosity hair, a cream absorbs better when it goes on before any oil — oil first can create a surface barrier that stops the cream from penetrating. The oil on top seals everything without reducing absorption.

**Works best for:**
- Low porosity hair — the cuticle is tight; anything that further reduces absorption (like oil first) leaves products sitting on the surface
- Type 3 and 2C hair — lighter patterns benefit from lighter sealing; cream then a few drops of oil is usually sufficient
- Hair where products seem to sit on top rather than absorbing

**How to apply:**
1. Leave-in on soaking-wet hair, section by section
2. Curl cream or light butter worked through each section
3. A few drops of light oil last, scrunched through to seal

---

## How to Know Which Is Working

**After LOC or LCO on wash day, by the next morning the hair should feel:** moisturised, not stiff, not greasy.

**If it feels dry:** the sealing isn't holding. Try the other sequence, or increase the cream amount.

**If it feels greasy or heavy:** either too much product or oil too heavy for the hair type. Reduce or switch to a lighter oil.

**If products are balling up or sitting on top:** likely low porosity and the oil is going on before the cream is absorbed. Switch to LCO.

## UK Hard Water Note

In hard water areas, mineral deposits on the hair shaft reduce how well both sequences work — products can't fully contact the hair underneath the mineral layer. Monthly chelating restores this. If LOC or LCO seems to stop working over time without changing products, chelate before troubleshooting the sequence.

## FAQs

**Do I have to use both oil and cream, or can I skip one?**

You can simplify. Many people use just leave-in and oil (LO) or just leave-in and cream (LC) with good results. The full three-step sequence gives more moisture retention but isn't mandatory.

**Can I use the same products for LOC and LCO?**

Yes — it's the order that changes, not the products.

**My LOC routine worked and now it doesn't. What changed?**

Porosity can shift over time with damage or chemical treatments. Hard water builds up over months. Both change how the sequence performs. Test porosity fresh on clean hair; chelate if you haven't recently.

**I use LOC but my hair still feels dry by day three. What should I add?**

A heavier cream or butter as the final layer, or a sealant oil (castor oil blended with a lighter carrier) at the oil step. Also check whether your leave-in is heavy enough — some liquid leave-ins are too watery for type 4 high porosity hair.
