The ideal school braid is one you can install on Sunday and not think about again for two to three weeks. It survives PE, swimming, rain, lunch, and sleeping — and it looks presentable throughout. This guide covers which styles actually do that, what to ask the stylist, and how to maintain them with minimal effort during the school week.

---

## Styles That Work for School

### Straight-back cornrows

The most practical school style for primary-age children. Rows running from front to back, flat against the scalp — nothing to snag on clothing, survive PE without coming loose, no loose ends to catch on things. Installation takes one to two hours; last two to three weeks.

For PE and swimming: a swimming cap fits over them easily. They don't need to be taken down for any school activity.

### Ghana braids (feed-in cornrows)

Similar to straight-back cornrows but with extension hair fed in gradually, giving a slightly fuller look. Lower tension at the root than standard cornrows with extensions — appropriate for younger children. Last three to four weeks. Can be decorated with beads at the ends.

### Box braids

For older primary and secondary students. Individual braids that hang freely — slightly more maintenance than cornrows because the ends can frizz and individual braids can come loose. But they last four to six weeks and can be worn up or down.

For school: wear in a high puff or ponytail for PE. No swimming cap needed; a loose ponytail is enough to keep them out of the way.

### Two-strand twists

For shorter hair that can't be braided easily. Last one to two weeks typically. The root frizzes faster than braids. Protective and low-manipulation.

## What to Ask at the Salon

**For primary-age children:**
- "Can you keep the tension at the hairline loose? Her scalp is sensitive."
- "We need this to last at least two weeks for school — what size would you recommend?"
- "She has PE twice a week and sometimes swimming — will these need any extra securing for that?"

**For secondary students:**
- Let them be part of the conversation — they know what they want and what feels uncomfortable
- "She had traction issues last time — can you braid the edges especially loosely?"

## What the School Cannot Do

Braids — cornrows, box braids, Ghana braids, twists — are legitimate protective styles with deep cultural significance. Schools cannot ban them or treat students with braids less favourably than students with other hairstyles. This is protected under the Equality Act 2010.

If the school raises any issue with the style:
1. Request the policy in writing
2. Ask whether the restriction applies equally to all hair types
3. Reference the 2023 government statutory guidance on school uniform policies

A clean, well-maintained braid style is entirely appropriate school wear.

## Beads

Beads on braids are a traditional and age-appropriate decoration for children. Most schools don't have a specific bead policy — but if beads do become an issue, the Equality Act applies here too. Beads on braids have cultural significance and a policy specifically banning them while permitting other decorative accessories may be discriminatory in effect.

Practical note: very small or large beads can catch on clothing. Medium-sized barrel beads are the most practical for school — they don't catch, and they're easy to thread and remove.

## Maintenance During the School Week

**Daily: almost nothing.** The benefit of a good school braid is minimal weekday maintenance.

- Run fingers over the edges in the morning — if the hairline looks very dry, a small amount of edge oil applied with a fingertip
- A light satin scrunchy or scrunchie if the style needs securing for PE

**At bedtime:**
- A satin bonnet or satin pillowcase. This is the one non-negotiable — it extends the style significantly and prevents the root frizzing overnight.
- For children who resist bonnets: a satin pillowcase, a satin-lined sleep cap (looks less like a "hair bonnet"), or a loose satin scrunchie pineappling the braids.

**Every two to three days:**
- A light mist of water to the scalp between braids or rows, followed by a small amount of jojoba oil applied to the scalp parts. This is the whole routine.

**Every two weeks (if the style is staying in):**
- Scalp wash with diluted shampoo, applied with a squeeze bottle directly to the scalp. Rinse thoroughly. Dry fully before bed.

---

## After Removal

Don't rush from one protective style straight into the next. After the braids come down:

1. Detangle gently with generous conditioner — section by section, from the ends up
2. Wash and deep condition
3. Give the hair and scalp a rest — at least two to three days in a loose protective style without any tension before the next install

Repeated installation without rest leads to cumulative traction at the hairline. The edges need time between styles.

## FAQs

**My daughter is four. Are braids appropriate?**

Yes, for a child of four, but tension matters more than for older children. Very loose, gentle cornrows without extension hair are appropriate. Avoid heavy extensions or styles with a lot of weight — the scalp is more sensitive in young children and traction alopecia can develop quickly.

**The school wants all long hair tied back. Do braids count?**

Cornrows and flat braids are already secured against the scalp — they don't need additional tying back. Box braids hanging loose may need a loose ponytail or puff for PE. Ask the school to clarify whether cornrows are considered "tied back" for their purposes; they usually are.

**My son doesn't want to wear a bonnet. How do I get the braids to last?**

A satin pillowcase is the next best option. A satin-lined beanie (for older boys who accept it more readily) works too. If he'll accept anything satin touching his hair at night, the braids last significantly longer — that's the conversation worth having.

**The braids were taken down and there's a lot of hair in the sink. Is that breakage?**

Some shedding during braid removal is normal — hair that shed during the weeks the braids were in collects inside the braid rather than falling out daily. A significant amount of short hair in the sink (not long shed hairs) suggests breakage, usually from removal that was rushed or done dry. Next time: generous conditioner, patience, and unravel from the bottom up.
