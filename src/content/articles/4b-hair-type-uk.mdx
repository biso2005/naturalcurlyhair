4B hair sits between 4C and 4A on the typing scale, but it has its own distinct behaviour that neither description fully captures. The defining characteristic is the Z-pattern — sharp angles rather than consistent spirals — and that shape is why 4B tangles the way it does, holds moisture differently, and responds to styling in ways the standard curly-hair playbook doesn't quite account for.

This guide is specifically for 4B hair in the UK: the Z-pattern specifics, why common advice misses the mark, and a routine built around how 4B hair actually behaves.

---

## What Is 4B Hair?

4B hair forms tight coils with a pronounced Z or sharp S-shape — the strand bends at angles rather than curling in smooth spirals. When dry and without product, it typically shows very little visible curl definition: instead you see a dense, textured mass that looks different from both the tighter compactness of 4C and the visible coils of 4A.

The key characteristics:

- **Z or sharp S-pattern** — the strand direction changes abruptly rather than curving
- **High shrinkage** — typically 50–70%, sometimes more
- **Low clumping** — individual strand definition is harder to achieve than in 4A or 3C
- **Dense texture** — curl spacing is tight, which affects how products distribute

Most people with 4B hair have some variation across their head — 4C at the crown is common, 4A at the temples or nape. The typing system is a rough approximation; treat it as a starting point, not a fixed prescription.

### 4B vs 4A and 4C

| Type | Pattern | Definition when dry | Shrinkage |
|------|---------|-------------------|-----------|
| 4A | Tight S-coils | Some visible coil | High |
| 4B | Z or sharp S, angular | Minimal visible curl | Very high |
| 4C | Very tight Z, compact | Little to no curl visible | Extreme |

The practical difference between 4B and 4C is real but not dramatic. 4B often has slightly more clumping potential than 4C, and the Z-angle means it tangles in a distinctive way — angular strands hook into each other along the bends.

## Why the Z-Pattern Changes Everything

The Z-shape isn't just a visual characteristic. It determines how 4B hair behaves in every practical situation.

**Tangling:** The sharp angles mean strands hook into each other at the bend points. This is different from how 4C tangles (more about strands looping around each other) or how looser types tangle (friction between curls). 4B tangles happen at the Z-angles — which is why combing from the root down is counterproductive, and why 4B needs more detangling care than almost any other type.

→ [4B hair tangling and detangling](/4b/4b-hair-tangling-detangling-uk/)

**Moisture distribution:** The tight bends also mean your scalp's natural oils stall at the angles — they can't travel easily down the length of the hair shaft. Combined with 4B density, this is why the length stays dry even when the scalp feels healthy.

**Shrinkage:** The Z-coil springs back dramatically as wet hair dries. Unlike a spiral curl compressing, the Z-pattern folds back at angles — which is why 4B shrinkage looks severe even compared to other type 4 hair.

## A Routine for 4B Hair in the UK

### The hard water issue

If you're in London, the South East, East Anglia, or the Midlands, hard water is almost certainly a factor in your routine. Minerals in the water settle on your hair with every wash and block conditioner from absorbing properly. On 4B hair — which already has density working against product distribution — this makes things significantly harder.

A chelating shampoo used once a month strips those minerals off. Without it, you're conditioning over a mineral layer and wondering why your deep conditioner does nothing.

→ [4B hair and hard water UK](/4b/4b-hair-hard-water-uk/)

### Wash day

1. **Pre-poo:** Oil or conditioner applied to dry hair before washing. Reduces friction during shampooing.
2. **Chelating or clarifying shampoo** (monthly): Focus on the scalp; rinse through the length.
3. **Gentle shampoo or co-wash** (other weeks): 4B hair doesn't need strong cleansing every week.
4. **Deep conditioner in sections:** Work it through systematically — 4B density means product won't distribute on its own. Leave with heat for 20–30 minutes.
5. **Detangle during the deep conditioning step:** Slippery hair with conditioner in is the easiest state for separating 4B Z-angles. Not after the rinse.
6. **Cool rinse** then style immediately while wet.

### Moisturising and sealing

Apply leave-in to hair that's still dripping wet. The LOC or LCO method both work; which suits you depends on your porosity. Try each for a few wash days before deciding.

→ [4B hair porosity](/4b/4b-hair-porosity/)

Work in small enough sections that product reaches the inner strands of each curl cluster, not just the surface.

### Protective styling

Twists and braids suit 4B well — the Z-pattern holds a twist nicely once set. A few things to keep in mind:

- Moisturise thoroughly before installing, not just at installation and take-down
- The hairline is fragile — tight installation on the edges causes damage over time
- Two weeks between long-term styles lets you check the hair's condition

## Styling 4B Hair

### Getting definition

4B hair doesn't self-define the way 4A or 3C does. Twist-outs and braid-outs typically give better results than wash-and-go for most 4B hair.

**Twist-outs:** Two-strand twists on well-moisturised damp hair. Let dry completely before separating — separating damp gives frizz. Separate gently with a small amount of oil on fingertips.

**Braid-outs:** Similar to twist-outs; some people find braid-outs last longer because the braid creates a different angle in the hair.

**Wash and go on 4B hair:** Possible, but the result is textured definition rather than defined curls. A strong-hold gel applied to soaking-wet conditioned hair works best. Work in sections.

### Stretching without heat

Three methods that work on 4B:

- **African threading:** Thread wrapped around sections — very effective, stays in multiple days
- **Banding:** Loose hair ties at intervals down a damp section — elongates without heat
- **Braid-outs worn before styling:** Pre-stretching distributes shrinkage more evenly

## Common 4B Problems and What Actually Helps

### Dryness that won't shift

If your hair consistently feels dry a day after wash day, it's usually one of three things: hard water blocking products from absorbing, low porosity where products sit on top rather than penetrating, or sealing with oil before the moisture layer has had time to soak in. Diagnose the cause before buying more products.

### Breakage at the bends

The Z-angles are under more structural stress than the straight sections between them. This is where 4B breakage concentrates. Reducing manipulation — particularly combing when dry — reduces this type of breakage more than any product change.

### Products working in summer but failing in winter

UK winter is damp outdoors but dry indoors from central heating. Humectant-heavy products can work brilliantly in summer and pull moisture out of 4B hair in low-humidity indoor air in November. If your routine falls apart in winter, reduce glycerin-heavy products and increase sealants.

## FAQs

**Does 4B hair grow more slowly?**

No. Growth rate is genetics and scalp health, not curl pattern. 4B hair can seem to stall because shrinkage hides length gains and Z-angle breakage cancels them out. Reduce breakage first.

**Why does my 4B hair tangle so much?**

The Z-pattern causes strands to hook together at the bend points. 4B tangles happen differently from 4C or looser types — fingers before any tool, small sections, bottom-to-top only.

**Can 4B hair have low porosity?**

Yes. Porosity and curl pattern are separate characteristics. Test yours before building a routine on the assumption that 4B is automatically high porosity.

**How often should I wash 4B hair?**

Once a week to every ten days is a common starting point. Less frequent washing lets shed hair accumulate and tangle; too frequent washing without thorough conditioning dries the length out. Adjust based on your scalp, not a schedule.

**Why does my deep conditioner seem to do nothing?**

Two likely causes: hard water (a mineral layer is blocking absorption), or low porosity (the cuticle is resisting moisture). Test your water hardness first — it's the cheaper fix.
