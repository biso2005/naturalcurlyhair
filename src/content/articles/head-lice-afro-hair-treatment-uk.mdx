Head lice are not more common in Afro-textured hair — the lice don't discriminate by hair type. But detection is harder, wet combing works differently, and most of the advice circulating in school letters and Mumsnet threads is written for straight hair and doesn't translate.

Here's what works.

---

## Do Head Lice Spread in Afro Hair the Same Way?

Yes. Head lice spread through direct head-to-head contact regardless of hair type or texture. The louse grabs onto the hair shaft and moves between heads when they touch — playground contact, sleepovers, close play. Lice cannot jump or fly.

The myth that Afro-textured hair is somehow immune or resistant to lice is false. Lice are found in Afro hair with the same frequency as in straight hair.

---

## Detection: Why It's Harder

In straight hair, lice and nits (the egg casings) are visible on parting the hair because the strands lie flat and the scalp is easily visible.

In Afro-textured and very coily hair, the coil pattern makes parting the hair for visual inspection difficult without a fine-tooth comb and good light. Nits are easier to spot than live lice — they appear as small white or cream-coloured oval casings attached to the hair shaft close to the scalp, on individual strands.

**How to check:**
1. Part the hair in sections using a rat-tail comb under good light (a bright lamp or outdoors in daylight)
2. Look along the hair shafts within 1–2cm of the scalp — nits are attached to the shaft, not lying loosely
3. Look particularly at the nape of the neck, behind the ears, and the crown — lice prefer warmth and prefer these areas
4. Nits are glued to the shaft and don't slide off when touched (unlike dandruff or product residue)

## Treatment

The same treatments used for all hair types work on Afro-textured hair. The NHS recommends two main approaches:

**Option 1: Wet combing (detection combing)**

This is the primary NHS-recommended method and requires no product. It works by physically removing lice and nits using a fine-tooth detection comb (also called a nit comb) on wet, conditioned hair.

On Afro-textured hair, wet combing requires adjustment:
- The hair must be detangled thoroughly before you begin — a fine-tooth nit comb cannot go through tangled coily hair without causing significant breakage
- Apply a generous amount of conditioner (more than a normal wash) to give maximum slip
- Detangle gently with a wide-tooth comb first, then proceed with the fine-tooth detection comb
- Work in small sections — smaller sections than you would use for a normal detangle
- The comb must reach the scalp to catch lice; work systematically from scalp to tip in each section
- Repeat every three to four days for two weeks to catch hatching lice before they can lay new eggs

This is effective but time-consuming on thick Afro-textured hair. For many families, the chemical treatment option is more practical for the active treatment phase, with wet combing used for checking and monitoring.

**Option 2: Medicated lotion or spray**

Over-the-counter treatments available at Boots and Superdrug include:
- **Hedrin** (dimeticone): a physical (non-chemical) treatment that works by coating and suffocating lice. Available as lotion or spray. NHS-recommended.
- **Full Marks Solution** (dimeticone): same mechanism as Hedrin, slightly different formulation.
- **Lyclear** (permethrin): a chemical treatment. Some lice populations have developed resistance to permethrin; if one treatment doesn't work, switch to a dimeticone-based product.

**Application on Afro-textured hair:** the treatment must reach the scalp and coat the hair thoroughly. On thick or densely coiled hair, apply in sections with a dye brush or your fingers to ensure the scalp is covered, not just the surface of the hair. A single pass over the top of a large afro or bun will not coat the hair at the scalp.

Follow the package instructions — most treatments require leaving on for 8–12 hours and then washing out, with a second application after seven to ten days to catch newly hatched lice.

---

## After Treatment

Once treatment is complete, wash the hair normally. Deep condition — the chemical treatments and the extended application period will have dried the hair somewhat.

Continue to check weekly by parting and looking, or with occasional detection combing. Reinfestation from school contact is common — weekly checking means you catch it early rather than after a heavy infestation has developed.

---

## Protective Styles During and After Lice

If the hair was in braids or cornrows when lice were found:

- The style needs to come down for treatment — the lotion must reach the scalp throughout, which it cannot do through an intact braid
- After treatment and a wash, the style can be reinstalled
- A braid or cornrow style does not prevent lice — lice move between heads through direct contact regardless of style

## FAQs

**The school says my child has nits but I can't find anything when I check. What should I do?**

Ask the school to show you what they found. Dandruff, dry scalp flakes, and product residue are commonly mistaken for nits, particularly in Afro hair where white product residue can accumulate on the shaft. Nits are firmly attached to the hair shaft and cannot be flicked off — they require a sliding motion to remove. If unsure, treat preventively.

**I've used the lotion twice and still find lice. What's happening?**

Either the application didn't reach the scalp throughout (common on thick Afro hair), or the seven to ten day gap allowed hatched eggs to reach maturity before the second treatment. If using permethrin (Lyclear) and it's not working, switch to a dimeticone product (Hedrin, Full Marks) — resistance to permethrin is now widespread in the UK.

**My daughter has locs. How do I treat lice?**

Apply a dimeticone treatment in sections directly to the scalp and throughout the locs. The treatment needs to coat the entire length. This will require significant product quantity for long locs. Do not attempt fine-tooth combing through locs — that's not the tool for this situation. Focus on the scalp application and the soak time.

**Does using tea tree oil prevent head lice?**

There is some weak evidence that certain essential oils (tea tree, lavender) have a mild repellent effect on lice, but not enough to be recommended as a prevention method. A diluted tea tree oil spray applied before school won't hurt, but don't rely on it as the primary prevention strategy. The most effective prevention is regular checking so infestation is caught early.
