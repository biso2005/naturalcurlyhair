Flat twists are two-strand twists done against the scalp — the hair is twisted flat rather than hanging free. The result looks similar to cornrows from the front but uses a different technique (two sections crossing rather than three sections braiding). They're faster to install than cornrows and can be done in styles that follow the scalp contour without requiring the braiding technique.

---

## Flat Twists vs Cornrows vs Two-Strand Twists

| | Flat twists | Cornrows | Two-strand twists |
|--|------------|---------|------------------|
| Sections used | 2 | 3 | 2 |
| Lie flat against scalp | Yes | Yes | No — hang free |
| Technique difficulty | Medium | Medium-high | Low |
| Duration as style | 1–2 weeks | 2–3 weeks | 1–2 weeks |
| When to use | Home install, quick flat style | Longer-lasting flat style | Hanging protective style |

---

## When Flat Twists Make Sense

**Home installation:** if you can't cornrow your own hair (which requires working behind your head with three sections), you may be able to flat twist your own hair. The two-section technique is easier to control on yourself.

**Quick protective styles:** flat twists in two to four sections down the scalp make a quick, low-manipulation style for a few days that doesn't require a salon.

**As a twist-out base:** large flat twists produce a different wave pattern when unravelled than two-strand twists — more uniform, more elongated. A common technique is doing four to six large flat twists, leaving them overnight, and unravelling in the morning for a defined wave pattern.

---

## How to Flat Twist

**Start on freshly washed, damp hair with products applied:**

1. Take a small section at the front of the head where you want the twist to start
2. Divide it into two equal parts
3. Cross the right over the left
4. Pick up a small amount of new hair from the scalp and add it to the section on the right
5. Cross right over left again
6. Repeat — adding hair from the scalp with each cross, like adding to a braid
7. When you reach the end of the section, continue as a regular two-strand twist to the end of the hair
8. Secure the end

The key difference from a regular two-strand twist is step 4 — picking up new hair with each cross to incorporate the hair from the scalp. Without this, the twist lifts away from the scalp rather than lying flat.

## Products

Same principle as two-strand twists — apply product before twisting, on damp hair:

- Leave-in conditioner to provide moisture
- A medium-hold cream or gel to give the twist hold and keep it against the scalp

For flat twists specifically, a slightly firmer-hold product than you'd use for hanging twists helps them lie flat rather than springing away from the scalp.

---

## Duration

**As a flat twist style:** one to two weeks. The scalp-lying structure means they stay neater slightly longer than hanging twists, but they're not as durable as cornrows.

**As a twist-out base:** overnight to two nights. Unravel when the hair is dry throughout.

---

## UK Weather Notes

UK outdoor humidity will affect the flat twist edges in the same way it affects any twist or braid style — the hairline frizzes first. A light anti-humidity gel or spray along the parts helps.

Central heating dryness in winter is a bigger factor than humidity for flat twists — the style dries out the scalp faster than any other time of year. Light scalp oil every two days while the style is in.

## FAQs

**What's the difference between a flat twist and a cornrow in terms of result?**

Flat twists unravel more easily than cornrows — the two-section structure is less secure than the three-section braid. They also don't last as long. For a style that needs to stay neat for two to three weeks, cornrows are more durable. Flat twists are better for a one-to-two-week style you can do yourself at home.

**Can I flat twist on wet hair?**

Better on damp rather than dripping wet. Very wet hair makes it harder to control the sections and the product doesn't adhere as well. After washing, towel blot to damp (not wet) and apply products before twisting.

**My flat twists look like cornrows. Is that right?**

It means the technique is correct — flat twists and cornrows produce a very similar appearance. The difference is in the feel and the durability: the cornrow feels more secure; the flat twist will unravel more easily.

**Can I do flat twists on type 3 or 2C hair?**

Yes, though looser patterns need more hold product to keep the twist lying flat against the scalp rather than springing away. A firmer-hold gel and working in smaller sections tends to work better for looser patterns.
