If you're a white parent with a Black or mixed race child and you're trying to figure out how to care for their hair, you're in the right place. This guide doesn't assume you know nothing — it assumes you know a lot about parenting and nothing specific about this hair type, which is the most useful starting point.

The short version: natural Black and mixed race hair needs moisture, gentle handling, and a routine that protects it. It doesn't need to be complicated, and you don't need to have this hair yourself to care for it well. What you need is accurate information, some practice, and the willingness to adjust as you learn what works for your specific child.

---

## The Fundamentals

### Moisture is the main job

Type 4 and coily hair (the pattern most common in Black and mixed heritage children, though this varies) has a spiral structure that means the scalp's natural oils don't travel down the shaft the way they do on straight hair. The hair needs external moisture applied regularly to stay healthy, flexible, and manageable.

Dry hair is hard to manage, hard to detangle, and breaks easily. Most of the practical problems white parents encounter with their Black child's hair — tangles, breakage, difficulty styling — have the same root cause: the hair needs more moisture than it's getting.

### Gentle handling prevents breakage

Natural coily and curly hair is not fragile in the sense of being weak — it's strong. But the curl structure means that tension, friction, and pulling concentrate force at individual points along the shaft rather than distributing it. The result: aggressive brushing, tight styles, and rough drying all cause more breakage than they would on straight hair.

The technique that prevents this: section the hair, work from the ends upward when detangling, use your fingers before a comb, and always work with wet or damp conditioned hair, never dry.

### Regular washing and conditioning

Every seven to ten days is the typical frequency for coily and curly type 4 hair. Less than this and product and scalp buildup accumulates; more than this and the hair is stripped of the oils and moisture it needs between washes.

Between washes: a small spritz of water and leave-in conditioner keeps the hair moisturised without a full wash.

## What You Need

You don't need a cupboard full of products. Start with three:

1. **A gentle shampoo or co-wash** — sulphate-free is ideal; these clean without stripping.
2. **A rinse-out conditioner** — the main detangling tool. Generous amount, fingers-first, wide-tooth comb, always from the ends upward.
3. **A leave-in conditioner or curl cream** — applied to soaking-wet hair after washing to lock in moisture.

Optional but useful:
- A lightweight oil (coconut, jojoba) applied over the leave-in to seal moisture in
- A microfibre towel or old t-shirt for drying without frizz
- A satin bonnet or satin pillowcase for overnight

## UK-Specific: Hard Water

If you're in London, the South East, East Anglia, or the Midlands, your tap water is hard — high in calcium and magnesium. These minerals coat the hair shaft with every wash and make it harder to moisturise and harder to detangle.

Signs of hard water buildup on a child's hair: hair that feels rough or dull despite regular conditioning; conditioner that doesn't seem to do much; buildup that accumulates quickly.

Fix: add a mild chelating shampoo once a month. Look for EDTA or phytic acid on the ingredient list.

## Learning as You Go

### Watch, read, and observe

The most useful sources for learning your child's specific hair care are: Black-owned or Black-authored natural hair communities (UK-based where possible — hair care differs by climate), and your child's own hair. The latter is the most important. Products and techniques that work vary by individual.

This site organises advice by curl type — find your child's approximate pattern and start there. If you're not sure of the type, the 4B and 4C guides are a reasonable starting point for typical Black Caribbean and West African hair textures; 3B and 3C for looser curl patterns common in some mixed heritage children.

→ [Understanding curl types](/hair-care/)

### What to do when you get it wrong

You will. Everyone's early wash days with coily hair, especially when learning, end with the hair more tangled than before or a child in tears. This is not permanent damage in most cases. Wet the hair again, add conditioner, work gently in small sections from the ends. The hair is more forgiving than it seems when wet and conditioned.

### When to ask for help

A professional — a natural hair loctician or a stylist specialising in Afro hair — can show you technique in person in a way that no article can fully replicate. One appointment where you watch someone detangle and style your child's hair is worth more than hours of reading. Many also offer parent tutorials.

## Common Mistakes

### Using products designed for straight hair

Shampoos that lather heavily are usually too stripping for coily hair. Most standard conditioners don't have enough slip for detangling type 4 hair. Look specifically for products designed for natural, curly, or Afro-textured hair.

### Brushing dry hair

Brushing coily or type 4 hair dry causes breakage. Detangle only when the hair is wet and has conditioner in it.

### Tight styles consistently

Braids, twists, and protective styles are great for Black children's hair. Done too tightly, repeatedly, they cause traction alopecia — hair loss at the hairline from repeated tension. Styles should not hurt. The child should not be in pain during installation. The hairline should not look pulled or tented.

### Washing too often or too infrequently

More than weekly: strips moisture. Less than every ten days: buildup accumulates and detangling gets harder.

## The Longer Picture

Hair care for a Black or mixed race child is also part of how they learn to see and relate to their own hair. Children who grow up seeing their natural hair celebrated, cared for, and treated as beautiful carry that. It's worth investing the time to learn, not just because the routine prevents damage, but because the message it sends — that their hair is worth caring for properly — matters.

Some resources that go beyond technique: books by Black authors on natural hair for children, Black-led natural hair communities online, and, where available, natural hair groups for parents in the UK.

## FAQs

**Do I need to learn to do natural hairstyles, or can I just keep it loose?**

Loose is fine for daily care. Protective styles (braids, twists, flat twists, puffs) reduce manipulation and help retain length, but they're not required. Start with what you can do — a well-moisturised, loose puff is a perfectly good hairstyle.

**My child's hair is very knotted. How do I start?**

Saturate the hair with water first. Apply a generous amount of conditioner. Work through the knots with your fingers, section by section, from the very ends upward. Don't try to detangle dry. If it's very matted, this may take more than one session — stop if the child is distressed and come back.

**Is it wrong for me to style my Black child's hair as a white parent?**

Learning to care for your child's hair is one of the most important practical things you can do for them. Doing it thoughtfully — drawing on Black expertise, learning from the community, avoiding styles that cause pain or damage — is the goal. Choosing not to engage because of uncertainty doesn't serve your child.

**My child is starting to have opinions about their own hair. How do I involve them?**

As early as possible. Explain what you're doing and why. Let them choose a product scent. Give them agency over styles as they get older. The earlier they feel ownership of their own hair care, the better they'll manage it independently.
