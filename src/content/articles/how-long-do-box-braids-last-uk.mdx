Box braids typically last four to six weeks before the new growth at the root starts to look fuzzy and the scalp needs attention. Eight weeks is the maximum most people can go without scalp health issues. The style doesn't just "end" — it degrades at the root over time and the scalp gets progressively harder to maintain.

**What you should plan for: four to six weeks.** Eight weeks if the installation was tight and the scalp routine is consistent.

---

## What Actually Determines Duration

### New growth

Hair grows approximately 1–1.5cm per month. After six weeks, that's 1.5–2.5cm of new growth at the root. New growth plus the braid root creates a fuzzy, unravelled appearance at the scalp. This is the main visible reason people remove braids.

### Scalp health

Even with good maintenance, scalp buildup accumulates under braids. Sebum, dry skin, and product residue build up where you can't reach properly. Most people start experiencing significant itching or flaking around weeks five to seven — this is the scalp telling you it needs a proper wash and rest.

### Installation quality

Tight, even installation lasts longer than loose installation because the braid root holds longer before unravelling. This is one reason professional installation from an experienced stylist produces braids that last better than home installation for most people.

### Maintenance

**What extends duration:**
- Scalp moisturising every two to three days
- Scalp washing every two weeks with diluted shampoo
- Satin bonnet at night (reduces frizz at the root)
- Sleeping on satin or silk surface

**What shortens duration:**
- Skipping scalp moisturising (the braid root gets dry and brittle, unravels faster)
- Washing too often or too aggressively (distorts the braid pattern)
- Sleeping on cotton without protection (frizzes the root)
- Swimming without a cap

### UK-specific factors

**Hard water:** washing braids with hard water can leave mineral deposits on the kanekalon hair, making it feel rough and stiff. The braid exterior looks dull faster in hard water areas. If you're washing your scalp while braids are in, diluted ACV rinse after the shampoo rinse helps.

**Central heating:** indoor dry air in UK winter dries the scalp faster under braids. More frequent scalp moisturising (every two days rather than three) compensates.

## The Deterioration Pattern

Most box braid installations follow a predictable pattern:

**Weeks 1–2:** new, tight, best-looking. Possible tension headache in the first 48 hours.

**Weeks 3–4:** settled in, comfortable, good appearance. This is the best period for the style.

**Weeks 5–6:** new growth visible at the root. The scalp may start to itch. The braid root looks slightly fuzzy. Still wearable and presentable.

**Weeks 7–8:** significant new growth. Scalp itching is usually more persistent. The root looks noticeably fuzzy. Reaching the scalp for moisture is harder. Braid removal is appropriate for most scalps by this point.

**Beyond 8 weeks:** the new growth starts to mat into the braid root. Removal becomes significantly harder and causes more breakage. Not recommended.

---

## Refreshing vs Removing

At weeks five to six, some people choose to refresh rather than remove — taking down the braids that have deteriorated the most (typically the hairline and crown) and replacing them. This can extend the overall style by two to three weeks.

Not every salon offers this; ask when booking your initial appointment whether they do refresh appointments.

## FAQs

**My braids only lasted three weeks. What went wrong?**

Either the installation was too loose (the braid root unravelled quickly), the maintenance routine wasn't consistent, or the hair used was poor quality and shed faster than expected. If installation quality is the issue, a different stylist is the answer. If maintenance is the issue, more consistent scalp care and satin at night.

**Can I make braids last longer by not washing?**

The scalp still needs washing regardless of the braid style. Skipping washing doesn't preserve the braids — it accumulates sebum and product buildup that makes the scalp itchy and unhealthy, which shortens how long you can tolerate the braids in. Scalp washing every two weeks maintains the scalp without disturbing the braid pattern.

**My braids look fine but my scalp is driving me crazy. Do I have to take them down?**

Address the scalp first — a scalp wash with diluted shampoo and a good rinse usually resolves persistent itching. If the itching returns within a day or two of washing, the scalp may have a product buildup or scalp condition that needs the braids out to properly treat. Persistent itching that doesn't respond to washing is a signal the braids have been in too long.

**Someone told me I should grease my scalp while braids are in. Is that right?**

Heavy grease (petroleum-based) is not ideal — it accumulates at the root and is difficult to wash out through braids. A lightweight oil (jojoba, argan) or a water-based scalp spray is better. Moisture at the scalp is the goal; heavy grease provides a seal but limited moisture.
