Some of the most popular styles for natural hair are also the ones most likely to cause breakage and thinning over time. That's an uncomfortable thing to say directly, but it's the honest verdict — and knowing it lets you make better choices about frequency, technique, and when to rest.

---

## Tight Braids and Cornrows

**The problem:** braids and cornrows installed with excessive tension at the root create traction on the follicle. The hairline is most vulnerable — finer hair, less density, more exposed to tight installation at the edges.

**The damage:** traction alopecia. Gradual hairline recession and edge thinning that starts with tension headaches after installation and progresses to visible loss over months and years of repeated tight installs.

**Who is most at risk:** people who install braids at home or at salons with high tension, or who wear very small braids (micro braids, some knotless braids) that multiply the traction points across the entire head.

**What to do:**
- The braids should feel secure, not painful, during and after installation
- The skin at your temples and hairline should not be visibly pulled during installation
- Take braids down if significant pain persists past 48 hours
- Rest the hairline between installs — at least one week, ideally two

---

## Lace Front Wigs With Hairline Adhesive

**The problem:** adhesive applied repeatedly to the hairline bonds to the fine edge hairs as well as the skin. Removal — even with a dedicated adhesive remover — creates mechanical stress on the fragile hairline hair each time.

**The damage:** progressive hairline thinning that mimics traction alopecia but from a chemical/mechanical rather than style-tension cause. The frontal hairline is most commonly affected.

**Who is most at risk:** people who wear lace fronts daily or several times a week with adhesive each time.

**What to do:**
- Use glueless lace fronts where possible
- If adhesive is necessary, apply to the wig's lace, not directly to the skin and hair
- Use a dedicated adhesive remover and don't rush the process
- Take breaks from adhesive — glueless wear periods allow the hairline to recover

---

## Constant High Ponytails and Tight Buns

**The problem:** pulling the hair back to the same point repeatedly creates traction at that specific point on the hairline. High ponytails that pull tightly at the temples are particularly damaging.

**The damage:** thinning at the temples and sides of the hairline. A tight bun worn daily creates a traction ring at the point where the elastic sits.

**Who is most at risk:** people who wear their hair in the same tight style most days as a default — common for people who want a simple, low-maintenance daily look.

**What to do:**
- Vary the position of ponytails and buns (high today, low tomorrow, to the side the next day)
- Use satin scrunchies rather than rubber bands — rubber bands cut into the hair and cause breakage at the contact point
- Loosen the pull — the hair doesn't need to be pulled taut to look neat
- Give the hair days in loose or protective styles rather than pulled back constantly

## Sleeping Without Protective Covering

This is not a hairstyle but it's the most pervasive breakage cause. Cotton pillowcases create friction on the hair shaft for seven to eight hours every night. On natural coily hair with multiple bends and angles, this friction is significant.

**The damage:** end breakage, frizz, and gradual damage to the cuticle layer over months and years.

**What to do:** a satin bonnet or satin pillowcase. This is not optional if breakage is a concern.

## Extensions That Are Too Heavy

**The problem:** extension hair significantly heavier than the natural hair creates traction at the attachment point. Box braids with very long or thick extensions, locs extensions, heavy faux locs — the weight pulls continuously at the root.

**The damage:** thinning at the attachment points, particularly at the nape and hairline where the hair is naturally finer.

**Who is most at risk:** people with fine or shorter natural hair who choose very long or heavy extension styles.

**What to do:** choose extension size and length proportional to the natural hair's density and strength. The extension shouldn't feel noticeably heavy — if it does, it's too much for the natural hair to bear.

---

## Wet Hair in Tight Styles

**The problem:** wet hair is more elastic than dry hair — it stretches further before snapping. Putting wet natural hair into a tight braid or ponytail, then allowing it to dry under tension, puts the hair under sustained stress at the elastic maximum for the drying period.

**The damage:** breakage at the tension point as the hair dries and contracts against the tight style.

**What to do:** style on dry or mostly-dry hair. If you must put wet hair up, use a very loose style that won't create tension as it dries.

## FAQs

**If tight braids cause damage, should I stop getting them?**

Not necessarily — the damage is from tight installation, not from braids as a style. Well-installed braids with appropriate tension are protective. Ask your stylist specifically about hairline tension; choose knotless braids for lower root tension; give the hair rest periods between installs.

**I've worn high ponytails my whole life. Is it too late to recover?**

If the damage is moderate — thinning at the temples — recovery is possible by removing the source of tension and resting the area. If the thinning is severe and has been ongoing for many years, some follicles may be permanently affected. A trichologist assessment gives you the clearest picture.

**What's the safest way to wear my hair up?**

A loose pineapple or bun with a satin scrunchie, positioned differently each day. Not pulled taut; not in the same position daily. The looser and more varied the position, the lower the traction.

**I have to wear my hair up for work. What do I do?**

A low, loose bun rather than a high tight one. A satin scrunchie rather than a rubber band. Varying the position day to day. The goal is minimising traction at any single point — distributed, loose, rotated styles cause significantly less damage than the same tight style every day.
