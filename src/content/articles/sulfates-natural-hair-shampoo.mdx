The natural hair community treats sulphates as a category to avoid. The accurate position is more specific: sodium lauryl sulphate (SLS) is harsh enough to strip natural coily hair of moisture on regular use. Other sulphates — sodium laureth sulphate (SLES), sodium coco sulphate, ammonium laureth sulphate — are significantly milder and work fine for most natural hair types on regular use.

The blanket avoidance of all sulphates means some people are avoiding products that would work perfectly well for them, and others are using "sulphate-free" products that don't cleanse effectively enough to prevent buildup.

---

## The Sulphates That Cause Problems

**Sodium Lauryl Sulphate (SLS):** the harshest common shampoo sulphate. It's an effective cleanser — it removes oils, buildup, and product — but it also strips the hair of the natural oils that coily hair already struggles to retain. On type 4 hair used regularly (weekly), SLS produces the dry, rough, stripped feel after washing that most people associate with sulphate shampoos generally.

**Position on the SLS verdict:** avoid SLS in regular shampoos for type 4 hair. It's appropriate for an occasional clarifying wash (quarterly) where stripping power is actually what you need. As a weekly shampoo it's too aggressive.

---

## The Sulphates That Are Fine

**Sodium Laureth Sulphate (SLES):** sulphated and ethoxylated, which makes it significantly milder than SLS. Cleanses effectively without the same level of moisture strip. Many "curly hair" shampoos that aren't marketed as sulphate-free still use SLES as their primary cleanser and perform well on natural hair.

**Sodium Coco Sulphate:** derived from coconut oil. Mild, cleanses without significant stripping. Often found in natural-leaning shampoos. Fine for regular use.

**Ammonium Laureth Sulphate:** similar mildness level to SLES. Works well for normal to dry hair types.

**Sodium Lauroyl Methyl Isethionate / Sodium Lauroyl Isethionate:** these are often listed alongside sulphates as cleansing agents but are actually isethionates — a different chemistry, even milder than SLES, and technically not sulphates at all. Very gentle, appear in many "sulphate-free" products as the actual cleanser.

---

## Reading the Label in Practice

When checking a shampoo:

- **"Sodium Lauryl Sulphate" or "SLS" near the top:** avoid for regular use on natural hair
- **"Sodium Laureth Sulphate" or "SLES":** fine for regular use
- **"Sulphate-free" on the label:** check what the actual cleanser is — many use isethionates or mild coco-derived surfactants. If the cleanser is near the bottom of the ingredient list, the shampoo may not cleanse effectively enough to prevent buildup

## The Sulphate-Free Problem

The natural hair community moved toward sulphate-free shampoos to avoid SLS stripping. The side effect is that some sulphate-free shampoos don't cleanse effectively enough — they remove light buildup but not heavy product buildup, not sebum accumulation, and not mineral deposits from hard water.

For natural hair in the UK on a sulphate-free routine:

- **Monthly clarifying wash** is necessary even without SLS in the routine — a sulphate-free clarifying wash (like As I Am Curl Clarity) or an occasional SLES-based shampoo used quarterly resets the hair
- **Monthly chelating wash** in hard water areas (London, SE, East Anglia, East Midlands) to remove mineral deposits that a sulphate-free routine leaves behind
- **Co-washing between wash days** only works if there's a proper clarifying wash on the rotation — co-washing alone doesn't cleanse

## Who Should Be More Flexible About Sulphates

**Low porosity hair:** the flat cuticle that makes moisture uptake difficult also makes product and sebum removal harder. Low porosity hair often needs a more effective cleanse than mild sulphate-free shampoos provide. An SLES-based shampoo used regularly is appropriate for low porosity hair.

**Hair worn in wash-and-go styles weekly:** high product use (gel, cream, leave-in) accumulates quickly. A more effective cleanser is needed than for hair mostly in protective styles with low product use between washes.

**Scalp-focused routines:** the scalp is skin and produces sebum regardless of hair type. A sulphate-free co-wash doesn't adequately cleanse the scalp. A shampoo — SLES or SLS-free-but-genuinely-cleansing — is needed at least fortnightly.

## FAQs

**I switched to a sulphate-free shampoo and my hair feels better. Wasn't I right to avoid sulphates?**

You avoided SLS stripping, which is real. But if the shampoo you switched to uses SLES or an isethionate cleanser, you're not fully sulphate-free — you just switched from a harsh sulphate to a mild one. That's the right outcome; the label is slightly misleading.

**My sulphate-free shampoo doesn't seem to remove buildup. What should I do?**

Use a genuine clarifying wash quarterly. As I Am Curl Clarity is sulphate-free and more cleansing than a standard sulphate-free moisturising shampoo. Or use a monthly SLES-based shampoo as your clarifying step — the mild sulphate is appropriate for a monthly cleanse.

**Can I just use SLS shampoo if I always deep condition after?**

Weekly SLS followed by deep conditioning is better than SLS weekly without conditioning, but you're fighting a cycle of strip-and-restore that a milder cleanser would avoid entirely. Switch to SLES or a sulphate-free option for weekly use; reserve SLS for a quarterly clarifying wash.

**Are "sulphate-free" shampoos really sulphate-free?**

Not always. "Sulphate-free" on a UK cosmetics label means no SLS or SLES — but sodium coco sulphate, for example, is technically a sulphate and appears in some "sulphate-free" products. It's mild enough that this usually doesn't matter, but it's worth knowing that "sulphate-free" is a marketing category, not a strictly regulated claim.
