Natural hair routines are hard enough to maintain in a single household. Across two households with different levels of knowledge, different products, and possibly different views on what matters, they're harder. The child's hair often shows it.

This guide is practical. It doesn't take sides. It's aimed at getting to an arrangement that keeps the child's hair healthy across both homes.

---

## Why Co-Parenting Hair Routines Are Difficult

**Different knowledge levels.** If one parent grew up with this hair type and the other didn't, the knowledge gap is real. The parent who doesn't know what to do isn't necessarily unwilling — they may just not know.

**Different priorities.** One household may prioritise elaborate styles and a full wash day routine; the other may see it as less important. Neither position is inherently wrong, but the hair experiences both.

**Tension between households.** If the co-parenting relationship is already difficult, hair care becomes another battleground. The child's hair is caught between two adults.

**The child returning in a state.** Hair that's been done up, compressed, or not moisturised for a week returning to the other parent requiring hours of work. The frustration is valid; the response to it shapes what happens next.

---

## What the Research Says (the Short Version)

Natural coily and curly hair needs consistent moisture and gentle handling to stay healthy. Inconsistent care — well-maintained in one home, neglected in the other — doesn't produce stable results. The hair's condition reflects the least-maintained environment.

This is not about which parent cares more. It's about the hair type's requirements.

---

## Getting to a Workable Arrangement

### Start with what the child needs, not what you want

The conversation is most productive when it stays focused on the child's hair health rather than on what each parent is or isn't doing. "Here's what the hair needs to stay in good condition" is a more neutral starting point than "you're damaging her hair."

The basics for most natural curly/coily hair:
- Wash every seven to ten days
- Condition and detangle with conditioner in, fingers-first from the ends
- Apply leave-in to soaking-wet hair after washing
- Moisture maintenance every one to two days between washes
- Satin bonnet or pillowcase at night

These are not demanding requirements. They are the minimum that keeps the hair manageable and prevents significant setback between visits.

### Reduce the dependency on both parents doing it the same way

The more the routine can be simplified and the more the child can do themselves, the less the inter-household variation matters. A twelve-year-old who can do their own wash day does it wherever they are.

For younger children, focus on the non-negotiables: moisturise between washes, satin at night, don't brush dry. Everything else is secondary.

### A shared product set at both homes

Agreeing on one or two products that are used in both homes removes the "the products aren't here" problem and creates more consistency. Split the cost or rotate buying. A simple leave-in conditioner and one gel or curl cream is enough.

### Written routine for the child

A short, simple document — or notes in an app — that describes the routine for the child's hair. Not addressed to the other parent; addressed to whoever is helping with hair. Age-appropriate if the child can use it themselves. This is useful for any situation where the primary caregiver isn't present: the other parent, a grandparent, a childminder.

## When One Parent Understands and the Other Doesn't

If the knowledge gap is significant, a practical session is worth more than any number of written instructions.

A stylist appointment where both parents attend — if that's possible — or a video call where one parent walks through the routine with the other while the child's hair is being done. Seeing it done is much more effective than reading about it.

If direct communication is not possible due to the state of the co-parenting relationship, a neutral third party — a family member, a mediator, or a family support worker — can sometimes broker this.

---

## When the Routine Is Being Ignored

If the hair is consistently returning in poor condition despite agreement on a routine, this is harder.

Options, roughly in order of effort:
1. Document what the hair looks like before and after visits, without making it confrontational
2. Raise it through a co-parenting communication channel (app, mediator) as a child wellbeing issue
3. If there are existing family proceedings, raise it with a solicitor as a factual matter about care needs
4. Ask a GP or health visitor to note the child's hair condition as part of a broader welfare discussion

These escalations are for situations where the child's hair health is genuinely suffering, not for disagreements about style preference.

## FAQs

**My ex refuses to engage with the hair routine. What can I do?**

Focus on what you can control: how the hair is cared for in your home, and making sure the child can care for their own hair as they get older. A teenager who can do their own wash day doesn't depend on either parent.

**The child comes back from the other home with severe tangles every time. How do I handle this practically?**

A warm conditioner detangle session on arrival, without comment in front of the child. Address the pattern with the other parent separately, calmly, and in terms of the child's comfort during detangling — not hair aesthetics.

**We've agreed on the routine but they use different products. Does that matter?**

Less than you'd think, if the products are broadly appropriate. A different leave-in or a different curl cream doesn't undo a good routine. Focus on the process (moisture, gentle detangling, protection at night) rather than the specific products.

**The other parent wants to put the child's hair in tight braids that I think are too tight. What's the right call?**

Tight braids over a sustained period cause traction alopecia — hair loss at the hairline from repeated tension. This is a legitimate hair health concern, not a style preference. If you can see hairline thinning or the child is reporting pain during installation, that's worth raising as a medical concern rather than a style argument.
