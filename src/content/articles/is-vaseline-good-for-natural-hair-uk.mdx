Vaseline (petroleum jelly) is not inherently good or bad for natural hair. It's a very strong occlusive — it creates a barrier that almost nothing gets through, including moisture. That makes it useful for one thing (sealing in moisture that's already there) and counterproductive for another (applying to dry hair, or to the scalp).

The community debates this more than almost any product, and both camps are partly right.

## What Vaseline actually does

Petroleum jelly is a mineral-derived occlusive. It doesn't moisturise, condition, treat, or penetrate the hair shaft. What it does is form a coating on the surface that's extremely resistant to water evaporation. If your hair has adequate moisture in it, Vaseline can keep it there for longer than lighter oils. If your hair is dry when you apply it, you're sealing in dryness.

It also doesn't wash out easily. Sulphate shampoo removes it. Co-washes and sulphate-free cleansers don't, or not completely, which means repeated use without adequate cleansing creates build-up that's hard to shift.

## When it's useful

**Sealing ends on protective styles.** A very small amount on the tips of box braids, twists, or cornrows — where the hair is most exposed to friction — slows moisture loss. This is the application where it's hardest to argue against.

**Edge laying.** The texture of petroleum jelly works well for slicking edges, and it holds longer than most edge products. For occasional use, this is fine. For daily use on already stressed edges — particularly where tension from styles has thinned the hairline — the build-up risk and the occlusive effect on the follicle area are a reason for caution.

**Cold weather.** In genuinely cold, dry conditions, a thin layer over the hair shaft before going outside reduces wind and cold damage. This is the greenhouse effect in miniature — seal in whatever moisture is there before stepping out.

**Sealing the ends before swimming.** A barrier of petroleum jelly on the ends before entering a chlorinated pool reduces chemical uptake.

## When it's not useful

**Low-porosity hair.** Low-porosity hair already has tightly closed cuticles that resist moisture entry. A heavy occlusive on top means products applied afterwards can't get in at all. If you have low-porosity hair, petroleum jelly as a sealant is likely to make your hair feel coated and unresponsive.

**The scalp.** Petroleum jelly on the scalp can block follicles. This is a real risk with daily application. If you're applying it to the scalp — a common practice in traditional West African and Black British hair care — limit frequency and cleanse thoroughly between applications.

**As a moisturiser.** It isn't one. If your hair is dry, Vaseline will not fix that. You need water-based moisture first.

**Before a deep conditioner.** If you apply petroleum jelly and then try to condition over it, the occlusive barrier reduces absorption. Oil after water, not before.

## How to use it if you're going to

Apply to slightly damp hair (not soaking wet, but not dry). Use the smallest amount that does the job — a small amount goes a long way and a lot is very hard to wash out. Use it on ends and mid-lengths, not the scalp. Clarify properly every three to four weeks to prevent build-up.

## The honest position

Vaseline is not a wonder product and it's not harmful. It's a sealant tool — effective in its specific lane, counterproductive outside of it. People who swear by it and people who swear against it are both describing their own hair type and application method. Neither camp is universally right.
