Braids that look great at installation and deteriorate within two weeks almost always fail for the same reasons: the scalp isn't being moisturised, the hair is sleeping on cotton, or the scalp has gone unwashed long enough to build up to the point of itching and flaking. The fixes are simple and take a few minutes every couple of days.

---

## The Non-Negotiable Basics

### Satin at night

Cotton pillowcases create friction against the braid root. Over nights, this frizzes the root faster than any other factor. A satin bonnet, satin pillowcase, or satin-lined sleep cap is the single change with the most impact on how long braids look good.

**For large braids that don't fit in a bonnet:** a large satin bonnet over the front hairline, with the braid length loose behind. Or a satin pillowcase covering at least the hairline.

**For box braids:** a pineapple (all braids gathered loosely at the top of the head with a satin scrunchie) keeps them off the pillow surface and extends the style.

### Scalp moisture every two to three days

The natural hair under braids still needs moisture. The scalp still produces sebum — but you can't brush it through the length. Moisture has to come from what you apply to the scalp directly.

**What works:**
- A spray bottle with water and a small amount of leave-in conditioner, applied directly to the scalp between braids or rows
- A lightweight scalp oil (jojoba, argan, sweet almond) applied with a dropper or nozzle to the parting

**What doesn't work:**
- Heavy butters or pomades applied to the braid length — they accumulate on the synthetic hair and create buildup at the root
- Petroleum-based grease at the scalp — seals without providing moisture; accumulates

**UK winter adjustment:** central heating dry air draws moisture from the scalp faster. Every two days rather than three during the heating season.

### Scalp washing every two weeks

Braids trap sebum, sweat, dry skin, and product residue at the scalp. This accumulates into the buildup that causes itching and flaking — the main reason people take braids down earlier than needed.

Diluted shampoo applied to the scalp with a small squeeze bottle or applicator, massaged in with fingertip pads (not nails), rinsed thoroughly. Dry fully.

This does not disturb the braid pattern if done carefully — the shampoo cleanses the scalp without reaching the braid structure meaningfully.

## Extending Specific Styles

### Box braids

- Sleep in a pineapple — loose, high, satin scrunchie
- Refresh frizzy roots with a diluted gel spritz (water and a small amount of flaxseed gel) and smooth with fingers
- Avoid heavy styling products on the braid length — they accumulate

### Cornrows and Ghana braids

- An edge control or light gel along the rows in the morning keeps them looking neat
- The edge control should be water-based, not petroleum-based
- Satin bonnet that covers the full cornrow pattern, not just the front

### Micro braids

- More susceptible to frizz at the root because of the fine sections
- Satin bonnet is more important, not less
- Scalp washing is harder to do without disturbing the fine braids — a very gentle scalp wash with diluted shampoo applied carefully, rinsed carefully

## Hard Water and Braids in the UK

In hard water areas, washing the scalp while braids are in deposits minerals on the extension hair over time. The kanekalon synthetic hair becomes rough, stiff, and dull faster than it would in soft water.

Two approaches:
1. **ACV rinse after washing:** diluted apple cider vinegar (1 part ACV to 4 parts water) poured over the braids after shampoo, left briefly, then rinsed. Reduces surface mineral deposits.
2. **Filtered water for rinse:** a shower filter significantly reduces water hardness and is the longer-term solution.

## Refreshing vs Taking Down

At weeks four to five, many braid styles can be refreshed rather than taken down entirely. The most deteriorated braids (often the hairline, the nape, and the crown) can be removed and replaced while the rest stay in. This extends the style by two to three weeks.

Not every stylist offers refresh appointments — ask when booking the initial installation whether they do.

---

## Braid Removal: Preventing Breakage

**The most common mistake in braid removal is rushing.** Rushing causes pulling, which causes breakage.

1. Cut the braid end to reduce weight (if extension hair is long)
2. Saturate the root area with a conditioner or detangling spray
3. Unravel from the very bottom of the braid upward — never from the root down
4. Work in sections; detangle each section before moving to the next
5. Do not pull through a knot — add more conditioner and work through it

After removal: wash, deep condition, allow the hair to rest before the next install.

## FAQs

**My braids always look good the first week and deteriorate fast after that. What's the pattern?**

The first week is installation tension holding everything together. After that, the maintenance routine determines how the style holds. If week two onward goes downhill, the scalp isn't being moisturised consistently enough and/or the night protection is missing. Add both and compare the next install.

**Can I exercise with braids in?**

Yes. Secure the braids in a high bun or ponytail for workouts. After exercise, spritz the scalp with water, apply a small amount of scalp oil, and if the roots got sweaty and are now frizzy, smooth with a light gel.

**The synthetic hair smells after a few weeks. What do I do?**

This is product and sweat accumulation on the extension hair, combined with inadequate drying after washing. A diluted shampoo wash followed by thorough drying usually resolves it. If the smell returns quickly after washing, the scalp washing frequency needs to increase.

**Should I oil my braids themselves?**

Light oils on the exterior of the braid can reduce dullness. A very small amount of argan or jojoba oil worked over the palm and smoothed over the braid length once a week is fine. Not heavy oils; not inside the braid. The goal is surface sheen, not soaking the braid.
