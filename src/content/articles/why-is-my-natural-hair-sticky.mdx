Sticky natural hair almost always comes from one of three sources: glycerin or humectants pulling moisture from the air, product build-up that hasn't been cleared properly, or a product that wasn't fully absorbed and is sitting on the surface of the hair shaft.

## Glycerin and humectants

Glycerin is in most natural hair products because it's an effective humectant — it draws moisture from the atmosphere into your hair. In moderate humidity, that's useful. In high humidity (a British summer, or an unusually humid day), it draws too much moisture and hair becomes sticky and swollen.

Other humectants do the same thing: aloe vera, honey, agave, propylene glycol. If your stickiness happens in warm or humid weather but not in winter, glycerin or another humectant is the likely cause.

**Fix:** In summer or in humid conditions, use products that are lower in glycerin (check the ingredients list — glycerin lower in the list means less of it). Products marketed as "humidity-resistant" often reformulate to reduce humectant content.

## Product build-up

If your hair is sticky even on day one after washing, and the stickiness doesn't vary with humidity, you're likely dealing with build-up. Silicones, some waxes, and certain oils accumulate on the shaft over time and create a tacky layer that products can't penetrate properly.

**Fix:** Clarify with a sulphate or clarifying shampoo. If you've been using a silicone-containing product, you need sulphates to remove it — co-washes and conditioner-only washing won't clear silicones. After clarifying, assess whether your routine is using silicone-heavy products regularly, and whether those need to be reduced.

## Too much product

Applying more product than your hair can absorb leaves the excess sitting on top of the strand. This is particularly common with thick butters and heavy creams on fine or low-porosity natural hair — the hair simply can't take in that much product, and the rest stays on the surface.

**Fix:** Use less. Apply in smaller sections, work the product in properly, and see if the stickiness reduces. Low-porosity hair generally does better with lighter products (liquids and light creams rather than butters) applied to very damp hair while the shaft is still open from the shower.

## A note on hard water

In hard water areas, mineral deposits and product residue interact and can create a tacky film on the hair that's different from normal build-up and doesn't always respond to regular clarifying. A chelating shampoo (specifically formulated to remove mineral deposits) once a month, followed by a clarifying wash, addresses this layer separately.

## Quick diagnosis

- Sticky in summer / humid weather but not winter → glycerin or humectants
- Sticky regardless of weather, worse over time → build-up
- Sticky immediately after washing with fresh products → too much product, or wrong product weight for your hair type
