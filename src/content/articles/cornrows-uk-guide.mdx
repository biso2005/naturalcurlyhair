Cornrows are braids that run flat against the scalp in continuous rows, braided using an underhand technique. They're one of the oldest and most culturally significant hairstyles in Afro and Black heritage — and practically, one of the most versatile and lower-tension protective styles available.

They're also the foundation of almost every other braided style: box braids, feed-in braids, and crochet installs all often begin with cornrows underneath.

---

## What Cornrows Cost in the UK

**Typical range: £40–£120.** Variation by:
- **Number and size of rows:** six straight-back cornrows is quick; an intricate pattern with curved parts and varying sizes takes much longer
- **Style complexity:** straight back vs freestyle curved designs vs patterns
- **Extensions or no extensions:** cornrows on natural hair only cost less than feed-in cornrows with added kanekalon
- **Location:** London salons charge more than salons outside London

A standard six straight-back cornrow appointment takes one to two hours. Intricate designs with extensions can take four to six hours.

---

## Styles

**Straight back:** the most common — rows running from front to back. Low manipulation, classic, works on most hair lengths.

**Side swept:** rows angled toward one side. Works well for framing the face.

**Curved and patterned:** freestyle designs, swirling patterns, geometric shapes. More skill required; longer installation; more expensive.

**Feed-in cornrows:** extension hair added gradually as the braid progresses, making the cornrow thicker and longer. Creates box braids (when the extension extends beyond the natural hair) or just fuller cornrows. The same lower-tension feed-in technique as knotless braids.

**Cornrow base for protective styles:** many crochet installs and weave installs use a cornrow base. The cornrows hold the install; the crochet or weft sits over the top.

---

## How Long Cornrows Last

**On natural hair without extensions:** two to three weeks before significant frizzing and new growth distorts the pattern.

**With extension hair:** three to four weeks, sometimes five for tight, well-installed rows.

The hairline is always the first area to frizz — the fine baby hairs don't hold as long as the hair behind them.

---

## Tension and the Hairline

Cornrows have a different tension profile from box braids — they're braided flat against the scalp rather than hanging with the weight of extension hair. But the edges and hairline still experience tension from the starting point of each row.

The hairline should not be braided so tight that the skin is visibly stretched or lifted. If this is happening, say something during the appointment. Tight edges are the most common cause of traction alopecia from cornrows, and it accumulates over repeated installations.

## Care While In

**Scalp moisture every two to three days:** a lightweight oil or scalp spray applied between the rows to the scalp. Jojoba or a diluted leave-in spray applied with a dropper or nozzle bottle directly to the part.

**Satin at night:** a bonnet or satin pillowcase. Cornrows frizz significantly from cotton friction.

**Scalp washing every two weeks:** diluted shampoo applied to the scalp and rows, massaged in gently, rinsed thoroughly. Dry fully.

**In the morning:** a light spritz of water to smooth any frizz that's developed, then a very light gel or edge control along the rows.

---

## UK-Specific Considerations

**Hard water:** the same mineral buildup issue as all styles washed with hard water. An ACV rinse after washing helps reduce surface buildup on the cornrow.

**Winter moisture:** central heating dry air means the scalp loses moisture faster. Every two days rather than three for scalp oil during autumn and winter months.

**School:** cornrows are a legitimate, protected hairstyle under the Equality Act 2010. Any school policy that specifically restricts cornrows for Black pupils while permitting equivalent styles for other pupils is discriminatory.

## Removal

Cornrows without extension hair: unravel each row from the end upward. Apply conditioner as you go. Detangle each section before moving to the next.

With extension hair: find where the extension hair begins (typically a few centimetres from the root) and separate it from the natural hair. Remove the extension portion first, then unravel the cornrow from the bottom up with conditioner.

After removal: wash, deep condition, and allow the natural hair to rest before installing another protective style.

## FAQs

**How do I find a good cornrow stylist in the UK?**

Look at their portfolio specifically for cornrows — not just any braid work. Ask whether they do the style you want (straight back, curved, feed-in). Ask about their tension approach at the hairline. Personal recommendations from people whose cornrows you've seen are the most reliable indicator.

**Can I cornrow my own hair?**

Yes, with practice. Self-cornrowing is one of the most useful skills for natural hair and many people with medium to dense type 4 hair find it manageable. The difficulty increases with finer hair types and shorter hair. There are many UK-produced tutorials — look for ones that match your hair type and length.

**My edges are thin from previous tight cornrows. Can I still get cornrows?**

Allow the edges to recover first — visible regrowth and no traction pain. When you return to cornrows, ask specifically for loose installation at the hairline and avoid any style that starts with a tight edge.

**The cornrow parting shows my scalp. Is that normal?**

Yes. The part of each cornrow section is visible because the hair is braided flat rather than hanging as a unit. This is part of the style. The scalp showing at the part is normal; it's not the same as thinning.
