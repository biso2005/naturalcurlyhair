## Why Won't My 4C Hair Grow?

Your 4C hair almost certainly *is* growing — at around 0.9 cm a month. The problem is that it is not **retaining** that length. Every bend in a 4C coil is a potential breaking point, and if your ends are snapping off as fast as your roots are growing, your length stays the same. That is a retention failure, not a growth failure — and it is fixable.

The fix for retention is completely different from the fix for a growth problem. This article works through both, with particular attention to conditions in the UK that US-written guides consistently ignore. If you are not sure of your hair's porosity — which shapes which retention strategies will work — read our guide to [finding your hair porosity](/hair-care/hair-porosity-test-natural-hair-uk/) first.

## First, Understand How 4C Hair Actually Grows

### Growth vs. length retention — why they're not the same thing

Hair growth happens at the follicle, beneath the scalp. Barring a medical condition, your follicles are producing new hair continuously. The anagen (active growth) phase of the hair cycle determines how long a strand can potentially grow before it sheds naturally — and this phase is largely genetic.

Length retention is entirely different. It describes how much of the hair that has grown you actually *keep* on your head. 4C hair — with its tightly coiled, Z-shaped curl pattern — is structurally more vulnerable to mechanical damage than looser curl types. Each bend in the strand is a potential breaking point. If your ends are snapping off at roughly the same rate your roots are growing, your hair will appear to stay the same length indefinitely. That is not a growth failure. It is a retention failure, and it is fixable.

### What a realistic growth rate looks like for 4C hair

African hair grows at a measurably slower rate than Caucasian or Asian hair — around 0.9 cm a month versus 1.2–1.3 cm, according to Loussouarn et al.'s 2001 study of scalp hair growth parameters across ethnicities (*British Journal of Dermatology*, 2001, 145:294–297. doi:10.1046/j.1365-2133.2001.04350.x). But even at that slower rate, that is roughly 11 cm of new growth a year. If your length is not changing, the gap between that 11 cm and your mirror is the retention problem — your hair is breaking off at the ends at roughly the rate it is growing at the root.

What also varies is how that growth presents. Because 4C hair shrinks dramatically when dry — often 70–80% of its actual length — many people genuinely cannot see how much length they have. Measuring stretched, dry hair from root to end on a consistent date each month is the most reliable way to track real growth.

## The Real Reasons Your 4C Hair Isn't Retaining Length

### Breakage at the ends (the main culprit)

The oldest parts of your hair live at the ends. They have been through every wash day, every style, every night without a bonnet, every rummage in a wool-lined hood. End breakage is the single most common reason 4C hair appears not to grow.

Signs of end breakage rather than normal shedding: short broken pieces (not full strands with a white bulb at the root) collecting in your comb or on your clothing, and ends that look tapered, wispy, or uneven when the hair is stretched.

The primary interventions are:

- **Consistent moisture** — dry, brittle ends break. 4C hair needs regular sealing after moisturising.
- **Protective styling** — keeping ends tucked away reduces daily friction and exposure.
- **Trimming** — counterintuitive, but removing split and damaged ends prevents the split from travelling up the shaft and causing more breakage.
- **Protein treatments used judiciously** — protein strengthens the hair shaft, but over-use makes hair brittle. A balance of protein and moisture is the goal. There is no peer-reviewed frequency trial for protein treatment use on 4C hair specifically; monthly or as needed based on how your hair responds (stiffness, loss of elasticity) is the working guidance.

### Wet hair is vulnerable hair

Wet hair is mechanically weaker than dry hair — the internal bonds that give dry hair its strength are temporarily loosened by water and reform as it dries. Handling wet hair roughly (aggressive detangling, tight styling on freshly washed hair, tugging through knots) is where the damage actually happens.

The popular term "hygral fatigue" implies a swelling-damage cycle that is not well supported by current evidence — the concern is not the wetting itself but what you do to the hair while it is wet. If your hair feels mushy, stretches without springing back, or breaks when you handle it wet, the priority is gentler handling and — if the hair is consistently weak — a protein treatment to reinforce the shaft structure before the next wash.

### Protective styles worn too long

Protective styles — braids, twists, cornrows, wigs — are genuinely useful for retaining length. The problem comes when they are left in beyond their useful life. After roughly six to eight weeks, most installation-based styles begin to cause more damage than they prevent — this figure is widely cited by trichologists and Afro hair practitioners, though no controlled study defines an exact safe threshold. The practical signs matter more than the calendar: new growth matting at the root, visible scalp tension or soreness, and hair that has not been moisturised inside the style are all signals to take down regardless of how long it has been in.

- New growth at the root mats and tangles with the old hair
- Tension at the hairline can cause traction alopecia over time
- The hair inside the style dries out without regular moisturising access

Take down styles carefully, section by section, with plenty of slip from a conditioner or detangling product. Never pull or rush.

### Hard water damage (especially relevant in the UK)

This is a factor that virtually every US-written guide on this topic ignores — but it is highly relevant if you live in England, particularly in the south and east. We cover the mechanism and UK regional data in detail in [Why Is My 4C Hair Always Dry?](/hair-care/why-is-my-4c-hair-always-dry-uk/) — here is the retention-specific summary.

Hard water contains elevated levels of dissolved calcium and magnesium minerals. When hard water dries on your hair, it leaves a mineral deposit on the shaft. Over time, this build-up:

- Coats the hair cuticle and prevents moisture from penetrating
- Makes the hair feel rough, dull, and more prone to tangling
- Reduces the effectiveness of conditioners and styling products

The Thames catchment — including London and the South East — sits on chalk and limestone. Thames Water and Affinity Water consistently report hardness in the range of 250–350 mg/L CaCO₃, with some eastern zones touching 400 mg/L. Greater Manchester sits around 60–80 mg/L; Wales and the North West are generally soft, many areas below 50 mg/L. ([TapWater.uk, sourced from Drinking Water Inspectorate data](https://www.tapwater.uk/guides/water-hardness-map)) If you are in London or the South East and your hair changed when you moved there, the water is the first thing to investigate.

A **chelating shampoo** — one formulated with EDTA or similar chelating agents — chemically binds and removes mineral ions from the hair shaft. It is distinct from a clarifying shampoo, which removes product build-up with higher-surfactant formulas but does not remove dissolved minerals. Monthly or every six to eight weeks is a reasonable starting frequency; no peer-reviewed trial defines the safe interval for 4C hair specifically, so adjust based on how your hair responds rather than a fixed schedule.

A **shower head water filter** can reduce what your hair is exposed to during washing. However, most filters marketed in the UK — including KDF and carbon/activated carbon types — are designed to remove chlorine, not calcium or magnesium. The only filter type that actually reduces dissolved water hardness is one containing **ion exchange resin** (the packaging should explicitly name "cation exchange resin" and state calcium or magnesium reduction capacity). Filters that use only KDF, vitamin C, or carbon media will not soften the water. At London hardness levels, a compact ion exchange cartridge typically requires replacement every one to three months. No UK consumer body has independently validated shower filter performance claims for hard water reduction.

### Manipulation and detangling habits

Every time you touch 4C hair — especially dry 4C hair — you risk breakage. High-manipulation styles (re-doing your hair daily, detangling aggressively, using fine-tooth combs on dry hair) add up.

Practical adjustments:

- Detangle only on wet, well-conditioned hair, working from ends to roots
- Use a wide-tooth seamless comb or your fingers — seamless means no seam running down the tooth of the comb that can snag and tear the hair
- Reduce how often you fully re-style; stretched styles that can be refreshed with water and product rather than fully redone are gentler

### Diet and deficiencies common in the UK (vitamin D, iron)

Hair growth and shedding can be affected by nutritional deficiencies. Two are particularly worth noting in the UK context.

**Vitamin D deficiency** is prevalent across the UK population, and disproportionately so in people with melanated skin. If you are in the UK with melanated skin, the NHS recommends a daily 10 microgram vitamin D supplement year-round — not just over winter. The UK Biobank data behind that guidance is stark: 38.5% of Black African and 36.5% of mixed-ancestry UK adults are severely vitamin D deficient through winter and spring, against 17.5% of white Europeans (Sutherland et al., 2021, *Clinical Nutrition*, 40:3436–3447). The same study found that spending more time outdoors in summer did not reliably close the gap for Black African participants — UK latitude plus melanin plus a British summer does not produce enough UV exposure for adequate synthesis. Supplementation is the practical fix. ([NHS Vitamin D guidance](https://www.nhs.uk/conditions/vitamins-and-minerals/vitamin-d/))

The link between vitamin D deficiency and hair loss is real but should be stated precisely: there is a documented association with telogen effluvium (a form of diffuse shedding), but **vitamin D deficiency does not cause routine 4C breakage**. If you are shedding full strands with a white bulb at the root — rather than short broken pieces — a blood test via your GP is worth requesting.

**Iron deficiency** (and iron-deficiency anaemia) is another documented cause of hair shedding, and is more common in women, particularly those with heavy periods. Again, the symptom to look for is full-strand shedding, not short breakage pieces. A GP can test ferritin levels — ferritin (stored iron) rather than serum iron is the more relevant marker for hair-loss assessment in this context.

## A Simple Length Retention Routine for UK 4C Hair

This is a foundational framework. The right routine for your hair will depend on your porosity, density, and scalp behaviour — but this gives you a starting point grounded in what the evidence supports.

### Wash day steps

1. **Pre-poo** — apply a generous amount of conditioner or oil to dry hair before wetting it. This limits protein loss during washing and helps high-porosity hair absorb water more gradually, reducing the mechanical stress of handling wet hair.

2. **Shampoo** — use a gentle, sulphate-free shampoo for regular wash days. On chelating days (monthly or every six to eight weeks if you have hard water), use a chelating shampoo instead, followed by a deep conditioner.

3. **Deep condition** — apply a deep conditioner with heat (a heated conditioning cap or sitting under a hooded dryer) for 20–30 minutes. Heat helps lift the cuticle and allows conditioning ingredients to penetrate.

4. **Rinse with cool water** — this helps close the cuticle, reducing frizz and helping the hair retain moisture longer.

5. **Apply leave-in conditioner while hair is still damp** — then seal with an oil or butter. The LOC method (Liquid, Oil, Cream) and LCO method (Liquid, Cream, Oil) are both used successfully on 4C hair; which works better depends on your specific porosity.

### Protective styling guidance

- Choose styles that do not pull at the hairline or scalp — discomfort immediately after installation is a warning sign, not something to push through
- Moisturise your hair *inside* the style weekly — a light water-based moisturiser spritzed at the roots, followed by an oil
- Set a take-down date before you install, and keep it
- After taking down a style, give your hair at least one full wash and conditioning cycle before reinstalling

### Nighttime care

Friction against cotton pillowcases and headrests causes significant breakage over time. The solution is either:

- A **satin or silk bonnet** — both polyester satin and natural silk create a smoother, lower-friction surface than cotton and reduce mechanical stress on the hair during sleep. No peer-reviewed clinical study has directly compared the two fabrics for hair, and none has studied 4C hair specifically. The practical differences: polyester satin may generate more static in dry, centrally heated conditions, which can worsen frizz; silk is significantly more expensive. For a bonnet worn over hair that is already in a protective style or bun, the functional difference between the two is likely negligible. Both are substantially better than cotton.
- A **satin or silk pillowcase** — useful if bonnets come off in the night

## When to See a Trichologist (UK-Specific Advice)

Most 4C length challenges come down to retention, and retention can be improved with consistent care. But some hair and scalp issues need professional assessment. Knowing the difference is important.

### Signs breakage is not routine

Seek professional advice if you notice:

- **Patchy hair loss** — areas where hair is noticeably thinner or absent, particularly around the hairline, crown, or temples
- **Significant scalp changes** — persistent itching, flaking, soreness, or visible scarring at the follicle
- **Sudden or dramatic increase in shedding** — more than usual over a period of several weeks, particularly after illness, significant stress, surgery, or giving birth
- **Hair that breaks very close to the scalp** — which can indicate tinea capitis (a fungal infection) or a traction-related condition

These are not situations to try to manage with product changes alone.

### How to find an Afro hair specialist in the UK

Not every trichologist has specific training and experience with Afro-textured hair, and that experience matters for accurate assessment. When looking for a specialist:

- The [Institute of Trichologists](https://www.trichologists.org.uk/qualified-trichologists-united-kingdom/) maintains a searchable register accredited by the Professional Standards Authority — the only UK trichology body with PSA accreditation
- The [Trichological Society](https://www.hairscientists.org/consultants/hair-consultants/trichologists) directory is searchable by region
- Ask explicitly whether the practitioner has experience with Afro-textured and 4C hair before booking — not every trichologist has this experience and it matters for accurate assessment
- Your GP can refer you to a dermatologist if there are signs of a scalp condition, though NHS waiting times vary considerably by region

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is 4C hair really slower-growing than other hair types?

Marginally, yes — but not by enough to explain why length stalls. Loussouarn et al.'s 2001 research measured African hair growing at around 0.9 cm per month versus 1.2–1.3 cm for Caucasian and Asian hair. That difference amounts to roughly 3–4 cm per year. The far larger factors are dramatic shrinkage (which makes length invisible) and end breakage (which offsets root growth). Retention is the variable you can actually change.

### How do I know if my hair is breaking or just shedding?

Shedding produces full-length strands with a small white or translucent bulb at the root — that is the follicle root sheath, and it is normal. Breaking produces short pieces without a bulb. If the short pieces are turning up everywhere — on your clothing, in your comb, on your pillow — breakage is the issue, not shedding.

### Does hard water actually affect hair growth?

Hard water does not affect the follicle or the growth process. What it does is deposit calcium and magnesium minerals on the hair shaft as water dries, creating build-up that blocks moisture, increases friction, and over time contributes to breakage. In hard water areas of the UK — particularly London and the South East — this is a real and underappreciated factor in length retention.

### How often should I wash 4C hair?

There is no single correct answer. Most 4C hair does well with a wash every one to two weeks — frequent enough to keep the scalp clean and the hair hydrated, infrequent enough that wet-hair handling stays manageable. If you have a dry scalp or use heavy products, you may need to wash more often. If your hair retains moisture well and your scalp is healthy, less often may suit you better.

### Can a bad diet really affect my hair?

Yes, but the mechanism matters. Severe deficiencies in iron and vitamin D are both documented causes of increased hair shedding — both are covered in this article with cited sources. Inadequate dietary protein can also affect hair structure over time, as hair is primarily composed of keratin. Ordinary dietary variation within a reasonable range is unlikely to visibly affect hair health. The question to ask is not "am I eating perfectly?" but "do I have any symptoms of deficiency, or has my GP identified any?" If so, treating the deficiency is the priority.

### Is it worth buying a shower filter for my hair?

Possibly, but read the specification carefully. Most shower filters sold in the UK — including those marketed for hair and skin — filter chlorine, not calcium or magnesium. KDF, activated carbon, and vitamin C filters all reduce chlorine effectively but do not remove dissolved hardness minerals. The only filter type that actually softens the water is one containing ion exchange resin that explicitly states calcium or magnesium reduction. At London hardness levels, expect to replace the cartridge every one to three months. A monthly chelating shampoo wash is a simpler and cheaper alternative for most people.

## Where to start

If you take one thing from this article: check your ends before you check your products. Short, broken pieces in your comb mean your retention strategy — not your growth rate — needs attention. Work through the seven causes above in order of likelihood for your situation, starting with water hardness if you are in London or the South East.
