**Protein treatment strengthens hair that stretches too far before snapping. Deep conditioner moisturises hair that snaps too quickly without stretching.** These are different problems and different products. Using the wrong one makes the situation worse.

The stretch test tells you which you need. Do it now before continuing.

---

## The Stretch Test

Take a single strand from a wet section of your hair (no product applied). Hold it at both ends and pull gently.

**Stretches significantly (more than a third of its length) before snapping, or feels mushy or limp when wet:** protein deficiency. The hair lacks structural integrity. You need a protein treatment, not more moisture.

**Snaps immediately with little to no stretch, feels stiff or dry:** moisture deficiency or protein overload. You need a moisturising deep conditioner. Avoid protein.

**Stretches slightly then snaps cleanly:** balanced. Maintain with both protein and moisture on rotation — protein treatment monthly, deep conditioner weekly.

---

## Protein Treatment: What It Does and When

Protein treatments (hydrolysed keratin, rice protein, ORS Hair Mayo, Aphogee Two-Minute) fill structural gaps in the hair shaft. They restore elasticity to hair that has lost it through:
- Chemical processing (bleach, colour, relaxer)
- Heat damage
- Repeated tight protective styles
- Prolonged dryness without treatment

**When protein treatment wins:**
- Stretch test shows mushy, over-stretching hair
- Hair feels limp and flat despite moisturising
- Definition collapses despite products
- Hair came out of a long protective style feeling weak

**After protein treatment:** always follow immediately with a moisturising deep conditioner. Protein without moisture makes the hair stiff and brittle.

---

## Deep Conditioner: What It Does and When

Deep conditioners (SheaMoisture Manuka Honey Masque, Camille Rose Algae Renew, Aphogee Keratin Reconstruction) restore moisture and improve manageability. They work on the hair surface and some penetrate the shaft, restoring softness and reducing breakage from dryness.

**When deep conditioner wins:**
- Stretch test shows hair snapping without stretching
- Hair feels rough, dry, or brittle
- Detangling is difficult despite slip products
- Post-protein treatment stiffness
- After chelating or clarifying wash

**Deep conditioning alone does not fix protein-deficient hair.** If the stretch test showed mushy, limp hair, adding more moisture makes the imbalance worse — hair that's already over-moisturised becomes softer, not stronger.

---

## Can You Use Both in the Same Session?

Yes, in the right order: **protein treatment first, then deep conditioner.**

Protein treatment fills the structural gaps; deep conditioner applied after moisturises the now-strengthened shaft and prevents the post-protein stiffness. Never apply a deep conditioner before a protein treatment in the same session — the conditioner coats the hair and reduces protein penetration.

The combined session is particularly appropriate for:
- Hair coming out of a long protective style
- Post-bleach or post-colour treatment
- Hair that shows both stretch test signs — some sections mushy, some brittle — because the hair is severely depleted of both

## FAQs

**I used a protein treatment and my hair is worse. What happened?**

Either the hair didn't need protein (was moisture-deficient, not protein-deficient), or protein overload has occurred (too much protein applied too frequently). Apply a moisturising deep conditioner, avoid all protein for two to three weeks, reassess.

**How often should I use a protein treatment?**

Light protein (Aphogee Two-Minute, ORS Hair Mayo): every four to six weeks for most hair. Heavy protein (Aphogee Two-Step): maximum monthly, and only when the stretch test clearly indicates it's needed. If in doubt, use the light protein — it's much harder to overdo.

**My conditioner contains protein. Should I use a separate protein treatment?**

Check the ingredient list and where the protein appears. Protein near the bottom of a conditioner's ingredient list is a trace amount — unlikely to cause overload. A dedicated protein treatment has protein high in the list as a primary active. If your conditioner has trace protein, it's probably not doing the structural work of a protein treatment.

**I alternate protein and moisture each wash day. Is that right?**

For most hair: too much protein. Weekly protein treatment is unnecessary and risks overload. The typical rotation is: deep condition weekly, light protein treatment monthly, heavy protein treatment only when the stretch test shows clear deficiency.
