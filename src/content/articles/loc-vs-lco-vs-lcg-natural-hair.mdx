**LOC for high porosity and type 4 hair. LCO for low porosity and type 3. LCG when your wash-and-go needs gel hold without losing definition to cream.**

That's the verdict. The rest is the evidence for when to choose which.

---

## What the Letters Mean

Every method starts with **L — Leave-in conditioner.** This is always first: water-based moisture applied to damp hair directly after washing.

**LOC:** Leave-in → Oil → Cream. The oil seals in the leave-in before the cream is applied.

**LCO:** Leave-in → Cream → Oil. The cream is applied over the leave-in; the oil seals over both.

**LCG:** Leave-in → Cream → Gel. No oil in the layering; gel replaces oil as the final step.

---

## LOC — When to Use It

LOC works for **high porosity hair** — hair with a raised cuticle that absorbs moisture quickly but loses it just as fast. The oil layer between the leave-in and the cream acts as a barrier that slows moisture evaporation before the cream seals further.

High porosity hair is common in:
- Type 4B and 4C hair
- Bleached, colour-treated, or heat-damaged hair
- Hair that has been in tight protective styles for extended periods

Also appropriate for **type 4 hair generally** — the coil structure and typical dryness of 4B and 4C means that more layers of moisture retention help, and the oil-then-cream sequence creates a stronger seal than LCO.

---

## LCO — When to Use It

LCO works for **low porosity hair** — hair with a flat cuticle that resists moisture uptake and doesn't need as much sealing because it retains moisture once it's in.

Low porosity hair is common in:
- Type 3A, 3B, and 3C with finer strands
- Some 4A hair with smooth, tightly packed coils
- Hair that tends to buildup quickly

In LCO, the cream goes directly over the leave-in, allowing the cream to penetrate while the cuticle is still warm and slightly open from washing. The oil goes last as a light seal, not a moisture barrier.

Also appropriate for **looser curl types** — type 2 and 3 hair generally needs less moisture layering than type 4, and LCO provides a lighter result than LOC.

---

## LCG — When to Use It

LCG is the method for wash-and-gos where gel is the final product. The sequence: leave-in on wet hair, curl cream for definition and moisture, gel immediately over the wet cream for hold and cast formation.

The oil is removed from the sequence because oil between cream and gel can prevent the gel from bonding with the hair shaft and forming its cast — the result is less hold, more frizz, and the gel layer sliding around rather than setting. If your gel isn't performing, an oil in the routine before the gel is often the cause.

---

## Can You Mix Methods?

Yes. Some people use LOC in winter (more moisture layering needed in dry central-heated air) and LCO or LCG in summer. Some use LCO for fine sections and LOC for denser sections of the same head.

The method is a starting framework, not a rule. If LOC is leaving the hair weighed down, try LCO. If LCO isn't retaining moisture long enough, try LOC. The right method is the one that produces the best result for your hair's current state.

## FAQs

**I've been doing LOC for months and my hair is still dry. What's wrong?**

Either the products aren't right for your porosity (a cream that sits on top rather than penetrating, or an oil too heavy for the hair to absorb), or there's product or mineral buildup blocking moisture from entering. Clarify and chelate first, then reassess the method. In UK hard water areas, mineral buildup is the most common reason a previously effective method stops working.

**Does the oil type matter in LOC/LCO?**

Yes. Penetrating oils (coconut, olive, avocado) work well in the middle of LOC because they can enter the shaft. Sealing oils (castor, shea butter, grapeseed) work better as the final layer in LCO because they coat the surface. Using a sealing oil in the middle of LOC and a penetrating oil as the final layer produces a less effective result than the reverse.

**Can I use the same products for LOC and LCO?**

Yes — the products are the same, the order changes. Switch the order of your oil and cream and compare results over two wash days.

**Is LCO or LOC better for a twist-out?**

LCO generally for twist-outs — you want the cream to interact directly with the leave-in for the definition the twist-out will set around. Gel (LCG) can replace or augment the oil for a more defined twist-out with harder hold.
