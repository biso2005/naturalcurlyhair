Box braids are one of the most popular protective styles in the UK — and one of the most expensive and time-consuming to install. Getting them right involves choosing the right salon, knowing what to ask for, managing the first few nights, and maintaining the scalp for the four to eight weeks they're in.

This guide covers all of it, including the things salons often don't mention.

---

## Cost in the UK

**Typical range: £80–£200.** Variation depends on:
- **Length:** waist-length box braids take significantly longer than shoulder-length
- **Size:** small braids (more of them) cost more than medium or large
- **Location:** London salons typically charge more than salons outside London
- **Stylist experience:** a specialist braider with a long booking list will charge more than a general natural hair salon

What's included: the stylist's labour and usually the hair. Some salons ask you to bring your own hair — confirm when booking.

**Tipping:** not universal in UK braiding salons, but becoming more common. A £10–£20 tip for a six-hour appointment is appropriate if you're happy with the work.

## Before the Appointment

### Prepare your natural hair

Box braids installed on healthy, moisturised, tangle-free hair last longer and cause less breakage when removed. The day before or morning of:

1. Wash and condition your natural hair
2. Apply leave-in and detangle fully
3. Do not put any heavy product on top — the stylist will work with your hair and product residue can make installation harder

Arrive with the hair clean, moisturised, and loose (not in a style).

### The hair itself (kanekalon)

Most box braid installations use kanekalon synthetic hair. The brand matters less than the quality of the installation — cheap kanekalon installed well lasts longer than premium kanekalon installed with uneven tension.

**Volume:** a standard pack covers approximately 10–12 braids. For a full head of medium box braids, expect 5–8 packs. Ask your stylist how many they'd use for your desired style before buying.

**Colour:** kanekalon comes in a wide range — 1B (off-black) is the most natural-looking for most dark brown to black natural hair.

## The Installation: What to Expect

A medium box braids appointment takes 5–8 hours. It is a long time to sit. Wear comfortable clothes. Bring headphones, phone charger, food. Don't plan anything demanding for the evening.

**Tension:** this is the thing most people wish they'd been told. Box braids should feel secure but not painful during installation. If you feel significant pulling at the root, or if the skin at your hairline or temples is pulling visibly, say something during the appointment. Tension that causes pain during installation causes traction alopecia over the weeks the braids are in.

Many stylists braid the hairline tighter than the rest of the head — the finer hair there holds less well and some tension compensates. This is also where traction alopecia most commonly occurs. If the hairline is visibly stretched during installation, ask for the tension to be reduced.

## The First Night: What Nobody Mentions

**Tension headaches:** tight new braids commonly cause headaches within the first 12–24 hours. This is from the scalp tension of new installation — especially if the braids are heavy or the scalp isn't used to the weight.

What helps: take paracetamol before bed, sleep with the braids loose or in a very loose pineapple, and give it 48 hours. If pain is severe or doesn't ease after 48 hours, the tension may be too tight and some braids may need to be redone.

**Smell from synthetic hair:** some kanekalon hair has a chemical smell that's more noticeable in the first few days. An apple cider vinegar rinse over the braids before the appointment, then dried, reduces it. Some people do this the day before; some ask their stylist to prep the hair before installation.

## Maintaining Box Braids

### Scalp moisturising

The most important maintenance task. Natural hair under braids still needs moisture, and the scalp still produces sebum — but it can't be detangled or fully reached. Apply a lightweight oil or scalp spray to the scalp between the braids every two to three days.

**What works:** a diluted spray bottle with water and a small amount of leave-in conditioner, applied directly to the scalp. A light scalp oil (jojoba, argan) applied with a dropper to the parting between braids.

**What doesn't work:** heavy butters or oils applied to the braid length — these create buildup on the synthetic hair and accumulate at the root.

### Protecting at night

Braids against a cotton pillowcase create frizz at the braid roots and dry out the scalp more quickly. A satin bonnet large enough to hold all the braids, or a satin pillowcase, significantly reduces this.

For long braids that don't fit in a bonnet: a loose pineapple secured with a large satin scrunchie, or a satin bonnet over the front hairline with the length loose behind.

### Washing

Braids can and should be washed. Scalp buildup and sebum accumulation under braids causes itching, flaking, and odour over the weeks they're in. A diluted shampoo applied to the scalp — applied with a small applicator bottle, massaged in, rinsed thoroughly — keeps the scalp healthy without disturbing the braid pattern.

**How often:** every two weeks is the standard guidance. For more active people or oilier scalps, weekly scalp washing.

**Drying:** braids hold water. Don't sleep on damp braids — they develop mildew. Sit under a hooded dryer or use a diffuser on low heat to dry the scalp and roots after washing.

## Duration and Removal

**How long box braids last:** 4–8 weeks for most people. The limiting factor is usually scalp health (buildup and itching) and new growth (the root starts to look fuzzy). Keeping braids in beyond eight weeks increases the risk of matting where the new growth meets the braid root.

**Removal:** budget a half day. Snip the braid at the end to reduce weight, then unravel from the bottom up. Apply generous conditioner to each section as you go. Remove in sections and detangle each section before moving to the next. Do not pull or rip.

After removal, the hair needs a thorough wash and deep condition before any next style.

---

## What Not to Do

- Don't scratch the scalp with a sharp object — fingernails or a fine-tooth comb between braids creates micro-abrasions that allow infection
- Don't add extensions or weight to the ends of braids after installation — this increases tension at the root
- Don't keep braids in past eight weeks without checking the new growth for matting
- Don't sleep with wet braids

## FAQs

**The braids are hurting. Is that normal?**

Some tension for the first 24–48 hours is normal. Pain that's severe, pain that persists past 48 hours, or visible skin pulling at the hairline is not normal and should be addressed. If the braids are causing significant pain, some may need to be redone with less tension — return to the salon and say so.

**Can I swim in box braids?**

Yes, with a swimming cap to protect against chlorine. Rinse the braids thoroughly after swimming and dry fully. Chlorine damage to kanekalon affects the feel and appearance of the braids and can accelerate the timeline to removal.

**How do I find a good braiding salon in the UK?**

Ask for recommendations from people whose braids you've seen and admired. Look at the salon's portfolio specifically for box braids (not just any braid style). Check whether they specialise in natural hair. Book a consultation if possible — how they handle your questions before the appointment is a reasonable indicator of how they'll handle the appointment itself.

**The stylist wants to braid on dry hair. Is that okay?**

Some stylists prefer dry hair for better grip and control during installation. This is a stylist preference, not a problem. The important thing is that your natural hair was properly moisturised before the appointment.
