If your postcode is in London, the South East, or the Midlands, hard water is almost certainly a factor in your curl problems. The fix is a monthly chelating wash — not a new product range, not a porosity test, not a different technique. Here's how to know if it applies to you, and exactly what to do.

## Which Parts of the UK Have Hard Water?

The UK is not uniformly hard. Water hardness is determined by the geology the water passes through before it reaches your tap. Chalk and limestone areas — which cover much of central and south-east England — dissolve calcium and magnesium carbonate into the water supply. Highland and upland areas, by contrast, sit on granite and other hard rocks that contribute very little mineral content.

Broad regional picture, based on data from TapWater.uk and the Drinking Water Inspectorate (DWI):

- **Very hard (300+ mg/L as calcium carbonate):** Thames Valley, East Anglia, Lincolnshire, parts of the East Midlands
- **Hard (200–300 mg/L):** Much of the East and South East of England, parts of the West Midlands
- **Moderately hard (100–200 mg/L):** Parts of the North of England, some areas of the South West
- **Soft to slightly hard (0–100 mg/L):** Most of Scotland, Wales (particularly the west and north), Northern Ireland, parts of the North West of England

London and the South East sit at 250–350 mg/L. Manchester is typically 60–80 mg/L. Most of Wales is under 80 mg/L. These are generalisations — within a single county, hardness can vary meaningfully depending on which reservoir or aquifer supplies your postcode.

### How to Check Your Exact Postcode

Do not guess. Every major UK water company publishes hardness data by postcode or area. Go directly to your supplier's website and use their water quality search tool.

- **Thames Water** — thameswater.co.uk → "Water quality" → enter your postcode
- **Anglian Water** — anglianwater.co.uk → "Your water" → hardness checker
- **Scottish Water** — scottishwater.co.uk → water quality report by postcode
- **United Utilities (North West)** — unitedutilities.com → "My water" → water quality
- **Severn Trent** — severntrent.com → "Water quality" tool

The result will give you a figure in milligrams per litre (mg/L) or degrees Clarke. Anything above 200 mg/L is firmly in "hard" territory and warrants the steps below.

If you rent and want a physical confirmation, a cheap TDS (total dissolved solids) meter — available for a few pounds online — will give you a rough cross-check, though it measures all dissolved solids, not calcium specifically. British Water test strips are more specific to calcium carbonate hardness.

## What Hard Water Does to Curly Hair

Calcium and magnesium ions in hard water bind to both the hair shaft and to the anionic (negatively charged) surfactants in shampoos and conditioners. The result is a layer of mineral deposits that sits on and within the cuticle.

The practical effects curly-haired people notice:

- Curl clumps that feel rigid or crunchy without any gel applied
- Hair that feels rough immediately after washing and rinsing
- Conditioner that seems to sit on the hair rather than absorb
- Build-up that clarifying shampoo does not fully shift
- Colour-treated hair that fades faster or turns brassy

### Mineral Buildup vs. Porosity Damage — Not the Same Thing

These are frequently conflated, and conflating them leads to the wrong fix.

**Mineral buildup** is a coating. Calcium and magnesium ions physically coat the outside of the hair shaft and can partially block the cuticle. This is largely reversible. Remove the minerals, and the hair often responds differently within a single wash.

**Porosity damage** refers to structural changes to the cuticle itself — lifted, chipped, or missing cuticle scales caused by mechanical damage (heat, over-manipulation), chemical processing, or prolonged high-pH exposure. This is not reversible in the same way. A chelating wash will not repair a damaged cuticle.

Why does this distinction matter? If your hair feels porous — absorbs water quickly but loses it just as fast, tangles easily, soaks up product — you may have both issues at once. Address the mineral coating first (with chelation, covered below), then assess what remains. If roughness and porosity persist after a thorough chelating wash, you are dealing with structural damage that requires protein treatments and protective practices, not more chelation.

Hard water can *worsen* the appearance of high-porosity hair because minerals roughen the cuticle surface further, but hard water alone does not create permanently high porosity. If structural damage is significant enough to warrant a professional assessment, the [Institute of Trichologists](https://www.trichologists.org.uk/qualified-trichologists-united-kingdom/) (PSA-accredited) maintains a UK directory.

## How to Tell If Hard Water Is Your Problem (Not Your Products)

### The Simple Diagnostic Checklist

Run through these before spending money on new products or a shower filter.

**Strong indicators of hard water involvement:**

- [ ] Your hair felt noticeably different (softer, more defined curls) when you used water from a different source — hotel, gym, friend's house in a soft water area, or even bottled water as a final rinse
- [ ] Hair feels coated or rough immediately after washing, even with a conditioner you trust
- [ ] Curl definition improves after a chelating wash (with EDTA or phytic acid — more on this below) compared with your usual clarifying shampoo
- [ ] You have limescale deposits on your taps, showerhead, or kettle (a reliable proxy for very hard water)
- [ ] Your shampoo does not lather well even on freshly washed hair
- [ ] Hair colour fades faster than your colourist expects, or goes brassy between appointments

**Indicators pointing away from hard water and towards product buildup or technique:**

- [ ] The problem appeared when you switched products, not when you moved
- [ ] A standard clarifying shampoo (no chelating agent) restores your curl pattern fully
- [ ] Hair feels fine immediately post-wash but gets heavy or greasy by day two or three (more consistent with product buildup)
- [ ] You have genuinely soft water per your supplier's postcode lookup

The single most useful home test: do one wash with a proper chelating shampoo (check the ingredient list for disodium EDTA, tetrasodium EDTA, or phytic acid), follow with your usual conditioner, and compare the result with your previous wash. If there is a meaningful improvement — softer, less crunchy, better curl definition — hard water is a significant factor.

## Clarifying and Chelating: What's the Difference and Which Do You Need?

This is the most misunderstood distinction in the hard water conversation, and getting it wrong means spending money on the wrong products.

**Clarifying shampoos** use a higher concentration of surfactants (often sulphates, sometimes stronger non-sulphate surfactants) to strip product residue, oils, and sebum from the hair. They are good at removing silicones, waxes, and oil-based buildup. They are *not* effective at removing mineral ions. Calcium and magnesium deposits are not lifted by surfactant action alone — only chelating agents can do this (source: Tangieco hard-water guide; Malibu C reference page).

**Chelating shampoos** contain chelating agents — molecules that chemically bind to metal ions (calcium, magnesium, iron, copper) and hold them so they can be rinsed away. Common chelating agents to look for on the ingredient label:

- **Disodium EDTA** or **tetrasodium EDTA** — the most widely used synthetic chelators; very effective against calcium and magnesium
- **Phytic acid** (also listed as inositol hexaphosphate) — a plant-derived chelator, found in some "natural" formulations; effective but slightly milder
- **Citric acid** — used in some rinses; provides mild chelating action as well as lowering pH, which helps close the cuticle

A product labelled "clarifying" is not automatically chelating. Read the ingredient list. If none of the above appear, it will not remove mineral deposits regardless of how the marketing describes it.

### How Often to Use Each in Hard Water Areas

These intervals reflect practitioner consensus and general guidance. No peer-reviewed study has established specific chelating frequency recommendations for textured hair — treat this table as a starting framework, not a clinical protocol, and adjust based on how your hair responds.

| Water hardness | Chelating frequency | Clarifying frequency |
|---|---|---|
| Soft (under 100 mg/L) | Rarely or never needed | Every 4–6 weeks if you use heavy stylers |
| Moderately hard (100–200 mg/L) | Once a month | Every 2–4 weeks |
| Hard (200–300 mg/L) | Every 2–3 weeks | As needed between chelating washes |
| Very hard (300+ mg/L) | Every 1–2 weeks | Less necessary — chelating covers this ground |

In very hard water areas, chelating becomes your primary reset wash. Standard clarifying on top of a chelating schedule can over-strip the hair; scale back if your hair feels dry or loses elasticity.

## Shower Filters: Do They Actually Work for Curly Hair?

Shower filters are marketed heavily at people with hard water and hair concerns. The honest answer is that their effectiveness depends entirely on the type of filter, the incoming hardness level, and what specifically you are trying to remove.

### What to Look For (What the Specs Actually Mean)

There are two fundamentally different types of shower filter sold in the UK, and they work by different mechanisms.

**KDF (Kinetic Degradation Fluxion) filters** use a copper-zinc alloy medium. According to manufacturer technical specifications and water treatment references, KDF is effective at reducing chlorine and some heavy metals (iron, lead). Its effect on calcium and magnesium hardness — the ions that cause the mineral buildup described above — is limited. KDF filters are common in high-street "hard water shower filter" products. If reducing chlorine irritation is your primary concern, these may help. If mineral buildup on your hair is the problem, KDF filters are unlikely to produce a dramatic result. This characterisation is based on manufacturer data; independent peer-reviewed testing of KDF at shower-scale flow rates in UK hard water conditions has not been located. {/* TODO: confirm KDF hardness-reduction claim against independent water treatment source before publish */}

**Ion exchange water softeners** swap calcium and magnesium ions for sodium ions, which genuinely softens the water. Whole-house softeners use this technology and are effective. Most shower-head-attached filters do *not* use true ion exchange at the scale required to meaningfully soften water at shower flow rates — the contact time is too brief and the resin capacity too small.

**Vitamin C (ascorbic acid) filters** neutralise chloramine and chlorine specifically. They do not affect hardness.

What this means practically: a shower filter may improve your hair if chlorine is your main irritant, but if your postcode lookup shows 300 mg/L hardness, a shower filter attachment is unlikely to be your solution. A whole-house softener or a chelating wash programme is more directly targeted.

### How to Judge a Chelating Shampoo (Criteria, Not Marketing)

Until we have completed testing, here is what to evaluate when you assess one yourself:

**On the ingredient label:**
- Is a chelating agent (EDTA, phytic acid, citric acid) present and near enough to the top of the list to be at an active concentration?
- What surfactant is used? Sulphate-based chelating shampoos are more effective at mineral removal but more stripping. Sulphate-free chelating shampoos are gentler but may require longer contact time.
- Are there heavy silicones or waxes in the formula? These can redeposit and partially undermine the clarifying effect.

**On your hair, wash by wash:**
- Does the lather improve compared with your regular shampoo? (Better lather in hard water often indicates effective chelation is occurring.)
- Does the hair feel lighter and less coated after rinsing?
- Does your conditioner absorb more readily than usual?
- Does the curl pattern show more definition without added product?

**After repeated use:**
- Does your hair maintain elasticity, or does it become brittle? (Over-chelating can over-strip; reduce frequency if this occurs.)
- Does the improvement plateau, suggesting the backlog of mineral deposits has been cleared?

## Rinse-Based Solutions: Apple Cider Vinegar, Citric Acid, and More

Rinse-based approaches are the lowest-cost entry point and genuinely have a chemical rationale, though their limitations are equally real.

### What Works, What Doesn't — and Why

**Citric acid rinse**

Citric acid is a weak chelating agent as well as an acid. Dissolving half a teaspoon of food-grade citric acid powder in a litre of water and using it as a final rinse after conditioning does two things: it mildly chelates remaining calcium and magnesium ions, and it lowers the pH of the hair surface, which helps close the cuticle and reduce frizz.

This approach is genuinely useful for light hard water maintenance between chelating washes, and for people in moderately hard water areas who find chelating shampoos too stripping. The pH of a citric acid rinse at this dilution is estimated at roughly 3–4, which is within the range considered beneficial for cuticle closure — though this figure has not been independently verified with a calibrated meter at this exact dilution. {/* TODO: verify pH claim with a pH meter test at the recommended dilution before publishing */}

Drawbacks: it does not address heavy mineral buildup as effectively as an EDTA-based chelating shampoo. Used too often or at too high a concentration, it can over-acidify the hair and affect elasticity.

**Apple cider vinegar (ACV)**

ACV is acetic acid, and its mechanism is similar to citric acid — mild pH reduction and very mild chelating effect. It is frequently recommended in the curly hair community, partly because of its accessibility and partly because of routine tradition rather than robust evidence.

The practical drawbacks are real: the smell, even when diluted, lingers until the hair is fully dry for some people. The chelating action is weaker than citric acid. The acetic acid concentration in ACV varies between brands, making consistent results harder to achieve than with measured citric acid powder.

If you prefer ACV for routine or sensory reasons, dilute to approximately one part ACV to ten parts water. Do not use undiluted. Do not use if you have a sensitivity to the smell or if you find your hair over-softened after use.

**Distilled water or filtered water as a final rinse**

If you have access to distilled or genuinely softened water (from a whole-house softener), using it as a final rinse after your usual wash routine removes the hard tap water from the equation at the final step. This is impractical for most people daily but worth trying as a diagnostic: if your hair responds positively, it confirms hard water is a factor.

## Your Hard Water Routine: A Practical UK Framework

### Soft Water Areas (Scotland, West Wales, Much of Northern Ireland)

If your supplier confirms you are below 100 mg/L, mineral buildup from your water supply is unlikely to be your primary curl concern. Standard clarifying every four to six weeks is sufficient for product-buildup management. You do not need to invest in chelating shampoos or shower filters for water-related reasons.

Focus your attention on other factors: porosity management, protein-moisture balance, and product compatibility with your curl type.

### Moderately Hard Areas (100–200 mg/L)

A monthly chelating wash is a reasonable baseline. Use a chelating shampoo (check the label for EDTA or phytic acid) once a month, and a standard clarifying or co-wash routine in between based on your styling product load.

A citric acid rinse used every two to three weeks can act as a light maintenance step between chelating washes.

Deep conditioning after every chelating wash matters here. Chelating strips protective lipids alongside minerals; replacing moisture immediately reduces dryness and breakage.

### Very Hard Areas (Thames Valley, East Anglia, Lincolnshire and Similar)

This is where a systematic approach pays off most. At 300+ mg/L, mineral buildup accumulates faster than a monthly chelating wash can clear it, and product performance degrades noticeably between washes.

A practical framework:

1. **Chelating wash every one to two weeks** — use an EDTA-based formula, not just a clarifying shampoo. Leave the shampoo on for two to three minutes before rinsing to maximise contact time with mineral deposits.
2. **Deep condition immediately after every chelating wash** — chelation removes protective lipids alongside minerals. A moisture-focused deep conditioner with humectants (glycerin, aloe, panthenol) used under heat for 20–30 minutes is the standard recovery step.
3. **Citric acid or distilled water final rinse** — as a maintenance step between chelating washes, not a replacement for them.
4. **Reassess after four to six weeks** — once the backlog of mineral buildup has been cleared, you may find you can space chelating washes further apart. Hair that felt dry or stiff throughout often improves significantly once the mineral layer is gone.

Note on glycerin in hard water areas: UK indoor winter humidity drops to 30–45% in centrally heated homes (Guinness Homes; Sussex Damp Experts). At low humidity, glycerin can draw moisture from the hair shaft rather than from the air. This does not mean avoiding glycerin entirely — it means layering it under a sealing product to reduce off-gassing. Glycerin absorbs approximately 25% of its weight at 50% relative humidity (Chemists Corner, Perry Romanowski); below that threshold, the equation shifts.

### What Doesn't Change With Water Hardness

Regardless of your hardness level, protein-moisture balance remains the constant. A chelating wash removes minerals; it does not rebuild the cuticle or restore elasticity lost to damage. If your hair is both mineral-coated and structurally damaged, chelation is step one of two, not the whole answer.

## FAQs

**My products stopped working — could this be hard water?**
Yes, this is one of the most common hard water symptoms. Mineral buildup interferes with conditioner absorption and prevents styling products from bonding to the hair properly. A single chelating wash is often enough to determine whether hard water is the cause — if your products work noticeably better in the days following a chelating wash, that is your answer.

**I moved to London six months ago and my curls have completely changed. Is this hard water?**
Almost certainly a factor. London's water is among the hardest in the UK at 250–350 mg/L. The change in water hardness is usually noticeable within weeks for people with textured hair, even when nothing else in the routine has changed.

**Do I need a shower filter if I'm already chelating?**
Not necessarily. Chelating washes address mineral buildup directly. A shower filter may reduce chlorine exposure (which can cause scalp dryness and irritation independently of mineral buildup), but it is not a substitute for chelation in hard water areas and is not required if chelation is already working for you.

**Can I chelate too often?**
Yes. Over-chelating strips the hair of protective lipids and can cause dryness and brittleness over time. If your hair feels dry, loses elasticity, or becomes more prone to tangling after starting a chelating regimen, reduce frequency and increase deep conditioning. Hair that has been over-stripped with chelating washes responds the same way as hair that has been over-clarified — the fix is moisture, not more stripping.

**Is ACV a substitute for chelating shampoo?**
No. ACV lowers the pH of the hair surface and provides mild chelating action via acetic acid, but it is not effective against the level of mineral buildup that accumulates in hard water areas at 200+ mg/L. It is a useful maintenance rinse between proper chelating washes, not a replacement for them.
