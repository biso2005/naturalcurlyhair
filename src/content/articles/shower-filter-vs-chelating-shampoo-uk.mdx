**A shower filter reduces the mineral content of the water going onto your hair. A chelating shampoo removes minerals that have already accumulated in the hair shaft.** They address different stages of the same problem. In most of England's hard water areas, both are needed — but the chelating shampoo matters more if you can only do one.

---

## The Hard Water Problem in the UK

London, the South East, East Anglia, and the East Midlands have very hard to extremely hard water — calcium and magnesium levels above 200mg per litre in many areas. Every time you wash your hair with this water, some of those minerals deposit on the hair shaft. Over weeks and months the buildup:

- Makes the hair surface rougher (causing more mechanical friction and frizz)
- Reduces moisture uptake (minerals coat the shaft and block product absorption)
- Makes coily hair feel brittle and increasingly dry despite a good moisture routine
- Creates a dull, flat appearance on coily hair that should have definition

A regular clarifying shampoo removes product buildup but does not remove mineral deposits. Mineral removal requires chelating agents — EDTA, citric acid in meaningful concentration, or phytic acid.

---

## What a Shower Filter Does

A shower filter fitted to the shower head (vitamin C or KDF filters are the most commonly used for hard water) partially removes or neutralises minerals in the water before it contacts the hair and skin.

**What it does well:**
- Reduces ongoing mineral deposition with every wash
- Also reduces the harshness of hard water on skin — a secondary benefit for scalp sensitivity
- Set-and-forget: once installed it works passively

**What it doesn't do:**
- Remove minerals already in the hair shaft
- Fully soften the water to soft-water levels in very hard areas — most filters reduce but don't eliminate mineral content
- Replace a chelating shampoo for existing buildup

**Cost:** shower filters range from £20–60 for the unit plus occasional cartridge replacement (£10–20 every one to three months). Widely available on Amazon UK and at Boots.

---

## What a Chelating Shampoo Does

A chelating shampoo (Malibu C Hard Water Wellness Shampoo, Joico Clarifying Shampoo) contains EDTA or a similar chelating agent that binds to the calcium and magnesium ions deposited on the hair shaft and removes them when rinsed out.

**What it does well:**
- Removes existing mineral buildup in a single wash — noticeable immediate effect
- Addresses the accumulation that has built up over months without a shower filter
- Restores the hair's moisture uptake and product performance after a chelating wash

**What it doesn't do:**
- Prevent future mineral deposition — it clears what's there, not what's coming
- Condition the hair — always follow with a deep conditioner

**Cost:** Malibu C Hard Water Wellness Shampoo is around £15 for 266ml at LookFantastic — at monthly use (the recommended frequency for hard water areas), a bottle lasts two to three months.

---

## Which Matters More

**If you can only choose one: the chelating shampoo.**

A monthly chelating shampoo used consistently prevents serious mineral accumulation. The hair may never reach optimally soft water performance, but regular chelating keeps mineral buildup manageable and the routine functioning.

A shower filter alone — without chelating — slows the rate of mineral accumulation but doesn't address what's already there, and in very hard water areas the filter's partial reduction still allows significant deposition over time.

**If you can use both:**

Install the shower filter to reduce ongoing deposition. Continue monthly chelating to clear any residual buildup the filter doesn't prevent. In very hard water areas (London, areas above 300mg/L), the combination produces noticeably better results than either alone.

---

## Signs That Mineral Buildup Is the Problem

- Routine that worked well has gradually become less effective over six to twelve months
- Deep conditioner doesn't seem to absorb — hair feels the same before and after
- Hair has become progressively drier and less defined despite consistent routine
- One chelating wash restores the routine's performance immediately

That last point is the diagnostic: if one chelating wash makes a dramatic improvement, mineral buildup was the primary issue.

## FAQs

**I live in Scotland / Wales / northwest England. Do I need this?**

Scotland and Wales have mostly soft water; most of northwest England is also soft to moderately hard. Mineral buildup is much less significant in these areas. A quarterly chelating wash is maintenance; monthly isn't necessary. A shower filter is optional.

**Can I use apple cider vinegar instead of a chelating shampoo?**

ACV rinses are mildly acidic and can close the cuticle and add some shine, but they do not chelate. Chelating requires agents that specifically bind to metal ions (calcium, magnesium) — ACV does not do this. For hard water mineral removal, use a chelating shampoo with EDTA.

**My shower filter says it softens water. Isn't that the same as chelating?**

No. Softening the water reduces future mineral deposition. Chelating removes minerals already in the hair. Even if you install a shower filter that fully softened the water from today, the mineral deposits that have already accumulated in the hair shaft would remain until a chelating shampoo removes them.

**How do I know if my water is hard?**

Your local water company publishes water hardness data online. Thames Water, Anglian Water, and Southern Water all serve very hard water areas. Yorkshire Water is variable. United Utilities (NW England), Welsh Water, and Scottish Water serve mostly soft water areas. Search "[your water company] water hardness" for your specific postcode.
