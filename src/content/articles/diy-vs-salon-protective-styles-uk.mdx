**Go to the salon for styles that require skills you don't have or that need to last six weeks or longer. DIY for styles you can do to an adequate standard that you'll redo every two to three weeks.**

Both have a place. Most people end up with a combination — salon for major installs, DIY for maintenance and shorter-duration styles.

---

## When the Salon Is Worth It

**Styles with a complex technique you haven't learned.** Knotless braids, Ghana braids, butterfly locs, and Sisterlocks all require techniques that take significant practice to do well. A bad attempt on yourself isn't just aesthetically disappointing — it can cause breakage from uneven tension.

**When the style needs to last six weeks or more.** A salon-installed protective style that will be in for six to eight weeks is holding your hair for a significant portion of the year. The quality of the installation — even tension, well-prepped ends, appropriate extension weight — directly affects how much of that time is protective rather than damaging. Paying for a skilled installation protects the investment.

**Your first time doing a style.** Seeing the technique done professionally once gives you a reference point for your own future attempts.

**When you want results for an occasion.** For a wedding, interview, or event where the style matters specifically, salon installation removes the risk of a DIY attempt that doesn't go to plan.

**For colour and chemical services.** Never attempt bleaching, relaxing, or significant colour changes on your own natural hair without experience. The risk of irreversible damage is too high.

---

## When DIY Makes Sense

**Styles you've learned and can do adequately.** Two-strand twists, flat twists, cornrows (once learned), buns, and puffs are all achievable at home with practice. "Adequately" is the standard, not "perfectly" — a home-done twist-out that lasts two weeks is more protective than leaving the hair unprotected for two weeks waiting for a salon appointment.

**Styles you'll redo frequently.** Two-strand twists that you redo every two weeks: DIY makes financial and practical sense. Box braids that will be in for six weeks: salon is worth it.

**Maintenance between salon appointments.** Cornrow maintenance at the nape, edge laying, moisturising scalp treatments while in a style — these are all maintainable at home between salon visits.

**When salon access is limited.** Salon desert areas, schedule constraints, or cost constraints are all legitimate reasons to develop DIY skills for styles you'd otherwise go to a salon for.

## The Cost Comparison

UK salon pricing for protective styles:

| Style | Typical salon price |
|-------|---------------------|
| Two-strand twists (own hair) | £40–80 |
| Box braids | £80–200 |
| Knotless braids | £90–220 |
| Cornrows (basic) | £40–100 |
| Ghana braids | £80–160 |
| Faux locs | £100–280 |
| Locs installation | £80–200 |
| Sisterlocks | £350–700+ |

DIY costs: extensions (£5–20 per pack, 3–6 packs for full head depending on style) + your time. A box braid install that costs £120–150 at a salon can be done DIY for £20–40 in materials if you have the skill, at the cost of 4–8 hours of your own time.

The break-even calculation depends on how much you value your time and how often you'd be paying salon prices.

## Developing DIY Skills

If you want to DIY styles you currently visit the salon for:

**Start with the simpler version.** Two-strand twists before cornrows. Basic cornrows before feed-in braids. Build from what you can already do.

**YouTube.** The natural hair community on YouTube has detailed tutorials for every style, including slow-motion technique explanations. Search the specific style plus "tutorial 4C hair" or whatever your hair type is.

**Practice on a mannequin head.** For braiding especially, practicing on a mannequin head (widely available on Amazon UK, £10–15) lets you develop technique without the pressure of working on your own hair or a willing family member.

**Accept the learning curve.** First attempts are rarely salon-quality. The goal is adequate, not perfect. With repetition, most styles become faster and better.

## FAQs

**I tried to cornrow my own hair and it looks terrible. Should I give up?**

Cornrowing your own hair (especially the back) is genuinely difficult — you're working blind on hair you can't see properly. Part-section practice, regular breaks to check in the mirror, and the realistic expectation that back cornrows are the hardest DIY skill in natural hair styling. Most people learn to do the front and sides and go to a salon or trusted friend for the back.

**My friend does my braids but they're always too tight. Is it worth paying for a salon?**

Tight braids from a friend are as damaging as tight braids from a salon. "Free" is not the relevant variable if the tension is causing traction damage. Either teach your friend the tension technique, find a different friend, or pay a stylist who knows what appropriate tension feels like.

**Can I learn to do my own locs maintenance at home?**

Yes, for retwisting. Most loc wearers learn to retwist their own locs within the first year. The technique is simpler than braiding — twist in the direction of the loc's existing twist pattern and use enough product to hold. The back sections are harder; some loc wearers go to a stylist for the back only.

**Is it cheaper to DIY in the long run?**

For styles done frequently (every two to three weeks), yes significantly. For styles done quarterly, the savings are real but smaller — the time investment in learning the skill and doing the install needs to be weighed against quarterly salon visits.
