The test is simple. Take a clean, damp strand and stretch it slowly.

**If it stretches a little and snaps:** needs moisture.
**If it stretches a lot and doesn't snap, or feels mushy when wet:** needs protein.
**If it barely stretches and snaps immediately:** needs moisture, and may have too much protein.

That's the diagnosis. Everything below explains what to do with it.

---

## What Protein and Moisture Actually Do

**Moisture** (from water-based products, conditioners, leave-ins) keeps the hair shaft flexible and elastic. Without enough, hair is brittle — it snaps easily, feels dry and rough, loses curl or wave definition.

**Protein** temporarily fills gaps and damage in the hair cuticle, making the strand feel stronger and less prone to over-stretching. Too much protein makes the hair stiff and brittle in a different way from dryness — it snaps with almost no stretch.

Most natural hair needs a balance of both. The proportion shifts based on hair type, porosity, damage level, and what products you've been using.

---

## The Stretch Test in Detail

Take a single strand from the middle of the hair (not the ends, which are always more damaged). Dampen it. Hold each end between thumb and forefinger and pull slowly.

**Healthy hair:** stretches up to 30% of its length and returns to shape without snapping.

**Moisture-deficient (needs more moisture):**
- Snaps quickly with little stretch
- Feels rough or dry when wet
- Curl pattern looks shrunken or undefined
- Breakage that's mostly short pieces

**Protein-deficient (needs protein):**
- Stretches far and either doesn't snap back or breaks after excessive stretch
- Hair feels soft and mushy when wet — like wet cotton
- Curl pattern looks limp or undefined even when the hair is moisturised
- Breakage that's mid-length rather than short pieces

**Over-proteined (too much protein, needs moisture):**
- No stretch at all — snaps immediately
- Feels stiff and almost plastic-like when wet
- More brittle than before you added protein
- Curl pattern looks tight and crunchy in a harsh rather than defined way

---

## Moisture-Deficient: What to Do

This is the most common situation in natural hair.

- **Increase deep conditioning.** A deep conditioner with heat (a warm cap or warm towel over a shower cap) every one to two weeks until the hair recovers.
- **Check your leave-in.** A leave-in that's too light for your porosity or hair type isn't retaining moisture. Shift to a heavier one or add a cream on top.
- **Seal properly.** After leave-in, use oil then cream (LOC) or cream then oil (LCO) to trap moisture in the shaft.
- **Check for hard water.** In hard water areas, mineral deposits prevent conditioners from fully contacting the hair — chelate monthly.
- **Check product alcohol.** Some products contain drying alcohols high in the ingredient list (alcohol denat, isopropyl alcohol). These dry hair out over time.

## Protein-Deficient: What to Do

- **Use a protein treatment.** Types in order of strength: protein-rich conditioner (gentlest), hydrolysed protein treatment, hard protein treatment (strongest, for very damaged hair). Start with the mildest effective option.
- **How often:** every four to six weeks as maintenance; more frequently if recovering from chemical or heat damage.
- **Deep condition after every protein treatment.** Protein alone without moisture after it will tip the balance the other way.
- **Watch for over-correction.** If after a protein treatment the hair feels stiff or the stretch test shows almost no elasticity, moisture is now the priority.

---

## Over-Proteined: What to Do

- **Stop all protein immediately.** Check every product — many contain protein under names like hydrolysed wheat protein, keratin, silk amino acids, or rice protein.
- **Deep condition with moisture-only conditioner** (no protein listed). With heat if possible.
- **Repeat until elasticity returns.** This can take two to four weeks of protein-free moisture focus.
- **When you add protein back:** start with a gentle protein-rich conditioner rather than a treatment.

---

## How Often to Do the Stretch Test

Whenever the routine seems to stop working. If definition is gone despite correct products and technique, or breakage has increased, run the test before changing products.

Natural hair doesn't need protein on a fixed schedule — it needs it when the test shows protein deficiency or when damage warrants it. Many healthy natural hair routines never use a separate protein treatment because moisture-only deep conditioning is sufficient.

## FAQs

**My hair feels dry but the stretch test shows too much elasticity. Which problem do I fix?**

Protein first. The mushy, over-stretchy feeling when wet is protein deficiency regardless of the dry feeling. A protein treatment followed by deep conditioning usually resolves both.

**I did a protein treatment and now my hair is stiffer than before. What happened?**

Over-proteined. Switch to moisture-only products for two to four weeks, deep conditioning weekly with heat. No protein.

**How do I know what products have protein in them?**

Common protein ingredients: hydrolysed keratin, hydrolysed wheat protein, hydrolysed silk protein, rice protein, collagen, biotin, silk amino acids, casein. Any ingredient with "hydrolysed" or "protein" in the name counts.

**Can I just use a moisturising conditioner every time and skip protein entirely?**

For many people, yes. Healthy low-to-medium-damage natural hair often maintains good elasticity with moisture alone. Protein becomes necessary after chemical treatment, heat damage, colour, or breakage that indicates structural weakness. If the stretch test shows good elasticity consistently, protein treatment isn't necessary.
