Most detangling breakage comes from one of three things: starting at the root instead of the ends, using a fine-tooth comb on dry hair, or skipping the conditioner that makes the process possible. Fix those three things and most of the breakage stops.

---

## The Three Rules

### 1. Always start at the ends

The tangles accumulate from the ends upward. Starting at the root and pulling down through tangles forces every knot through the whole length beneath it. Starting at the ends and working upward clears each tangle at its location rather than compressing everything into a larger knot.

Every detangling attempt — fingers, wide-tooth comb, detangling brush — starts at the very tip of the hair and moves toward the root. No exceptions.

### 2. Always detangle wet, with conditioner in

Dry natural hair has no slip. The coils lock against each other at every contact point. Pulling a comb through dry coily hair snaps the hair at every locked point.

Wet hair with conditioner has slip. The conditioner coats the hair shaft and allows tangles to slide apart rather than lock tighter under pressure. The amount of breakage difference between dry detangling and wet-with-conditioner detangling is not small — it's the difference between a handful of short strands in the comb and almost nothing.

Detangle during the wash process, while the conditioner is in the hair. Or detangle on very damp hair with a leave-in or detangling spray as the slip agent.

### 3. Fingers before tools

Fingers can feel resistance. They stop when something doesn't move. They separate tangles by finding the individual strands caught in the knot and freeing them one by one. A comb cannot do this — it applies uniform force, which means every knot that doesn't give becomes a snap.

Work through the section with fingers first, clearing the major tangles. Then use a wide-tooth comb if needed to smooth the section. A fine-tooth comb on natural coily hair is the wrong tool entirely — never use one.

## The Full Detangling Process

**Timing:** during wash day, conditioner in and not yet rinsed. This is when the hair has maximum slip and is easiest to work through.

**Preparation:**
- Section the hair into four to six sections before wetting — this makes the process manageable
- Apply conditioner generously to each section — more than you think you need
- Let the conditioner sit for a few minutes before starting to detangle

**For each section:**
1. Hold the section at mid-length with one hand — this creates a buffer that stops tension from reaching the root
2. Start at the tip with the fingers of the other hand
3. Work through any tangles at the ends before moving up
4. Move up the section incrementally — clear the bottom third, then the middle, then approach the root
5. When fingers have cleared the section, run a wide-tooth comb through from tip to root to smooth

**If you hit a knot that won't clear:**
- Don't force it
- Add more conditioner directly to the knot
- Work around the edges of the knot with fingers, freeing individual strands
- If it's a true single-strand knot (the hair has knotted around itself), the only option is cutting below the knot

## Detangling Tools

**Fingers:** the safest option. Always first.

**Wide-tooth comb:** appropriate for second-pass smoothing on thoroughly finger-detangled hair. Not for initial detangling of significant knots.

**Detangling brush (Tangle Teezer, Denman, flex-bristle):** can be used similarly to a wide-tooth comb — after fingers have cleared major knots, for smoothing. The flexible bristles reduce breakage compared to a standard comb. Not a substitute for the fingers-first stage.

**Fine-tooth comb:** not appropriate for natural coily or curly hair detangling. Do not use.

**Paddle brush:** for straight or loosely wavy hair only. On natural coily hair, a paddle brush catches on every coil and causes breakage.

---

## Frequency

Detangle on wash day. Between wash days, minimise manipulation. Daily detangling causes more cumulative damage than leaving the hair alone between washes.

If the hair has been in a protective style and comes out tangled, a thorough wet detangle with generous conditioner before washing gets the hair back to a workable state.

---

## UK-Specific Note

In hard water areas, mineral deposits on the hair shaft reduce slip — even with conditioner in, the hair feels rougher and detangles less smoothly. Monthly chelating removes mineral deposits and restores the conditioner's slip performance. If detangling has been getting progressively harder over months without changing your routine, chelating before the next wash day often resolves it immediately.

## FAQs

**I detangle in the shower with conditioner and still get breakage. What am I missing?**

Either still starting at the root (old habit), still pulling rather than working through patiently, or the conditioner doesn't have enough slip for your hair type. Try a dedicated detangling conditioner or a silicone-free detangling spray added on top of your regular conditioner.

**My 4C hair is almost impossible to detangle. What am I doing wrong?**

4C hair with very tight coils that haven't been stretched needs more conditioner, more patience, and smaller sections than any other hair type. Try stretching before detangling — a loose twist or braid left overnight while damp makes the hair more manageable for detangling the next day. Also consider whether the hair needs protein — over-stretched (mushy) hair tangles more severely.

**Is it better to detangle before or after washing?**

Both has merit. Detangling before washing removes major tangles before water causes the hair to swell and lock tighter. Detangling during washing (with conditioner in) has the most slip and is the most effective at clearing knots. Most people do a pre-wash finger-detangle to divide into sections, then a thorough conditioner-in detangle during washing.

**How do I detangle a child's natural hair without it being painful?**

Apply a generous amount of conditioner and let it sit for five minutes before starting. Small sections. Fingers first, always. Hold the section below where you're working to prevent pulling at the root. Work in a calm environment — rushing or the child anticipating pain makes the whole process harder. A detangling spray can be more child-friendly than conditioner applied in the shower.
