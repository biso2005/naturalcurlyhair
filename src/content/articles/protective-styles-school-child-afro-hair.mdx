The school-week hair brief is specific: install once, survive five days including at least one PE session, possibly a swimming lesson, outdoor breaks in all UK weather, and still look presentable on Friday. Protective styles are the obvious answer — but which style fits which child, age, and routine matters.

---

## What Makes a Style Work for School

**Durability:** it holds from Monday to Friday without daily redoing. Anything that requires daily product or retwisting is off the table during term time for most families.

**Low daily maintenance:** the child should be able to manage it themselves as they get older. Braids with a puff can be gathered up for PE without help. Cornrows don't need anything between washes.

**Age-appropriate:** for primary school children, comfort matters more than style. For secondary school, the child's own preferences start to lead.

**Scalp access:** the child's scalp should be reachable during the week for a light oil or moisture spray every two to three days. Styles that completely seal off the scalp (some very flat cornrow patterns) make this harder.

---

## Primary School (Ages 4–11)

**Best options:**

**Cornrows:** last two to four weeks, survive PE and swimming, look neat throughout. For younger children especially, a simple cornrow pattern in larger sections is easier to maintain and less tight on fine, still-developing hair. Avoid very small, thin cornrows on young children — the tension is too much for finer hair.

**Two-strand twists with ends gathered:** installed at the weekend, gathered into a puff or pineapple for the week, taken down and redone every two weeks. The puff is a versatile daily style that needs only a satin scrunchie swap.

**Box braids (for children 6+):** practical for school, can be gathered for PE, last four to six weeks. For younger children, extensions should be lightweight — heavy braids pull on developing hairlines. Braid down to the child's own hair if extension weight is a concern.

**Flat twists:** similar to cornrows in durability and school practicality. Slightly more volume than cornrows, which can be easier for children who are sensitive to the flat-against-the-head feel of cornrows.

**What doesn't work for primary school:**
- Wash-and-go styles — frizz by Tuesday and are difficult to manage with playground activity
- Loose afro worn down — tangles significantly over five days, requires daily detangling the child may not want to do
- Elaborate styles that require professional installation every week — not sustainable time or cost-wise

## Secondary School (Ages 11–16)

Secondary school introduces new variables: the child has more autonomy over their hair, PE is more intensive, and peer culture around hair becomes more significant.

**Practical styles:**

**Knotless braids:** currently the most popular choice among secondary school girls. Lower tension than box braids, look more polished, last four to six weeks. Can be gathered for PE. Allow the child more styling options (half-up, puff, or down) during the week.

**Cornrows or flat twists:** lower-maintenance than braids, suit children who don't want a big style or who prefer shorter styling sessions.

**Puff or bun of natural hair:** for secondary school students who prefer to wear their natural hair, a consistent gathered style (puff, bun, or pineapple with a satin scrunchie) is the lowest-maintenance option between wash days. Not fully protective but significantly reduces daily manipulation.

**Locs:** for students who have chosen locs as a long-term style, secondary school is a common starting point. Low daily maintenance once the style is established. See the separate locs articles for what to expect in the first year.

**Beads:** common among primary and secondary age children. Beads add weight — especially heavier wooden or metal beads — which can cause traction at the attachment point over time. Lighter plastic beads, not too many per braid, and styles that are not too tight at the root are the practical guidelines.

## PE and Sports

For most school PE, a gathered style (puff, pineapple, low bun) is fine. For sport-specific requirements:

- **Swimming:** pre-soak with conditioner, cornrow or twist before the session, rinse immediately after. → [Protecting Afro hair in chlorine swimming pools UK](/parents/protect-afro-hair-swimming-chlorine-uk/)
- **Gymnastics, dance, martial arts:** a style that sits close to the head avoids equipment snagging — cornrows, flat twists, or a low bun rather than a high puff
- **Football, rugby, or contact sports:** schools may ask for hair to be secured flat to the head under a sports cap or headband. Cornrows or flat twists under a satin-lined sports cap are the practical solution

## Scalp Care During School Protective Styles

Schools don't get a pass on scalp maintenance. The child's scalp still needs moisture two to three times a week, regardless of what style they're wearing.

For primary-age children: a parent does this at home in the evening — a light oil or diluted leave-in on the scalp with a cotton bud or fingertip applicator takes two minutes.

For secondary-age children: they can do this themselves, but may need reminding. A small bottle of scalp oil in their bag makes it easier.

## FAQs

**My daughter wants to wear her hair in a huge afro puff to school. The school says it's too big. What do I do?**

An afro puff is a natural style. A school cannot lawfully require it to be reduced. See the rights article. → [Afro hair discrimination at school UK](/parents/afro-hair-discrimination-school-uk-rights/)

**My son has started secondary school and is self-conscious about his hair taking long to do. What's the quickest option?**

A low-maintenance natural style — a puff or bun — that he can do himself in under a minute once he knows how. Alternatively, cornrows done at home or at a barber every two to three weeks that need nothing between visits. Boys' school-appropriate styles that require daily intervention rarely survive contact with a teenager's morning routine.

**My daughter's school wants all hair off the face for a photo. Her puff covers her forehead. What do we do?**

A headband that pushes the puff back, a pineapple that gathers the hair to the top and back, or a loose twist-out gathered at the sides. This is a reasonable request and achievable without changing the hair type or style.

**Are extensions appropriate for primary school age?**

Lightweight extensions for age 7+ are generally fine. Very heavy or very long extensions on young children create traction their finer hair isn't built for. Braid down to the child's own hair length if extension weight is a concern.
