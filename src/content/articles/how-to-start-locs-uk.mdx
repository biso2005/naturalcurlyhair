Most locs content shows the end result — mature, defined, beautiful locs — and skips the part where the hair looks like nothing in particular for six months to a year. That gap between "I started my locs" and "my locs look like locs" is the part most people aren't prepared for, and it's why so many people give up during the budding and teen stages.

This guide is about that honest first year.

---

## Before You Start: What Locs Actually Are

Locs are hair that has been encouraged to knit together into rope-like strands. Unlike any other style, they cannot be undone without cutting the locked portion off (or a very long and damaging unlocking process that doesn't always work). This is not a commitment to try something for a season. Most people who start locs expect to have them for years to decades.

That's not a reason not to start — but it's worth being clear about before you do.

## Starting Methods

### Two-strand twists

The most common starting method for type 3C–4C hair. Two sections of hair twisted around each other, left to loc over time. The twist provides the initial structure that the hair locs around. Easy to do at home or at a salon. The twists unravel in the early months before they loc — this is normal.

### Coils (palm rolling / comb coils)

Hair wound into tight coils with a comb or fingers. Works best on type 4 coily hair that coils naturally. Faster to install than twists. The coil shape becomes the starter loc. Also unravels in the early months.

### Interlocking

A method where new growth is pulled through the base of the loc using a small tool, rather than retwisted on top. More secure at the root, less unravelling in the early stages. Preferred by some for its staying power during the early months.

### Freeform

No method — hair allowed to mat and loc naturally with washing and minimal manipulation. Takes longer to loc and the final result is less uniform. The process itself is part of the aesthetic for many who choose this.

### Sisterlocks

A specific proprietary system with very small, uniform locs created with a patented tool. Requires a trained Sisterlocks consultant and is significantly more expensive than standard starter locs. See separate article.

## Salon vs Home Start

Starting locs at a salon gives you even sizing, better technique on the first install, and a cleaner starting point. The cost varies significantly — in UK cities, starter locs can range from £80 to £300+ depending on length, density, and salon.

Starting at home is possible with patience and the right technique, particularly with two-strand twists. The downsides: uneven sizing is harder to correct later, and technique errors at the start can affect how the locs form.

**Recommendation:** if budget allows, start at a salon with a loctician who has experience with your hair type. One appointment is enough — maintenance from there can be done at home.

## The First Year, Honestly

### Stage 1: Starter locs (weeks 1–8)

Your hair is in twists, coils, or interlocks. It looks like a style, not yet like locs. The ends are unravelled or loose. There is no "locs look" yet.

What happens: the hair begins to tangle internally within each section. You might see new growth starting to mat at the root. The twists or coils may partially unravel, especially around the hairline and at the nape.

What you do: wash gently without disturbing the sections. Let them be.

### Stage 2: Budding (months 2–6)

The interior of each starter loc begins to knit together. The base starts to feel firmer than the ends. The overall appearance is often described as "messy" — the sections don't look like twists anymore but don't look like locs yet either.

This is the stage most people find psychologically difficult. The hair looks like neither a neat style nor finished locs. It just looks unfinished.

What happens: the loc is forming from the root outward. The ends may still be unravelled. New growth at the root looks loose.

What you do: continue gentle washing. Retwist if using the twist method, but loosely. Don't try to force the locs to look neater — manipulation at this stage interrupts the locking process.

### Stage 3: Teen locs (months 6–12)

The locs have a defined shape. They're firmer. The ends are still loose on many. The locs may look uneven in thickness along their length — thicker at sections where the knitting is more advanced, thinner at sections where it's less. The hairline locs often lag behind the rest.

What you do: continue washing, retwisting new growth as needed. Patience.

### Stage 4: Mature locs (month 12 onwards)

The interior of each loc is fully knotted from root to tip. The ends are closed. The locs hang with weight and move as a unit rather than as strands. Definition and pattern are stable.

For most people on type 4 hair with the twist or coil method, this takes 12–18 months. For some it's longer.

## Washing Locs in the UK

Locs should be washed. There is a persistent myth that locs don't need washing or that washing delays locking. Neither is true. Dirty locs loc, but they also smell, carry buildup, and attract mould if dried improperly.

**How often:** every seven to fourteen days, depending on scalp oiliness and activity level.

**How to wash:**
1. Get the locs fully saturated with water — locs are dense and water takes time to penetrate
2. Apply a clarifying or residue-free shampoo diluted in water (apply to the scalp primarily, let it rinse through the locs)
3. Massage the scalp; squeeze the shampoo through each loc rather than rubbing
4. Rinse very thoroughly — residue left in locs causes buildup and mildew over time
5. Squeeze excess water from the locs
6. Dry fully before sleeping — this is non-negotiable

**Drying:** locs hold water far longer than loose hair. Air drying takes significantly longer. In UK winter, locs may not air dry completely before bedtime. A hooded dryer or diffuser used after air drying for most of the time ensures the interior is fully dry. Sleeping on damp locs develops mildew inside the loc — it smells musty and is very difficult to address once established.

## Hard Water in the UK

This is the factor most UK locs resources don't address. In hard water areas — London, the South East, East Anglia, the Midlands — calcium and magnesium minerals from tap water deposit inside locs every time you wash. Over months, this accumulation makes locs feel hard, rough, and heavy. Unlike loose hair, you can't easily get a chelating product into the interior of a loc to remove it.

Prevention is significantly easier than correction. Monthly chelating from the start — before significant mineral accumulation — keeps the interior of the locs clear.

## What to Expect at the Hairline

Hairline locs — the fine, short hairs at the temples, edges, and nape — always lag behind the rest. They're shorter, finer, and more prone to unravelling. They will look loose and unfinished long after the rest of the locs look mature. This is normal. They usually catch up by month 12–18.

Don't try to force the hairline to loc faster by retwisting tightly. Traction alopecia from tight retwisting at the edges is common and reversible only if caught early.

## FAQs

**How long until my locs look like locs?**

Most people on type 4 hair can expect 12–18 months to mature locs. For type 3 hair, which locs more slowly, it can be 18–24 months. Individual variation is significant — hair density, texture, washing frequency, and the starting method all affect the timeline.

**Can I colour my hair while starting locs?**

Better to start with already-coloured hair than to colour during the early stages. Bleaching or colouring disrupts the locking process by damaging the cuticle, and chemical processes on very new locs can cause breakage. Wait until the locs have budded (month four to six minimum) before any chemical colour.

**My starter locs keep unravelling. What do I do?**

Unravelling during the budding stage is normal. The hair hasn't knotted enough yet to hold the twist together. Light retwisting after washing helps. Avoid styles that pull the locs or put them under stress. If the unravelling is severe and happening all over, ask a loctician to check whether the sections are large enough to loc properly — very small sections can take much longer.

**Can I swim with starter locs?**

Yes, with a twist cap or swimming cap to protect from chlorine. Rinse the locs thoroughly after swimming and wash as soon as practical. Chlorine buildup and salt water both affect locs differently than loose hair — they can accelerate locking (sometimes too fast or unevenly) and cause dryness.
