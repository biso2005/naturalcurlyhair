Natural hair under a wig is in a sealed environment. No air circulation, no natural moisture from the environment, no access for product application. Without a deliberate maintenance routine, the natural hair dries out, the scalp builds up, and you take the wig off weeks later to find hair that needs significant recovery.

The maintenance routine for natural hair under a wig is not complicated — it's five minutes every two to three days and a proper wash once a week. Most people skip it because the hair is out of sight.

---

## The Routine

### Every two to three days: moisture and scalp access

Take the wig and wig cap off. Take five minutes.

1. Spritz the natural hair with water — enough to make it slightly damp
2. Apply a small amount of leave-in conditioner or a light scalp oil to the scalp and the natural hair underneath
3. Massage the scalp lightly
4. Put the wig cap and wig back on

That's it. This takes five minutes and is the difference between healthy natural hair at wig removal and dried-out, brittle hair.

**UK winter note:** central heating dry air makes the under-wig environment drier. Every two days rather than three during the heating season.

### Every one to two weeks: proper wash

Take the wig off. Wash the natural hair and scalp properly.

1. Remove the wig and wig cap
2. Wash the scalp with a gentle shampoo — focus on the scalp, let it rinse through the natural hair
3. Condition — apply generously, work through with fingers, detangle gently
4. Rinse thoroughly
5. Apply leave-in to damp hair
6. Style the natural hair in the under-wig style you use (flat twists, braids, or loose)
7. Dry fully before putting the wig back on — wet natural hair under a wig cap develops mildew over days

**Hard water note:** if you're in London or another hard water area and washing weekly or more, monthly chelating keeps mineral deposits from accumulating on the natural hair under the wig.

## Styling the Hair Under the Wig

The natural hair underneath needs to be in a style that:
- Lies flat against the head for the wig to sit correctly
- Doesn't add tension to the hairline
- Stays moisturised between wash days

**Best under-wig styles:**
- Flat twists — four to six large flat twists lying flat against the scalp. Simple to do, last the week between washes, don't add tension
- Large cornrows — braided flat, no extensions. More durable than flat twists; last one to two weeks
- Loose hair under a satin wig cap — works for shorter natural hair; slightly less predictable in terms of how the wig sits

**What not to do:**
- Tight braids near the hairline — the natural hair doesn't need tension on top of the wig's attachment pressure
- Slicked-down edges with hard-hold gel, left for days — the gel dries and breaks the fragile hairline hairs
- Leaving the natural hair completely loose and matted under the cap — it tangles and requires aggressive detangling at wash time

## Taking the Wig Off at Night

The natural hair under the wig needs to breathe. Taking the wig off every evening:
- Allows the scalp to air
- Removes the attachment pressure from the hairline
- Creates a moment for moisture application if the hair feels dry

Sleeping in a wig is not recommended for natural hair health. The natural hair under the wig is compressed against the cap for eight hours, without moisture or air circulation.

**If you must keep the wig on:** a satin-lined wig cap and a satin pillowcase at minimum. But removal is better.

---

## Checking the Edges

When you take the wig off — whether in the evening or at wash day — check the edges.

**Signs to watch for:**
- The hairline looking thinner than the last time you checked
- Small bumps or sensitivity at the temples or nape (attachment pressure points)
- Edge hairs that seem shorter than they used to be

If any of these are developing, the attachment method for the wig needs to change before you continue wearing it.

## FAQs

**Do I need to deep condition my natural hair while wearing a wig?**

Not every week, but periodically. Every two to three weeks, when the wig comes off for washing, do a deep condition under heat before re-styling. The hair under the wig is in a sealed environment that can dry it out over time; periodic deep conditioning restores it.

**My natural hair is longer now and doesn't fit under the wig cap easily. What do I do?**

Flat twists that cover the full head create a flatter surface. For very long natural hair, multiple flat twists or small cornrows and a larger wig cap size. Trying to force long natural hair into a small cap compresses it too tightly and increases scalp pressure.

**I wear a wig daily. How often should I really be washing the natural hair underneath?**

Every seven to ten days at minimum. Daily wig wearing means the scalp is covered most of the time — sebum accumulates faster than with loose hair. Some people with active scalps need every five to seven days.

**What's the best wig cap for natural hair?**

A satin-lined wig cap — satin against the natural hair, with a nylon or mesh outer layer for grip. Nylon directly against the natural hair creates friction and removes moisture. Satin reduces both. Available online and in some Afro hair BSS stores in the UK.
