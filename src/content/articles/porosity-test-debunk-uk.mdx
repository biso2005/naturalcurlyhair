## The honest position

The float test does not measure what you have been told it measures.

That is not a hedged opinion. It is the position of every chemist and trichologist who has looked at it carefully, stated clearly and on record. Dr Michelle Wong — chemistry PhD and the author of Lab Muffin Beauty Science — published her analysis in January 2026. Her finding: the float test detects damage to the surface of hair, not internal porosity. "These tests actually check for damage to the surface of hair," she writes.

What she means by that is worth understanding, because it changes what you should actually pay attention to.

## What the float test is measuring instead

Hair floats or sinks based on surface tension — the tendency of water molecules to hold onto each other at the surface rather than letting objects break through. Undamaged hair has a thin oily layer on the outside of each cuticle scale. That layer repels water, so the hair strand sits on the surface the way a dry leaf does.

When that outer layer is damaged — by heat, chemical processing, or the normal weathering that happens to the ends of a strand over years — the surface becomes more attractive to water molecules. Water-loving exposed cuticle disrupts the surface tension, and the strand sinks.

That is a measurement of surface damage. It has nothing to do with how well water and product move into the cortex of the strand, which is what porosity actually describes.

Three things compound this further.

**Product coatings change the result.** If there is any oil, silicone, or leave-in on the strand, it adds an artificial hydrophobic barrier. Hair that would otherwise sink floats instead. Wong notes specifically that using oils leads to a "low porosity" result regardless of actual internal structure. Most readers testing their hair have product residue on the strand even after washing — a single shampoo does not remove silicone buildup, and many shampoos leave conditioning agents behind.

**The result varies along a single strand.** The tip of your hair is older and has weathered more than the root. Test the tip and you may get "high porosity". Test the root and you may get "low porosity". Neither result is representative of "your hair" — it is representative of one point on one strand at one moment.

**Water temperature matters.** Cold water contracts the cuticle, hot water opens it. The test gives different readings at different temperatures. No standardised protocol exists for how warm the water should be, because the float test was not developed from a published protocol at all. It spread through online forums and into brand blogs from there. No trichology textbook validated it; it migrated outward from community advice.

> *"If you're a hairdresser — please don't use these tests to judge how long you need for chemical treatments!"*
> — Dr Michelle Wong, Lab Muffin Beauty Science, January 2026

Wong is talking about the consequences of misreading surface damage as porosity in a professional context. The same misread leads home users to the wrong product aisle.

**What actually matters**

Neither low-porosity nor high-porosity routines will help if your diagnosis was wrong from the start. And the behaviour your hair is showing — the thing you are actually trying to fix — has nothing to do with what happened in a glass of water.

## The behaviour check (30 seconds, this weekend)

Put the glass away. Pay attention to these three things during your next wash day.

**How fast does your hair get wet?**

Stand under the shower and time how long it takes for your hair to go from dry to fully saturated. If water beads up and runs off the surface for the first 30 seconds or more before the hair starts to absorb, your hair is behaving low-porosity-like. If it soaks through almost immediately, it is behaving high-porosity-like.

This is not a diagnosis. It is a starting observation.

**How heavy a product can your hair take before it pills or flakes?**

Apply your usual leave-in or cream. By the next morning, run your fingers through your hair. If you find white or grey balls of product — pilling — or if the product has flaked off in dry patches, your hair rejected the weight. That is low-porosity behaviour: the moisture could not get in, so the product sat on top and dried there. If a rich butter absorbs cleanly but your hair still feels dry within 24 hours, that is high-porosity behaviour: moisture went in but did not stay.

**How long does wet hair stay wet?**

Air-dry without diffusing after your wash. High-porosity hair often dries fully in 30–45 minutes because water escapes as fast as it enters. Low-porosity hair can stay damp for two hours or more because the sealed cuticle slows evaporation both ways.

### What to do with the answer

**If your hair behaves low-porosity-like:**
Apply leave-in to damp, warm hair — not dripping wet, not cold. Heat opens the cuticle slightly and lets product in. Use lighter layers (the LOC method, liquid before oil before cream, works well here). Avoid heavy butters as a first layer; they will sit on top before anything lighter can get through.

**If your hair behaves high-porosity-like:**
Seal with a heavier butter or oil on the lengths *after* the leave-in, not instead of it. The leave-in adds moisture; the sealant slows how fast it leaves. Protein treatments help here too — they temporarily fill gaps in the cuticle and slow moisture loss. But introduce protein carefully: too much causes brittleness, and high-porosity hair is already under stress.

**Success marker:** within one wash day, hair feels less coated (if low-porosity-like) or holds moisture for longer into the week (if high-porosity-like). If nothing improves after two consistent wash days following this approach, the issue is more likely product buildup or hard water than porosity — and those have separate routes.

**One thing this does not fix**

If your hair is snapping, breaking mid-shaft, or feels brittle regardless of how much product you use, that is not a porosity question. That is the protein-moisture balance, and the check for it is different — stretch a single wet strand gently and watch what happens. If it snaps without stretching, it needs protein. If it stretches and does not spring back, it needs moisture. There is a separate piece on this when it is written; in the meantime, the [diagnostic tool](/tool/) will route you correctly from your symptom.

If scalp symptoms are accompanying the dryness — flaking, itching, sensitivity — route to the scalp track rather than continuing to adjust product weight.
