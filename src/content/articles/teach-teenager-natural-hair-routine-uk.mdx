At some point, the hair care routine shifts from something you do to your child to something they do themselves. That transition is smoother when it's deliberate rather than when it happens by default — when they're at university and their hair is in a state.

This guide is about making that transition well: what to teach, when, and how to hand over without the routine falling apart.

---

## Why This Matters

Teenagers with natural curly or coily hair who don't know how to manage it independently often end up in one of two places: they neglect it and the condition deteriorates over years, or they switch to a low-maintenance chemical process (relaxer, permanent colour) because that's easier to manage without knowing what they're doing.

A teenager who understands their hair — what type it is, why it needs moisture, how to detangle, what products work and why — has the foundation to manage it well through adulthood. The time to build that is before they leave home.

---

## When to Start Handing Over

Earlier than feels comfortable. Most teenagers can start participating meaningfully in their own hair care from age ten or eleven.

The progression:
- **Ages 8–10:** they apply their own leave-in and understand why. They know what their hair type is and roughly how it works.
- **Ages 10–13:** they can complete most of wash day with you supervising. They're choosing the styles they want. They know what happens if they skip the satin bonnet.
- **Ages 13–16:** they're doing most of it independently. You're available if they need help or the routine isn't working.
- **Ages 16+:** full independence. They buy their own products, manage their own routine, and troubleshoot problems.

## What to Actually Teach

### The fundamentals

**Why the hair needs moisture:** natural coily and curly hair doesn't receive the scalp's oils along the shaft the way straight hair does. They need to add external moisture, regularly. This is the single most important concept.

**Why detangling on dry hair breaks it:** the structure of the curl or coil concentrates tension at individual points. Detangling on wet hair with conditioner distributes the force and reduces breakage.

**Why satin matters:** cotton pulls moisture out and creates friction. Satin doesn't. One minute with a bonnet prevents an hour of extra work on wash day.

### The routine

Teach the steps in order. Let them do each step while you watch before they do it alone. This is not about doing it perfectly — it's about understanding the sequence and the reason for each step.

**Wash day:**
1. Section the hair
2. Wet thoroughly
3. Shampoo at the scalp
4. Condition mid-length to ends
5. Detangle with conditioner in, from tips upward
6. Rinse
7. Apply leave-in to soaking-wet hair
8. Dry without touching

**Between wash days:**
- Spritz with water when hair feels dry
- Apply a small amount of leave-in
- Satin bonnet at night

### How to troubleshoot

This is the part most teaching skips. When the routine stops working, they need to know why.

- Hair feels dry despite moisturising → porosity, hard water, or products too light
- Detangling is harder than usual → skipped bonnet recently, or needs to chelate
- Products stopped working → mineral buildup from hard water
- Hair breaking more than usual → too much tension from styles, or needs protein

Teaching them to diagnose by symptom rather than just follow a recipe makes them genuinely independent.

## Hard Water

If they're going to university in London, the South East, or the Midlands, they need to know about hard water before they go. The hard water in most university towns will degrade their routine within three to six months if they don't chelate monthly. Tell them before it happens.

## Products

Go through your teenager's products with them. What each one does. Why you use that one and not a different one. What would happen if they ran out and had to substitute.

If they're going to be buying their own products, they need to know what they're looking for, not just which specific bottle to buy. The bottle will change; the ingredient criteria won't.

---

## What Doesn't Help

**Doing it for them until they leave home:** they'll leave knowing nothing. Do it with them, then watch them do it, then let them do it alone.

**Correcting every decision:** if they try a different product and it doesn't work perfectly, let them experience that and figure out why. The mistake is more instructive than the correct answer.

**Making it about appearance:** "You need to do your hair so you look presentable" is less useful than "You need to do your hair so it stays healthy and manageable." The second framing builds toward their own reasons.

## FAQs

**My teenager has decided they're not interested in their hair routine. What do I do?**

Short-term neglect doesn't cause permanent damage. Long-term neglect leads to matting and breakage that's harder to address. Let them experience the consequences of skipping the routine — that's the most effective motivation. Be available when they ask for help rather than imposing.

**They want to do their hair differently from how I've been doing it. Should I let them?**

Yes, within reason. They're finding their own approach. As long as the fundamentals are there — moisture, gentle detangling, protective practices — the specific products and techniques can vary. If they're doing something that's clearly damaging (very tight styles, no moisture, never washing), say so once and leave them with the information.

**They're going to university and I'm worried they won't manage. What should I do now?**

A few wash days doing it together and talking through the troubleshooting are worth more than any amount of instruction. Consider booking them a natural hair stylist appointment near the university for their first year — someone they can go to when the routine isn't working and they need help.

**My teenager doesn't know their hair type. Where do we start?**

Start here on the site — the curl type guides are organised by pattern. Identifying their type isn't strictly necessary to care for the hair well (the routine fundamentals apply across types), but knowing it gives them a vocabulary and a shortcut to relevant advice.
