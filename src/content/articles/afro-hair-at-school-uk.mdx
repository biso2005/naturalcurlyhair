School is harder on natural hair than most other environments — PE, swimming, wind, hats, and five days of wearing the same style without a wash day take a toll. This guide covers what actually survives a school week, what the law says about hair policies, and how to handle it when a school gets things wrong.

---

## Styles That Survive the Week

The key requirement for school styles is durability without daily intervention. A style installed on Sunday should still be acceptable on Friday without significant retwisting, product reapplication, or restyling.

**High-durability styles (Monday to Friday without intervention):**
- Cornrows or flat twists (two to four weeks)
- Box braids or knotless braids (four to eight weeks)
- Two-strand twists with a bun or puff at the end
- Bantu knots left in (not unravelled as a knot-out)
- Neat puff or pineapple secured with a satin scrunchie

**Styles that need daily work and don't survive school:**
- Wash-and-go on type 4 hair — frizzes significantly by day two
- Twist-outs or braid-outs — definition collapses quickly in UK outdoor air
- Loose hair on very coily hair types — high daily manipulation, tangles accumulate

**Practical tip:** Sunday evening install with a fresh cornrow or braid style, taken down and redone every two to three weeks, is the routine most primary school families settle on because it's the best tradeoff of durability, scalp access, and hair health.

## PE, Wind, and Hats

**PE:** braids and cornrows survive PE well. A loose puff or afro can be gathered into a pineapple or low puff for sports. For gymnastics or dance, a style that stays close to the head (cornrows, flat twists, neat bun) is easier than a high puff under a roll mat.

**School hats and uniform:** if a school hat or sports cap sits tightly on a high puff or braids, the daily removal and replacement creates traction on the hairline. A satin cap liner under a school hat reduces friction. Alternatively, a style that sits lower (cornrows or braids without a gathered puff) fits more easily under a cap.

**Outdoor air in UK winters:** November to March, the hair absorbs outdoor cold and then meets central-heated indoor air repeatedly during the school day. This cycling dries the hair faster than any other season. A sealed style (braids, twists) performs better in winter than a loose style.

## School Policies and Your Child's Rights

The law is clear: natural Afro-textured hair is a protected characteristic under the Equality Act 2010 (race). A school that tells a Black child their natural hair is against policy, that they must change their hairstyle, or that their braids are not permitted is likely in breach of the Act.

The 2023 government guidance on the Equality Act makes this explicit in the school context. Headteachers who claim their uniform policy is race-neutral need to demonstrate that it does not have a disproportionate impact on a particular racial group — and a policy that restricts cornrows, braids, afros, or locs clearly does.

**What's covered:**
- Natural Afro-textured hair (afro, puff, coily styles)
- Protective styles — cornrows, braids, twists, locs
- Hair accessories associated with these styles (beads, satin scrunchies)

**What schools can legitimately require:**
- Hair tied back for specific safety reasons in science labs, food tech, or PE with equipment
- Hair out of the eyes for practical safety

**What schools cannot legitimately require:**
- That Afro-textured hair be straightened or relaxed to meet a "neat" standard
- That cornrows or braids be removed or not worn
- That natural styles be altered to match a Eurocentric hair norm

## School Swimming

Swimming is a significant stressor for coily and Afro-textured hair. Chlorine strips moisture rapidly and leaves the hair brittle. Cold pool water causes immediate shrinkage and subsequent tangles on hair that's not protected.

**Before swimming:**
- Apply a generous amount of conditioner to the hair — wet, conditioned hair absorbs less chlorinated water than dry hair
- Put hair in cornrows or twists before the swimming session
- A swim cap over the top reduces (but does not eliminate) chlorine exposure

**After swimming:**
- Rinse the hair with clean water immediately — the sooner the chlorine is out, the less time it has to strip the hair
- A post-swim leave-in conditioner and resealing is needed if hair comes out

## FAQs

**My child's school has told them their afro is too big. What do I do?**

This is very likely unlawful under the Equality Act 2010. An afro is a natural characteristic of Afro-textured hair. Write to the school in writing (not just a conversation), citing the Equality Act and the 2023 government guidance. If the school doesn't respond appropriately, escalate to the academy trust, the local authority, or contact Acas (0300 123 1100) for guidance.

**The school says cornrows breach their uniform policy. Can they do that?**

Only if they can demonstrate the policy applies equally across racial groups and has no disproportionate impact on Black pupils — which a cornrow restriction clearly fails. Cornrows are directly associated with Black hair and culture. A school banning cornrows is almost certainly in breach of the Equality Act 2010.

**My child is the only Black child in their class and is self-conscious about their hair. How do I help?**

This is a bigger question than policy. For practical support at home: make sure they see natural hair celebrated — books, YouTube channels, community groups where their hair type is normal, not unusual. At school: speak with the teacher about inclusive representation in the classroom. If the issue is specific bullying or comments from other children, that's a safeguarding matter to raise formally with the school.

**My child's teacher touched their hair without asking. What should I say?**

Raise it with the class teacher directly first — sometimes this is thoughtless rather than malicious, but it needs to stop. If it continues, escalate to the head. A child's right to bodily autonomy applies at school. For older children, having the words to say "please don't touch my hair" is a useful tool to give them.
