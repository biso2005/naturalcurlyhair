## Starting locs

**What are the different ways to start locs?**
The main methods: **freeform** (let the hair naturally loc without manipulation — takes longest, most organic result), **two-strand twists** (twisted sections that will eventually loc together), **interlocking** (threading new growth through the base of the loc), **comb coils** (using a comb to create tight coils that loc together), **sister locs** (a specific small-loc installation using a patented grid pattern). The right method depends on your hair texture, desired loc size, and how much salon involvement you want.

**How long does it take locs to fully mature?**
Typically 12–24 months from installation to fully mature, locked locs that don't unravel when wet. The timeline varies by method, hair texture, and maintenance frequency. 4C hair usually locs faster than looser curl types. The stages: starter locs → budding (locs begin to swell and fuzz) → teenage (awkward, fluffy, not yet fully locked) → mature.

**How long does my hair need to be to start locs?**
A minimum of about 5cm for comb coils or twists, though more length (8–10cm) makes the process easier and the starter locs more durable. Interlocking can be done with slightly shorter hair. Sister locs require enough hair to create the grid pattern — typically 5–7cm minimum.

**Can I start locs on my own or do I need a loctician?**
You can start freeform locs on your own — it requires no installation, just washing and leaving the hair to loc naturally. For installed locs (twists, comb coils, interlocking), a good loctician produces cleaner, more consistent results and gives you a proper foundation. For your first retwist, watching an experienced loctician do it is worth the cost.

**What's a loctician and how do I find one in the UK?**
A loctician is a stylist who specialises in loc installation and maintenance. Not all natural hair stylists are experienced with locs — ask specifically about their loc experience and ask to see photos. → [Finding an Afro hairdresser in the UK.](/hair-care/afro-hairdresser-near-me-uk/)

## Maintenance

**How often should I wash my locs?**
Every one to two weeks for most people. Washing too infrequently causes build-up and smell; washing too frequently can prevent budding locs from locking (the constant moisture loosens them). In the UK, hard water is a particular consideration — mineral build-up in locs is very difficult to remove once established.

**Do I need to use special loc products?**
Not necessarily. The basics: a residue-free shampoo (look for shampoos that say "residue-free" or "for locs" — regular shampoos can leave build-up inside locs that's hard to remove), a light oil for scalp moisture between washes, and for starter locs a residue-free gel or cream for retwisting. Avoid heavy waxes and butters inside locs — the interior doesn't dry properly and residue accumulates.

**How often should I retwist?**
Every four to eight weeks for most people, depending on how fast your hair grows and how much new growth you're comfortable with before tightening. Retwisting too frequently — every week or two — puts repeated tension on the base of the loc and can thin the root over time. Too infrequently causes the locs to merge at the roots.

**Should I interlock or palm roll?**
Palm rolling is gentler and creates a rounded, smooth loc. It's a surface technique — it doesn't lock the interior of the loc. Interlocking is more secure and better for people who swim or wash very frequently (the loc stays tight longer), but incorrect interlocking technique causes thinning at the base. Both are valid; the right choice depends on your lifestyle and your loctician's recommendation.

**Can I swim with locs?**
Yes. Rinse with clean water before entering the pool to reduce chlorine uptake (wet hair absorbs less chlorine than dry hair). Shampoo thoroughly after swimming. In the UK's outdoor lido and wild swimming scene, this is increasingly a practical question. Chlorine damage accumulates over time in locs — regular rinsing and washing after swimming manages it.

## Hard water and locs (UK-specific)

**Does hard water affect locs differently than loose natural hair?**
Yes, and more severely. Mineral deposits that accumulate on loose hair can be removed with chelating shampoo. In locs, mineral deposits accumulate inside the loc where they're much harder to reach. Over time, hard water causes locs to feel rough, rigid, and stiff — a different texture than healthy, flexible mature locs. Prevention is far easier than removal.

**How do I prevent hard water build-up in my locs?**
Use a chelating shampoo (not just a clarifying shampoo) monthly. A shower filter reduces the mineral content at source. Rinse thoroughly after washing — mineral deposits enter the loc with the rinsing water as much as the washing water. → [Check your water hardness.](/tools/water-hardness/)

**My locs feel stiff and rigid. Is that hard water build-up?**
Possibly. Stiff, brittle-feeling locs in a hard water area, particularly if the hair colour has also changed slightly (mineral deposits can slightly discolour locs), is consistent with mineral build-up. An apple cider vinegar rinse (diluted — one part ACV to three parts water) can help with surface deposits, but deep mineral build-up inside established locs is very difficult to remove. Prevention matters more.

## Common problems

**My locs smell musty. What causes it and how do I fix it?**
Almost always locs that didn't dry fully after washing. The inside of a loc is dense and takes a long time to dry — sitting with a satin bonnet or headwrap over damp locs for hours creates an environment for bacterial growth. Dry locs fully before covering: air dry in a warm room, use a hooded dryer, or a diffuser on low. If the smell persists after improving drying, clarify with a residue-free shampoo.

**My locs are thinning at the roots. What's happening?**
Root thinning is the most common serious problem with locs and usually comes from one of two causes: retwisting too frequently (repeated tension at the base), or interlocking done incorrectly. Reduce retwist frequency. If interlocking is causing it, consider switching to palm rolling or a gentler interlocking technique. If thinning has progressed significantly, a loctician can advise on whether the loc can be reinforced or whether it needs to be combined with a neighbouring loc.

**Can I colour my locs?**
Yes. Locs can be coloured with standard hair dye — the result is similar to colouring loose natural hair. Bleaching locs removes pigment but also weakens the structure; this is riskier in locs than in loose hair because the interior of the loc is harder to deep condition after bleaching. Henna is a safer option for colour without chemical damage, and it's popular in the UK loc community.

**Can I undo my locs?**
Starter locs (under six months old, not fully matured) can often be carefully unravelled with conditioner and patience. Mature locs generally can't be unlocked without cutting. This is a widely misunderstood point — "you can always undo them" is only true in the early stages.
