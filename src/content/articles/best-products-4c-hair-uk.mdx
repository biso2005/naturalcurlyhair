## Shampoo

### What 4C hair needs from a shampoo

The goal is to clean without stripping. 4C hair already struggles to hold moisture — a harsh shampoo makes that harder. Most 4C hair does better with a gentle, sulphate-free cleanser for regular washes, and a separate clarifying or chelating shampoo once a month.

**For regular washes:** look for a shampoo where the first few ingredients are water, a gentle cleanser, and something moisturising like aloe vera or glycerin.

**For monthly resets:** you need two different things depending on your problem. A clarifying shampoo removes product build-up — gels, creams, silicones. A chelating shampoo removes mineral deposits from hard water. They're not the same thing, and if you're in a hard water area (most of England), you need the chelating one. Check the label for EDTA or phytic acid.

**Avoid:** anything that leaves your hair squeaky after rinsing. Squeaky on 4C hair means stripped.

## Deep Conditioner

### The protein vs moisture question

4C hair needs both, but not at the same time and not in equal measure.

**Moisture treatments** — the weekly baseline for most 4C hair. They restore softness, reduce tangles, and improve how the hair feels day to day.

**Protein treatments** — useful when the hair is showing signs of damage: snapping when wet, feeling mushy, or losing its spring. Use them every four to six weeks as a check-in, not every wash.

**Too much protein** makes 4C hair brittle and prone to snapping — the opposite of what you want. If your hair feels stiff and breaks easily after a protein treatment, that's protein overload and you need to swing back to moisture.

### What to look for

The most important quality in a 4C deep conditioner is slip — the lubricated feeling that lets a comb or fingers move through the hair without catching. This is where a lot of otherwise decent conditioners fall short on tighter textures. If you can't feel the product reducing friction when you run it through a section, it won't help during detangling.

Heat makes a real difference. A shower cap plus a warm towel, a heat cap, or a steamer gives conditioner a much better chance of absorbing rather than sitting on the surface.

## Leave-In Conditioner

The leave-in is your moisture foundation — everything else sits on top of it. Apply it to hair that's still dripping wet, not just damp. The wetter, the better for 4C hair.

**For low-porosity 4C hair** (products tend to sit on top rather than absorbing): a lightweight, liquid leave-in works better than a thick cream. Apply with a heat cap if moisture isn't sinking in.

**For high-porosity 4C hair** (hair absorbs fast but loses moisture fast too): a thicker cream leave-in gives more staying power.

Either way, the first ingredient should be water. If it's an oil or butter, it's a sealant, not a leave-in.

## Oils and Sealants

Oils don't moisturise. They seal. The distinction matters because using oil before water-based products blocks moisture from getting in. Use it after.

**Lighter oils** (jojoba, argan, grapeseed) are better for low-porosity hair or as a scalp treatment. They absorb more readily and are less likely to cause build-up.

**Heavier sealants** (castor oil, shea butter, mango butter) sit on the surface and lock moisture in — useful for high-porosity hair that loses moisture quickly. On low-porosity hair they can cause build-up if used heavily.

A tip for getting oil onto a dense 4C scalp: a bottle with a nozzle applicator makes parting and direct application much easier than pouring into your palm and trying to work it in.

## Stylers: Creams, Gels, and Butters

**Curl creams** give softness and some definition without a hard cast. Good for wash-and-go styles on 4C hair, though they tend to have less hold than gels and may need refreshing after 24–48 hours.

**Styling butters** are thicker and better suited to twist-outs, braid-outs, and protective styles where definition rather than a wet-look finish is the goal.

**Gels** give the most hold and the best frizz control, but flaking is a real problem on 4C hair. When gel meets the oils and butters underneath, some formulas break down and leave white flakes. This isn't a universal problem — it depends on the specific products — but it's worth testing before committing to a gel.

One thing almost all stylers for 4C hair need: they have to work in UK humidity. Products formulated for drier climates can revert faster here. If a styler that worked brilliantly on a dry day falls flat in drizzle, it's the formulation, not your technique.

## LOC vs LCO: Does It Actually Matter?

The LOC method (Leave-in, Oil, Cream) and LCO (Leave-in, Cream, Oil) are both layering approaches to seal moisture in after washing. The idea is that the order changes how well each product can do its job.

For most 4C hair, the difference comes down to porosity:

- **Low porosity:** LCO often works better. A thick oil straight over the leave-in can block the cream from reaching the hair. Cream first, then oil to seal.
- **High porosity:** LOC is often the better fit. The oil layer slows how quickly moisture escapes, and the cream adds weight and further protection.

Neither is universally right. Try both over a few wash days — don't judge after one try — and go with whichever leaves your hair softer the next morning.

## What to Watch Out for on 4C Hair

A few things that come up frequently as problems:

**Gel that flakes:** some gels don't play well with oils and butters. If you're getting white residue, try reducing the oil layer before the gel, or switch to a different gel formula.

**Products that work in summer but not winter:** UK winter is damp outdoors but dry indoors from central heating. Some humectant-heavy products (especially high-glycerin formulas) pull moisture out of the hair in low-humidity indoor air. If your routine stops working in November, the season is a factor.

**Conditioner that seems to do nothing:** often a hard water problem rather than the wrong product. If you're in a hard water area, minerals coat the hair shaft and stop conditioner absorbing. A monthly chelating wash fixes this.

## FAQs

**How do I know if a product is right for my porosity?**

Low-porosity hair will show build-up with heavy products — the hair looks weighed down or waxy. High-porosity hair will feel dry quickly even after applying a lot of product. Start with a moisture-only approach after a clarifying wash to get a clean baseline reading.

**Is expensive product better for 4C hair?**

Not reliably. Some of the most expensive curl creams are formulated for looser curl types and do very little on tight coils. Some drugstore options work exceptionally well. Focus on the first few ingredients rather than the price.

**How often should I clarify vs chelate?**

Clarify (for product build-up) every two to four weeks depending on how many products you use. Chelate (for mineral build-up) monthly if you're in a hard water area, less often if you're in a soft water region like Scotland or Wales.
