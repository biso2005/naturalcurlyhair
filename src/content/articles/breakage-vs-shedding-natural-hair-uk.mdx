**Short hair strands — no bulb at the end — are breakage.** The hair snapped partway along the shaft. Something caused it to break.

**Longer strands with a white or translucent bulb at the root end are shedding.** The hair completed its growth cycle and fell out naturally. The bulb is the root — it detached cleanly from the follicle.

That's the diagnostic. Everything else follows from it.

---

## Shedding: Normal and Expected

Every hair follicle cycles through growth, transition, and rest phases. At the end of the rest phase, the hair sheds — falls out from the root — and the cycle begins again. The average person sheds 50–100 hairs a day. For natural coily hair, those hairs don't fall out as they shed — they tangle into the coil pattern and collect. On wash day, all the accumulated shed hairs come out at once.

**What it looks like:** a significant amount of longer strands (closer to your full hair length) with a tiny white or slightly dark bulb at one end. The amount can look alarming if you wash every seven to ten days — that's seven to ten days of accumulated shed hairs coming out together.

**When it's not just normal shedding:** if the shed hair seems to have increased significantly over a period of weeks without an obvious cause (stress, illness, post-partum, major dietary change), or if the shedding is concentrated in specific areas (a patch, one side), see a GP or trichologist. Diffuse hair loss from hormonal or nutritional causes presents as increased shedding.

## Breakage: What's Causing It and How to Fix It

Breakage is the hair shaft snapping rather than the hair completing its cycle. The strand is shorter than your full hair length — sometimes much shorter. No bulb.

### The most common causes

**Dryness.** Dry hair is brittle hair. A strand with no moisture has no elasticity — it snaps under normal manipulation. Most natural hair breakage traces back to insufficient moisture, insufficient sealing, or hard water mineral buildup preventing moisture absorption.

**Over-manipulation.** Detangling on dry hair, rough handling, constant re-styling. Every time the hair is manipulated — especially dry — there's mechanical stress at every bend in the coil.

**Tight styles.** Repeated tension at the same point breaks the hair shaft at that point over time. The section where a tight hair tie sits, the point where a braid attaches, the root of a tight ponytail. Breakage concentrated in one area usually has a tension cause.

**Protein imbalance.** Either too little protein (hair over-stretches and snaps mid-length) or too much protein (hair can't stretch at all and snaps immediately). The stretch test diagnoses which.

**Hard water buildup.** Mineral deposits prevent moisture from reaching the hair shaft. The hair behaves as though it's dry even when you're moisturising regularly. Common in London and the South East.

### How to address breakage

Identify which cause is present before adding more products. Adding moisture to hair that's breaking from tight styles doesn't fix the cause. The sequence:

1. **Assess where the breakage is happening.** At the root → tension cause. Mid-length → dryness or protein. At the ends → dryness, damage, needs a trim.
2. **Address the cause.** Tension → change the style or the tightness. Dryness → check moisture routine and hard water. Protein imbalance → stretch test and adjust.
3. **Trim damaged ends.** Breakage that's already happened leaves split or frayed ends. Trimming removes these so they don't travel up the shaft.

## When You Have Both

Most people experiencing significant breakage also have normal shedding happening simultaneously. The distinction matters because:

- If it's mostly shedding, adding protein treatments won't help
- If it's mostly breakage, the problem is the routine or the styles, not the follicle

A wash day audit: after detangling, look at what's in the comb or your fingers. Mostly long strands with bulbs = shedding. Mostly short strands without bulbs = breakage. A mix of both = normal, with the ratio telling you where to focus.

## FAQs

**I shed a lot more in autumn. Is that normal?**

Yes. Seasonal shedding in autumn is well-documented — a higher proportion of follicles enter the resting phase in summer and shed in autumn. The effect is usually temporary and resolves within six to eight weeks. If it continues past that, or is significantly more than previous autumns, check with a GP.

**My hair seems to break mostly at the ends. What does that mean?**

The ends are the oldest part of the hair — most exposed to mechanical damage over the longest period. Breakage concentrated at the ends usually means dryness (ends are the hardest to moisturise) and/or damage that requires trimming. More moisture, better sealing, and a trim of the most damaged ends usually addresses it.

**I lose a lot of hair when I detangle. Is that breakage or shedding?**

Both, typically. Some of what comes out on the comb is shed hair that was tangled in the coil pattern. Some is hair that breaks during detangling. Check the strands: long + bulb = shed; short + no bulb = broke during detangling. If you're seeing a lot of short strands in the comb, detangling technique is a factor — work on dry vs damp, fingers first, smaller sections.

**Could my breakage be from hard water?**

If you're in London or the South East and haven't chelated recently, yes. Hard water mineral deposits on the hair shaft prevent moisture absorption, making the hair behave as though it's perpetually dry. The characteristic sign is that moisturising doesn't seem to help. Chelate with an EDTA product and reassess.
