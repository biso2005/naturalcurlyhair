## 4C Wash Day Routine UK

A 4C wash day runs six steps: pre-poo (optional but worth it for brittle hair), detangle on conditioner-saturated sections from ends to roots, shampoo the scalp only, deep condition with heat for 20–30 minutes, layer leave-in and sealant using your porosity-matched order, then style or protective-style damp. In London and the South East, add a chelating shampoo once a month — mineral build-up from hard water is why products stop working, not the products themselves.

---

## What Makes 4C Wash Day Different from Other Curl Types

4C hair sits at the tightest end of the curl spectrum. The coils are so small and close together that sebum — the scalp's natural conditioning oil — cannot travel down the shaft the way it does on straight or loosely waved hair. That means the mid-lengths and ends of 4C hair receive almost no natural conditioning, which is why every wash day step matters more here than on other curl types.

### Shrinkage, density, and moisture loss explained plainly

Shrinkage is not damage. It is the elastic quality of a healthy coil returning to its natural shape. The practical problem on wash day: you cannot see how much hair you are working with, sections pull unevenly, and it is easy to miss patches when applying products. Length is better measured stretched on wash day, not eyeballed.

Density means more strands per square centimetre — and more opportunity for shed hairs to weave through your coils during washing. This is why detangling order matters more for 4C hair than any other type: shed hair that gets locked in during shampooing becomes the knot you fight on the next wash day.

Moisture loss is faster because the tightly coiled structure keeps the cuticle layers from lying completely flat. 4C hair absorbs water quickly and loses it quickly. Sealing — the final active step of wash day — is not optional; it is doing the job that natural sebum cannot.

## Before You Start: What You Actually Need

### Tools checklist

- **Wide-tooth comb** — for detangling on conditioner-saturated hair; widely spaced teeth, no seams along the tooth spine (seams snag and break strands)
- **Detangling brush** — flexible bristles that bend rather than force through knots; optional if your wide-tooth comb already clears tangles cleanly
- **Sectioning clips** — at least six; working without sections on dense 4C hair is what makes wash day take all day
- **Spray bottle** — fine mist, for re-wetting sections that dry between steps
- **Microfibre towel or old cotton T-shirt** — standard terrycloth roughs up the cuticle and sets frizz before you have finished styling
- **Heat cap or hooded dryer** — for deep conditioning; not essential but makes a measurable difference to penetration on low porosity hair
- **Timer** — deep conditioning has an optimal window; guessing wastes product

### Products checklist (generic categories)

Not every category is needed on every wash day. The list below is for a full routine; the refresh version is covered later.

- Pre-poo oil or conditioner (optional — see Step 1)
- Clarifying or chelating shampoo (monthly) / sulphate-free shampoo (weekly)
- Co-wash or cleansing conditioner (refresh days only)
- Deep conditioner — protein-free for regular use
- Protein treatment — separate product, used as needed, not weekly
- Leave-in conditioner
- Moisturising cream or butter
- Sealing oil — heavier consistency for 4C
- Scalp oil or treatment if needed

No specific products are named here — product verdicts are added once testing is complete. The categories above are what to look for.

## The Full 4C Wash Day Routine — Step by Step

### Step 1 — Pre-poo: worth it or not?

Pre-poo means applying a product — usually a penetrating oil or conditioner — to dry hair before shampooing. For 4C hair specifically, it is worth doing when your hair is brittle, breaks easily when wet, or takes a long time to detangle. It is optional when your hair is in good condition.

An oil-based pre-poo — coconut oil is the one with the strongest evidence for reducing protein loss during washing, covered in [Why Is My 4C Hair Always Dry?](/hair-care/why-is-my-4c-hair-always-dry-uk/) — applied to dry sections and left for 20–30 minutes before washing reduces the mechanical stress of the wash itself.

A conditioner-based pre-poo adds slip and makes the detangling step that follows faster. If detangling is your main wash day bottleneck, start here rather than with oil.

If you are in good condition and your wash days are already running long, skip it. Pre-poo is useful, not foundational.

### Step 2 — Detangling

Detangle on well-saturated hair with conditioner or a detangling product providing slip. Not bone dry. Not barely damp. The hair should have enough product on it that a comb moves through with gentle resistance, not drag.

1. Divide hair into four to eight sections depending on density — more sections means smaller pieces and less pulling per pass
2. Apply conditioner or detangling product generously to each section before touching it with a tool
3. Use fingers first to remove large tangles and shed hair from the coil
4. Follow with a wide-tooth comb from the ends upward, in small increments toward the root
5. Only move to the next section when the current one moves freely

For dense or longer 4C hair, detangling takes 30–45 minutes on its own. That is not a sign of a problem — it is the amount of time the step takes done without causing breakage. Rushing it compounds future wash days.

### Step 3 — Shampoo

Apply shampoo directly to the scalp, not the lengths. Work through the scalp with fingertips — not nails — in a grid pattern. The lather travels down the shaft as it rinses; scrubbing the length of 4C hair with shampoo is unnecessary and strips more than it cleans.

**Which shampoo when:**

- **Chelating shampoo** — once a month, or whenever hair feels weighted, stiff, or stops responding to products. Chelating agents (look for EDTA or citric acid derivatives in the ingredients) bind to and remove mineral deposits that standard shampoos leave behind. Follow immediately with deep conditioning — chelating is thorough and removes more than build-up.
- **Sulphate-free shampoo** — for regular weekly or fortnightly wash days; cleans without stripping aggressively
- **Co-wash** — for refresh days only. Exclusive co-washing over time causes build-up that makes every wash day harder; it is not a substitute for periodic shampooing

Rinse thoroughly before moving to deep conditioning.

### Step 4 — Deep conditioning

Deep conditioning is the step that determines how your hair feels for the rest of the week. Twenty to thirty minutes under a plastic cap with no heat is the minimum. With a heat cap or hooded dryer, fifteen to twenty minutes is sufficient — gentle external heat opens the cuticle and increases penetration.

For a weekly deep conditioner, use a protein-free formula. Signs your hair may need a separate protein treatment rather than a moisturising DC: hair that stretches too far before snapping when wet, feels mushy or weak, or has been chemically processed recently. Protein and moisture deep conditioners serve different functions; using a protein-heavy formula every week on already-protein-sufficient hair causes brittleness, not strength.

Rinse with cool water to help close the cuticle.

### Step 5 — Leave-in, cream, and sealant (LOC or LCO)

Apply each layer while hair is still damp — not dripping, not dry. Work in sections.

Which order to use depends on your porosity:

- **High porosity** (hair absorbs water quickly, dries quickly, feels dry within hours): LOC — Leave-in, Oil, Cream. The oil between the leave-in and cream slows moisture loss before the cream seals.
- **Low porosity** (hair beads water initially, takes a long time to fully wet): LCO — Leave-in, Cream, Oil. Cream can penetrate more easily without an oil barrier sitting between it and the strand; oil seals last.

If you do not know your porosity, [the porosity test guide](/hair-care/hair-porosity-test-natural-hair-uk/) is the fastest way to find out — it changes which products and methods will actually work for your hair.

For sealing, heavier-consistency oils that do not absorb instantly sit on top of the strand and form a barrier that lighter oils cannot. This is what holds the moisture in through the rest of the day.

### Step 6 — Styling

4C hair tolerates several styling approaches from this point:

- **Twist-out or braid-out:** Twist or braid damp, sealed sections and allow to dry completely before unravelling. Defines the curl pattern without additional products.
- **Wash and go:** Apply a defining gel or custard over the sealed sections and allow to dry undisturbed. Works well on shorter or medium-length 4C hair; on very dense hair, drying time is long.
- **Protective style:** Move directly into braids, twists, or a bun to preserve moisture while the hair dries. The most practical option if you are short on drying time.

Allow hair to dry completely before sleeping on it. Sleeping on damp 4C hair increases tangling, frizz, and — in very tight styles — the risk of mildew near the scalp.

## How UK Water Hardness Affects Your 4C Wash Day

Most of England has hard or very hard water. London and the South East sit on chalk and limestone — Thames Water and Affinity Water report hardness consistently in the range of 250–350 mg/L CaCO₃, with some eastern zones reaching 400 mg/L. Greater Manchester sits around 60–80 mg/L. Scotland, Wales, and much of the North West are soft, many areas below 50 mg/L. ([TapWater.uk, sourced from Drinking Water Inspectorate data](https://www.tapwater.uk/guides/water-hardness-map))

Hard water deposits calcium and magnesium minerals on the hair shaft. Over time this:

- Creates a coating that prevents moisture and conditioning ingredients from penetrating
- Makes products feel like they have stopped working — they have not; the mineral layer is blocking them
- Leaves hair feeling rough, dull, or stiff even after a thorough wash day

The mechanism and regional data are covered in detail in [Why Is My 4C Hair Always Dry?](/hair-care/why-is-my-4c-hair-always-dry-uk/). The summary relevant to wash day: if your results have gradually got worse without a change in products or routine, and you are in a hard water area, the water is the first thing to address.

### Practical fixes

**Chelating shampoo monthly** is the most practical fix for most people. EDTA and citric acid derivatives chemically bind to mineral ions and remove them in a way that standard shampoos cannot.

**Shower filter with ion exchange resin** reduces the mineral load at source. Most shower filters sold in the UK only remove chlorine (KDF, activated carbon, vitamin C filters). The only type that reduces calcium and magnesium is one containing ion exchange resin — the packaging should explicitly state calcium or magnesium reduction capacity. At London hardness levels, replace the cartridge every one to three months. No UK consumer body has independently validated shower filter claims for hard water reduction.

**Apple cider vinegar rinse** (one part ACV to three parts water) used before deep conditioning can dissolve some surface mineral deposits and restore a slightly acidic pH after shampooing. Use once a month alongside chelating, not as a substitute for it. The hair shaft performs best in the pH range 4.5–5.5; ACV used diluted is consistent with that — covered in full in [Why Is My 4C Hair Always Dry?](/hair-care/why-is-my-4c-hair-always-dry-uk/).

## Time-Saving Adjustments for a Realistic Routine

A full 4C wash day takes time. That is not a problem with your hair or your routine — it is the amount of time the steps take done without causing damage.

### Full wash day vs. refresh wash day

**Full wash day** (monthly or fortnightly):

| Step | Approximate time |
|---|---|
| Pre-poo | 20–30 min |
| Detangling | 30–45 min |
| Chelating or clarifying shampoo | 10–15 min |
| Deep conditioning | 20–30 min with heat |
| LOC/LCO application | 20–30 min |
| Styling | 20–45 min |
| Drying | 1–3 hours |

Total active time: roughly 2–3 hours. Total elapsed time including drying: up to 5–6 hours for dense or long hair.

**Refresh wash day** (weekly or as needed):

| Step | Approximate time |
|---|---|
| Co-wash or sulphate-free shampoo | 15–20 min |
| Quick conditioner rinse (no heat) | 10 min |
| Leave-in and oil only | 10–15 min |
| Re-twist or refresh existing style | 10–20 min |

Total active time: roughly 45–60 minutes.

### Night-before prep

Split the routine across two evenings when a single long session is not practical:

- **The night before:** Apply pre-poo oil to dry sections, loosely twist, cover with a satin bonnet and sleep on it. Eight or more hours of pre-poo time is more effective than the standard 20–30 minutes.
- **Morning of wash day:** Move directly to shampooing — the pre-poo step is already done. Pre-detangling with fingers only (oil in, no comb) the night before also shortens the shower detangling step significantly.

## Common 4C Wash Day Mistakes

**Skipping clarifying for too long.** Using co-wash and sulphate-free shampoo exclusively causes build-up over weeks. If your hair has felt progressively heavier, drier, or less responsive to moisture, a chelating or clarifying wash before anything else is the fix — not a product switch.

**Detangling without enough slip.** Conditioner or a dedicated detangling product is not optional for this step. Dry or barely damp detangling causes the breakage that shows up in the comb and on your clothing over the following week.

**Applying products to dry hair after washing.** Leave-in, cream, and oil all perform differently on damp versus dry hair. On dry hair they sit on top of the strand rather than distributing through it. Apply each layer while the hair is still damp and work in sections.

**Over-using protein.** Protein strengthens the hair shaft, but 4C hair that receives protein treatments too frequently becomes brittle rather than resilient. If your hair snaps with very little stretch when dry, and feels stiff rather than strong, protein overload is likely. A clarifying wash followed by a moisture-only deep condition and a break from protein is the reset.

**Deep conditioning for too long with heat.** Twenty to thirty minutes with a heat cap is sufficient. Significantly longer — two hours or more — can cause over-conditioning on some hair types, particularly if the formula contains protein. Use a timer.

**Sleeping on damp hair.** Damp 4C hair in a bonnet or braid overnight tangles more than dry hair does because the strands are still shifting as they finish drying. Dry the hair to at least 80% before sleeping, or style in a way that doesn't require full drying (flat twists, braids) where the style itself holds the shape.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How long should a 4C wash day take?

Realistically, two to three hours of active time for a full routine — plus one to three hours of drying depending on your method and hair length. Dense, long 4C hair can run longer. Building the routine into a day when you have the time rather than fitting it around other commitments makes the process less stressful and the results better.

### Can I wash 4C hair every week?

Yes, for most people with healthy 4C hair, weekly washing with a sulphate-free shampoo and a full deep conditioning step works well. The concern about over-washing applies to harsh or clarifying shampoos used too frequently, not to gentle washing. If your scalp is dry or your hair is very fragile, every ten to fourteen days may suit you better.

### Why does my 4C hair feel dry immediately after washing?

The most common causes are product build-up preventing moisture from penetrating (try a chelating wash), hard water mineral deposits doing the same, not sealing after applying leave-in, or applying products to hair that was already too dry rather than still damp. In winter in the UK, low indoor humidity — centrally heated rooms often drop to 30–35% RH — also draws moisture out of hair faster than usual.

### Do I need to detangle before or after shampooing?

Before. Shampooing tangles 4C hair further as the strands move through the water. Detangling after shampooing is harder and causes more breakage. The sequence is: pre-poo (optional) → detangle → shampoo → deep condition → style.

### Is the LOC method better than LCO for 4C hair?

It depends on your porosity, not your curl type. High porosity hair generally responds better to LOC; low porosity hair to LCO. [The porosity test](/hair-care/hair-porosity-test-natural-hair-uk/) is the fastest way to find out which applies to you.
