## Why Is My 4C Hair Always Dry? (A UK-Specific Answer)

If your 4C hair feels dry no matter what you put on it, you are not doing anything wrong. You are probably doing several things right — and still losing moisture to factors that most haircare advice never mentions, because most haircare advice is written for a different country, a different climate, and a different water supply.

This article works through the real reasons 4C hair struggles to hold moisture, then adds the layer that almost no guide bothers with: what is specifically making this harder in the UK.

---

## What Makes 4C Hair Structurally Prone to Dryness

### The curl angle problem — why sebum cannot travel the shaft

Sebum is the scalp's natural moisturising oil. On a straight strand it slides from root to tip almost automatically. On a 4C coil — which curls back on itself multiple times per centimetre — sebum has to negotiate every bend. It rarely makes it past the first few centimetres of growth. This is not a deficiency; it is geometry. The result is that the mid-lengths and ends of 4C hair receive almost no natural conditioning from the scalp, which means external moisture and sealing are not optional extras — they are doing the job sebum does automatically on other hair types.

### High porosity vs low porosity — which is actually more common in 4C hair?

Porosity describes how easily water enters and exits the hair shaft. High porosity hair (damaged or lifted cuticle) absorbs water fast and loses it fast. Low porosity hair (tightly sealed cuticle) resists water entry but holds onto moisture well once it is inside.

Neither is universal in 4C hair. The common assumption is that 4C hair is high porosity, but many people with 4C hair have low porosity strands — particularly if the hair has not been chemically processed. The practical difference matters enormously:

- **High porosity:** needs heavy sealants to stop moisture escaping; benefits from protein to fill gaps in the cuticle
- **Low porosity:** needs heat or steam to open the cuticle during conditioning; struggles with heavy butters that sit on top rather than penetrate

A simple strand test (place a shed hair in a glass of room-temperature water and watch whether it sinks quickly, slowly, or floats) gives a rough indication, though it is not laboratory-accurate. A more reliable signal is how your hair behaves: does it absorb water in seconds and dry out within hours (high porosity), or does it bead water initially and take a long time to fully wet (low porosity)?

People with textured hair are more likely to have higher porosity than those with straight hair — curls and coils are characterised by twists that cause cuticle lifting at various points along the fibre, and this is more prevalent in the more elliptical hair fibres characteristic of people of African ancestry. ([NYSCC, "An Overview on Hair Porosity"](https://nyscc.org)) That said, porosity is not fixed by hair type: chemical processing, heat damage, and weathering all increase it over time, and untreated 4C hair can be low porosity.

## UK-Specific Factors Nobody Mentions

### Hard water coverage across UK regions and what it does to the cuticle

Approximately 60% of UK postcodes receive hard or very hard water. The Thames catchment — including London and large parts of the South East — sits almost entirely on chalk and limestone; Thames Water and Affinity Water both report hardness consistently in the range of 250–350 mg/L CaCO₃, with some eastern supply zones touching 400 mg/L. Greater Manchester typically measures around 60–80 mg/L. Wales and much of the North West are generally soft, many areas below 50 mg/L. ([Water hardness by region: TapWater.uk, sourced from Drinking Water Inspectorate data](https://www.tapwater.uk/guides/water-hardness-map))

Hard water carries dissolved calcium and magnesium ions. When this water contacts your hair, those minerals deposit on and within the cuticle. Over time this build-up:

- Raises the hair's pH, which causes the cuticle to swell and lift
- Creates a mineral film that blocks moisturising ingredients from penetrating
- Makes hair feel rough, dull, and — critically — dry, regardless of what products you apply

If you live in London, Birmingham, Leicester, Cambridge, or large parts of the South East and are wondering why your routine that worked elsewhere has stopped working, hard water is a serious candidate. Products cannot moisturise through a mineral barrier.

You can check your local water hardness at your supplier's website — Thames Water, Severn Trent, Anglian Water, and others all publish hardness data by postcode.

### Central heating season — humidity levels in UK homes

UK winters involve months of continuous central heating. Centrally heated indoor air is dry air. The ideal indoor humidity is around 40–45%, but in centrally heated UK homes it frequently drops below that — to 30–35% RH through the coldest months. At those levels you notice dry, itchy skin and flyaway hair. The air in a centrally heated flat in January is not far off the conditions inside an aircraft cabin.

Hair is hygroscopic — it constantly exchanges moisture with the surrounding air. At 30–35% RH, the air around your hair is pulling moisture out of it continuously, throughout the day, regardless of what you applied at wash time. This is not product failure. This is physics.

## The Seven Most Likely Reasons Your 4C Hair Is Dry

### Your water is depositing minerals that block moisture (test this)

If you are in a hard water area, try washing once with bottled water or filtered water and assess the difference in how your hair feels post-wash. If it feels noticeably softer, the minerals in your tap water are working against everything else you do. A shower filter designed for limescale reduction is a more practical long-term solution than bottled water; look for filters with confirmed calcium and magnesium reduction ratings that fit standard UK shower fittings (typically ½ inch BSP).

### You are using the wrong humectants for UK humidity levels

Humectants — glycerin, honey, aloe vera — attract water molecules from the surrounding environment and bind them to the hair shaft. Glycerin absorbs approximately 25% of its weight in water at 50% relative humidity. Below that threshold the direction reverses: the humectant draws moisture out of the hair rather than from the air. ([Perry Romanowski, Chemists Corner](https://chemistscorner.com)) That 50% threshold matters because a centrally heated UK flat in January regularly sits at 30–35% RH.

A centrally heated UK flat in January can sit at 30–35% RH. A glycerin-heavy leave-in or styler that worked well in summer may be actively dehydrating your hair in winter. The product has not changed. The air has.

**What to do:** In autumn and winter, look at the ingredient list of your leave-ins, curl creams, and stylers. If glycerin appears in the first five ingredients, consider switching to a product where it appears lower in the list, or use a heavy occlusive sealant over it immediately to trap the moisture before the humectant can reverse-pull.

### Over-manipulation and mechanical dryness

4C hair is fragile at the curl junction — the tightest point of the coil. Detangling too often, too aggressively, or on dry hair creates micro-breakage along the shaft. Broken cuticle scales cannot retain moisture regardless of what is applied. Detangle only on wet, conditioned hair, work section by section from ends to roots, and use your fingers or a wide-tooth comb rather than a fine-tooth comb or brush on dry hair.

### Protein overload masquerading as dryness

This is one of the most common misdiagnoses. Hair that has received too much protein becomes stiff, brittle, and snaps easily — symptoms that can feel identical to dryness. The key differences:

- Protein-overloaded hair feels hard and almost plasticky, not just dry
- It snaps with very little stretch when you pull a wet strand gently
- It does not respond to adding more moisture — it may feel worse

If this sounds familiar, a clarifying wash followed by a moisture-only deep condition (no protein) and a break from protein treatments is the place to start. Hydrolysed protein size matters: lower and mid-molecular weight compounds penetrate deep into the hair cortex; higher molecular weight peptides adsorb onto the surface and only slightly penetrate the outer fibre layers. ([Malinauskyte et al., "Penetration of different molecular weight hydrolysed keratins into hair fibres," *International Journal of Cosmetic Science*, 2021, 43:26–37](https://doi.org/10.1111/ics.12671)) Too much of either — but especially repeated use of penetrating proteins — can lead to overload on fine or low porosity 4C strands.

### Your LOC or LCO order is wrong for your porosity

The LOC method (Liquid, Oil, Cream) and LCO method (Liquid, Cream, Oil) both aim to layer moisture and sealing in the right sequence. Which order works depends on your porosity:

- **High porosity:** LOC is generally recommended — oil before cream creates an additional barrier to slow moisture loss before the cream seals
- **Low porosity:** LCO often works better — a lighter cream absorbs first, then a small amount of oil over the top, because thick oils applied first can prevent a cream from reaching the shaft at all

If your hair is eating through product without retaining moisture, try swapping the order.

### Product layering is sealing nothing in

Applying multiple moisturising products without a final occlusive layer means you are hydrating the hair and then letting that hydration evaporate freely. Oils with a high oleic acid content (such as olive, avocado, or marula) are denser and more occlusive than oils high in linoleic acid (such as grapeseed or rosehip). For sealing high porosity 4C hair in the UK winter, a heavier butter or oleic-dominant oil applied as the final step is the barrier that keeps moisture in. Apply it to damp — not wet and not dry — hair.

### Diet and internal hydration

Chronically under-hydrated bodies produce drier skin and hair. There is no topical product that compensates for inadequate water intake. Eight glasses per day is a commonly cited figure, though individual needs vary. Omega-3 fatty acids (from oily fish, flaxseed, or walnuts) support scalp sebum production. For general dietary guidance, see [NHS Eat Well](https://www.nhs.uk/live-well/eat-well/).

## A Simple Routine Framework for Dry 4C Hair in the UK

### Wash day

1. **Pre-poo (optional but useful for high porosity hair):** Apply a penetrating oil such as coconut oil to dry hair 30–60 minutes before washing. Coconut oil is the only oil shown to reduce protein loss measurably in both undamaged and damaged hair when used as a pre-wash treatment; sunflower and mineral oils do not produce the same effect. ([Rele AS, Mohile RB., *Journal of Cosmetic Science*, 2003, 54(2):175–92](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12715786/))
2. **Clarify monthly or when product build-up is noticeable:** Use a sulphate-based clarifying shampoo to remove mineral deposits and product build-up. Hard water makes this more urgent — monthly is a reasonable baseline in hard water areas.
3. **Co-wash or low-poo between clarifying washes:** A conditioning cleanser or sulphate-free shampoo cleanses without stripping.
4. **Deep condition with heat:** Steam or a heat cap helps the cuticle open and allows the conditioner to penetrate. This is particularly important for low porosity hair. 20–30 minutes is sufficient for most formulations.
5. **ACV rinse (optional):** A diluted apple cider vinegar rinse (approximately 1 part ACV to 3–4 parts water) can help lower the hair's pH after washing, encouraging the cuticle to lie flat. The hair shaft has an isoelectric point of around pH 3.67 and performs best in the slightly acidic range of pH 4.5–5.5. Do not use undiluted.
6. **Apply leave-in on soaking wet hair.**
7. **Layer your LOC or LCO** (choose based on porosity as above).
8. **Seal with a butter or oleic oil as the final step.**

### Refresh days

- Spritz with plain water or a water-based refresher spray to reactivate the leave-in already on the hair.
- Apply a small amount of the cream or oil used on wash day to any areas that feel dry.
- Avoid fully re-wetting the hair more than two or three times per week. Wet hair is mechanically weaker than dry hair — the hydrogen bonds that give the shaft its tensile strength are temporarily broken by water and reform as it dries. Handling wet hair roughly (aggressive detangling, tight styling) is where real damage happens; the wetting itself is not the problem. The popular term "hygral fatigue" implies a swelling-damage cycle that is not well supported by current evidence, but the underlying principle holds: wet hair needs gentle handling.
- Protect at night with a satin or silk bonnet or pillowcase; cotton pulls moisture from the hair throughout the night.

## When to Suspect Something Beyond Routine

Most 4C dryness responds to the adjustments above. But some symptoms point to something that a product change will not fix.

**See a trichologist if you notice:**

- Significant shedding beyond what you consider your normal baseline (typical hair loss is around 50–100 strands per day, though individual normal ranges vary)
- Dry, flaking, or itchy scalp that does not respond to medicated or soothing shampoos
- Patches of hair loss or thinning at the edges, crown, or parting
- Hair that breaks at a consistently short length regardless of routine changes — this may indicate a structural or nutritional issue rather than a moisture problem
- Scalp soreness, redness, or crusting

Trichologists can be found through the [Institute of Trichologists](https://www.trichologists.org.uk/qualified-trichologists-united-kingdom/) — the only UK body whose register is accredited by the Professional Standards Authority — or the [Trichological Society](https://www.hairscientists.org/consultants/hair-consultants/trichologists), which is searchable by region. A GP referral is not always required for a private appointment, and an assessment is worth pursuing if scalp symptoms persist for more than a few weeks without improvement.

Dryness is the most common 4C hair complaint, and the most fixable — once you know which of the above factors is actually driving it. In the UK, that usually means starting with the water.
