Being refused service, overcharged, or told your hair is "too difficult" to style in a UK salon because you have Afro-textured hair is unlawful under the Equality Act 2010. This article sets out what counts as discrimination in a salon context, what to do immediately after, and how to escalate if needed.

---

## What Counts as Discrimination in a Salon

The Equality Act 2010 makes it unlawful for a service provider — including a hair salon — to discriminate on grounds of race. Discrimination takes two forms:

**Direct discrimination:** treating someone less favourably because of their race. A salon that turns away a Black client because they "don't do Afro hair" while accepting clients of other racial groups is directly discriminating. A salon that refuses to price a Black client's service in advance while pricing for white clients is directly discriminating.

**Indirect discrimination:** applying a policy that appears neutral but disproportionately disadvantages people of a particular racial group. A salon that only takes clients whose hair is "manageable" — and applies that standard in a way that excludes Afro-textured hair — may be indirectly discriminating.

**Common situations that may constitute discrimination:**

- Refusal to book an appointment for a client with natural or Afro hair when the salon accepts clients with other hair types
- "We don't do Afro hair" as a policy, without a legitimate reason such as lacking the training (and a referral offered)
- Charging significantly more for the same or comparable service on Afro-textured hair compared to other hair types, without a legitimate time or complexity basis
- Being turned away mid-appointment after the stylist assessed your hair type
- Being told natural styles (afros, locs, braids) are "not appropriate" in a salon
- Receiving a significantly worse service (rushed, uncompleted, or damaging) compared to other clients

**What is not discrimination (though it may still be poor practice):**

- A salon that genuinely lacks the training for Afro-textured hair and says so honestly, with a referral to someone who can help
- Higher pricing for a service that genuinely takes longer, where the pricing is consistent and disclosed in advance

## What to Do Immediately

**Document everything.** Write down what happened as soon as possible: date, time, salon name and address, name of the person involved (if you know it), what was said or done, and any witnesses. Take a screenshot of any online communication (booking, cancellation, message exchange).

**Ask for the reason in writing.** If you were refused service verbally, you can follow up with a short message: "I'm writing to confirm the reason my appointment was refused today. Could you confirm this in writing?" A salon that has discriminated may not respond; one that had a legitimate reason usually will.

**Note the comparison.** If you saw or can establish that clients of other racial groups received different treatment, document that too.

## How to Escalate

### Complaint to the salon directly

Write to the salon owner or manager (not just the stylist), citing the Equality Act 2010, describing what happened, and stating what you want: an apology, a refund, confirmation that the policy will change.

> Dear [Salon name],
>
> I am writing regarding an incident on [date] at your salon. [Describe what happened].
>
> Under the Equality Act 2010, a service provider cannot refuse service or treat a customer less favourably on grounds of race. Natural Afro-textured hair is directly associated with race, and a policy or practice that excludes, overcharges, or treats clients with Afro-textured hair differently constitutes race discrimination.
>
> I am requesting [apology / refund / written confirmation of your policy / all three].
>
> If I do not receive a satisfactory response within fourteen days, I will escalate this matter to the Equality and Human Rights Commission and consider whether to pursue it through the county court.
>
> Yours sincerely,
> [Your name]

### Equality Advisory and Support Service (EASS)

The EASS provides free advice and support on equality matters in the UK. Helpline: 0808 800 0082. They can advise on whether your situation is likely to constitute discrimination and what steps to take.

### Equality and Human Rights Commission (EHRC)

The EHRC can investigate complaints of discrimination and has powers to take action against service providers who breach the Equality Act. They don't handle individual complaints directly but their technical guidance is useful and their reporting mechanism escalates systemic issues.

### County court claim

Discrimination in services can be pursued through the county court as a civil claim. The small claims track (up to £10,000) is the relevant route for most salon disputes involving discrimination and associated losses (refund, distress). Citizens Advice can advise on the process.

### Online reviews and social media

Posting a factual account of what happened as a public review is lawful and often effective. Describe what happened without exaggeration; let the facts make the case. A salon that has discriminated and sees a detailed, factual review often resolves the matter more quickly than a formal complaint.

## Situations That Don't Require Escalation

Not every bad salon experience is discrimination, and distinguishing between them matters:

**Incompetence is not discrimination.** A stylist who damaged your hair because they lacked skill did you wrong, but it's not automatically discrimination unless the bad service was applied specifically because of your race.

**Honest capability limits are not discrimination.** "We don't have the training for Afro hair" from a salon that refers you elsewhere is not the same as refusing to serve you. It's preferable to a salon that takes your money and does the work badly.

**Overcharging is discrimination if it's racially motivated; it's just overcharging if it's general.** Ask for pricing before committing; compare with the publicly listed prices. If the premium applies to you and not to other clients for a comparable service, that's the evidence.

## FAQs

**The stylist said my hair was "too tangled" to work with and turned me away. Is that discrimination?**

Possibly, depending on context. If the salon regularly handles hair in similar condition on clients of other racial groups, the "too tangled" reason may be a pretext. If the salon genuinely lacks experience with the coil pattern that causes tangling in natural Afro hair, it may be an honest capability assessment. Document the situation and ask for the reason in writing.

**I was charged significantly more than the price list showed. What can I do?**

Ask for a written breakdown of the pricing at the time of payment, or immediately after. If the overcharge is significant and the reason given doesn't hold up (the service didn't take noticeably longer; no materials surcharge was disclosed in advance), a formal complaint citing the Equality Act and a county court claim for the difference is the route.

**I'm worried that making a complaint will make me look difficult. Should I bother?**

That worry is understandable. It's also part of what allows discriminatory practices to continue — individual incidents not reported because the person affected doesn't want to make a fuss. The complaint doesn't have to be public. A private written complaint to the salon, citing the law, is a quiet action that nonetheless has real consequences.

**Other Black clients use this salon without issue. Can it still be discrimination?**

Yes. Discrimination can be directed at a specific person or decision even if other members of the same group have not experienced it. The question is whether your specific treatment was influenced by race.
