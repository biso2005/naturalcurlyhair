Split ends are the most common hair damage complaint and the one with the fewest actual solutions — products that claim to repair splits don't. A split end is a break in the hair shaft structure that can only be removed by cutting below the split.

The question worth answering is not "how do I repair splits" but "how do I reduce how often I get them and identify when they need trimming."

---

## Types of Split Ends

Natural coily hair develops more types of splits than straight hair because of the structural stress at every bend in the coil.

**Basic split (Y-split):** the classic — the hair shaft divides into two at the end. The starting point for all other damage.

**Tree split / feathering:** multiple branches along the last centimetre or two of the shaft. More advanced damage — the split has been travelling up from the end for some time.

**Mid-shaft split:** a split that has occurred not at the tip but partway along the shaft. Usually from mechanical damage (tight ponytail, rough handling) or significant protein deficiency at that point. More serious than end splits.

**Single-strand knot (fairy knot):** the hair has knotted around itself. Common in coily hair types where the tight curl allows the strand to loop. See separate article.

**White dot:** a visible dot of depleted cuticle on the shaft. Not a split yet, but a point of weakness that will split with further manipulation.

## What Causes Splits on Natural Hair

**Dryness.** Brittle hair splits under normal handling. Moisture and proper sealing significantly reduce split rate.

**Mechanical damage.** Rough detangling, tight hair ties at the same point repeatedly, hair rubbing against cotton, aggressive styling. Every point of mechanical stress is a potential split site.

**Hard water mineral buildup.** UK hard water leaves calcium deposits on the hair shaft that make it rougher and more prone to mechanical damage. Regular chelating reduces this.

**Heat damage.** High heat disrupts the protein structure of the hair shaft at the application point. Splits develop at these weakened points.

**Age of the hair.** The ends of longer hair are the oldest portion — they've experienced the most cumulative mechanical stress and weathering. Splits at the ends are more common with longer hair.

## The Only Fix: Trimming

Products that claim to "repair" or "bond" splits don't. The hair cuticle at a split site cannot be permanently reattached. Products can temporarily smooth the appearance of a split — a glaze product or silicone can coat the split and make the strand feel smooth — but this doesn't stop the split from travelling up the shaft.

**When to trim:**

- When running fingers down the length produces a rough or jagged feel at the ends (cuticle damage even before visible splits)
- When the ends of the hair look thin, stringy, or darker than the rest of the length
- When detangling produces consistent single-strand knots at the ends
- When tree splits or feathering are visible on multiple strands

**Trim size:** the minimum needed to get below the damage. If splits are confined to the tips, a centimetre or two clears them. If feathering has been allowed to travel further, more is needed. Search-and-destroy trimming (trimming individual splits without taking off the full length) is appropriate for early damage that's not widespread.

## Preventing Splits in UK Conditions

**Moisture and sealing:** the LOC or LCO routine keeps the hair shaft flexible enough to resist splitting under normal handling.

**Protective styling:** keeping the ends tucked away (in buns, braids, twists) removes them from daily mechanical contact. The ends are most vulnerable; protective styles are most protective at exactly this point.

**Satin at night:** cotton friction contributes significantly to end damage over time. A satin bonnet or pillowcase reduces friction on the ends overnight.

**Monthly chelating (hard water areas):** softer hair surface = less mechanical abrasion = fewer splits.

**Trimming on schedule rather than in crisis:** many people wait until splits are severe before trimming, which means a larger trim is needed. A small trim every ten to twelve weeks takes off less total length over a year than two or three emergency trims.

## FAQs

**Can I use a split end serum to avoid trimming?**

You can use one to temporarily smooth the appearance and prevent the split from catching on things, but it doesn't remove the need to trim. The split will continue travelling up the shaft — the serum just postpones the visible sign of the damage. Trim eventually; use a serum if it helps in the meantime.

**My ends are always splitting even with a good routine. Why?**

Either the ends are old (the longer the hair, the older the ends) and need trimming more often, or there's a specific mechanical damage source — a hair tie, a sleeping position, a collar or headwear that rubs the ends consistently. Identify where the splits are occurring and what might be causing contact at that point.

**I want to retain length but also need to trim. What's the least I can take off?**

Trim only below the visible damage. A search-and-destroy trim — going through the hair section by section and snipping individual split strands — removes damage while taking off minimal length. It's time-consuming but preserves more length than a blunt trim across the full length.

**Does bleaching or colouring cause splits?**

Yes. Chemical treatments disrupt the cuticle structure and significantly increase split rate. After any chemical treatment, more frequent trimming and more intensive moisture routine are needed to manage the accelerated damage.
