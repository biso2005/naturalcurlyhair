A consultation or even a short phone exchange before booking tells you more than a portfolio does. Portfolios show the best work; a conversation shows you how the stylist thinks about hair. These seven questions get to that quickly.

---

## The Seven Questions

### 1. "Can you show me five examples of [the style I want] on hair like mine?"

This is the most important question. Not "have you done this before" — that's a yes/no with no verifiable answer — but "show me five examples." If the stylist can pull up five examples from their own portfolio in under a minute, they have consistent experience. If they struggle to find three, or show you photos from other stylists' pages, that tells you something.

Be specific about hair type: "on 4C hair" or "on natural type 4 hair" rather than just "on Black hair." Styles on relaxed, texturised, or type 3 hair require different techniques.

### 2. "How do you handle tension at the hairline?"

A stylist who takes this question seriously will tell you specifically what they do: feed-in technique for braids rather than one-pull, leaving the hairline section looser than the rest, asking clients to communicate discomfort during installation. A stylist who says "I'm always gentle" without specifics is giving you a reassurance, not an answer.

Why it matters: traction alopecia from tight braids is the most common salon-caused hair damage. A stylist with real experience managing this knows the specific techniques because they've had to learn them.

### 3. "What products do you use for [my service]?"

For natural hair, the products matter. A stylist who uses products not formulated for natural or Afro-textured hair — mainstream drugstore products without mention of brands formulated for the hair type — may not have the experience they're presenting.

You're not looking for brand loyalty; you're looking for literacy. A stylist who can say "I use [product] because it does [specific thing] for this hair type" understands what they're doing. "I use whatever works" from a stylist who can't elaborate is not a confidence-building answer.

### 4. "How long will this service take?"

This is a practical question and a diagnostic. Experienced stylists know their timing. Box braids: 4–8 hours depending on size and length. Cornrows: 1–3 hours. Locs installation: 4–8 hours. A stylist who gives you a confident, specific range has done it enough times to know. A stylist who says "it depends" without elaborating doesn't know their own pace.

Also ask whether they work alone or with assistants — a stylist who works solo will always take longer than one with a trained assistant.

### 5. "What's your policy if I'm not happy with the result?"

A good stylist has an answer. Whether that's "come back within [x days] and I'll fix it" or "I don't offer refunds but I'll address any technical issues" — having a policy means they've thought about it. No policy at all means either they've never had an unhappy client (unlikely) or they haven't thought about it (more likely and a flag).

### 6. "Do you offer a consultation before the full appointment?"

Not all stylists do. Some charge for consultations; many don't for a brief chat. A stylist who is confident in their work and client fit is usually happy to have a consultation — they've learned from experience that a poorly matched client wastes everyone's time. A stylist who resists consulting before a major appointment is one to approach with more caution.

### 7. For colour specifically: "Do you use a bond repair treatment during or after colour?"

Bleaching or colouring natural hair without bond repair (Olaplex or K18) leaves the hair significantly more damaged than with it. A colourist working on natural hair who doesn't use bond repair either doesn't know about it or doesn't think it applies to natural hair — both are flags. Olaplex in-service is now standard in reputable UK salons doing any significant lightening.

## Questions Not Worth Asking

**"How long have you been doing hair?"** Experience in years doesn't tell you much about experience with your specific hair type. A stylist who's been doing hair for twenty years on primarily European hair types may have less relevant experience than one who's been doing natural hair for five.

**"Are you trained?"** Most stylists are trained. Training quality varies enormously and the qualification doesn't tell you much about whether the stylist is right for your specific hair and style.

---

## How to Read the Answers

The goal isn't trick questions — it's to find out if the stylist thinks about hair the way someone experienced with your hair type thinks about it. Someone who has done a lot of natural hair talks about it with specificity: tensions, products, timing, what can go wrong, how they prevent damage. Someone less experienced talks in general reassurances.

Trust what you observe in the conversation, not what you hope you heard.

## FAQs

**The stylist seemed great on the phone but the result was bad. What happened?**

Phone manner and salon manner don't always translate. The consultation question in person — for any major service — is a better filter than a phone call.

**I'm nervous about asking these questions and seeming difficult. What should I do?**

Frame them as you wanting to make sure the appointment goes well rather than as a test. "I just want to make sure you have experience with 4C hair specifically — can you show me some examples?" is a reasonable thing for any client to ask. A stylist who treats those questions as difficult is telling you something about how they handle clients who have needs.

**My regular stylist retired and I need to find someone new. How do I start?**

Start with your stylist if possible — they may have a recommendation or know who's taken over their clients. If not, Instagram and local Facebook groups are the fastest routes to finding someone in your area with visible experience in your hair type.
