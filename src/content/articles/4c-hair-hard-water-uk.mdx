If your 4C hair feels like straw after washing, your deep conditioner seems to do nothing, or your hair is breaking more than it should — and you live in England — hard water is a serious candidate for why. It's not your products, and it's not your technique. It's the water.

## How Hard Water Affects 4C Hair

Tap water in most of England contains dissolved minerals — mainly calcium and magnesium — from the chalk and limestone that water passes through before it reaches your tap. When that water hits your hair, those minerals settle on the strands. Ordinary shampoo doesn't remove them.

The result on 4C hair:

- Deep conditioner sits on top rather than absorbing — you're conditioning over a mineral layer
- Curls feel rough or stiff without any product applied
- Coils don't clump or define the way they should
- Breakage increases because the hair shaft has less flexibility
- A greyish or ashy cast that doesn't shift with conditioner

This gets confused with protein overload quite often — the symptoms overlap. If you've cut protein from your routine because you thought that was the problem and nothing improved, hard water might be the actual issue.

## Are You in a Hard Water Area?

If you're in London, the South East, East Anglia, or the East Midlands, yes — almost certainly.

Quick reference:

| Region | Water hardness |
|--------|---------------|
| London (Thames Water) | Hard to very hard (250–400 mg/L) |
| South East (Southern Water) | Hard to very hard |
| East Anglia (Anglian Water) | Hard to very hard |
| East Midlands (Severn Trent) | Hard to very hard |
| Yorkshire | Variable — soft to moderate |
| North West (United Utilities) | Mostly soft |
| Wales (Welsh Water) | Soft |
| Scotland | Very soft |

Most water companies have a postcode checker on their website — worth looking up your specific zone.

A quick home test: buy aquarium water hardness strips from any pet shop or Amazon. Fill a glass with your bathroom tap water, dip the strip, and check the result in under two minutes.

## The Fix: Chelating Shampoo, Once a Month

A clarifying shampoo won't help here. Clarifying removes product build-up (gels, creams, oils). It does not remove minerals. To remove minerals you need a chelating shampoo — check the label for EDTA or phytic acid in the ingredients.

If you're in a hard water area, use it once a month on top of your regular wash routine. That's the full intervention. After the chelating wash, your deep conditioner will actually be able to reach your hair.

→ [Chelating vs clarifying: which one your curls actually need](/hair-care/chelating-vs-clarifying-shampoo-curly-hair/)

**What to expect after your first chelating wash:**

- Your hair may feel stripped immediately after — this is normal; the chelating shampoo has done a thorough job
- Deep condition straight after, with heat if you have it
- Within 24 hours your hair should feel noticeably softer and more responsive to moisture

If you notice no difference after two or three monthly chelating washes, hard water may not be the main issue — or you may need to address damage separately.

## Between Washes

If you use a water-based spritz on your hair between wash days, that spray bottle contains hard water. Every spritz deposits more minerals.

Two options:

- Fill your spray bottle with filtered or distilled water (cheap at most supermarkets) rather than tap water
- Add a small amount of aloe vera juice or diluted apple cider vinegar to your spritz — both are mildly acidic and help counteract mineral build-up between washes

## Shower Filters: What They Do and Don't Do

Shower filters are often recommended for hard water. They help — but they remove chlorine, not calcium and magnesium. Chlorine can strip moisture and irritate the scalp, so filtering it out is worth doing. But a shower filter alone will not fix a hard water problem on 4C hair. You still need the monthly chelating wash.

## A Simple Hard Water Protocol for 4C Hair

**Once a month:**
- Chelating shampoo — focus on scalp, rinse through the length
- Deep conditioner immediately after — with heat if possible, 20–30 minutes minimum
- Seal well after rinsing — 4C hair needs that seal especially after chelating

**Every wash day:**
- Pre-poo optional but useful — oil applied before washing creates some buffer
- Gentle shampoo or co-wash for maintenance weeks
- Leave-in and seal while hair is still soaking wet

**Between washes:**
- Distilled or filtered water in your spritz bottle if you're refreshing curls
- Satin bonnet at night — high-porosity 4C hair, which is more susceptible to mineral build-up, loses moisture to cotton pillowcases quickly

That's the routine. One chelating wash per month in a hard water area changes the baseline for everything else.

## FAQs

**How do I know if hard water is my problem?**

Buy an aquarium water test strip and check your postcode. If you're above 200 mg/L and your deep conditioner seems to do nothing, hard water is a likely factor. The fastest confirmation is a chelating wash — if your hair responds noticeably better afterwards, that's your answer.

**My hair feels worse after chelating. Why?**

Chelating shampoos are strong. If you didn't deep condition immediately after, or you skipped a heat step, your hair may feel stripped. The chelating wash itself is correct — the step after needs to be thorough. Try with a heat cap and a richer deep conditioner.

**Can I use apple cider vinegar instead of a chelating shampoo?**

ACV provides mild acid rinsing and helps close the cuticle, but it doesn't remove mineral deposits the way a proper chelating shampoo does. It's a useful addition between washes; it's not a substitute for chelating.

**Do I need to chelate if I'm in Scotland or Wales?**

Probably not regularly. Soft water areas don't deposit the same mineral load. If you've moved from a hard water area and your hair is still struggling, one chelating wash as a reset is fine — then reassess whether you need to keep it up.
