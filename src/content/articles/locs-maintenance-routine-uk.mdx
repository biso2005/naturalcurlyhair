Mature locs are low maintenance compared to any other natural hair style. The routine is simpler than wash day for loose hair. The things that go wrong — mildew, thinning, frizz, buildup — almost always come from either not doing the few essential things or doing too much.

---

## The Maintenance Routine: Full Version

### Washing (every 7–14 days)

How often depends on your scalp. An oily scalp or active lifestyle needs washing every seven days. A drier scalp or less active week can go to ten to fourteen days. The test is the scalp, not the locs — the scalp is where the decision is made.

**How to wash:**
1. Saturate the locs fully — run under the shower for longer than you think. Locs are dense and slow to fully wet through.
2. Apply a residue-free or clarifying shampoo diluted in water. Apply to the scalp; let it rinse through the length.
3. Massage the scalp with fingertip pads (not nails). Squeeze the shampoo through the locs — don't rub.
4. Rinse very thoroughly. Residue left inside locs causes buildup and eventually mildew.
5. Apply a lightweight scalp oil if the scalp is dry — jojoba is good. Nothing heavy on the locs themselves.
6. Squeeze out excess water.
7. Dry fully.

### Drying (non-negotiable)

Locs that are not completely dry before sleeping develop mildew inside the loc. Mildew smells musty and is extremely difficult to remove once established — often requiring loc removal. UK winter makes this harder because air drying takes much longer in cold, damp conditions.

**Drying protocol:**
- Air dry for as long as possible — 1–3 hours
- Finish with a hooded dryer, diffuser, or sit dryer for 20–30 minutes
- Squeeze the thickest locs to check the interior — it should feel warm and dry, not cool and damp

In summer, air drying outside in warm weather works well. In winter, plan wash day for earlier in the day or always finish with heat.

### Retwisting (every 4–8 weeks)

Retwisting addresses the new growth at the root — the loose hair that grows out between the loc and the scalp. It integrates new growth back into the loc.

How often depends on how fast your hair grows and how much loose root bothers you. Most people retwist every four to eight weeks. Over-retwisting — tighter than necessary, more frequently than needed — causes traction at the root and eventually thinning.

**How to retwist:**
1. Do it on freshly washed, fully dry locs
2. Apply a small amount of loc butter or wax to the root
3. Twist the new growth in the same direction as the existing loc
4. Do not retwist the full length of the loc — just the root
5. If using a dryer after retwisting, roll the locs lightly in clips or on rods to keep the shape while drying

### Between wash days

Locs need less between washes than loose natural hair. A light spritz of water or aloe vera juice keeps them from feeling dry without product accumulation.

- **If the scalp feels dry:** a small amount of jojoba or argan oil to the scalp only, not along the length
- **If the locs feel dry:** a light mist of water, that's usually enough for mature locs
- **At night:** a satin bonnet or satin-lined sleep cap. Locs against cotton create frizz and lint accumulation over months.

## Chelating: The Maintenance Step UK Locs Routines Skip

In hard water areas, calcium and magnesium from tap water accumulate inside locs every wash. Unlike loose hair where chelating removes surface deposits, in locs the deposits accumulate in the interior where they're harder to reach. Once established, mineral buildup makes locs feel rough, heavy, and stiff.

Monthly chelating with a product containing EDTA or phytic acid prevents this. Apply to saturated locs, work through, leave two to three minutes, rinse thoroughly. Do this before regular shampoo.

## What Products to Use

**Shampoo:** residue-free or clarifying. Not moisturising shampoos with heavy conditioners — these leave deposits in locs. Not anything with oils or silicones high in the ingredient list.

**Scalp oil:** light. Jojoba, argan, sweet almond. Not castor oil or heavy butters — these clog and accumulate inside the loc over time.

**Loc butter or wax (for retwisting):** less is better. A thin layer at the root. Heavy wax accumulates inside locs and is almost impossible to remove later.

**Nothing inside the loc:** products applied to the length of locs accumulate inside and contribute to buildup. The scalp needs conditioning; the loc itself does not.

## Seasonal Adjustments

**Winter:** locs take longer to dry in cold weather. Wash earlier in the day. Central heating creates dry indoor air — more attention to scalp oil. If the scalp develops dandruff or itching in winter, a ketoconazole shampoo (Nizoral) used monthly addresses seborrhoeic dermatitis that commonly worsens in UK winter.

**Summer:** humidity in UK summers is moderate, not tropical — locs handle it fine. The risk in summer is sweat accumulation from heat and activity, which requires more frequent washing rather than less.

## FAQs

**My locs smell musty after washing. What happened?**

Mildew inside the locs from not drying completely. Wash the locs with a clarifying shampoo and dry fully with heat, several wash cycles. If the smell persists after two to three washes with full drying, the mildew may be established deeply — see a loctician.

**My scalp is dry but I've been told not to put anything on locs. What do I do?**

Apply scalp oil to the scalp only, not to the loc itself. A small amount of jojoba or argan oil on the fingertips, massaged into the scalp between the locs. Avoid getting the oil onto the loc length.

**Do I need to condition my locs?**

Not the same way you condition loose hair. The interior of a loc doesn't need softening conditioner — adding it creates product residue. Some people use a very light leave-in spray on the exterior of mature locs. The scalp is what needs moisture maintenance.

**How do I know when it's time to retwist?**

When the loose new growth at the root starts to bother you or tangle with adjacent locs. There's no fixed schedule that's right for everyone. Two to three centimetres of loose root is typically when most people retwist.
