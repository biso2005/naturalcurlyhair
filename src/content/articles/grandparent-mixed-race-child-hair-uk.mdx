If you're a grandparent looking after a mixed race grandchild and you've realised their hair needs different care than you knew how to give, you're in good company. Most grandparents of mixed race children in the UK are in exactly this position — caring deeply about the child, uncertain about the hair, and looking for somewhere helpful to start.

This guide is that somewhere. It doesn't assume prior knowledge. It doesn't judge. It starts at the beginning.

---

## Why This Hair Type Needs Different Care

Natural curly, coily, and Afro-textured hair has a spiral structure that works differently from straight hair. The oils from the scalp that travel down a straight hair shaft easily get stopped at every bend in a coily strand. This means the hair dries out faster and needs external moisture applied regularly.

Dry natural hair:
- Tangles easily
- Breaks when combed or brushed dry
- Looks dull
- Can form mats that become painful to detangle

This isn't something the child is doing wrong or that you're doing wrong by not knowing. It's how the hair type works. Once you know, you can adjust.

## The Three Things That Matter Most

### 1. Keep it moisturised

The most important thing. A small amount of leave-in conditioner or curl cream applied to slightly damp hair every two to three days keeps the hair manageable and prevents the tangles and dryness that make everything harder.

You don't need to know what product to buy immediately. Ask the child's parent what leave-in they use and use that. If that's not possible, a gentle moisturising conditioner from the supermarket, applied and left in without rinsing, does the job.

### 2. Don't brush it dry

Never brush or comb natural coily or curly hair when it's dry. The curl structure means pulling a brush through dry hair breaks it at every bend rather than moving through smoothly.

When the hair needs detangling or tidying:
- Dampen it with a water spritz or run it under the tap
- Apply a small amount of conditioner
- Work through gently with your fingers first, from the very ends upward
- Then use a wide-tooth comb if needed, still from the ends upward, in small sections

### 3. Protect it at night

Cotton pillowcases pull moisture out of natural hair and create friction. A satin bonnet for bed — the child's parent will likely have one — or a satin pillowcase keeps the hair much better overnight. Without this, the hair can be significantly more tangled and dry by morning.

## Wash Day

If you're caring for the child for more than a week, they'll need their hair washed. Every seven to ten days is the right frequency for natural curly and coily hair.

You don't need to do this perfectly. A simple version that works:

1. **Wet the hair thoroughly** in the bath or shower with warm water
2. **Apply a gentle shampoo** to the scalp, massage gently, let it rinse through the length
3. **Apply conditioner** generously to the mid-length and ends — this is where the detangling happens
4. **Work through the tangles gently with your fingers**, starting at the very ends and moving upward, while the conditioner is still in
5. **Rinse** the conditioner out (or leave a small amount in — either is fine)
6. **Apply a leave-in conditioner or curl cream** while the hair is still wet
7. **Leave to dry** without combing or brushing

If the child's hair is very tangled when you start, be patient. Don't pull. More conditioner and smaller sections. Work from the ends upward. If you can't get through the tangles comfortably, it's fine to stop and try again the next day with the hair damp and conditioned.

## Products to Use

Ask the child's parent what products they use and use those. This is the easiest path — you know the product is right for the child's hair and you're not guessing.

If that's not possible, the basics from a supermarket or chemist work:
- A gentle shampoo or conditioner (Dove, Simple, or any sulphate-free option)
- A leave-in conditioner or curl cream (SheaMoisture, Cantu, and Bouclème are available in Boots and Superdrug and are widely used on natural hair)

Do not use:
- Vaseline or petroleum jelly on the hair (it creates buildup that doesn't wash out easily)
- Heavy pomades
- Products with strong alcohol high in the ingredients list (these dry natural hair out)

## Hard Water

If you live in London, the South East, East Anglia, or the Midlands, your water is hard. Hard water leaves mineral deposits on the hair that make it harder to manage over time. If you're caring for the child regularly, a mild chelating shampoo once a month (look for EDTA or phytic acid on the label) removes those deposits and keeps the hair easier to manage. This is one of those things that sounds technical but is just a different type of shampoo used occasionally.

## Things That Are Fine to Do

- A loose puff or two plaits for bedtime — protective, easy
- Moisturising the hair every few days even if it's not wash day
- Letting the hair air dry naturally
- Asking the child's parent for specific advice — they will appreciate that you asked

---

## Things to Avoid

- Brushing or combing the hair dry
- Tight styles that pull at the hairline — they should not hurt
- Washing more than once a week unless the hair is visibly dirty (strips moisture)
- Products with a strong alcohol smell — they dry the hair

## FAQs

**The hair looked fine when they arrived and now it's very tangled. What happened?**

Probably a combination of dry air and a cotton pillowcase over several nights. The satin bonnet or pillowcase at night is the biggest change you can make. For the current tangles: wet, conditioner, fingers first from the ends upward, small sections.

**I tried to comb the hair and the child cried. What did I do wrong?**

The comb went through dry or insufficiently conditioned hair, or you started at the root rather than the ends. Wet the hair, apply conditioner generously, leave it for a minute, then work through with fingers starting at the very tips. The comb should encounter no resistance if there's enough conditioner.

**The child's parent wants me to do the hair a specific way but I find it very difficult. What do I say?**

Be honest. "I find this step difficult — can you show me a simpler way to achieve the same thing?" Most parents are more flexible than they initially appear when they understand the difficulty is practical rather than indifference.

**I'm worried I'll damage the hair. Can I just leave it in a bun for the whole visit?**

A loose bun is a valid protective style for short periods. For longer visits, the hair needs moisture every few days and a wash every seven to ten days. A tight bun held with rubber bands causes more damage over time than an imperfect wash day routine.
