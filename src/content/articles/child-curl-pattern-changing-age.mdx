If your child's curl pattern seems different from how it was a year or two ago, that's likely because it is. Curl and coil patterns in children change at several predictable points — and the change is normal, not a sign that something is wrong or that the routine is failing.

This guide covers when patterns change, what typically happens, and how to adjust care when it does.

---

## When Curl Patterns Change in Children

### Birth to age three: the biggest shift

This is the most significant period of curl pattern change. Babies are often born with a texture that doesn't represent their adult pattern — hormones from the womb influence the initial hair, and as those hormones leave the system the hair that grows in can be quite different.

For mixed race children specifically, the pattern that emerges between ages one and three is often tighter, more defined, or more coily than the hair at birth. This is the most common pattern change parents notice and the one most likely to require a significant routine adjustment.

### Ages four to seven: settling

Most children's patterns are fairly stable from about age four onward through primary school. Small variations happen but dramatic changes are less common during this window.

### Puberty: another shift

Hormonal changes at puberty can change the hair texture — sometimes significantly. Type 3 hair can become tighter or looser. Type 4 can become denser or change porosity. This is less universally acknowledged than the baby-to-toddler change but is real.

Patterns typically settle again in the mid-to-late teens. The routine that worked at age ten may need adjusting at age thirteen.

## What Usually Changes

### Pattern tightens

More common in the baby-to-toddler window and sometimes at puberty. Looser waves or curls become tighter coils. The practical adjustment: more moisture, heavier leave-in, more careful detangling. The same product weight that was appropriate for the previous pattern may not be enough.

### Pattern loosens slightly

Less common but it happens. Tight coils from toddlerhood can loosen slightly in the primary school years. The adjustment: you may be able to use slightly lighter products and less intensive detangling.

### Density changes

More hairs per square centimetre, or fewer. Density affects how long detangling takes, how much product is needed, and how well the hair holds moisture. A denser pattern after puberty can make an existing routine feel less effective — more product per section is needed, not a different product.

### Porosity changes

High porosity (from damage, sun, or chemical treatment) can develop over childhood without any obvious cause. If the hair is suddenly absorbing products differently or drying faster than before, porosity has likely shifted. A protein treatment every six to eight weeks can help if the hair feels mushy or stretchy when wet.

---

## Signs the Routine Needs Updating

- Products that worked well are suddenly sitting on top of the hair without absorbing
- The hair is consistently drier between wash days than it used to be
- Detangling is taking significantly longer or causing more discomfort
- The curl or coil looks different wet versus dry in a way that's changed
- The hair feels denser or thinner than before

Any of these suggest the pattern or porosity has shifted. The fix is usually a product weight adjustment and possibly a different leave-in or conditioner.

---

## How to Adjust

**If the pattern has tightened:** shift toward heavier leave-in, more generous conditioner, and a deeper conditioning treatment every two to three weeks. Detangling may need more sections and more time.

**If the pattern has loosened:** lighter leave-in. If the hair is getting weighed down by products that previously worked, reduce the amount or switch to a lighter formulation.

**If the hair is suddenly harder to moisturise:** test porosity. The spray bottle test on clean, dry hair is reliable. Low porosity needs heat during deep conditioning; high porosity needs a sealing step after leave-in.

## FAQs

**My child's curls were beautiful as a toddler and now look different at age seven. Is that permanent?**

Not necessarily permanent — puberty may bring another shift. But the pattern at age seven is likely close to the child's actual texture for childhood. The routine may simply need updating to match the current pattern.

**My daughter's hair changed dramatically after she started a new school. Could stress cause this?**

Physical or emotional stress can affect the hair indirectly — through changes in diet, sleep, or routine that affect moisture levels. A dramatic texture change (not just condition) in a short time is worth mentioning to a GP if other changes accompany it.

**My son's hair changed at age twelve and became much denser. Normal?**

Very common at puberty. The routine that worked at ten may need more product volume — same steps, more per section. A deep conditioning treatment every two to four weeks if the hair feels dry.

**Should I adjust the routine for each change or wait to see if it settles?**

Adjust when the current routine is clearly not working — not pre-emptively. If the hair is dry, adjust. If it's tangling more, adjust. If things are fine, leave them.
