Natural hair content focuses almost entirely on the length — the curl pattern, the products, the moisture. The scalp gets a few lines at the end about not scratching. This is backwards. A scalp that's producing too much sebum, too little, or dealing with buildup directly affects curl definition, hair growth rate, and how long any product routine actually works.

This guide is the scalp section that most routines skip.

---

## What a Healthy Scalp Looks and Feels Like

A healthy scalp is not something you notice. It's not itchy, not flaky, not tight, not oily to the touch. Hair grows from it without excessive shedding. After a wash, it doesn't feel immediately dry or immediately itchy within a day.

If any of these are present, the scalp needs attention before the routine can work properly:

- Persistent flaking (white or yellowish flakes, or both)
- Itching between washes
- Tightness or soreness
- Excess oil at the scalp within a day of washing
- Noticeable hair thinning at the hairline or parting

## The Most Common Scalp Issues in Natural Hair

### Dryness and flaking

In UK winter, central heating removes significant moisture from indoor air. The scalp responds to dry air the same way the hair does. Flaking from dryness is white, fine, and appears without redness or irritation.

Fix: a lightweight scalp oil (jojoba or sweet almond) applied directly to the scalp on wash day. Not heavy oils — those clog. A light oil applied sparingly after washing, before the routine, feeds the scalp without buildup.

### Product buildup on the scalp

When leave-in, cream, gel, or oil accumulates at the scalp rather than the lengths — because the product was applied too close to the roots, or because it wasn't fully rinsed — the scalp gets blocked. It looks like a flat, somewhat shiny coating at the root. The hair feels flat at the scalp and may smell slightly stale between washes.

Fix: clarify with a clarifying or chelating shampoo, massaging the scalp thoroughly. Apply leave-in from mid-length only, not at the scalp. Gels and creams are for the lengths.

### Hard water mineral buildup

In hard water areas, calcium and magnesium deposits accumulate on the scalp as well as the hair. It presents as itching or tightness that appears gradually and worsens over months. It's often confused with dry scalp.

Fix: monthly chelating with a product containing EDTA or phytic acid. This removes mineral deposits from both the scalp and the hair shaft. Clarifying shampoos don't do this — only chelating products remove minerals.

### Seborrhoeic dermatitis

A very common scalp condition — not an infection, not caused by bad hygiene. It produces yellowish, slightly oily flakes and itching, often at the hairline and around the ears. It can worsen with stress, cold weather, or hormonal changes. UK winters tend to trigger flare-ups.

Fix: a medicated shampoo containing ketoconazole (Nizoral, available over the counter at Boots and pharmacies) used every seven to ten days. Use as the shampoo step, leave on the scalp for three to five minutes, then rinse. Continue the rest of the routine as normal. It's compatible with natural hair care — rinse it out, then condition, leave-in, and seal as usual. For persistent or severe seborrhoeic dermatitis, see a GP.

### Traction and tension

Repeated tight styles — high ponytails, tight braids, extensions that are too heavy or installed too tightly — cause traction at the hairline and edges. It presents as hairline recession, small bumps at follicles, or pain during style installation.

Fix: rest protective styles. Give the hairline and edges period without tension. For styles, braid loosely from the hairline rather than pulling taut. If hairline recession is visible, see a trichologist — it responds to early treatment and may not respond to late treatment.

## Washing the Scalp Correctly

Most people either skip the scalp during washing (leaving buildup) or scrub it with fingernails (causing micro-abrasion and irritation). The correct approach:

- Apply shampoo to the scalp, not the lengths — it will rinse through the length as it rinses
- Massage with the pads of the fingers, not the nails
- Small circular motions, covering the full scalp including the nape and the area behind the ears
- Rinse thoroughly — insufficiently rinsed shampoo on the scalp causes dryness and irritation

Condition the lengths, not the scalp. Conditioner at the scalp adds to sebum and product buildup.

## Scalp Care by Season

**Autumn/winter:** central heating creates dry indoor air. Scalp oils help. If seborrhoeic dermatitis is a pattern condition, start the medicated shampoo before the flare-up rather than after.

**Spring/summer:** humidity increases but central heating decreases. Scalp often needs less intervention. This is also when pollen and pollution can trigger irritation — if the scalp becomes itchy or inflamed without another cause, seasonal allergies are worth considering.

## FAQs

**I have a dry scalp and also 4C hair. Should I oil my scalp?**

Yes, lightly. Jojoba oil closely resembles sebum and is absorbed without heavy buildup. A few drops massaged into the scalp after washing, before leave-in. Not on a dirty or unclean scalp — oil traps everything underneath it.

**My scalp is fine between washes but itchy within a few days of washing. Why?**

Most likely a reaction to something in the shampoo — fragrance, sulphates, or a preservative. Switch to a gentler sulphate-free or low-sulphate shampoo. If it persists with multiple different shampoos, see a dermatologist.

**I have seborrhoeic dermatitis and natural 4B hair. Does the medicated shampoo work?**

Yes. Ketoconazole shampoo works on any hair type. Apply to the wet scalp, leave three to five minutes, rinse. Then apply conditioner to the lengths, detangle, rinse. Continue LOC or LCO as usual. The medicated shampoo does not substitute for a regular conditioner on the lengths — use it on the scalp, condition the length separately.

**My edges are thinning. Is this scalp care or style care?**

Probably both. Traction at the edges from tight styles is the most common cause of edge thinning. Rest the edges from tight styles immediately. If after six to eight weeks of no tension the edges haven't started to fill in, see a trichologist — some cases respond to treatment if caught early.
