Your teenager wants to relax their hair. Before you decide how to respond, it helps to understand what's usually driving the request — because it's rarely just about the hair.

This guide isn't going to tell you what decision to make or what to tell your teenager to decide. That's for you and them. What it offers is a framework for having the conversation well, information about what a relaxer actually does, and some questions worth working through together.

---

## What's Usually Behind the Request

Most teenagers who want to relax their hair are not making a purely aesthetic decision. They're responding to something:

**Peer and social pressure:** if the majority of friends or peers have straight hair, and media representation skews toward straight hair, wanting to fit in is developmentally normal. This doesn't mean the request should be dismissed — it means the context is worth understanding.

**Managing their own hair:** some teenagers find natural coily hair difficult to manage, especially if they've been doing it themselves for the first time. The desire to relax it can be about reducing that friction rather than about appearance.

**Identity:** for some mixed race teenagers, hair is part of a broader identity negotiation that's ongoing through adolescence. A request to relax might be about trying on a different part of an identity that's still forming. It might also be about a teenager choosing their own path with their own body.

**A specific practical problem:** sometimes it's simpler — they can't get a style they want, or natural hair feels unmanageable for sport or activities they care about.

Understanding which of these is driving the request shapes the conversation.

## What a Relaxer Actually Does

A relaxer is a chemical treatment that permanently breaks and reforms the bonds in the hair shaft, making coily or curly hair straight. Key things to know:

**It is permanent.** The straightened hair stays straight. New growth continues to come in natural, so the straight-natural boundary moves as hair grows. Managing that boundary (touch-ups every six to twelve weeks) is a long-term commitment.

**It changes the hair's structure.** Relaxed hair is more fragile and more porous than the same hair in its natural state. It needs a different care routine and is more susceptible to breakage, especially if not maintained correctly.

**It requires maintenance.** The application process involves strong chemicals and must be done correctly. Over-processing causes significant damage. Under-maintained relaxed hair breaks at the new growth boundary.

**It can be reversed.** You cannot chemically un-relax hair. The only way back to natural hair is growing it out and cutting off the relaxed portions, which takes time.

**At what age?:** most stylists in the UK recommend not relaxing hair under the age of 14–16, as the scalp is more sensitive in younger children and the commitment to maintenance is harder to sustain. This is worth discussing with any salon before booking.

## Questions to Work Through Together

Before a decision is made, these are worth exploring:

**What specifically prompted this?** Is it a particular situation at school, a comment someone made, a style they want that they can't get with natural hair? Understanding the specific driver helps.

**Have they tried the styles they want with natural hair?** Some styles teenagers associate with relaxed hair can be achieved with stretched or blow-dried natural hair. Worth knowing whether they've actually tried.

**Do they understand the maintenance commitment?** A relaxer is not a one-time solution. Touch-ups every six to twelve weeks for as long as they want the straight result. Chemical cost, salon time, and care product cost ongoing.

**Are they prepared for the transition back if they change their mind?** This is not a scare tactic — it's practical. Knowing that they'd need to grow it out over one to two years if they change their mind in a year is relevant information for the decision.

**What does their natural hair feel like to manage now?** If the underlying issue is that the current routine isn't working, fixing the routine might address the desire for a relaxer without the permanence.

## Your Role in the Decision

If your teenager is fourteen or older, they have a significant degree of bodily autonomy. The decision is increasingly theirs to make.

Your role is to ensure they're making it with full information and for reasons that actually reflect what they want — not purely in response to external pressure they haven't examined. That's different from making the decision for them or overriding their choice.

If after a genuine conversation they still want a relaxer and they're old enough for it to be their decision, that is their decision. Your relationship with them and their confidence in talking to you about these things matters more than this particular outcome.

If they're younger (under 14), the decision is more appropriately yours, with their preference seriously weighed.

---

## What If You're Not Comfortable With It?

If you have concerns — about their age, about their reasons, about the process — it's worth saying so clearly and once, not repeatedly.

"I want us to wait six months so you can see if you still feel this way, because it's permanent" is a reasonable position. "I'm not comfortable with the chemical process until you're older" is also reasonable.

What tends to backfire: treating the request as a betrayal of identity or cultural pride. A teenager who feels judged for wanting something is more likely to pursue it covertly and with less parental input into the decision.

---

## Alternatives Worth Exploring First

If the conversation surfaces that the underlying issue is manageability or wanting specific styles:

- **Silk press:** a professional straightening using heat only. Temporary (reverts with water or humidity). Allows them to try the aesthetic of straight hair without chemical commitment.
- **Blow-out:** a stretched natural look that's significantly easier to manage than shrunken coils. Not straight, but different.
- **A better natural routine:** if the current routine is genuinely hard, the problem might be solvable. A natural hair stylist appointment to reset the routine and show technique can change how manageable the hair feels.

## FAQs

**My daughter is thirteen and says all her friends have relaxed hair. Should I let her?**

Most stylists and dermatologists recommend waiting until at least 14–16. It's worth a six-month wait to see whether the pressure from friends sustains. In the meantime, explore whether the styles she wants are achievable with temporary straightening or better natural styling.

**My mixed race son wants to relax his hair because he gets comments from other kids. What do I do?**

Separate the two issues: address the comments (that's a school conversation and a self-esteem conversation), and discuss the relaxer on its own merits. Relaxing his hair to stop comments relocates the problem rather than resolving it.

**My teenager has been managing their own natural hair for a year and says it's too much work. Is that a valid reason to relax?**

Yes, that's a valid reason. A routine that's genuinely unsustainable isn't serving them. Worth first exploring whether a better routine makes it less difficult, but if natural hair management remains genuinely unsustainable for their life at this age, that's a real factor.

**I had my hair relaxed at their age and I regret it. Should I tell them?**

Yes, share your experience — once, honestly, without pressure. Your experience is relevant context. Then let them make their own assessment.
