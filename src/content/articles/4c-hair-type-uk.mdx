If you've got 4C hair, you already know the standard advice doesn't quite land. Products that work for every curly influencer you follow sit on your hair and do nothing. Routines built for "natural hair" seem to assume a curl pattern you don't have. This guide is specifically for 4C hair in the UK — the tightest coils, the biggest shrinkage, and the complications that come with hard water and British weather that no American YouTube tutorial mentions.

## What Is 4C Hair?

4C is the tightest curl pattern on the typing scale — coils so compact that when your hair is dry, you often can't see a defined curl at all. Instead you get a dense, springy texture that compresses into itself. The coil is there, it's just so tight it folds back on itself rather than hanging in a visible spiral.

This isn't a flaw or damage. It's just how 4C hair is built.

### How to tell if you have 4C hair

Look at a single dry strand with no product in it. 4C coils are very tight — usually smaller than a pencil tip — and often show a zig-zag rather than an S-shape. The hair clumps minimally on its own without product, and shrinkage is extreme: many people with 4C hair see 50–75% shrinkage, sometimes more.

Most people don't have one single texture across their whole head. You might have 4B at your hairline and denser 4C at the crown. The type is less important than understanding how your specific hair behaves — porosity and density will shape your routine more than the letter and number.

### 4C vs 4A and 4B

| Type | What it looks like | Shrinkage |
|------|-------------------|-----------|
| 4A | Tight S-coils, usually visible when dry | High |
| 4B | Z/S hybrid, sharp angles, less defined | Very high |
| 4C | Tight Z-coil or no visible curl shape when dry | Extreme |

The practical difference between 4B and 4C is mostly degree. The care principles overlap enough that if you're unsure which you are, try the same routine and adjust based on how your hair responds.

## Why 4C Hair Behaves the Way It Does

### Shrinkage

Shrinkage happens because when 4C hair is wet it temporarily stretches out, then springs back to its resting state as it dries. The tighter the coil, the more dramatic that spring-back. Shrinkage is not damage — it's actually a sign of good elasticity. The problem is when people fight it with heat or tension, which is where real damage happens.

### Why moisture is harder to hold

The tight curl means your scalp's natural oils can't travel easily down the length of the hair — every bend interrupts the journey. This is why 4C hair can feel dry even on a healthy scalp, and why it needs regular moisturising from products rather than relying on scalp oil to do the job straight hair can rely on.

## A Basic 4C Routine That Works in the UK

Most 4C content online is written for US readers. The routines aren't wrong, but they're missing two things that matter in the UK: hard water and damp weather.

### Wash day

If you're in London, the South East, or the Midlands, your water is almost certainly hard — meaning it deposits minerals on your hair with every wash. Those minerals coat the shaft and stop your conditioner from working properly. A chelating shampoo (not the same as a clarifying shampoo — they do different things) used monthly strips those minerals off and resets your hair. Without that step, you're conditioning over a mineral layer and wondering why nothing helps.

→ [4C hair and hard water UK: what to do](/4c/4c-hair-hard-water-uk/)

A simple wash day for 4C hair:

1. **Pre-poo (optional but useful):** Apply oil or conditioner to dry hair before washing. Helps reduce friction during the wash.
2. **Chelating or clarifying shampoo** (monthly): Focus on the scalp; let it rinse through the length.
3. **Gentle shampoo or co-wash** (other wash days): 4C hair doesn't need harsh cleansing every week.
4. **Deep conditioner:** Apply to freshly washed, damp hair. A heat cap or steamer helps it penetrate rather than sit on top.
5. **Cool rinse:** Closes the cuticle and adds shine.

### Moisturising and sealing

After washing, apply products while your hair is still soaking wet — not damp, soaking. The LOC method (Leave-in, Oil, Cream) or LCO (Leave-in, Cream, Oil) both work; which one suits you depends on your porosity. Try both over a few wash days and see which leaves your hair softer the next morning.

→ [4C hair porosity: how to tell what yours is](/4c/4c-hair-porosity/)

### Protective styling

Braids, twists, and buns reduce daily manipulation, which is one of the biggest contributors to 4C breakage. A few things to keep in mind:

- Moisturise the hair before and during the style, not just at the start and end
- Tight installation on the hairline causes damage over time — if it hurts or you can see white bumps at the roots, it's too tight
- Give your hair two weeks out between styles to check its condition

## Common 4C Problems and What Actually Helps

### Shrinkage you can't manage

If shrinkage is making length impossible to see or detangling unbearable, stretching helps. Three methods that work without heat:

- **Banding:** Loose hair ties placed at intervals down a damp section. Once dry, some elongation remains.
- **African threading:** Thread wrapped around sections — common in West African hair traditions and very effective.
- **Twist-outs and braid-outs:** Twist or braid damp hair, let it dry fully, then separate gently. Distributes shrinkage more evenly and gives definition.

### Single-strand knots

When a strand of hair loops around itself and ties, you get a single-strand knot (sometimes called a fairy knot). They're nearly impossible to remove without breakage — the goal is prevention. Keep hair stretched, seal the ends regularly, and detangle in small sections from ends upward.

### Breakage at the edges

The hairline is the most fragile part. The hairs there are shorter and finer and more exposed to friction. A satin pillowcase or bonnet at night makes a real difference. Avoid tight styles that pull on the hairline. Give the edges a rest occasionally during protective style periods.

## Where to Find 4C Hair Products in the UK

UK availability has improved, but it's still patchy. A few reliable options:

- **Afro-Caribbean beauty supply shops** — best stocked for type 4 hair, especially in London, Birmingham, Manchester, Leeds, and Bristol
- **Boots and Superdrug** — natural hair ranges have grown, though selection is limited compared to specialist shops
- **Sally Beauty UK** — broader range than most high street chemists
- **Amazon.co.uk** — wide selection, but check seller ratings; third-party sellers can be inconsistent

Price doesn't reliably predict performance on 4C hair. Some of the most expensive products sit on high-porosity hair and do nothing. Start with what you can return if it doesn't work.

## FAQs

**Is 4C hair the same as Afro hair?**

Not exactly. "Afro hair" is a broader term covering types 3C through 4C. 4C is one texture within that range. Not all people with 4C hair use the same language to describe it.

**Does 4C hair grow more slowly?**

No — growth rate is genetics and health, not curl pattern. 4C hair can seem not to grow because shrinkage hides length gains and breakage cancels them out. The solution is reducing breakage, not trying to speed growth.

**How often should I wash 4C hair?**

Every one to two weeks is a common starting point. Washing too often without enough conditioning after dries the hair out; leaving it too long lets build-up and tangles compound. Pay attention to how your scalp feels rather than following a fixed schedule.

**Why does my 4C hair feel dry immediately after moisturising?**

Two likely causes: the product doesn't have enough water-based ingredients, or you're missing a sealant layer on top. Apply leave-in to soaking-wet hair, then seal with an oil or butter while it's still damp. If the dryness persists, check whether hard water is blocking your products from working.
