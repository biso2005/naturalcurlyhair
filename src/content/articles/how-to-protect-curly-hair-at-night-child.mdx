Most of the morning hair problems that parents of children with curly or coily hair face — tangles, dryness, one side more defined than the other — happen overnight. Eight hours on a cotton pillowcase is enough to compress curls, create friction frizz, and dry out hair that was well-moisturised the night before.

The fix is simple and takes less time than most parents expect.

---

## Why Overnight Protection Matters

### Cotton pillowcases dry hair out

Cotton is absorbent. It pulls moisture out of the hair shaft while the child sleeps. For straight hair this barely matters. For curly and coily hair, which already loses moisture faster than straight hair, eight hours of cotton contact removes a significant amount of what was applied during the day's moisturising routine.

### Cotton creates friction

The movement of the child's head against cotton overnight creates friction that disrupts curl and coil patterns. This is why curly-haired children often wake up with one side of their hair significantly more compressed or frizzy than the other — it's whichever side they slept on most.

### Central heating in UK winter makes it worse

UK homes with central heating running overnight dry out the bedroom air. This compounds the cotton-dryness problem. Children with type 4 or 3C hair sleeping in a centrally-heated UK bedroom in winter without protective measures can wake up with significantly drier, more tangled hair than they went to bed with.

---

## Option 1: Satin Bonnet (Most Effective)

A children's satin bonnet worn to bed is the highest-impact overnight protection for most curl and coil types.

Satin doesn't absorb moisture the way cotton does. It also creates less friction — the hair glides against the satin rather than catching. The result: the child wakes up with hair closer to what it looked like the night before.

**Choosing one:** look for 100% satin or silk (not "satin-feel" polyester). Children's sizes exist from approximately age two; younger babies can use a child-sized version if it fits. Look for elasticised edges that won't be too tight.

**Getting the child to wear it:** introduce early. Children who have worn a bonnet since they could remember wearing one rarely resist it. For older children who haven't worn one before, frame it as something their hair needs, not a punishment. Letting them choose the colour or design helps. Some children accept it more readily as part of a bedtime routine that's consistent.

---

## Option 2: Pineapple (For Older Children or Longer Hair)

The pineapple: gather the hair very loosely at the very top of the head — the absolute crown — and secure with a satin scrunchie (not a rubber band or metal hair tie). The hair goes up high enough that lying on it doesn't compress the curl or coil.

This works best for children whose hair is long enough to gather into a puff. It doesn't provide the same full protection as a bonnet because the scalp and lower sections are still on the pillowcase, but it protects the length and curl pattern from compression.

**Pineapple plus satin pillowcase** is a good combination for children who won't wear a bonnet — the pineapple protects the hair, the satin pillowcase reduces friction and moisture absorption at the scalp.

---

## Option 3: Satin Pillowcase

If a bonnet or pineapple is not working, a satin pillowcase is a lower-barrier alternative. It reduces friction and moisture absorption compared to cotton. Less effective than a bonnet because the hair isn't gathered and protected, but better than nothing.

Satin pillowcases are widely available and last well. Worth having as a backup or for nights when the bonnet has been lost.

---

## The Night Routine

A two-to-three minute bedtime routine that makes morning hair significantly easier:

1. **Spritz lightly with water** if the hair has become dry during the day
2. **Apply a small amount of leave-in or curl cream** through the hair
3. **Pineapple or smooth into a loose protective style**
4. **Satin bonnet on**

In the morning: remove the bonnet, release the pineapple, spritz lightly, smooth into a style. The hair is moisturised, protected, and far less tangled than it would be without this.

---

## For Babies and Young Toddlers

Bonnets for babies exist in very small sizes. For very young babies, a satin pillowcase is often more practical — they move in their sleep in ways that pull bonnets off.

For toddlers from about 18 months: a child's satin bonnet that's not too tight. Some parents introduce the bonnet around the same time as other bedtime objects — it becomes part of going to sleep.

---

## UK Winter Specifics

Central heating running overnight creates dry bedroom air that compounds overnight hair dryness. Extra steps that help in autumn and winter:

- A slightly more generous leave-in application before bed
- A humidifier in the child's bedroom if overnight dryness is consistently a problem
- The satin bonnet becomes even more important in winter — it's not optional in a heated bedroom

---

## FAQs

**My child refuses to wear a bonnet. What do I do?**

Start with a satin pillowcase — less intrusive, still reduces friction and dryness. Try different bonnet styles; some children accept a headband-style satin wrap better than a full bonnet. For older children, explain why — some children engage better when they understand the purpose.

**Does a bonnet work for looser curl types (type 2 or 3)?**

Yes, though the benefit is less dramatic than for type 4. Type 2 and 3 hair doesn't lose moisture as fast, but cotton pillowcase friction still disrupts the wave or curl pattern. A satin bonnet or pillowcase helps maintain definition for the next morning.

**My child's bonnet keeps coming off in the night. What should I try?**

Try a larger size with a more secure elastic, or a bonnet with a tie. Some parents use a slightly smaller bonnet that fits more snugly (without being tight). A satin pillowcase as a backup means even if the bonnet comes off, the child isn't sleeping on cotton.

**Do I need to re-moisturise in the morning even with a bonnet?**

A light morning spritz and smoothing is still worthwhile — the bonnet slows overnight drying significantly, but some moisture loss still happens. The morning routine is shorter and easier with a bonnet, not eliminated.
