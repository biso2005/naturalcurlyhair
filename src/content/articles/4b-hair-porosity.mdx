Porosity determines how easily your hair lets moisture in and how quickly it lets moisture back out. For 4B hair — which already has density and Z-angles working against even product distribution — knowing your porosity is the difference between a routine that works and one that just costs money.

The widespread assumption is that 4B hair is always high porosity. It isn't. Test yours before building a routine around an assumption that might be wrong.

## What Porosity Means for 4B Hair

**Low porosity 4B hair:**
- Takes a long time to fully wet in the shower
- Products sit on top rather than absorbing — the hair looks moisturised but dries out quickly
- Builds up easily: creams and oils pile on the surface
- Responds well to heat during conditioning — warmth helps the cuticle open
- Can feel weighed down or waxy with heavy products, even though the hair seems dry

**High porosity 4B hair:**
- Absorbs water and product quickly
- Loses moisture just as fast — dryness returns within hours
- Frizzes in humidity
- Feels rough or coarse to the touch on dry strands
- More prone to breakage, especially at the ends

**Medium porosity 4B hair:** Absorbs and retains reasonably well. Less common in type 4 hair, but it exists — don't rule it out.

## Skip the Float Test

The float test — drop a strand in water, watch if it sinks or floats — is cited in almost every natural hair porosity article. For 4B hair, it's unreliable.

The tight bends in 4B hair trap air pockets that keep strands afloat even when the cuticle is open. Product residue on the shaft changes the result further. The test doesn't reliably translate to tighter textures.

## Two Tests That Work on 4B Hair

### The spray bottle test

Spritz a small section of clean, dry hair with plain water and watch for 30 seconds.

- Water **beads on the surface** and takes a while to absorb — low porosity
- Water **absorbs quickly** and the section darkens fast — high porosity
- Water **sinks in but slowly** — medium porosity

This is the most practical test for 4B hair because it shows how a whole curl cluster behaves. Do it after a clarifying wash — product buildup can make low-porosity hair appear to be high porosity.

### The feel test

Run a single clean, product-free strand between your fingers from tip to root.

- **Smooth, almost slippery** — cuticles are flat, likely low porosity
- **Rough or bumpy, snags slightly** — cuticles are raised, likely high porosity
- **Fragile or breaks mid-stroke** — high porosity with some damage alongside it

## Roots vs Ends: Why 4B Hair Often Has Mixed Porosity

Your roots are recent growth. They haven't been through years of washing, detangling, and wear. Your ends are much older — and older hair is almost always more porous than newer growth.

If your 4B hair has any real length, your roots may resist moisture (low porosity) while your ends are thirsty (high porosity). Using the same products and amounts on both means you're always under-moisturising the ends and potentially overloading the roots.

Do the spray bottle test on roots separately from ends. If the absorption speed is noticeably different, you have mixed porosity — and your routine needs to account for it.

## What to Do Based on Your Result

### Low porosity 4B hair

The challenge is getting moisture in. Once it's in, your hair holds it reasonably well.

- **Use heat when deep conditioning:** A heat cap, steamer, or warm damp towel helps moisture reach the hair. Without it, conditioner sits on the surface and rinses off without doing much.
- **Apply products to soaking-wet hair:** The wetter, the better. Don't let the hair dry between conditioning and product application.
- **Choose lighter, water-based products:** Heavy butters and thick creams on low-porosity 4B hair pile up on the surface. A lighter leave-in, then a sealing oil is usually more effective than cream-heavy layering.
- **Clarify regularly:** Buildup is a bigger problem for low-porosity hair because products just keep accumulating. Monthly clarifying keeps the baseline clear.

### High porosity 4B hair

The challenge is keeping moisture in once it's there.

- **Use heavier sealants:** Castor oil, shea butter, and thick creams create a physical barrier that slows moisture leaving the hair. Apply while the hair is still wet.
- **Consider occasional protein treatments:** Protein can temporarily reduce how fast moisture escapes — useful for chemically treated or heat-damaged 4B hair. Don't overdo it; too much protein makes 4B hair brittle and prone to snapping.
- **Protect overnight:** High-porosity hair loses moisture to cotton pillowcases quickly. A satin bonnet makes a measurable difference.
- **Layer the LOC or LCO method deliberately:** For high porosity, LCO (cream before oil) often provides better moisture retention than LOC. Try both and compare.

### Mixed porosity 4B hair

- Apply lighter products at the roots, heavier products at the ends
- Focus deep conditioning time on the ends — use more product there
- Use heat to help the roots absorb moisture while the ends benefit from extra product

## Common Mistakes

**Building a whole routine on an assumption.** If you've read that 4B hair is inherently high porosity and built your routine around that without testing, you may have gotten it backwards. Low-porosity 4B hair exists. Test before you buy.

**Adding more product when nothing absorbs.** With low-porosity hair, more product doesn't solve the absorption problem — the cuticle is the barrier, not the quantity. More product just creates more buildup. Heat is the fix, not volume.

**Using the same approach on roots and ends.** If your hair has any real length, your porosity varies by zone. A single approach leaves something under-served.

**Not accounting for build-up as a variable.** Hard water deposits on the hair shaft mimic the symptoms of low porosity (products sitting on top, moisture not absorbing). If you're in a hard water area and nothing seems to work, a chelating wash to clear minerals may be the actual fix — not a porosity treatment.

## FAQs

**Is 4B hair always high porosity?**

No. Curl pattern and porosity are separate characteristics. Low-porosity 4B hair is common. Test before assuming.

**Can porosity change?**

Yes. Chemical treatments, consistent heat damage, and simple age all shift hair toward higher porosity. If a routine that worked before has stopped working, re-test rather than assuming the products are the problem.

**My products worked last year and now they don't. Is it porosity?**

Possibly — especially if you've done any chemical treatments or significant heat styling. Re-test. But also check your water: if you've moved to a harder water area, mineral build-up could be the culprit.
