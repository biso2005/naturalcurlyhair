Most braid problems that bring people back to the salon in three weeks instead of six — persistent itching, flaking, thin edges, hair that breaks during removal — originate at the scalp during the weeks the braids are in, not from the installation or removal itself. The scalp is out of sight and mostly out of mind under braids. That neglect is what causes the damage.

This guide is specifically about the scalp — not braid aesthetics, not removal technique. The scalp, while the braids are in.

---

## What Happens to the Scalp Under Braids

**Sebum accumulates.** The scalp continues producing oil. With loose hair, it spreads down the shaft. With braids, the oil can't travel past the braid root — it pools at the scalp under each section. Over two to three weeks without cleansing, this becomes the waxy, slightly smelly buildup that's very difficult to address while the braids are still in.

**Product residue adds to it.** If you're applying leave-in or oils to the braid roots (common but usually too much), the product accumulates there with the sebum.

**The scalp gets dry simultaneously.** Despite all the sebum buildup, the scalp surface can become dehydrated — especially in UK central heating. This sounds contradictory but it's the same mechanism as oily-but-dehydrated skin: the barrier is compromised, and moisture and oil accumulate at the surface rather than being distributed. The result is both greasy and itchy.

**Hard water minerals deposit on the scalp.** Every scalp wash with hard water in London or the South East adds mineral deposits to the scalp surface as well as the extension hair. These accumulate over weeks and contribute to itching.

## The Care Routine While Braids Are In

### Every two to three days: scalp moisture

The scalp needs moisture even when the hair is protective-styled. Apply to the scalp directly, not to the braid length.

**How:** a small nozzle bottle or dropper with a light moisture solution — diluted leave-in in water (roughly 1 part leave-in to 4 parts water), or a light scalp spray product.

Apply directly to the scalp at the parting between braids or rows. A few drops per section; don't saturate. Massage lightly with fingertip pads.

**Follow with a small amount of light scalp oil** — jojoba or argan — along the same parts. The moisture first, the oil to seal.

**What not to do:** apply heavy grease, castor oil, or thick butter to the scalp under braids. These accumulate faster than you can remove them, and they're very difficult to wash out through the braid structure.

### Every two weeks: scalp wash

Non-negotiable for most scalps. Some people with drier scalps can go three weeks, but most need this at two weeks.

**Method:**
1. Mix shampoo with water in a squeeze bottle — about 1 part shampoo to 3–4 parts water. Diluting is important; full-strength shampoo applied to braids can be drying and difficult to rinse fully.
2. Apply the mixture directly to the scalp, section by section, between the braids or rows
3. Massage with fingertip pads (not nails) using small circular motions across the full scalp
4. Rinse very thoroughly — residue left in the scalp under braids is worse than not washing at all
5. Apply the scalp moisture as above after washing
6. **Dry fully.** Wet scalp under braids develops mildew over days — this smells musty and is difficult to address without removing the braids. Use a hooded dryer, sit dryer, or diffuser after air drying to ensure the scalp is completely dry.

### Addressing hard water (hard water areas)

After the shampoo rinse and before drying, an ACV rinse reduces surface mineral deposits:
- 1 part apple cider vinegar to 4 parts water
- Pour over the scalp and braids
- Leave two minutes
- Rinse and dry fully

Monthly, use a chelating shampoo (containing EDTA) instead of regular shampoo for the scalp wash. Mineral buildup under braids is harder to remove than on loose hair — prevention through monthly chelating is significantly more effective than trying to address established buildup.

## The Edges: The Most Vulnerable Area

The hairline and temples have finer, shorter hair that's more susceptible to the combined effects of braid tension and scalp neglect.

**Signs the edges need attention:**
- Visible thinning or hairline recession from previous styles
- The skin at the temples looks raised or bumpy after installation — folliculitis from tension
- Itching concentrated at the hairline even after scalp washing

**What helps:**
- A very light oil (jojoba, sweet almond) applied to the edges daily — not a heavy edge control
- No additional tension on the edges — don't pull the braids back tightly; let them lie naturally
- If there's folliculitis (small bumps): a diluted tea tree oil solution (2 drops in a tablespoon of carrier oil) applied with a cotton pad to the affected area. See a GP if it doesn't resolve within a week.

---

## When the Scalp Is Telling You to Take the Braids Down

Itching that doesn't respond to scalp washing, persistent flaking despite moisture routine, visible hairline recession during the style, or pain at any point in the braid — these are signals the braids need to come down.

Taking braids down two weeks early because the scalp needs it is better than keeping them in and causing damage that takes months to recover from. Protective styles protect the hair; when they're not protecting the scalp, they've reached the end of their useful life.

## FAQs

**My scalp itches badly three weeks into braids. What's happening?**

Almost certainly scalp buildup — sebum, product, and possibly hard water minerals that haven't been addressed. Do a thorough scalp wash with diluted shampoo, an ACV rinse, then dry fully. If the itching resolves for a few days and comes back, you need to wash more frequently. If it doesn't resolve at all after washing, the braids may need to come down.

**I applied oil to my scalp and now there's white flakes along the parts. What are those?**

Likely the oil sitting on top of dry skin and product buildup — not dandruff necessarily, but accumulated residue visible at the scalp. A scalp wash will address it. After washing, apply less oil and apply it more sparingly.

**I have seborrhoeic dermatitis. Can I get braids?**

Yes, with more frequent scalp washing (every week rather than two) and a medicated shampoo (ketoconazole, available over the counter as Nizoral). Apply to the scalp through the braids, leave three to five minutes, rinse thoroughly. Continue the rest of your seborrhoeic dermatitis management as usual. The braids don't worsen the condition if the scalp is being properly managed — neglect does.

**After removing my braids, my edges are thin. What do I do?**

Stop all tension on the hairline — no tight styles, no heavy extensions. Light scalp oil daily to the edges. Give the area six to eight weeks without any traction. If the edges don't start to fill in after that rest period, see a trichologist — early traction alopecia responds to treatment; established scarring does not.
