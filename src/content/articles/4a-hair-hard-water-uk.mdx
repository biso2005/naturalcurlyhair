If your 4A routine involves conditioner that seems to do nothing, coils that have lost definition for no obvious reason, or a general dullness that no product fixes — and you're in England — hard water is the most likely explanation before anything product-related.

The minerals in hard water coat your hair shaft every time you wash. Conditioner can't get through that layer. You're moisturising the mineral film, not the hair.

## What Hard Water Does to 4A Coils

The minerals in hard water — mainly calcium and magnesium — settle on the hair shaft with each wash. On 4A hair specifically, this shows up as:

- **Coils that lose definition faster than they should** — mineral film on the cuticle prevents coils from holding their shape as cleanly
- **Conditioner that seems to do nothing** — you're applying it on top of a mineral layer, not to the hair
- **Rough texture on dry hair** that doesn't resolve with product
- **Frizz in humidity that's worse than expected** — the rough mineral-coated cuticle catches moisture from the air more than a smooth cuticle would
- **A dull or ashy tone** that conditioning doesn't fix

Hard water damage on 4A hair is often misread as protein overload because the symptoms overlap — rough texture, stiffness, product that won't absorb. If you've reduced or eliminated protein and nothing improved, check the water.

## How to Check If You're in a Hard Water Area

If you're in London, the South East, East Anglia, or the East Midlands, the answer is almost certainly yes.

| Region | Water hardness |
|--------|---------------|
| London (Thames Water) | Hard to very hard |
| South East (Southern Water) | Hard to very hard |
| East Anglia (Anglian Water) | Hard to very hard |
| East Midlands (Severn Trent) | Hard to very hard |
| Yorkshire | Variable — soft to moderate |
| North West | Mostly soft |
| Wales | Soft |
| Scotland | Very soft |

For your specific address: your water company's postcode checker gives you an exact hardness figure. For a direct test, aquarium water hardness strips from a pet shop give a colour result in under two minutes from your tap water.

## The Fix: Chelating Shampoo Monthly

A chelating shampoo removes mineral deposits from the hair shaft. It's different from a clarifying shampoo, which removes product buildup (oils, silicones, styling product residue). Clarifying does not remove minerals.

Check the label for EDTA (disodium EDTA or tetrasodium EDTA) or phytic acid. If neither is listed, it's not a chelating shampoo regardless of what it claims.

Use it once a month in a hard water area. Everything else in your routine stays the same — you're just adding one monthly reset step.

**After your first chelating wash:**
- Your hair may feel stripped immediately after — chelating shampoos are thorough
- Deep condition straight away, with heat if you have it
- Within 24 hours, your conditioner should feel like it's actually working again

## Between Wash Days

If you spritz water on your 4A hair between washes to refresh coils, that water is depositing minerals each time you use it.

Two options:
- Use distilled or filtered water in your spritz bottle (available cheaply at most supermarkets)
- Add a small amount of aloe vera juice to your spritz — mildly acidic, which helps neutralise mineral deposits between washes

## Shower Filters: Helpful But Not Sufficient

Shower filters marketed for curly hair remove chlorine — which does dry out hair and irritate the scalp. They're worth using if chlorine is an issue. But most shower filters cannot remove calcium and magnesium hardness at domestic flow rates. A shower filter alone will not fix a mineral buildup problem.

If you use a shower filter, still add the monthly chelating wash. They address different things.

## Does Hard Water Affect 4A Hair Differently Than 4B or 4C?

The mechanism is the same — mineral film blocks the cuticle — but the effect shows up differently.

On 4B and 4C hair, hard water tends to increase existing stiffness and roughness, making definition even harder to achieve. On 4A hair, where natural clumping and definition are the starting point, hard water is more likely to degrade coil quality — coils that used to hold well start falling apart faster, frizz increases, and the hair looks dull rather than vibrant.

The other difference: 4A hair is generally more responsive to product change than 4B or 4C. This means 4A naturals in hard water areas sometimes successfully switch products and temporarily get better results — but the improvement is short-lived because the underlying mineral accumulation continues. The pattern of "tried a new product, worked for a month, then stopped working" is a strong indicator of hard water.

## FAQs

**I moved to London from a soft water area and my 4A routine stopped working. What happened?**

Classic hard water transition. Everything you were using worked because soft water wasn't depositing minerals. Now it is. Add a chelating shampoo and your routine should recover.

**Can I use apple cider vinegar instead of a chelating shampoo?**

ACV is mildly acidic and helps close the cuticle and reduce some surface mineral residue, but it doesn't fully remove mineral deposits the way a chelating shampoo does. Useful as a rinse between chelating sessions; not a substitute for chelating itself.

**How do I know if it's hard water vs protein overload?**

The easiest diagnostic: check your water hardness. If you're in a hard water area, do a chelating wash first and see if that resolves the issue before cutting protein. Hard water mimics protein overload symptoms, and cutting protein when you actually have mineral buildup doesn't help.

**I'm in Scotland — do I need to worry about hard water?**

No. Scottish water is soft to very soft. Hard water is very unlikely to be your problem. Focus on porosity and your product routine instead.
