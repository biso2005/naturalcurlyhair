All oils sit on the surface to some degree, slowing moisture evaporation. The difference between oils is how much they penetrate the hair shaft, how heavy they feel, and how much buildup they create over time. Pick by your hair's porosity and weight, not by which oil gets the most mentions online.

---

## Penetrating vs Sealing Oils

**Penetrating oils** absorb into the hair shaft rather than sitting purely on the surface. They provide internal conditioning as well as surface sealing. Useful for high porosity hair that needs internal support.

**Sealing oils** sit on the cuticle surface and slow moisture evaporation. They don't penetrate much, but that surface action is what sealing is for.

Most oils do a bit of both, weighted toward one or the other.

## Oils by Type

### Light oils — good for fine hair, 2A–3B, low-porosity hair

**Argan oil:** light, absorbs quickly, minimal buildup. Works on all hair types. A few drops is enough — doesn't need to be applied heavily to seal. Available widely in Boots and Superdrug.

**Jojoba oil:** very light, closely resembles sebum, low buildup risk. Good scalp oil as well as hair sealant. Widely available, reasonable cost.

**Grapeseed oil:** very light, almost no smell, quick-absorbing. Good as a daily refresh sealant for lighter curl types.

**Baobab oil:** light, good penetration, conditioning. Less common but available in Afro hair sections of larger Boots stores and online.

### Medium oils — good for 3C–4B, medium porosity

**Olive oil:** moderate weight, penetrating properties, conditioning. A classic for a reason. Can build up over time if applied heavily — use a few drops, not tablespoons.

**Avocado oil:** heavier than olive, good penetration, conditioning for dry or damaged hair. Works well in the LOC oil step for type 4 hair.

**Sweet almond oil:** light to medium, gentle, good for scalp use. Less rich than avocado but absorbs without heaviness.

### Heavy oils and butters — for 4B–4C, high porosity, very dry hair

**Castor oil:** the heaviest common oil. High viscosity means it's a strong sealant — it doesn't penetrate much, but it holds moisture in effectively. Too heavy to use alone; blend with a lighter carrier oil (half castor, half jojoba or argan) for easier application. Common in UK Afro hair shops and online.

**Coconut oil:** penetrating, solid at room temperature, effective for high-porosity hair. Controversial because some people find it causes protein-like stiffness over time — monitor how your hair responds. Available everywhere.

**Shea butter:** not strictly an oil but used the same way. Very heavy, best for 4B/4C or very dry high-porosity hair. Works as the cream step in LOC rather than the oil step.

## How to Choose

**For fine or 2A–3B hair:** argan, grapeseed, or jojoba. A few drops over leave-in as the sealant step. Nothing heavier or the hair goes flat.

**For medium porosity 3C–4A hair:** olive, avocado, or sweet almond. Moderate amount over leave-in, under cream.

**For high-porosity 4B–4C hair:** castor blend, coconut, or avocado. More product needed; heavier is appropriate. Use in the LOC oil step, sealed with a butter or cream.

**For low-porosity hair:** lighter oils, less of them. Sealing is less critical because the cuticle is tight and retains moisture well. Focus on getting moisture in (water, heat during deep conditioning) rather than sealing intensively.

## UK Buying Notes

**Grocery stores:** rapeseed oil (the UK equivalent of canola) is a decent light-to-medium sealant oil if nothing else is available — it's not commonly listed in hair content but it's light and works.

**Boots and Superdrug:** argan, coconut, and jojoba are widely stocked in the hair care aisle. SheaMoisture and Cantu products containing oils are also available.

**Afro hair BSS (beauty supply stores):** castor oil, baobab, and less common oils. Leeds, Manchester, Birmingham, and most larger UK cities have at least one. Usually significantly cheaper than supermarket prices for the same oil.

**Online:** the most cost-effective source for larger quantities of carrier oils. Look for food-grade or cosmetic-grade — both are appropriate for hair use.

## Hard Water and Oils

In hard water areas, mineral deposits sit on the hair shaft underneath any oil applied. The oil seals the minerals in along with the moisture. This is why hard water routines that include good sealing still stop working over time — the oil isn't the problem, the mineral layer underneath it is.

Monthly chelating removes the mineral layer and allows both moisture and sealant to contact the hair properly.

## FAQs

**Can I use cooking oils on my hair?**

Yes — olive oil, coconut oil, and avocado oil in their food-grade form are the same product as the "hair" versions. The packaging markup is significant. Food-grade cold-pressed olive oil from a supermarket works identically to the same oil in a hair product bottle.

**Do I have to use oil in my routine?**

No. Many people use leave-in plus cream only and don't add oil. The cream seals adequately on its own for many hair types. Oil is most useful for high-porosity hair that loses moisture fast, and for type 4 hair that benefits from extra sealing.

**My hair feels greasy after I apply oil. Am I using too much?**

Almost certainly yes. A few drops is the right amount for most application. Oils should feel absorbed, not coating. If you applied a tablespoon, that's the issue. Reduce to two to four drops and see if the result is different.

**Which oil is best for edges?**

A light oil rather than a heavy one — you want the edges moisturised and laying smooth, not coated in castor oil. Jojoba, argan, or a light edge control with an oil base. Castor oil on edges creates buildup at the hairline over time.
