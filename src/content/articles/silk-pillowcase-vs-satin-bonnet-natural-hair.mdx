**A satin bonnet protects natural hair better than a silk pillowcase.** It keeps the hair in one position all night, prevents the style from being displaced by movement, and doesn't allow the ends to escape and rub on anything. A silk pillowcase is better than a cotton one, but the hair still moves freely against the pillow surface.

If you can wear a bonnet comfortably, wear the bonnet.

---

## Why the Bonnet Wins Technically

**The hair doesn't move.** The bonnet contains the hair in one position. A gathered pineapple or puff inside the bonnet stays gathered. The curl pattern that went to bed is much closer to the curl pattern that wakes up.

**Ends are fully protected.** The ends of natural hair are the oldest, most fragile part of the hair. In a bonnet, the ends are folded up and away from any surface. On a silk pillowcase, the ends still make contact with the pillow and with bedding, collar fabric, or other surfaces during sleep.

**No product transfer.** A bonnet prevents styling products from transferring to the pillowcase. Leave-in and cream stay on the hair overnight rather than transferring to the pillow surface, which then re-absorbs moisture from the hair on the next contact cycle.

---

## When the Pillowcase Makes Sense

**Comfort and consistency.** Some people find bonnets uncomfortable to sleep in — they slip off, create pressure at the elastic, or feel too warm. A bonnet that comes off in the night provides no protection. A silk pillowcase worn consistently every night provides more protection than a bonnet worn occasionally.

**Partners and self-consciousness.** This is a real consideration. A silk pillowcase requires no change to how the bed looks or any explanation. A satin or silk pillowcase on the bed is invisible.

**Short natural hair (TWA or close crop).** For very short natural hair, a pillowcase provides nearly as much protection as a bonnet — there's little length for the hair to move through or ends to rub on the surface. The bonnet advantage is most significant for longer hair.

**Travel.** A silk pillowcase rolls up flat and adds almost no weight to a bag. A bonnet is small but easier to lose. Both are easy to travel with; the pillowcase is slightly more versatile (it fits any pillow).

## The Satin vs Silk Question

Both satin and silk reduce friction significantly compared to cotton. The difference:

**Silk:** a natural fibre with protein content that's genuinely beneficial to hair. More expensive (£30–80 for a quality silk pillowcase). Higher maintenance (hand wash or very gentle machine wash).

**Satin:** a weave structure, usually synthetic (polyester). Significantly cheaper (£5–15 for a good satin pillowcase or bonnet). Machine washable. Similar friction reduction to silk in practice.

For most natural hair routines, satin provides the same practical benefit as silk at a fraction of the cost. Silk has a slight advantage for skin (the protein content benefits skin too), but for hair protection the friction reduction is the primary mechanism and satin achieves this as well as silk.

The recommendation: start with satin. If you have the budget for silk and want the skin benefits too, upgrade. Don't feel that a £6 satin bonnet from Amazon is inferior to a £60 silk pillowcase for hair protection — it isn't.

## The Practical Setup

**Most protective:** satin bonnet with a pineapple or puff gathered inside + satin pillowcase under the bonnet for if the bonnet comes off during the night.

**Practical minimum:** satin pillowcase. No bonnet needed. Better than cotton, easier than the combined setup.

**For locs:** satin bonnet (for shorter locs) or satin pillowcase (for longer locs that don't fit comfortably in a bonnet). Locs against cotton create lint accumulation over time in addition to the friction issue.

**For braids and box braids:** a large satin bonnet or a satin pillowcase. Braids are less vulnerable than loose natural hair but the ends benefit from not rubbing on cotton overnight.

## FAQs

**My bonnet keeps falling off during the night. What do I do?**

A bonnet with tighter elastic (or adjustable drawstring), a satin sleep cap that fits like a beanie rather than an elastic bonnet, or combining the pineapple with the bonnet so the gathered hair creates a structure for the bonnet to grip. If nothing works, switch to the pillowcase — consistent use matters more than optimal method.

**I use both a bonnet and a silk pillowcase. Is that overkill?**

The bonnet is the primary protection; the pillowcase is the backup for when the bonnet comes off. The combination is the most protective option and not excessive if the bonnet does move during the night.

**Does it matter what my duvet cover is made of?**

Somewhat. If the hair regularly escapes the bonnet and contacts the duvet cover, a cotton duvet cover creates the same friction issues as a cotton pillowcase. Satin or silk duvet covers exist; most people find this excessive and address it by keeping the hair better contained in the bonnet rather than replacing all bedding.

**What's the minimum price for a satin bonnet that actually works?**

The cheapest satin bonnets (£3–6 on Amazon UK) work for friction reduction — the satin weave is the mechanism, not the price. Where cheaper bonnets fail is elastic quality (breaks or loses tension faster) and size (may not accommodate a large puff or longer hair). A mid-range option (£8–12) with adjustable drawstring or good-quality elastic lasts longer and fits better.
