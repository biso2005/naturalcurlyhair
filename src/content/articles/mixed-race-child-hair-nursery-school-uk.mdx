School is where your child's hair spends most of its day. What happens there — how it's treated during PE, whether it's touched by other children, whether the school's hair policies affect natural styles — matters for both hair health and how your child feels about their hair.

This guide covers the practical and the less obvious: what to send your child in with, what to watch for, and what you can push back on.

---

## Practical: Sending Your Child to School With Natural Hair

### Styles for the school day

The school day involves movement, leaning against things, and sometimes other children pulling or touching hair. Styles that protect the hair from manipulation during the day make wash day easier and reduce breakage.

**Best styles for primary school:**
- Puffs or pineapples secured with a satin scrunchie
- Two-strand twists or flat twists (stay in for several days)
- Box braids or cornrows (protective, low manipulation)
- For shorter hair: a well-moisturised, defined wash-and-go

Avoid: elaborate styles that will need re-doing daily. The goal is protective and low-maintenance during school hours.

### PE

PE is the most challenging time for natural hair at school. Sweat, hair bands that pull, and compression from hats or helmets all cause damage over time.

**What helps:**
- Two-strand twists or braids before PE days
- A satin-lined sports cap or headband that prevents direct cotton contact
- Replacing rubber hair bands with satin scrunchies — cotton and rubber create friction that breaks natural hair

**What to avoid:** tight high ponytails on washday hair that hasn't been moisturised, or PE on a day when the hair is already at maximum stretch.

### Headwear

Some schools require hats for outdoor activities. If the hat is cotton-lined, ask whether a satin-lined alternative is permitted or bring one from home. Repeated cotton hat contact dries natural hair and creates friction frizz over time.

## The Policy Question: What Schools Can and Can't Do

### Protective styles are legitimate hairstyles

Braids, cornrows, twists, locs, and afros are not "extreme hairstyles." They are culturally significant protective styles with practical benefits for natural hair. Any school policy that bans or discourages them because they are associated with Black or mixed heritage students is likely unlawful under the Equality Act 2010.

In 2023, the UK government updated guidance to make clear that schools cannot impose hair rules that discriminate on racial grounds. Several high-profile cases have established that schools excluding children for Afro hair or locs are acting unlawfully.

If your child's school has a hair policy that restricts natural styles:
- Request the policy in writing
- Ask whether the restriction is applied equally to all hair types
- Raise it with the head teacher in the first instance
- If unresolved, contact the local authority or seek advice from organisations including the Equality and Human Rights Commission

### Touching without permission

Other children touching your child's natural hair is common. It is not something your child has to accept. Teach your child that they can say "please don't touch my hair" and mean it, and that they can report it to a teacher if it continues.

If it's persistent or feels targeted, raise it with the school. Children treating a classmate's hair as a curiosity they're entitled to handle is worth addressing as a school behaviour issue.

## Nursery and Early Years

Younger children in nursery settings often have their hair managed by nursery staff during sessions — hair is tied up for messy play, put into a hat for outdoor time, or tidied up by staff.

Worth discussing with nursery staff before issues arise:

- **How they handle wet or dirty hair:** natural coily and curly hair should not be brushed dry. If the child's hair gets wet or dirty during nursery, the staff should know to leave it until it can be properly dealt with at home, rather than attempting to brush it dry.
- **Protective styles before nursery:** send the child in braids or twists that can survive messy play without the staff needing to manage them.
- **Hats and headwear:** ask whether the nursery uses cotton hat-style headwear and whether you can bring a satin-lined alternative.

Many nursery staff are trying their best with unfamiliar hair and a short conversation at the start of the year prevents most problems.

## Secondary School and Older Children

Older children increasingly manage their own hair and their own relationship with school policy. The relevant considerations:

**For the child:** they should know they have the right to wear natural styles and that restrictions on Afro hair or natural hairstyles are challengeable. This is part of the broader conversation about their rights at school.

**For the parent:** secondary schools have more complex peer dynamics around hair. If your child is experiencing comments or targeting related to their natural hair, take it seriously — it often intensifies during puberty when identity becomes more freighted.

**For both:** the teenager who understands their hair, can care for it themselves, and knows it's protected by law is better equipped than the one who doesn't.

## FAQs

**My child's school says their afro is "extreme." What can I do?**

Request the policy in writing. Ask whether the policy would be applied to a white child with hair the same volume. If the restriction only applies to Afro-textured styles, it is almost certainly discriminatory under the Equality Act 2010. The head teacher is the first point of contact; the local authority or EHRC are next if that doesn't resolve it.

**The nursery staff brushed my daughter's dry coily hair and now it's very frizzy and tangled. How do I address this?**

Address it calmly with the nursery manager. Explain that dry brushing causes damage to natural coily hair and ask that staff leave the hair alone if it needs attention, rather than attempting to manage it. Bring the child in protective styles that require less intervention.

**Other children in the class keep touching my child's hair. The teacher says it's just curiosity. What do I do?**

It is curiosity — and it's still not acceptable. Your child's hair is not a tactile exhibit. Raise it as a behaviour issue with the class teacher and, if it continues, the head. Frame it as one child repeatedly touching another child without consent, which is the accurate description.

**My child's school is excellent in other ways and I don't want to cause problems. Do I have to push back on hair policies?**

That's your call. Some parents choose to avoid conflict on this. Others see it as a values question worth the friction. Worth knowing: you have more standing than you might think, and schools generally respond to a politely worded letter citing the Equality Act and requesting the policy be reviewed.
