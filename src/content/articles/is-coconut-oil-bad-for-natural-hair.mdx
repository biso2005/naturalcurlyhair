Coconut oil is not bad for natural hair. It's one of the few oils that actually penetrates the hair shaft rather than sitting on top of it, which makes it genuinely useful for reducing moisture loss. The problems come from how it's used: too much of it, on the wrong hair type, or without understanding what it does and doesn't do.

## Why some people have a bad experience with it

**Protein sensitivity.** Coconut oil is high in lauric acid, which has an affinity for hair proteins. For most hair, this is fine. For hair that's protein-sensitive — hair that reacts to protein with stiffness, brittleness, or increased breakage — coconut oil can trigger that same response. If your hair goes stiff or crunchy after coconut oil use, protein sensitivity is the likely cause.

**Low-porosity hair.** Low-porosity hair already has tightly closed cuticles that resist moisture entry. Applying a penetrating oil on top makes it harder for water and water-based products to get in. If you have low-porosity hair, coconut oil works better applied to damp hair right after washing (when the cuticle is open) rather than to dry hair.

**Using it as a moisturiser.** Coconut oil is a sealant — it locks in moisture. It doesn't add moisture. If you apply it to dry hair hoping it will hydrate, it will seal in the dryness. The correct order is: water or water-based product first, then oil on top to seal.

**Too much.** A thick coat of coconut oil on the scalp blocks follicles, creates build-up, and goes rancid over time. A little goes a long way — a small amount worked through the mid-shaft and ends is enough.

## When coconut oil works well

- As a pre-shampoo treatment (applied to dry hair, then washed out) — it reduces protein loss during washing
- Sealing the ends of stretched or blown-out styles
- Mixed into a deep conditioner in small amounts
- Protecting hair before swimming in chlorinated water

## What to do if you've had a bad experience

If your hair has felt stiff or protein-overloaded after using coconut oil, do a moisture treatment (deep conditioner, no protein) and see if that resolves it. If it does, your hair is likely protein-sensitive and coconut oil isn't the right oil for your routine.

Alternatives that penetrate the shaft without the protein affinity: argan oil, marula oil. Alternatives that sit on the surface and seal effectively without penetrating: castor oil (mixed with a lighter carrier), jojoba oil.

## The bottom line

Coconut oil is a useful tool for natural hair — but it's not universal, and it's not a moisturiser. If you've avoided it because of a bad experience, the issue is usually application method or protein sensitivity, not the oil itself.
