Olaplex works on natural hair. It does not work on all natural hair problems, and it's not a general conditioning or moisturising treatment. Understanding what it does and doesn't do prevents spending £30 on a product expecting one result and getting another.

---

## What Olaplex Actually Does

Olaplex's active ingredient (bis-aminopropyl diglycol dimaleate) works by relinking broken disulphide bonds in the hair shaft. Disulphide bonds are the structural protein bonds that give hair its strength and curl pattern. They're broken by:

- Chemical processing (relaxers, perms, bleach, colour)
- Sustained high heat (flat iron, hot comb at excessive temperatures)
- Some extreme mechanical damage

When disulphide bonds are broken, the hair's structural integrity is compromised. Olaplex relinks these broken bonds. The effect is real and measurable: hair that has had disulphide bond damage treated with Olaplex shows improved strength and elasticity compared to untreated hair.

This is different from what protein treatments, deep conditioners, and moisture treatments do. Those products coat, fill, or hydrate the hair. Olaplex alters the structural bond configuration.

## When Olaplex Is Appropriate for Natural Hair

**Yes — for this:**
- Natural hair that has been bleached or coloured (bleaching breaks disulphide bonds significantly)
- Heat damage — but only if the damage is moderate, not severe. Severe heat damage breaks disulphide bonds past the point where relinking is effective; mild to moderate heat damage can be improved. → [Heat damage on natural hair: is it reversible?](/hair-care/heat-damage-natural-hair-reversible-uk/)
- Transition from relaxed to natural — the remaining relaxed portions have chemically broken disulphide bonds that Olaplex can improve

**No — not appropriate for this:**
- General moisturising or conditioning (Olaplex is not a conditioner)
- Protein deficiency (Olaplex addresses bond damage, not cuticle gap filling — that's hydrolysed protein's job)
- Breakage caused by dryness or mechanical damage alone (no disulphide bond damage involved)
- As a regular part of a natural hair routine with no chemical processing or heat damage history

## How to Use Olaplex on Natural Hair

**No. 0 + No. 3 (at-home treatment):** the most common starting point. No. 0 is applied to damp hair, left for ten minutes; No. 3 Hair Perfector is applied over No. 0 and left for thirty minutes to overnight. Rinse and continue with regular routine.

**No. 3 alone:** still produces results, less dramatically than combined with No. 0. Appropriate monthly for hair with mild chemical or heat damage.

**Frequency:** monthly or as needed, not weekly. Over-use of bond repair treatments on hair without significant bond damage doesn't produce additional benefit and can contribute to protein overload in combination with other protein-containing products.

**Combination with moisture:** Olaplex does not condition or moisturise. After an Olaplex treatment, follow with a full wash (shampoo) and a moisturising deep conditioner. The improved bond structure makes the subsequent conditioning more effective — the conditioner can work on a more structurally sound shaft.

## Olaplex on Coily vs Straight Hair

The disulphide bond chemistry is the same in all hair types. However:

- Coily hair has more stress points per unit length (each curve in the coil is a stress point), so disulphide bond damage from the same amount of heat exposure may be greater
- Coily hair is more sensitive to protein-like ingredients, which means pairing Olaplex with high-protein products risks protein overload
- Coily hair's moisture needs are typically higher, so the bond repair treatment needs to be followed by more intensive conditioning than straight hair would need

The treatment protocol is the same; the context around it needs to account for coily hair's characteristics.

## FAQs

**My hair has never been chemically processed and I don't use heat. Should I try Olaplex?**

Probably not. If there's no disulphide bond damage, there are no broken bonds to relink. The product won't harm the hair, but you're spending £30 on something that isn't addressing a problem your hair has. A good deep conditioner and protein treatment serve unprocessed natural hair better.

**Can I use Olaplex No. 3 as a leave-in?**

No. Olaplex No. 3 requires rinsing. The bis-aminopropyl diglycol dimaleate needs to be present on damp hair to work, then rinsed once the treatment time is complete. Leaving it in doesn't improve results and may leave a residue.

**Is Olaplex worth the price for natural hair?**

For hair with actual disulphide bond damage (bleached, heat damaged): yes. The price (around £28 for No. 3 at Boots) is high for a monthly treatment but the structural improvement is real and nothing cheaper does the same job. For unprocessed natural hair: no. The money is better spent on a SheaMoisture masque and a protein treatment.
