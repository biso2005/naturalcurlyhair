Hard water damages locs more than it damages loose natural hair, and the damage is harder to reverse. That sentence doesn't appear in most UK locs resources. This article is the exception.

If you're in London, the South East, East Anglia, or the Midlands, your water is hard to very hard. If you've had locs for a year or more and they feel rough, heavy, stiff, or have a grey-ish cast in good light, hard water mineral accumulation is almost certainly part of the reason.

---

## Why Hard Water Is Worse for Locs Than Loose Hair

On loose hair, hard water minerals — calcium and magnesium — deposit on the hair shaft surface. A chelating shampoo can reach the surface, dissolve the minerals, and rinse them away.

On locs, every wash pushes hard water through the entire interior of the loc. The minerals deposit on the hair strands inside the loc as well as outside. The interior of a loc is dense, compact, and difficult for any product to fully penetrate. This means:

1. Mineral accumulation happens faster per wash cycle, because the water moves through more hair surface area
2. Chelating products can't reach the interior deposits as effectively as they can reach the loose hair surface
3. Over time, interior mineral accumulation makes the loc feel stiff and rigid even when moisturised on the outside
4. The grey-ish or dull cast that hard water causes in loose hair appears in locs as a persistent overall flatness and lack of sheen

Prevention is significantly easier than treatment. Starting chelating from the beginning of the loc journey prevents the accumulation problem. Trying to remove established interior mineral deposits is very difficult.

| Region | Water hardness | Risk for locs |
|--------|----------------|--------------|
| London | Very hard | High — chelate monthly from day one |
| South East | Very hard | High |
| East Anglia | Hard to very hard | High |

| Yorkshire | Variable | Medium |
| North West | Mostly soft | Low |
| Wales | Soft | Low |
| Scotland | Very soft | Low |

If you're in a high-risk area, chelating is maintenance. If you're in a low-risk area, you have more flexibility.

## Signs of Hard Water Buildup in Locs

- Locs feel rough or coarse when you run your hand down them
- Locs feel heavier than they used to, without a corresponding increase in length
- Locs look dull or matte even when you've moisturised the exterior
- A slight grey or whitish cast visible in direct light, particularly at the ends
- Products seem to stop absorbing — the loc exterior feels coated
- Scalp itching or tightness that appeared gradually rather than suddenly

These symptoms appear slowly over months, which is why they're often attributed to other causes. If you're in a hard water area and these have been building over six or more months, mineral accumulation is the most likely explanation.

## The Prevention Protocol

Start from day one of your loc journey. Monthly chelating with a product containing EDTA or phytic acid:

1. Wet the locs fully — takes longer than you expect on any length of loc
2. Apply the chelating product to the scalp and locs, working it through as well as possible
3. Leave on for two to three minutes
4. Rinse very thoroughly
5. Follow with a residue-free shampoo as normal
6. Dry fully

The chelating step goes before the shampoo, not after.

**Products:** EDTA or phytic acid are the active ingredients to look for. Malibu C Hard Water Wellness shampoo is widely available in the UK via Amazon and some beauty supply stores. Kérastase Bain Divalic contains EDTA. For loc-specific options, check Afro hair beauty supply stores — some stock chelating products marketed to loc wearers.

## Treating Established Buildup

If you're reading this because you already have locs with mineral accumulation, it's harder to address than prevention but not impossible.

**The chelating soak method:**

1. Mix chelating shampoo or a concentrated EDTA solution in a bowl of warm water
2. Submerge the locs in the bowl and soak for five to ten minutes
3. Gently squeeze through each loc
4. Rinse very thoroughly
5. Repeat over two to three wash sessions

This can reduce surface and near-surface accumulation. Interior deposits in mature locs may not respond fully — the loc is too dense for the chelating product to penetrate deeply enough.

**Vinegar rinse (less effective than EDTA but accessible):**

Apple cider vinegar has mild chelating action and is the traditional home remedy. Mix 1 part ACV with 3–4 parts water, pour over wet locs, leave two minutes, rinse. It removes light surface mineral deposits only. Not a substitute for proper chelating in hard water areas.

## Locs and Filtered Water

Shower filters that reduce water hardness are available from plumbing suppliers, Amazon, and some bathroom retailers. They vary in effectiveness — most reduce hardness to some degree but don't fully soften water. A decent filter costs £20–£50 and replaces approximately every six months.

For loc wearers in very hard water areas, a shower filter combined with monthly chelating is the most robust approach.

## FAQs

**I've had locs for two years in London and never chelated. Do I have significant mineral buildup?**

Almost certainly some accumulation. How much depends on how often you wash, your exact water hardness, and the products you've been using. Do a chelating soak and assess — if the water runs milky or brownish during the rinse, that's minerals coming out.

**Will chelating damage my locs?**

No. EDTA and phytic acid remove mineral deposits only; they don't affect the hair structure or the locking process. The concern is over-chelating (more than monthly) with very concentrated products, which can cause dryness. Monthly chelating at normal concentrations is well within the safe range.

**My loctician says locs don't need chelating. Who's right?**

Locticians working in soft water areas often haven't encountered severe hard water mineral accumulation and may not emphasise chelating. If you're in a hard water area and your loctician is not, the regional difference matters. Ask specifically about your water hardness and their experience with clients in similar water conditions.

**Can I use a water softener instead of chelating?**

A whole-house water softener replaces calcium and magnesium with sodium, producing genuinely soft water. If your property has one, chelating is much less critical. Most UK properties don't have softeners — they're expensive to install and often not present in rental properties.
