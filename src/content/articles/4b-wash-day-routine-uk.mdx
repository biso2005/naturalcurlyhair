4B wash day has a few things working against it that most wash-day guides don't account for: the Z-pattern tangles that happen before you've even started washing, hard water across most of England that coats the hair shaft and blocks conditioner from working, and the density that means products take much longer to reach the inner strands of each section.

This is a wash day routine built around those specifics.

---

## Before You Wash: Pre-Poo

A pre-poo — applying oil or conditioner to dry hair before the shampoo step — is optional for most curl types. For 4B hair, it's worth making a habit.

**Why it helps:** Shampooing dry, shrunken 4B hair creates maximum friction at the Z-angles. A pre-poo softens the hair, adds slip, and reduces friction during the wash. You'll have noticeably fewer tangles to deal with after rinsing.

**What to use:** Any oil you have — coconut, olive, or jojoba work well. Apply section by section to dry hair, work through each section gently, clip away, and leave for 20–30 minutes before washing. You don't need to work it all the way through — coat the length and focus on the ends, which need it most.

---

## Shampoo

### Weekly wash: gentle shampoo or co-wash

4B hair doesn't need strong cleansing every wash day. A gentle, sulphate-free shampoo or a co-wash (cleansing conditioner) is enough for maintenance weeks. Apply to a wet scalp in sections, work gently with fingertips (not nails), and let it rinse through the length.

Signs you need a stronger wash this week: product buildup making the hair feel heavy or waxy, scalp itching, or hair that looks dull even when freshly washed.

### Monthly reset: chelating shampoo

If you're in a hard water area — London, South East, East Anglia, Midlands — add a chelating shampoo to your monthly routine. This is the step most 4B naturals in the UK are missing, and it explains why deep conditioning often seems to do nothing.

Hard water deposits minerals on the hair shaft with every wash. A clarifying shampoo removes product buildup; it does not remove minerals. A chelating shampoo does. Check the label for EDTA or phytic acid. Use it once a month, then deep condition straight after.

## Deep Conditioning

Apply deep conditioner to freshly washed, soaking-wet hair. Work in sections — 4B density means you cannot simply work it through from root to tip in one motion and expect even coverage.

**For each section:** apply conditioner, then use your fingers to work it through from tip upward, separating the hair as you go. The hair should feel fully coated and slippery.

### Use heat

A heat cap, steamer, or warm damp towel over a shower cap makes a real difference for 4B hair. It helps the conditioner actually penetrate rather than sit on the surface. Leave for 20–30 minutes minimum.

If you have low-porosity hair, heat is particularly important — the cuticle resists moisture without it.

### Detangle during conditioning

This is the best moment to detangle 4B hair. The conditioner provides slip, the hair is pliable, and you have time to work carefully. Detangle each section — fingers first, then comb if needed — while the conditioner is in, before rinsing.

## Rinsing

Rinse with lukewarm or cool water. A cool rinse at the end helps close the cuticle and can add noticeable shine.

If you're in a very hard water area (London especially, or East Anglia), consider a distilled water final rinse — a pour-over of water you've bought from the supermarket. This is the step with the least friction to add and the clearest benefit: your freshly-conditioned hair doesn't immediately get re-coated with minerals from the tap.

## Applying Products

Apply everything while the hair is still soaking wet. Not damp. Soaking wet. The wetter the better for 4B hair — water is the primary moisturiser and everything else sits on top to lock it in.

**Leave-in conditioner:** The first layer. Apply section by section, rake through gently with fingers.

**Oil or cream (seal):** Apply straight after the leave-in, while the hair is still wet. This is the LOC (Leave-in, Oil, Cream) or LCO (Leave-in, Cream, Oil) method. Which order works better for you depends on your porosity — try both over a few wash days.

**Styler:** Apply last, if you're doing a defined style. For twist-outs or braid-outs, apply styler to each section before twisting or braiding. For wash-and-go, a strong-hold gel applied and raked through gives the best result.

---

## Drying

**Do not use a rough towel.** Terry cloth creates friction at the Z-angles and generates new tangles before you've even finished wash day. Use a microfibre towel or an old cotton t-shirt to gently squeeze excess water rather than rubbing.

**Air dry where possible.** Heat styling repeatedly causes damage over time, and 4B hair that's already dry is particularly susceptible. If you need to speed drying, use a diffuser on low heat with the hair scrunched upward rather than blowing from above.

**Protect the style while drying.** If you're doing a twist-out or braid-out, use clips or pins at the root of each section to keep the pattern while drying.

## Night Routine

Whatever day you wash, the night routine matters as much.

- **Satin bonnet or satin-lined pillowcase:** Cotton pillowcases create friction and absorb moisture from 4B hair overnight. A bonnet takes 30 seconds and makes a measurable difference.
- **Pineapple for loose styles:** Gather hair loosely at the very top of your head with a satin scrunchie. Keeps the length from compressing and re-tangling overnight.
- **Sealed ends:** The ends of 4B hair are the oldest, most fragile part. A small amount of butter or heavy oil on the ends before bed helps maintain moisture.

## Troubleshooting

**Deep conditioner seems to do nothing:** Usually one of two causes — hard water blocking absorption, or low porosity hair where the cuticle resists moisture. Test your water first; it's the cheaper fix.

**Hair tangles again before the rinse:** Your sections were too large. Use smaller subsections and clip each one away when done.

**Hair feels dry by the next day:** Check your sealing step. Apply oil or cream while the hair is still wet, not after it's partially dry. If that's already your method, increase the amount or switch to a heavier sealant.

**Wash day takes too long:** The time investment is mostly in detangling and conditioning — both of which scale with how long since the last session. Shorter intervals between wash days means less to work through each time.

## FAQs

**Do I really need a pre-poo?**

It's not mandatory but it noticeably reduces tangles during the wash for most 4B hair. If your wash day is consistently a battle with knots, try it for two wash days and see if the detangling step gets easier.

**How long should 4B wash day take?**

Realistically 2–3 hours for a thorough session, including conditioning time. If you're regularly going over 3 hours, reassess your section size and detangling order.

**Can I co-wash every wash day?**

Yes, if you're not using heavy products that require a shampoo to remove. If you're using butters, gel, and castor oil heavily, a clarifying shampoo every few weeks stops buildup from compounding.

**What's the one thing that would make the biggest difference to my wash day?**

Detangle with conditioner in, before the rinse — not after. This single change makes the rest of the session dramatically easier.
