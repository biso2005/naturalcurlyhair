A trichologist is a specialist in hair and scalp health — not a GP, not a hairdresser, but a clinician specifically trained in diagnosing and treating conditions that affect the hair shaft, follicle, and scalp. In the UK, trichology is not a regulated medical profession, but the Institute of Trichologists (IOT) sets professional standards and maintains a directory of qualified practitioners.

If you have thinning edges, unexplained hair loss, a persistent scalp condition that isn't responding to over-the-counter treatment, or hair breakage severe enough that you're not sure what's causing it — a trichologist is the right first call.

---

## When to See a Trichologist (vs a GP)

**See a trichologist for:**
- Traction alopecia — thinning at the hairline from tight hairstyles. A trichologist assesses whether follicles are dormant or scarred and advises on recovery or treatment options
- Scalp conditions — seborrhoeic dermatitis, psoriasis, folliculitis, or persistent flaking and irritation that hasn't responded to over-the-counter treatment
- Unexplained hair shedding — more than the normal 50–100 strands per day, if you've already ruled out seasonal shedding and diet
- Breakage patterns you can't identify — if the advice in this site and elsewhere hasn't resolved significant ongoing breakage
- Alopecia areata — patchy hair loss that needs professional diagnosis before treatment
- Hair and scalp assessment before undertaking major chemical treatment (bleaching, relaxing) on already-compromised hair

**See a GP first for:**
- Sudden or rapid hair loss — this may indicate a medical condition (thyroid disorder, iron deficiency, autoimmune condition) that needs blood testing and diagnosis before a trichologist can treat it
- Scalp with open sores, severe inflammation, or signs of infection
- Hair loss alongside other symptoms (fatigue, weight change, skin or nail changes)

GPs and trichologists work in parallel for complex cases — a GP addresses the underlying medical cause, a trichologist addresses the hair and scalp effects.

## Finding a Trichologist in the UK

**The Institute of Trichologists (IOT) directory.** The IOT is the primary professional body for trichology in the UK. Their website (trichologists.org.uk) maintains a searchable directory of qualified members by location. Searching your area gives you practitioners who have completed the IOT's qualification requirements.

**The Trichological Society.** A second professional body (a-t-s.org.uk) also maintains a practitioner directory.

**Finding one with Afro hair experience.** This matters. Trichology training covers all hair types, but clinical experience with Afro-textured hair varies significantly. When contacting a trichologist, ask specifically whether they have experience with Afro or natural hair, with traction alopecia in Black women, and with the specific condition you're dealing with. A trichologist who answers these questions specifically is better placed than one who gives a general "I see all hair types" response.

**Cost.** Trichologists are private practitioners — there is no NHS trichology service. Initial consultations in the UK typically cost £60–150, with follow-up appointments at similar rates. Treatment costs vary by what's recommended. Some dermatologists with trichology specialisms practice within NHS dermatology services; a GP referral is needed to access these.

## What to Expect at an Appointment

**The initial assessment** typically involves:
- Detailed history: diet, health conditions, medications, recent stressors, hair care history, styling history
- Scalp examination: often with a trichoscope (a magnifying device that examines the scalp and follicle at high magnification)
- Hair sample analysis: examining shed strands under magnification to assess whether loss is from the root or from breakage
- Possibly a recommendation for blood tests (usually referred back to your GP) to rule out nutritional deficiencies, thyroid issues, or hormonal factors

**The assessment takes** approximately forty-five minutes to an hour for an initial appointment.

**The recommendations** may include: topical treatments (minoxidil, scalp serums), lifestyle changes (diet, stress management), referral to a dermatologist for prescription medication, or simply a regime change if the problem is care-related rather than medical.

---

## What a Trichologist Cannot Do

A trichologist who is not a medical doctor cannot prescribe medication. They can recommend over-the-counter treatments including minoxidil (available at Boots without prescription), recommend diet supplementation, and refer you to a GP or dermatologist for prescription options. For conditions requiring prescription treatment (finasteride, topical steroids for scalp psoriasis, steroid injections for alopecia areata), the trichologist and GP or dermatologist work together.

## FAQs

**I've been told by a hairdresser I should see a trichologist. Does that mean something serious?**

A hairdresser noticing thinning or scalp issues and recommending a trichologist is doing the right thing. It doesn't mean the situation is serious — it means it's beyond what they can assess. It's a referral, not a diagnosis.

**Can a trichologist diagnose alopecia?**

They can assess and identify patterns consistent with various alopecia conditions and recommend treatment approaches. A formal diagnosis of scarring alopecias or autoimmune-related conditions (like lichen planopilaris) typically requires a dermatologist and sometimes a scalp biopsy.

**Is minoxidil recommended for Black women with traction alopecia?**

Minoxidil (topical, 2% or 5%) has evidence for stimulating hair regrowth in androgenetic alopecia and some other conditions. Its effectiveness in traction alopecia specifically is less established — the first step is removing the source of traction; minoxidil may help in cases where the follicle is dormant but not scarred. A trichologist can advise on whether it's appropriate for your specific situation.

**The nearest trichologist is far away. Can I do an online consultation?**

Many UK trichologists now offer video consultations, which can include photo or video assessment of the scalp. For a first assessment where you're trying to understand what's happening, a video consultation is a reasonable starting point. For more detailed physical scalp examination, an in-person visit may be recommended.
