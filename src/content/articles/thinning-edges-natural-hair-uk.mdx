Thinning at the hairline and temples is very common among natural hair wearers, and most of the time it has a mechanical cause — repeated tension on the same area from a tight style, a wig, a too-tight elastic band, or lace front adhesive applied repeatedly to the hairline.

That's the likely explanation, and it's worth saying clearly at the start because knowing the cause makes the path forward obvious. But some hairline thinning has a different cause — hormonal, nutritional, autoimmune — and knowing which is which affects what you do next.

This guide covers how to tell the difference, what to do in the most common case, and when to involve a medical professional.

---

## The Most Common Cause: Traction Alopecia

Traction alopecia is hair loss caused by repeated tension on the follicle. The hairline and temples are most vulnerable because:
- They have finer, shorter hair than the crown
- They're where tight styles attach most forcefully
- They have less recovery capacity than denser areas

**Signs of traction alopecia:**
- Thinning concentrated at the temples and hairline, not distributed across the scalp
- The thinning has developed gradually, not suddenly
- You wear tight styles regularly — braids, tight ponytails, lace front wigs, tight buns
- The affected area feels tender after style installation
- Small bumps (folliculitis) have appeared along the hairline after tight installs

**Early traction alopecia is reversible.** The follicles are dormant, not permanently damaged. Remove the source of traction and the hair typically begins to regrow within three to six months.

**Late traction alopecia may not be reversible.** Repeated long-term traction causes scarring at the follicle. Scarred follicles cannot regrow hair. This is why early action matters.

---

## What to Do for Traction Alopecia

**Step 1: Remove the source.** Stop all tight styles immediately. No tight braids, no lace front adhesive at the hairline, no tight ponytails or buns, no extensions that pull. This is non-negotiable — nothing else you do will work while the traction continues.

**Step 2: Rest the area.** Six to eight weeks of no tension on the hairline. Loose styles, glueless wigs on satin caps without hairline adhesive, no accessories that sit tight on the temples.

**Step 3: Light scalp oil.** Jojoba or sweet almond oil massaged gently into the thinning area daily. The massage increases local circulation. Don't apply pressure — a light touch.

**Step 4: Assess at six to eight weeks.** If the hair is starting to regrow — fine new hairs visible along the hairline — the follicles are recovering. Continue the rest period and maintain the routine.

**Step 5: If there's no visible regrowth after six to eight weeks, see a trichologist.** Not a GP initially — a trichologist is a hair and scalp specialist who can assess whether the follicles are dormant or scarred, and whether treatment (topical minoxidil, PRP, steroid injection) would help.

## Other Causes of Hairline Thinning

If the thinning doesn't match the traction pattern — if it appeared suddenly, or is affecting more of the scalp than just the hairline, or if you don't wear tight styles — other causes are worth investigating.

**Alopecia areata:** a patchy hair loss condition where the immune system attacks the follicle. Can affect the hairline, but typically produces defined circular patches rather than gradual thinning across the edge. See a GP.

**Androgenetic alopecia (female-pattern hair loss):** a genetic condition producing gradual diffuse thinning, often along the parting and crown rather than only at the hairline. Hormonal factors contribute. More common after menopause but can occur at any age. See a GP or trichologist.

**Post-partum hair loss:** significant shedding in the months after birth. Typically diffuse across the scalp, not concentrated at the hairline specifically. Usually resolves within six to twelve months. If it doesn't resolve or is severe, see a GP.

**Thyroid conditions and nutritional deficiency:** both can cause diffuse hair loss. If the thinning is widespread rather than edge-specific, and there are other symptoms (fatigue, cold intolerance, changes in skin or nails), see a GP for blood tests.

## When to See a Trichologist vs a GP

**See a trichologist if:**
- You suspect traction alopecia and want professional assessment and treatment options
- Six to eight weeks of rest haven't produced visible regrowth
- You want to know whether the follicles are still active

**See a GP if:**
- The thinning is sudden or rapid
- Patches of complete hair loss (not just thinning)
- Other symptoms are present (fatigue, skin changes, scalp inflammation)
- You suspect a medical cause rather than a mechanical one

Trichologists are not available on the NHS — appointments are private. Cost in the UK ranges from £60–£150 for an initial consultation. Many natural hair communities in the UK have recommendations for trichologists with experience in Afro-textured hair.

## FAQs

**My edges have been thinning for two years. Is it too late?**

Possibly not — early scarring alopecia can sometimes be treated. But the window for effective treatment narrows over time. A trichologist assessment is the right next step to determine whether the follicles are dormant (potentially recoverable) or scarred.

**Can I use edge control on thinning edges?**

Not products that dry hard and stiff. Hard-hold edge control dried tight against already-stressed follicles adds mechanical stress. A light oil or leave-in is all the hairline needs while recovering.

**My edges look better some months than others. What's causing the variation?**

Likely a seasonal pattern or cyclical hormonal variation. Some people see edge thinning worsen in autumn (seasonal shedding affects the hairline) or at specific points in the menstrual cycle. If it's consistently cyclical, tracking the pattern helps identify the cause.

**I've been wearing a tight headband daily for the gym. Could that be causing this?**

Yes. A tight elastic band sitting on the same hairline section every day mimics the effect of tight hairstyles. Switch to a wider, softer headband that sits higher on the forehead rather than pulling on the hairline.
