Shower filters are all over curly hair communities right now, particularly if you're in London or the South East. They're worth considering — but most people buy them expecting them to fix hard water, and that's not quite what they do. Here's the honest picture.

---

## What a Shower Filter Actually Does

Most shower filters sold for curly hair remove chlorine from the water. Chlorine is added to mains water as a disinfectant, and it can strip moisture from the hair and irritate the scalp.

**What filters do well:** reduce chlorine exposure, which can make your hair feel less stripped after washing and ease scalp sensitivity.

**What filters don't do:** remove calcium and magnesium — the minerals that cause hard water build-up on curly hair.

This is the gap that most filter marketing papers over. If your main problem is mineral deposits making your curls feel rough and resistant to conditioner, a shower filter alone won't fix it. You need chelating as well.

## So Is It Worth Buying One?

**Yes, if:** chlorine is irritating your scalp or leaving your hair feeling dry and stripped after washing. Some people notice a real difference — softer feeling hair, less scalp tightness — after switching to filtered water. It's worth trying if you've ruled out hard water as the main issue, or as a complement to chelating.

**Less useful if:** you're in a soft water area like most of Scotland, Wales, or the North West, where mineral content is already low. The chlorine reduction still applies, but that may not be your problem.

**Not a substitute for chelating** if you're in a hard water area. The two address different things. A filter deals with chlorine; chelating deals with minerals. If your water is 300+ ppm hard, you need both.

---

## What to Look for When Buying

Two main types are sold in the UK:

**Carbon/KDF filters** are the most common. They reduce chlorine effectively. Most fit standard UK shower hose connections (check the box if you have an unusual shower setup). Good for general chlorine reduction.

**Vitamin C (inline) filters** are a step up if your water supplier uses chloramine rather than chlorine — some UK suppliers do, and chloramine needs a different approach to remove. If you're not sure which your supplier uses, check their website or call them. Vitamin C filters are slightly more expensive to run.

Both types come in inline versions (attach to your hose) and shower-head replacements. Inline is usually cheaper and preserves your existing shower head.

**What to ignore:** claims that a filter "softens" water or removes calcium and magnesium. Most don't. Check independent reviews, not the product listing.

---

## The Honest Cost-Benefit

Filters need cartridge replacements — typically every few months, depending on how often you shower. Add up the cartridge cost per year before buying, not just the headline unit price. A cheap filter with frequent cartridge changes can cost more annually than a pricier option.

**Cheaper alternative for mineral build-up:** a citric acid rinse — half a teaspoon of food-grade citric acid powder in a litre of water — does what most filters can't (removes minerals from the hair) for almost nothing. It doesn't address chlorine, but it's the more direct fix for hard water deposits.

The combination that makes sense in a high hard-water area: filter for chlorine, chelating wash monthly for minerals, citric acid rinse for maintenance in between. Each doing its specific job.

## Who's Most Likely to Notice a Difference

**More likely to see a change:**
- Scalp is itchy or tight after showering even with a good routine
- Hair feels stripped and dry regardless of conditioner
- You're in a London or South East postcode with 250+ ppm water

**Less likely to see a dramatic change:**
- Your hair concerns are mainly structural — damage from bleach, heat, or over-manipulation
- You're in a soft water area
- Your issues are product-related rather than water-related

## FAQs

**Will a shower filter fix my hard water problem?**

Not on its own. It reduces chlorine, which helps. But the minerals that cause hard water build-up on curly hair need a chelating wash to remove — filters can't do that.

**Do I need to install anything?**

No plumbing required. Inline filters and most shower-head replacements use standard UK fittings and screw on by hand.

**How do I know if my water has chloramine or just chlorine?**

Check your water supplier's website — they publish what disinfection method they use. If it says chloramine, look for a vitamin C filter specifically.

**Can I just use bottled water for my final rinse?**

Yes, and it works well as a diagnostic: if your hair responds better, your tap water is a factor. It's impractical as a long-term daily solution, but useful to test.
