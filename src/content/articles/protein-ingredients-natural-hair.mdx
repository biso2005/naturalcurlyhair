Protein in natural hair products strengthens the hair shaft by temporarily filling in gaps in the cuticle. The effect is real. But different proteins do different things, and protein overload — using too much protein without balancing moisture — causes the hair to become stiff, brittle, and prone to breakage. Knowing which proteins to use and how often prevents the overcorrection that causes as many problems as the protein deficiency it was meant to fix.

---

## What Proteins Actually Do

Hair is made of keratin — a protein. When the hair shaft is damaged (from heat, mechanical stress, over-manipulation, chemical processing), the cuticle develops gaps and weak points. Hydrolysed proteins in hair products — proteins broken into small enough molecules to penetrate or coat the shaft — temporarily fill those gaps, improving the hair's elasticity, strength, and surface smoothness.

"Temporarily" is the key word. Protein treatments don't repair the hair permanently. They improve the hair's behaviour until the treatment washes out. Regular protein maintenance keeps the hair functioning well between washes; it doesn't reverse structural damage.

---

## The Main Proteins in Natural Hair Products

**Hydrolysed Keratin:** closest to the hair's own protein. Penetrates the shaft (small molecule), fills structural gaps, improves elasticity. One of the most effective proteins for strengthening hair. Appears in many protein treatment products.

**Hydrolysed Rice Protein:** small molecule, penetrates the shaft. Particularly effective at adding volume and strength to fine-stranded hair without adding heavy texture. Common in lighter protein treatments.

**Hydrolysed Wheat Protein:** mid-sized molecule, penetrates or coats depending on the formulation. Strengthening and moisturising — wheat protein has both protein and humectant properties. Good for hair that is both protein-deficient and moisture-depleted.

**Hydrolysed Silk Protein:** large molecule, primarily coats the hair surface rather than penetrating. Adds shine and smoothness to the hair surface. Lower strengthening effect than keratin or rice protein, better cosmetic effect (shine, slip). Appears frequently in premium conditioners.

**Hydrolysed Collagen:** penetrates the shaft. Adds elasticity. Less common than keratin but effective for hair that has lost spring or bounce.

**Vegetable Protein (Hydrolysed Soy, Corn, Lupine):** variable molecule size. Soy protein is common in both penetrating and coating formulations. Effective and less allergenic than some animal-derived proteins.

**Egg / Egg Yolk:** whole egg or egg protein in DIY treatments. Contains protein but also fats and cholesterol that can create a conditioning effect. The protein penetrates partially; the fats coat the surface. Popular in the natural hair community; effective if not left on too long (whole egg applied hot can scramble on the hair).

## Light vs Heavy Protein Treatments

**Light protein (conditioning treatment):** protein appears lower in the ingredient list or the product is primarily a conditioner with protein added. Can be used every two to four weeks. Found in products like Aphogee Two-Minute Keratin Reconstructor, ORS Hair Mayo, and many conditioners that list hydrolysed proteins mid-list.

**Heavy protein treatment:** protein is a primary ingredient, high in the ingredient list. Significant structural effect. Use monthly at most, with a deep moisture treatment immediately after. Aphogee Two-Step Protein Treatment, Joico K-PAK, Schwarzkopf Bonacure Protein+ treatment.

## How to Know If You Need Protein

The stretch test is the quickest indicator:
- Wet a single strand and pull gently
- If it stretches significantly (more than 30% of its length) before snapping, or feels mushy: protein deficiency — add protein
- If it snaps with minimal stretch, feels stiff or wiry: protein overload or dryness — add moisture, reduce protein
- If it stretches slightly then snaps with a clean break: balanced — maintain the routine

## Protein Overload

Using too much protein, too frequently, without balancing moisture causes protein overload. The signs:

- Hair feels stiff, dry, or wiry
- Hair snaps without stretching
- Hair feels rough even after conditioning
- Style doesn't hold and hair looks flat or dull

Fix: stop all protein immediately. Two to three weeks of moisture-only routine — a moisturising shampoo, deep moisture conditioner, moisture-focused leave-in. No protein in any product. The protein overload resolves within a few wash cycles.

## FAQs

**All my products have protein in them. Is that a problem?**

Check how far down the ingredient list the protein appears. A protein appearing at position 15 in a conditioner with 20 ingredients is a trace amount — unlikely to cause overload. Protein near the top of the list (positions 3–8) is a significant amount and will have a noticeable effect. Stacking multiple high-protein products causes overload faster than one moderate-protein product.

**Is DIY protein treatment (egg, yogurt, mayonnaise) as effective as a product?**

Partially. The proteins in egg and yogurt are real and do penetrate the hair shaft to some degree. The effect is milder than a concentrated hydrolysed protein product because the proteins haven't been processed to the molecular size that maximises penetration. For maintenance, DIY works. For significant protein deficiency, a concentrated product is more effective.

**Can protein sensitivity be permanent?**

Yes. Some people's hair is consistently protein-sensitive — it reacts with stiffness and breakage even to light protein. The stretch test helps identify this: if hair that doesn't feel moisture-depleted snaps without stretching after protein use, protein sensitivity is likely. The routine for protein-sensitive hair: moisture-only products, occasional very light protein (quarterly) if needed, and checking every new product for protein before use.

**Is "bond-building" the same as protein treatment?**

No. Bond-building treatments (Olaplex, K18, similar) target the disulphide bonds damaged by chemical processing and heat. They're different in mechanism from hydrolysed protein treatments, which fill cuticle gaps. Bond builders are primarily for chemically treated or heat-damaged hair; protein treatments are for structurally weakened hair from any cause. Some products combine both; they're different chemistries.
