Porosity tells you how easily your hair lets moisture in and how quickly it loses it again. For 4A hair, it matters more than you might expect — particularly because the widespread assumption that type 4 hair is always high porosity leads a lot of 4A naturals to build routines designed for the opposite of what their hair actually needs.

Test yours before deciding.

## What Porosity Means for 4A Hair

**Low porosity 4A:**
- Takes a long time to fully wet in the shower
- Products ball up on the surface or sit on top rather than absorbing
- Buildup accumulates quickly — creams and oils pile on without penetrating
- Hair feels moisturised right after washing but dries out faster than expected
- Responds much better to conditioning with heat

**High porosity 4A:**
- Absorbs moisture quickly
- Loses it just as fast — dryness returns within hours
- Frizzes easily, especially in outdoor humidity
- Ends feel rough or dry even with conditioning
- Takes colour or chemical treatments more intensely than expected

**Medium porosity 4A:** Absorbs and retains reasonably well. More common in 4A than in 4B or 4C.

## Why Low Porosity 4A Is Underdiagnosed

The assumption that all type 4 hair is high porosity is so widespread that low-porosity 4A often gets misdiagnosed as damage, or treated incorrectly for years. Low-porosity 4A hair that's been treated with high-porosity techniques — heavy butters, no heat, maximum product — will build up, feel greasy on the surface while still feeling dry, and seem impervious to conditioner.

This doesn't mean the hair is broken. It means the routine is wrong for the porosity.

## Skip the Float Test

The float test — dropping a strand in water to see if it sinks or floats — is unreliable for 4A hair. Air trapped within the S-coils creates false floats regardless of cuticle state. Product residue changes the result further. Two better tests:

### The spray bottle test

Spritz a small section of clean, dry hair with plain water. Watch for 30 seconds.

- Water **beads on the surface** and takes a while to absorb — low porosity
- Water **absorbs quickly** and the section darkens fast — high porosity
- Water **sinks in but slowly** — medium porosity

Do this after a clarifying wash for the most accurate result — product buildup can make low-porosity hair look high-porosity.

### The feel test

Run a single clean, product-free strand from tip to root between your fingers.

- **Smooth, almost slippery** — flat cuticles, likely low porosity
- **Rough or bumpy, snags slightly** — raised cuticles, likely high porosity
- **Fragile or breaks mid-stroke** — high porosity with some damage

## Roots vs Ends: Mixed Porosity in 4A Hair

Your roots are newer. They haven't been through years of washing, UV, detangling, and manipulation. Your ends have, and older hair is almost always more porous.

If your 4A hair has length, your roots may resist moisture (low porosity behaviour) while your ends absorb and lose it quickly (high porosity). Do the spray bottle test on roots and ends separately. If there's a noticeable difference, your routine should treat them differently.

## What to Do Based on Your Result

### Low porosity 4A

The goal is getting moisture past the cuticle.

- **Heat during deep conditioning is not optional.** A heat cap, steamer, or warm damp towel over a shower cap makes a significant difference. Without heat, conditioner on low-porosity 4A hair mostly stays on the surface. 20–30 minutes minimum.
- **Apply products to the wettest possible hair.** The more saturated the hair, the better the moisture uptake. Don't let it dry before applying leave-in.
- **Use lighter products.** Water-based leave-ins absorb more readily than thick creams. For sealing, jojoba or argan oil works better than castor oil or shea butter on low-porosity 4A — lighter oils don't pile up on top the way heavy ones do.
- **Clarify monthly.** Buildup accumulates faster on low-porosity hair. A clarifying wash resets the surface so conditioning products actually have a chance to work.

### High porosity 4A

The goal is slowing moisture loss.

- **Seal well and promptly.** Apply a sealant — oil, butter, or cream — immediately after leave-in, while the hair is still wet. This creates a barrier that slows moisture leaving the hair.
- **Layer heavier than low-porosity 4A.** A mid-weight cream as your final layer (rather than just oil) gives extra protection.
- **Protein occasionally, not constantly.** Protein treatments help high-porosity hair hold moisture more effectively, but overdoing protein on 4A hair causes brittleness. Every four to six weeks is a reasonable interval — only when the hair is feeling mushy, stretchy, or breaking easily when wet.
- **Satin bonnet at night.** High-porosity hair loses moisture to cotton pillowcases significantly faster than low-porosity hair. This is worth doing for both types, but it matters more for high porosity.

### Mixed porosity 4A

- Lighter products at the roots, heavier at the ends
- Focus deep conditioning on the ends
- Use heat to help roots absorb moisture that the ends take in more readily

## Common Mistakes

**Using heavy products on low-porosity 4A.** Castor oil and thick butters on low-porosity 4A create buildup without penetrating. The hair ends up feeling coated, not moisturised.

**Skipping heat when deep conditioning.** Low-porosity hair needs heat to open the cuticle enough for conditioner to do anything useful. Without it, you're conditioning the outside of the hair, not the inside.

**Applying products to partially dried hair.** Waiting until the hair is damp — rather than soaking wet — before applying leave-in means starting with less moisture and getting less out of the product.

**Confusing buildup with high porosity.** If products seem to sit on top, it could be low porosity — or it could be product buildup from previous sessions acting as a barrier. A clarifying wash first gives you a clean baseline before diagnosing porosity.

## FAQs

**I've been treating my 4A hair as high porosity for years and nothing works. Could it be low porosity?**

Very possibly. Test after a clarifying wash. If the spray bottle test shows slow absorption (water beads or takes time to sink in), you have low porosity and need heat during conditioning and lighter products.

**Does porosity change?**

Yes. Chemical treatments, consistent heat damage, and age all shift hair toward higher porosity. New growth is almost always lower porosity than older lengths. Re-test if your routine stops working.

**My 4A ends always feel dry even when my roots are fine. Is this a porosity issue?**

It's a mixed porosity issue — your ends are more porous than your roots and need more product and sealing attention. Focus your deep conditioner and sealant on the ends specifically.

**Hard water makes porosity testing harder — what do I do?**

Chelate first. A chelating shampoo removes the mineral film that can make low-porosity hair behave like high-porosity hair. Test your porosity after the chelating wash for the most accurate result.
