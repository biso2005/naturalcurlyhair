A school that tells a Black child their natural hair, cornrows, braids, or locs are against the uniform policy is very likely in breach of the Equality Act 2010. This article sets out the legal position, the escalation process, and the letter language that actually gets used.

---

## The Legal Position

Under the Equality Act 2010, race is a protected characteristic. The Act covers both direct discrimination (treating someone less favourably because of their race) and indirect discrimination (applying a policy that appears neutral but disproportionately disadvantages people of a particular racial group).

Natural Afro-textured hair — and the protective styles associated with it — are directly linked to race. A school uniform policy that:
- Requires hair to be "tidy" in a way that applies Eurocentric standards
- Prohibits cornrows, braids, twists, or locs by name
- Restricts hair that is "too big" or "too voluminous"
- Requires natural hair to be straightened or relaxed

…is applying a standard that disproportionately affects Black pupils. This is indirect race discrimination under the Act unless the school can demonstrate the policy is objectively justified by a legitimate aim — which aesthetic uniformity is not.

The **2023 statutory guidance** from the Department for Education makes this explicit. Schools must ensure their uniform and appearance policies do not discriminate directly or indirectly on grounds of race, and natural Afro-textured hair and associated protective styles must be accommodated.

The **Equality and Human Rights Commission (EHRC)** has published technical guidance to the same effect.

---

## What Schools Can and Cannot Require

**Schools CAN legitimately require:**
- Hair tied back during specific practical activities (science labs, food technology, PE with equipment) for genuine safety reasons
- Hair out of the eyes where there is a specific practical reason
- A general standard of hair being tidy — but "tidy" must be defined in a way that is workable for all hair types

**Schools CANNOT lawfully require:**
- That Afro-textured hair be straightened, relaxed, or otherwise chemically altered
- Removal of cornrows, braids, twists, or locs
- That natural styles be made to conform to a standard designed for straight hair
- Restrictions on hair beads or accessories associated with natural Afro hair styles, where the restriction has a disproportionate racial impact
- That a child's afro be "reduced" in size

## What to Do If Your Child's School Has Raised This

### Step 1: Get it in writing

If the school has raised the issue verbally — a teacher mentioning it to your child, a phone call — your first step is to request the school's written policy and ask them to confirm their position in writing.

Do not rely on verbal accounts (your child's or your own). You need the written policy and a written statement of what they're asking.

### Step 2: Write to the headteacher

A formal written letter citing the Equality Act 2010 is significantly more effective than an email or phone call. Use recorded delivery.

**Template letter:**

> Dear [Headteacher name],
>
> I am writing regarding [child's name]'s experience at [school name] concerning their hair.
>
> [Child's name] has been [describe: told to change their style / told their afro is against policy / etc.]. Their hair is [natural Afro-textured hair / cornrows / braids / locs] — a natural characteristic of their racial background.
>
> I draw your attention to the Equality Act 2010, under which race is a protected characteristic. A school policy that restricts natural Afro-textured hair or associated protective styles (including cornrows, braids, twists, and locs) constitutes indirect race discrimination unless it can be objectively justified. Aesthetic uniformity does not constitute objective justification.
>
> I also draw your attention to the 2023 statutory guidance from the Department for Education, which requires schools to ensure their appearance policies do not discriminate on grounds of race.
>
> I request:
> 1. A copy of the school's hair and uniform policy in full
> 2. Written confirmation of what the school is asking of [child's name] and the specific policy basis for this
> 3. A meeting within [ten] school days to discuss this matter
>
> I expect that this matter will be resolved without the need for formal escalation, but I am prepared to escalate to the academy trust / local authority and, if necessary, to the Equality and Human Rights Commission if the school's position cannot be reconciled with its legal obligations.
>
> Yours sincerely,
> [Your name]

### Step 3: Escalation if the letter doesn't resolve it

If the headteacher responds defensively or does not change the position:

**Academy trust / MAT:** most academies are part of a multi-academy trust with a CEO or executive headteacher above the school level. A letter to the trust citing the same legal basis sometimes resolves matters the headteacher has dug in on.

**Local authority:** for maintained (non-academy) schools, the local authority has oversight. Write to the director of children's services.

**Acas:** Acas (0300 123 1100) provides free guidance on equality matters. They can advise on the process and what documentation to gather.

**Equality and Human Rights Commission:** the EHRC can investigate formal complaints. Their helpline is 0808 800 0082. This is a later-stage escalation, not a first step.

**Media:** schools have resolved these situations quickly when they became local news stories. This is a legitimate option if internal escalation fails.

## For Older Pupils

Teenagers experiencing hair discrimination at school face the same legal situation as younger children, but often want to be involved in the response rather than having it handled entirely by parents. Involving them in drafting communications and understanding their rights is appropriate — and gives them tools for similar situations later in life.

The 2023 statutory guidance, the EHRC guidance, and the Equality Act 2010 are all publicly available and can be read by secondary school students.

## FAQs

**The school says the policy applies to everyone equally — it's not discriminatory.** 

The Equality Act covers indirect discrimination — policies that appear neutral but disproportionately affect a particular group. A "no braids" policy applies to everyone but disproportionately affects Black pupils. That is the definition of indirect discrimination unless it can be justified, which appearance-based uniform policies generally cannot be.

**Another parent told me the school's been challenged on this before and it's just their policy.** 

Schools that have maintained discriminatory policies are not entitled to continue them because they haven't been successfully challenged yet. They are in breach of the law. A formal written challenge with the legal citations is different from a parent complaint that doesn't cite the Act.

**I'm worried about my child being treated differently if I push back.** 

This is a legitimate concern. Keep a written record of everything — dates, what was said, responses. Any negative treatment of your child after you raise a discrimination concern is potential victimisation under the Equality Act, which is also unlawful.

**My child doesn't want me to make a fuss.** 

Some children — particularly teenagers — would rather comply than have the situation escalate. That's their choice to make about their own body and their own experience. Your role is to make sure they know what their rights are. They can decide how to use them.
