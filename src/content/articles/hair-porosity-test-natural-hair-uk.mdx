## What Hair Porosity Means for Natural Hair

Hair porosity describes how easily your hair absorbs and holds onto moisture. It comes down to the structure of your cuticle layer — the overlapping, scale-like cells that sit on the outside of each hair strand. How tightly or loosely those scales sit determines how quickly water, conditioner, and oils can get in (and how quickly they escape again).

For natural hair specifically, porosity matters more than it does for chemically relaxed or fine straight hair, because the coil or curl pattern already creates natural points of vulnerability along the strand. Understanding your porosity can stop you wasting money on products that simply sit on top of your hair or, at the other extreme, products that pass straight through without doing anything useful.

### Low Porosity

With low porosity hair, the cuticle scales lie flat and tightly packed together. Water beads on the surface rather than soaking in quickly. You may notice that your hair takes a long time to become fully wet in the shower, and that it also takes a long time to dry. Products tend to sit on top rather than absorbing, leaving a coating or build-up over time.

Low porosity hair is not damaged hair — it is simply less open. Many people with low porosity find their hair feels moisturised once they do get moisture in, because the tight cuticle also holds it there well.

### High Porosity

High porosity hair has cuticle scales that are lifted, gappy, or damaged. Water rushes in quickly but escapes just as fast, which is why high porosity hair often dries rapidly and still feels dry or brittle. It tends to be more reactive to humidity, expanding in damp UK weather and shrinking in dry central heating.

High porosity can be structural (genetic), but it is also commonly the result of chemical processing, heat damage, or the natural weathering that happens to the older parts of your hair — the ends — over time.

### Medium (Normal) Porosity

Medium porosity sits between the two. The cuticle allows moisture in at a reasonable rate and holds it without too much difficulty. Most routine advice is written with medium porosity hair in mind, which is part of the reason standard product recommendations often feel irrelevant if you sit clearly at either end of the spectrum.

## The Four Common Porosity Tests — and How Reliable Each One Is

There are four tests that appear repeatedly in articles and videos about hair porosity. What most of those articles do not tell you is that some of these tests have meaningful accuracy problems. Here is a clear-eyed assessment of each.

### The Float Test

**How it works:** Take a clean, shed hair strand and drop it into a glass of water. Wait two to four minutes. If the strand floats, you supposedly have low porosity; if it sinks, high porosity.

**How reliable is it?** Not very — and there is science behind that scepticism. Research published in the *Journal of Cosmetic Science* (La Torre & Bhushan, 2006) examined how surfactant and silicone residue deposits coat the hair shaft. Any residue on the strand changes its surface tension and affects whether it floats or sinks, independent of actual cuticle structure. A strand from unwashed hair will behave differently from a strand that has been clarified — even if they come from the same head.

The New York Society of Cosmetic Chemists has described both the float test and the spray test as "qualitative assessments with limitations," and Root2tip, a UK trichology-informed brand, notes that in trichology training, no single at-home test is treated as definitive.

There is also a specific problem for UK readers: if you live in a hard water area — which includes most of London and the South East, where calcium carbonate concentration can reach around 300mg/L {/* TODO: verify exact figure against current TapWater.uk or Water UK regional data */} — mineral deposits coat the hair shaft over time. When you perform the float test, you may be testing the behaviour of that mineral coating rather than your actual cuticle structure. A strand from a hard water area may sink faster than the same hair would in a soft water region, not because the porosity is higher but because mineral build-up increases the strand's density.

**Verdict:** The float test is a rough starting point only. If you use it, do it on a strand taken after a clarifying wash, and treat the result as one data point rather than a conclusion.

### The Slip-and-Slide Test

**How it works:** Take a single shed strand and run your fingers from the tip up towards the root. If you feel a slight roughness or bumpiness, that indicates raised cuticles (higher porosity). If the strand feels very smooth from tip to root, the cuticles are lying flat (lower porosity).

**How reliable is it?** More useful than the float test, because it is assessing texture directly rather than relying on an indirect proxy like buoyancy. The variation you feel along the strand is real and corresponds to cuticle condition.

The significant limitation is that you are likely to feel a difference between root and tip on almost any natural hair strand, because hair naturally weathers as it grows. Research published in the *Journal of Investigative Dermatology Symposium Proceedings* describes this as "progressive degeneration from root to tip" — the root end is newer, the tip end has been on your head for months or years longer and has experienced more UV exposure, manipulation, and friction. This does not mean the test is wrong; it means one strand can genuinely have different porosity at different points along its length.

**Verdict:** Useful and direct, but run it on several strands from different parts of your head, and compare root region to tip region separately.

### The Spray Bottle Test

**How it works:** Take a small section of clean, dry hair and spray it with a few mists of plain water. Watch how quickly the water absorbs versus how long it sits on the surface as droplets.

**How reliable is it?** Reasonably practical as a real-world observation. Unlike the float test, you are watching actual absorption behaviour on your hair while it is attached to your head, which is closer to how you use products day-to-day.

The caveat is that product build-up still affects results. If your hair has residue on it, water will bead more than it would on a clarified strand, nudging you towards a "low porosity" reading that may not reflect your true cuticle structure. The New York Society of Cosmetic Chemists classes this, alongside the float test, as a qualitative assessment with limitations. {/* TODO: verify primary source access for NYSCC statement */}

**Verdict:** Practical and worth doing, but only after a clarifying wash.

### The Feel Test

**How it works:** This is less a formal test and more a set of observations about how your hair behaves. Does it take a long time to get fully wet? Does it dry quickly or slowly? Do products seem to sit on top? Does your hair feel dry shortly after applying conditioner?

**How reliable is it?** Arguably the most reliable indicator over time, precisely because it is based on repeated observation rather than a single moment. The float test gives you one result from one strand. Your ongoing experience of how your hair absorbs and retains moisture is a much larger sample size.

The limitation is that it requires you to know what you are looking for and to have ruled out other causes (product choice, technique, hard water effects) before drawing conclusions.

**Verdict:** The best long-term guide, and the one that should confirm or question whatever the other tests suggest.

## What Your Result Actually Means for Your Wash Routine

Knowing your porosity is only useful if it changes something practical. Here is what to actually do differently.

### Low Porosity: What to Change

The challenge with low porosity is getting moisture past the tightly closed cuticle in the first place.

**Use heat or steam during conditioning.** Warmth encourages the cuticle to lift slightly, allowing conditioner to penetrate rather than coat the surface. A hair steamer is one option; sitting under a hooded dryer with a plastic cap works on the same principle. Even rinsing with warm water before applying conditioner (rather than cool water) can help. {/* TODO: verify whether published evidence supports steamer benefit for low porosity specifically, or whether this remains anecdotal in the hair science literature */}

**Clarify regularly.** Build-up is a particular problem for low porosity hair because products accumulate on the surface rather than absorbing. A clarifying shampoo used once a month or so — depending on how much you use styling products — removes that layer and lets moisture in more effectively.

**Choose lighter product textures.** Thick, heavy creams are more likely to sit on top of low porosity hair rather than absorbing. Lightweight leave-in conditioners and water-based products tend to work better.

**Avoid layering too many products.** With low porosity hair, more product rarely means more moisture — it often just means more build-up.

### High Porosity: What to Change

The challenge with high porosity is sealing moisture in once you have got it there.

**Layer products with the LOC or LCO method.** Applying a liquid (usually water or a water-based leave-in), then a cream, then an oil (or cream then oil, depending on your hair's preference) helps seal each layer in sequence. The oil acts as a barrier that slows moisture loss through the open cuticle.

**Consider protein treatments.** Proteins — particularly hydrolysed proteins, which are small enough to enter the hair shaft — can temporarily fill gaps in a damaged or porous cuticle, improving strength and reducing moisture loss. The difference between light hydrolysed proteins and heavier protein treatments matters: high porosity hair that is also fine can be overwhelmed by heavy proteins, leading to stiffness or breakage. Start with a lighter option and observe how your hair responds.

**Try a pH-adjusting rinse.** Apple cider vinegar diluted in water is often recommended as an acidic rinse that encourages the cuticle to lie flatter, which in theory slows moisture loss from high porosity hair. {/* TODO: verify mechanism against a cosmetic chemistry source — confirm whether pH lowering measurably affects cuticle state or whether the evidence is primarily anecdotal */}

**Seal with an oil or butter after conditioning.** For high porosity hair, this is less optional than it is for other porosity types — without a sealing layer, moisture can leave the hair shaft as quickly as it arrived.

## UK-Specific Considerations

### How Hard Water Affects Porosity Test Results

Hard water contains dissolved minerals, primarily calcium and magnesium bicarbonates. Most of England — including London, the South East, the East of England, and parts of the Midlands — sits in a hard or very hard water area. {/* TODO: confirm current Water UK or TapWater.uk regional map data for accuracy */}

When you wash your hair repeatedly in hard water, those minerals deposit on the hair shaft over time. This affects your hair in two ways relevant to porosity testing:

First, as noted above, mineral build-up on the strand changes the results of the float test. A mineral-coated strand may behave more like a high-porosity strand even when the underlying cuticle is in reasonable condition.

Second, and more practically, mineral deposits can make it harder for conditioner and moisture to penetrate the hair shaft — effectively mimicking the behaviour of low porosity hair, even on hair that is structurally more porous. This means some UK naturals may find their hair feels like it needs more moisture, but standard low-porosity techniques do not fully help either.

A clarifying wash specifically designed to remove mineral build-up — sometimes called a chelating shampoo — is different from a standard clarifying shampoo, which primarily removes product residue. If you live in a hard water area and feel your routine is not working despite knowing your porosity, mineral build-up is worth investigating.

### Does the UK Climate Matter?

Yes, in a practical sense. The UK climate tends towards humidity — grey, damp, and cool for much of the year — punctuated by dry spells of indoor central heating in winter. High porosity hair is more reactive to these humidity swings: it can swell and frizz in damp conditions and lose moisture rapidly in dry, heated environments.

This means high porosity naturals in the UK may find their hair behaves quite differently in December (dry indoor air) versus a mild, overcast October. Sealing products become more important in winter, and anti-humectant ingredients may be worth considering on unusually humid days, though this is highly individual.

Low porosity hair in the UK's generally mild, humid climate may actually perform reasonably well outdoors, but can struggle in centrally heated rooms where moisture is stripped from the air.

## When Porosity Tests Are Not Enough

### Signs Your Result Might Be Wrong

If you have taken a porosity test but your routine still is not working — products are not absorbing, your hair feels dry despite heavy conditioning, or your curl definition is inconsistent — there are a few reasons the test result may be misleading you:

- **You tested without clarifying first.** Product residue or mineral build-up can skew every at-home test towards a "low porosity" reading.
- **Your porosity varies along your strand.** This is not a flaw in your hair — it is normal. Research published in the *Journal of Cosmetic Science* (Hessefort et al., 2008) distinguishes between lab-measured porosity and the kind of qualitative assessment possible at home, and separately, the root-to-tip weathering documented in trichology literature means your roots and ends may behave quite differently from each other.
- **Your porosity has changed.** Chemical processing (colouring, relaxing, perming) and sustained heat styling can significantly increase porosity. UV exposure over time also contributes. If you have recently changed how you treat or style your hair, your previous porosity result may no longer apply. {/* TODO: verify with a peer-reviewed source that processing-related porosity change is measurable and clinically meaningful, not just theoretically noted */}
- **Different areas of your head have different porosity.** The hair around your hairline and nape may behave differently from the crown, partly due to different growth patterns and partly due to different levels of manipulation or heat exposure in those areas.

### When to Get a Strand Analysis

A professional trichologist can analyse your hair using microscopy or other physical assessment tools that go beyond what any at-home test can tell you. This is not necessary for most people, but it may be worth considering if:

- You have experienced significant hair loss, breakage, or texture changes and cannot identify the cause
- You have recently undergone chemotherapy, major hormonal changes, or a period of illness that may have affected your hair structure
- Your routine has been rebuilt multiple times based on porosity results, and nothing is working

A strand analysis will not always give you a simple "porosity number," but it can identify structural damage, weathering patterns, and other factors that at-home tests miss entirely.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is the float test accurate?

Not reliably. Surface tension, product residue, and mineral build-up from hard water all affect whether a strand sinks or floats, independently of cuticle structure. It is worth doing as a rough starting point only, and only on a strand taken after a clarifying wash. Do not base your entire routine on a single float test result.

### Can your hair porosity change over time?

Yes. Porosity can increase as a result of chemical processing (colouring, relaxing, bleaching), sustained heat damage, and natural weathering over time. UV exposure also contributes. New growth at the root will typically be lower porosity than older hair at the ends, even on the same head, on the same day. {/* TODO: verify with primary source — L'Oréal and Int J Trichology cited in brief; confirm accessible citation for this claim */}

### Does porosity vary from roots to ends?

Yes, and this is one of the most consistently overlooked points in standard porosity advice. The tip of your hair strand has been on your head for longer than the root end, and has experienced more friction, UV exposure, and manipulation in that time. This weathering effect means the ends of most natural hair are higher porosity than the roots, regardless of overall hair type. This is why testing a single strand and treating the result as a uniform answer for your whole head is limited.

### How does hard water affect hair porosity?

Hard water does not change your underlying cuticle structure, but it does deposit minerals on the hair shaft that can alter how moisture and products behave — and how at-home porosity tests read. In hard water areas (most of England), those mineral deposits can make hair behave as though it is less porous than it is, or can create a rough, dry feel that mimics the experience of high porosity damage. Using a chelating (mineral-removing) shampoo periodically can help get a clearer picture of your hair's actual behaviour.

### What is the most reliable way to test hair porosity at home?

The combination of the slip-and-slide test and the spray bottle test — both performed on clarified hair — gives a more grounded picture than the float test alone. But the most reliable guide over time is close observation of how your hair absorbs and retains moisture across multiple wash days. A single test in any format gives you a hypothesis. How your hair responds to a routine built around that hypothesis tells you whether it was right.
